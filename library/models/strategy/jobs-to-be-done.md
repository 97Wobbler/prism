# Jobs-to-be-Done

## Overview

Jobs-to-be-Done theory claims that people do not buy products; they hire products to accomplish specific jobs in their lives. A "job" is the progress a person is trying to make in a given circumstance — it has functional dimensions (the task to accomplish), emotional dimensions (how the person wants to feel), and social dimensions (how they want to be perceived by others). The model is predictive: it explains why a product succeeds or fails not by features, price, or demographics, but by the degree to which it helps the customer make the progress they are seeking. A product that perfectly executes on a job the customer does not actually need to do will fail; a clumsy product that solves a deep, underserved job will win. Applying the theory requires the procedure below to map the customer's situation, competing alternatives, constraints, and desired outcome.

## Core Variables and Relationships

The job and the forces that shape customer choice:

1. **The job itself** — the outcome the customer is trying to achieve in a specific circumstance.
   - Functional job: the practical task (e.g., get to work, preserve memories, feel organized).
   - Emotional job: the feeling sought (e.g., confidence, control, belonging, pride).
   - Social job: the identity or status sought (e.g., being seen as capable, tasteful, environmentally conscious).
   - Jobs are contextual and situation-specific; the same person hires different products for different jobs.

2. **Push of the situation** — the dissatisfaction or frustration that motivates the search for a solution.
   - Intensity of the push: how acute is the problem? Is it chronic or episodic?
   - Competing against nothing: the strongest competitor is often inaction (status quo), not another product.

3. **Pull of the new idea** — the attraction to a potential solution that promises to accomplish the job better.
   - Clarity of the promise: does the product clearly articulate which job it solves?
   - Credibility of the promise: has the customer seen evidence it works?

4. **Anxiety about switching** — the risk, cost, and habit friction of abandoning the current solution.
   - Switching cost: financial, time, effort, learning curve.
   - Habit lock-in: the incumbent solution is so embedded in routine that change feels risky.
   - Relationship switching cost: switching vendors may disrupt trusted relationships or processes.

5. **Job-solution fit** — the degree to which the product actually delivers on all three job dimensions.
   - Functional adequacy: does it accomplish the core task reliably?
   - Emotional resonance: does it deliver the feeling sought, or add unwanted emotions (friction, embarrassment, cognitive load)?
   - Social alignment: does using it align with or damage the identity the customer wants to project?

**The adoption decision balances:**
   Push (dissatisfaction) + Pull (new solution promise) − Anxiety (switching resistance) − Job misfit (unmet emotional/social needs) = Likelihood of hire.

High push and pull, low anxiety, and tight job-solution fit → hire the new product. Weak push or strong anxiety or job misfit → stick with incumbent or inaction.

## Key Predictions

- **A product can be technically superior and still fail** if it does not serve a job the customer actually needs to do, or if it solves the functional job but creates emotional friction (cognitive load, status damage, appearance of laziness). The iPhone succeeded not because it was the most feature-rich phone, but because it solved an emotional and social job (feeling smart, in control, connected) that competitors' functional superiority could not overshadow.

- **Demographic or psychographic segmentation alone is weak.** Two customers in the same demographic hire the same product for different jobs. Segmentation by job is far more predictive than segmentation by age, income, or "early adopter" status. The same person will reject a product in one circumstance and adopt it in another if the job changes.

- **The strongest competitor is often inaction, not another product.** Many product failures occur not because a competitor won, but because customers continued their old behavior (manual workaround, accept the problem, use a patchwork of tools). To win adoption, the product must overcome not just switching cost but the inertia and low-cost acceptance of the status quo.

- **Feature bloat and "improvements" often reduce adoption if they add complexity without serving the core job.** A product that excels at one job will fail if loaded with features for other jobs it was not hired to do. Simplicity that serves the focal job beats comprehensive capability that confuses it.

- **Early adopters and late adopters of the same product are often solving different jobs.** The early adopter may hire a product for a novel or niche job; the mainstream market hires it for a different job entirely. Messaging and positioning must shift to match the job the target segment is trying to do.

- **A product can own a job and grow the market** by making the job easier or more emotionally rewarding, or by reducing the anxiety cost of switching. Lowering switching cost (easier onboarding, lower price, clearer promise) can shift the balance even if the product itself does not change.

## Application Procedure

Instantiate the model against a specific customer segment and the decision to adopt or hire a product.

1. **Define the customer and the circumstance precisely.** Who is the customer? In what situation do they need to make progress? Write the circumstance in one concrete scenario (e.g., "A parent trying to keep their young child's photos organized and safe during their first year" — not "busy parents"). Vague circumstances produce vague jobs.

2. **Map the job in three dimensions.**
   - Functional: What is the core task? What outcome is the customer trying to achieve?
   - Emotional: How do they want to feel? What emotion would a successful outcome bring? (e.g., peace of mind, confidence, control, not shame or overwhelm).
   - Social: How do they want to be perceived by others? (e.g., organized, caring, tech-savvy, environmentally responsible).
   - Interview the customer or observe them; the job is often implicit and can be discovered only by looking at what they do, not what they say they want.

3. **Assess the push of the current situation.**
   - What is broken, frustrating, or inadequate about how they accomplish the job today?
   - How intense is the dissatisfaction (acute crisis vs. chronic annoyance)?
   - What are they doing now — using a competing product, using a patchwork of tools, accepting the problem?

4. **Identify the pull of candidate solutions.**
   - What new product or approach is the customer considering?
   - Does it have a clear, credible promise to accomplish the job?
   - How does it articulate which job it solves?

5. **Assess the switching anxiety.**
   - What is the cost to stop the current solution and switch?
   - Financial, time, learning curve, relationship, identity?
   - How embedded is the current approach in routine?

6. **Map job-solution fit.**
   - Does the product accomplish the functional job reliably?
   - Does it deliver the emotional outcome sought, or does it introduce unwanted emotions (friction, embarrassment, complexity, dependency)?
   - Does it align with the social identity the customer wants, or does it create status misalignment?
   - Identify any gaps: which job dimensions are underserved?

7. **Weigh the balance.**
   - Plot push, pull, anxiety, and fit on a qualitative scale (low/medium/high).
   - Identify the binding constraint: which factor is most likely to prevent adoption?

8. **Generate the prediction.**
   - Will the customer hire the new product, or stick with the incumbent/status quo?
   - Which job dimension is the leverage point for increasing adoption (reduce anxiety, clarify pull, increase emotional resonance)?

## Boundary Conditions

Jobs-to-be-Done is most reliable when applied to individual or household decisions with clear circumstance and deliberate choice. It is weaker or breaks down under:

- **Organizational or group purchasing decisions.** Jobs-to-be-Done is centered on the individual's circumstances and emotional needs. In organizations, political dynamics, sunk costs, consensus requirements, and vendor relationships add layers of job heterogeneity that a single "customer job" cannot capture. Supplement with organizational economics and stakeholder mapping.

- **Commodity or low-involvement purchases.** When the switching cost is near zero and the product is largely interchangeable (generic drugs, gasoline, salt), job precision matters little; price and convenience dominate. The model is overkill.

- **Extreme uncertainty or innovation in the job itself.** Jobs-to-be-Done assumes the customer can articulate (or be led to articulate) what progress they are seeking. In early-stage or discontinuous innovation, customers may not know the job exists yet, or the job itself may be nascent and rapidly evolving. Use exploratory innovation methods in parallel.

- **Network effects or status-signaling that override functional/emotional job fit.** If adoption is driven primarily by "everyone else is using it" (Snapchat, TikTok), the job becomes social-conformity rather than progress-toward-outcome. Job mapping still applies but the social dimension dominates, and switching cost includes social isolation. Supplement with network-effects analysis.

- **Habitual or non-deliberate choice.** Jobs-to-be-Done assumes the customer is actively seeking progress. For habitual, automated, or addictive product use, the job is often opaque even to the user themselves. Behavioral/neurological lenses may be necessary.

- **B2B or regulatory purchasing where the job is procurement compliance, not customer outcome.** If the buyer is a procurement officer constrained by vendor approval lists, the job is "check the box" not "make progress on the end-user's problem." The model must be applied to the *actual* job the buyer is solving, not the nominal job.

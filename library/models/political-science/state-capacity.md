# State Capacity

## Overview

State Capacity theory claims that the effectiveness, durability, and resilience of any regime — whether democratic, authoritarian, or hybrid — is governed not by formal institutions alone but by the underlying **administrative, coercive, and extractive capacity** of the state apparatus. The model is explanatory and predictive: it explains *why* a constitution on paper does not produce the outcomes it promises, why some states survive crises and others collapse, and why institutional reform often fails without corresponding capacity investment. It is particularly useful for diagnosing the gap between de jure and de facto state power. Applying it requires the procedure below to map a concrete state's capacity configuration.

## Core Variables and Relationships

State Capacity comprises three interrelated dimensions:

1. **Extractive Capacity** — the state's ability to tax, levy resources, and mobilize wealth from society.
   - Tax collection as % of GDP (actual vs. statutory rate)
   - Coverage and efficiency of revenue administration
   - Breadth of the tax base (formal vs. informal economy reach)
   - Ability to enforce tax compliance and detect evasion
   - Non-tax revenue sources (state enterprise, tariffs, resource extraction)
   - Fiscal surplus or deficit trajectory
   - Higher extractive capacity enables sustained spending on security, bureaucracy, and public goods; lower capacity forces reliance on short-term predation or external funding.

2. **Administrative Capacity** — the state's ability to implement policy, deliver services, and regulate economic and social activity at scale.
   - Civil service size, professionalization, and merit-based recruitment
   - Span of control (reach into local areas, rural hinterland)
   - Service delivery (education, health, infrastructure, permits)
   - Regulatory enforcement (labor standards, environmental rules, contracts)
   - Bureaucratic corruption and rent-seeking (captures whether capacity is deployed for public or private benefit)
   - Institutional memory and institutional stability (turnover, politicization)
   - Higher administrative capacity enables credible policy commitment, rule of law, and broad-based public goods; lower capacity produces fragmented service delivery and weak rule of law.

3. **Coercive Capacity** — the state's monopoly over force and its ability to suppress internal challengers and defend borders.
   - Military size and readiness
   - Police / internal security apparatus reach and professionalization
   - Ability to deter or suppress organized opposition (insurgency, protest)
   - Border control and territorial control
   - Legitimacy and cohesion of security forces (willingness to obey orders, not defect to opposition)
   - Intelligence and surveillance reach
   - Higher coercive capacity enables deterrence of rebellion, territorial defense, and order maintenance; lower capacity enables fragmentation, rival authority claims, and external vulnerability.

The three capacities are **partially independent but interdependent**:
- Extractive capacity funds coercive and administrative capacity, but extraction without administrative capacity breeds resentment and triggers evasion.
- Administrative capacity can reduce the coercive load if services and rule of law are credible; without it, states rely on coercion to maintain order.
- Coercive capacity can suppress opposition but cannot substitute for administrative capacity at scale without consuming resources and breeding instability.

The **binding constraint** is usually the weakest of the three: a state with high coercive but low administrative capacity is a police state; high administrative but low coercive capacity is vulnerable to organized opposition; high extractive but low administrative capacity breeds fiscal crisis and evasion.

## Key Predictions

- A regime with **high extractive, administrative, and coercive capacity** can sustain institutional promises (rule of law, elections, public goods) over time because it has the resources, reach, and enforcement to back them. Such regimes are durable through crises.

- A regime with **high coercive and extractive capacity but low administrative capacity** is a **rent-extracting autocracy** (e.g., Saudi Arabia pre-reform, Congo under Mobutu). It extracts resources and suppresses opposition but fails to provide credible public goods or rule of law, breeding elite instability and vulnerability to external pressure.

- A regime with **low coercive capacity but high administrative capacity** (e.g., Singapore under Lee Kuan Yew) can maintain order through legitimacy, service delivery, and rule of law rather than force. But it is vulnerable if opposition becomes organized and armed; it often compensates by maintaining selective coercive capacity in security apparatus.

- **Institutional reform (new constitution, elections, decentralization) without corresponding capacity expansion** typically fails or produces perverse outcomes: elections without administrative capacity become tribal patronage contests; decentralization without local administrative capacity breeds local warlordism; rule of law without coercive capacity enables mafia capture.

- **Capacity collapse accelerates state failure.** When extractive capacity erodes (fiscal crisis), administrative capacity typically follows (wage arrears, defections from bureaucracy), and coercive capacity defects or splinters (soldiers unpaid or defecting to warlords). The collapse is often non-linear: a threshold exists below which the state cannot fund itself and fragments rapidly.

- **Uneven capacity across regions** produces **de facto federalism** or **peripheral state failure** even under centralized formal institutions: if capacity is high in the capital but low in periphery, the periphery develops parallel authority (warlords, tribal councils, NGOs), and the center cannot project power without committing coercive resources unsustainably.

## Application Procedure

Instantiate the model against a specific state or region at a specific time horizon.

1. **Define the scope:** Which state and time period? Are you assessing the entire state or a region within it? Specify whether you are evaluating de jure (formal) or de facto (actual) capacity.

2. **Assess extractive capacity.**
   - What is the tax-to-GDP ratio (actual collection, not statutory)?
   - Who bears the tax burden (elites, middle class, labor, informal sector)?
   - What is the breadth of the tax base (formal economy reach)?
   - Is the state running a fiscal surplus or deficit? Is it declining?
   - Are there major non-tax revenue sources (resource rents, external aid)?
   - Classify as High / Medium / Low. Cite at least two observable indicators per classification.

3. **Assess administrative capacity.**
   - How large and professionalized is the civil service?
   - Does the state reach into rural and remote areas with services (education, health, justice)?
   - How effectively does the state enforce its own rules (corruption level, contract enforcement)?
   - How stable are institutions (turnover of officials, politicization)?
   - Classify as High / Medium / Low. Cite observable outcomes (e.g., school enrollment rates, permit delays, bureaucratic turnover).

4. **Assess coercive capacity.**
   - What is the military and police size relative to population and GDP?
   - How cohesive are security forces (defection risk, loyalty to center)?
   - Can the state suppress organized opposition (insurgency, large-scale protest)?
   - Does it maintain monopoly on force or do rival authorities (warlords, militias) control territory?
   - Classify as High / Medium / Low. Cite indicators (e.g., ability to quell recent rebellions, territorial control).

5. **Identify the binding constraint.** Which capacity is weakest? That dimension limits the state's effective reach and durability.

6. **Locate capacity gaps by region / ethnicity / sector.** Is capacity uniform across the state or highly concentrated? Uneven capacity predicts state fragility in low-capacity areas.

7. **Project capacity trajectory.** Is extractive, administrative, or coercive capacity growing, stable, or declining? Declining capacity predicts vulnerability to shocks.

8. **Read off predictions:**
   - What is the regime's likely durability under a fiscal shock, insurgency, or mass protest?
   - What institutional reforms would fail or backfire without capacity expansion?
   - Which regions or sectors are at risk of state failure or parallel authority?
   - What is the state's vulnerability to external pressure (military, sanctions, aid conditionality)?

9. **Check boundary conditions** (below). If any apply, flag that capacity assessment alone is insufficient.

## Boundary Conditions

State Capacity theory is a structural model of the state's reach and effectiveness but does not explain all regime outcomes. It breaks down or becomes partial under:

- **Elite fragmentation and civil war.** When competing elites or ethnic groups have mobilized armed followers, state capacity becomes less predictive than the balance of military forces and foreign support. The state may have high formal capacity but lose wars if elite coalitions splinter. Use conflict-dynamics models (contested sovereignty, arms races) in parallel.

- **Resource curse and external rent-dependency.** States that receive large external rents (oil, foreign aid, diaspora remittances) can sustain high coercive and extractive capacity without building administrative capacity or domestic legitimacy. Capacity measures (tax/GDP, fiscal deficit) become misleading; the binding constraint is political will, not technical capacity. Supplement with rent-dependency analysis.

- **Colonial or postcolonial legacy effects.** State capacity inherits colonial-era institutions and ethnic-regional divisions that formal capacity metrics do not capture. A state may have high extractive capacity but low administrative capacity in peripheries colonized by minority groups. Use institutional-history analysis to locate inherited fragmentation.

- **Transnational criminal networks and informal institutions.** When cartels, smuggling networks, or mafia organizations achieve higher local reach than the state, they can undermine coercive capacity from below even if formal security forces are large. Capacity metrics miss this parallel authority. Assess legitimacy and institutional competition explicitly.

- **Digital and informational asymmetry.** Modern states with low administrative capacity can achieve coercive reach via surveillance and digital control (e.g., authoritarian regimes using mobile-phone tracking and social-media monitoring) that traditional capacity metrics miss. Conversely, high formal capacity may be brittle if it relies on centralized digital systems vulnerable to cyberattack or sabotage.

- **Rapid demographic or migration shocks.** Large inflows of refugees, rapid urbanization, or youth bulges can degrade effective capacity even if formal metrics (headcount, budget) are stable, because institutions were not designed for the new scale. Assess institutional strain explicitly.

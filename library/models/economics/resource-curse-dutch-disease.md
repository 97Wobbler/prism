# Resource Curse / Dutch Disease

## Overview

Resource Curse and Dutch Disease models claim that a large, unexpected
inflow of foreign exchange from natural resource export (oil, minerals,
diamonds) causes **systematic de-industrialization and loss of
competitiveness in non-resource tradable sectors**, even though the
resource itself is a blessing. The mechanism is not one of corruption or
mismanagement (though those can amplify it) but of **spending and
resource-movement effects**: the windfall raises real incomes and demand
for non-tradables (services, housing), which bids up wages and land
prices. This renders non-resource tradables (manufacturing, agriculture)
uncompetitive globally. When the resource boom ends — as commodity cycles
always do — the economy finds itself with atrophied tradable sectors and
structural unemployment. The model is **predictive and diagnostic**: it
explains why resource wealth often correlates with slower long-term growth
and institutional fragility, not because resources themselves are bad but
because the macro adjustment is painful and poorly managed. Applied to a
concrete country and commodity, the procedure below reveals which outcomes
are structural versus which can be avoided through policy design.

## Core Variables and Relationships

The model operates through two channels, both driven by foreign-exchange
inflow (Δ FX):

**1. Spending Effect (demand-side)**

- **Windfall export revenue (Δ FX)** → increases real national income.
- **Income rises → demand for non-tradables increases** (healthcare,
  housing, entertainment, construction).
- **Non-tradable prices and wages rise** (no international competition on
  supply).
- **Rise in non-tradable sector wages pulls labor out of tradable
  sectors** (including non-resource tradables like manufacturing and
  agriculture).
- **Non-tradable-sector investment rises** (real estate, infrastructure),
  crowding out private investment in tradable sectors.

**2. Resource Movement Effect (supply-side)**

- **Δ FX raises the real exchange rate** (more foreign currency chasing
  same goods → local currency appreciates, or inflation in local-currency
  terms).
- **Appreciated real exchange rate makes non-resource exports more
  expensive** in world markets and import-competing sectors less
  competitive.
- **Capital flows into resource extraction** (firms, foreign investment).
- **Capital flows *out of* non-resource tradable sectors** (lower
  expected returns given exchange-rate headwind).
- **Skills, innovation, and supply chains in non-resource tradables
  atrophy** — the sectors shrink in size and lose agglomeration benefits.

**Key identity**: Real exchange rate appreciation (or sticky-price
inflation in local terms) is *not* a sign of strength; it is the
equilibrating mechanism that destroys competitiveness in tradables
outside the resource sector.

**Institutional feedback loops** (weakly predictive):

- Large Δ FX → weak incentive to build tax systems (easy to fund government
  from resource rents).
- Atrophied tradable sectors → weaker constituencies for investment in
  education, infrastructure, R&D.
- Boom-bust cycles → political economy of resource rents attracts
  rent-seeking and corruption.

These institutional effects are not the *cause* of the curse but
consequences that amplify it over decades.

## Key Predictions

- **Countries with large, unexpected commodity booms will experience a
  contraction in non-resource tradable sectors** (measured by employment,
  capital, export volume) *even if* the boom is real and not a supply shock.
  The contraction happens *during* the boom, not after.

- **Real exchange rate will appreciate** during the boom period. This
  appreciation is *not* a sign of overheating that will reverse; it
  reflects the structural shift and will persist as long as the resource
  revenue inflow is large.

- **Post-boom adjustment will be sharper and more painful** for countries
  that allowed non-resource tradables to atrophy. When commodity prices
  collapse, the real exchange rate must depreciate, but the tradable
  sectors are hollowed out — unemployment and lost output are larger.
  Countries that maintained or grew non-resource tradables suffer less.

- **The timing of the boom is critical.** A resource windfall arriving when
  the economy is at full employment will trigger wage-price spirals in
  non-tradables and faster real appreciation. A windfall during a recession
  may be partly absorbed into slack (though not fully — real appreciation
  still occurs).

- **Fiscal discipline partially but not fully insulates.** A government
  that saves the windfall in a sovereign wealth fund (SWF) and does not
  spend it domestically can dampen the spending effect. But the resource
  movement effect (real appreciation driven by the external surplus itself)
  persists even with high savings rates, compressing tradable sectors.

- **Small, highly open economies are more vulnerable than large,
  diversified ones.** Small economies cannot absorb the Δ FX without large
  real appreciation; large diversified economies have enough tradable
  sectors that one boom does not dominate the entire structure.

## Application Procedure

Instantiate the model against a specific country experiencing or facing a
commodity boom or bust.

1. **Define the commodity and the revenue scale.** What resource is booming
   (oil, copper, lithium, etc.)? What is the expected volume and price
   trajectory? Express total foreign-exchange inflow as a fraction of
   national income and as an absolute $ amount. Scope the time horizon
   (how long is the boom expected to last?).

2. **Establish the baseline (pre-boom) tradable and non-tradable sector
   structure.**
   - What fraction of employment is in non-resource tradables
     (manufacturing, agriculture, tourism, business services)?
   - What fraction is in non-tradables (healthcare, construction, retail,
     government)?
   - What is the current real exchange rate and has it appreciated or
     depreciated over the past 5 years?
   - What is the unemployment rate and degree of labor-market slack?

3. **Estimate the magnitude of the spending effect.**
   - By how much will national income rise per capita? (roughly, Δ income =
     resource revenue / population)
   - What is the income elasticity of demand for non-tradables in this
     economy? (for most countries, 0.8–1.2; high-income countries lean
     higher)
   - Estimate the wage pressure in non-tradables: if demand for
     non-tradables rises 10%, by what percentage do non-tradable wages rise?
     (depends on labor-supply elasticity; 5–10% is typical)

4. **Estimate the magnitude of the resource movement effect.**
   - What is the economy's sensitivity to real exchange-rate appreciation?
     (rule of thumb: a 10% real appreciation typically reduces tradable
     export volume by 5–15% over 2–3 years)
   - How large will the real appreciation be? (rough proxy: Δ real exchange
     rate ≈ Δ current account / trade elasticity; if the resource boom
     swings the current account by 5% of GDP, expect a 5–10% appreciation
     depending on elasticity)

5. **Trace the sectoral effects.**
   - Which non-resource tradable sectors are most exposed to
     exchange-rate appreciation? (tradables with low switching costs to
     imports are hit hardest)
   - Will labor move from non-resource tradables into non-tradables, or
     into unemployment?
   - Is there a construction / real-estate boom? (typical — construction is
     non-tradable and highly labor-intensive)

6. **Check fiscal and institutional constraints.**
   - Is the government saving the windfall or spending it? If spending,
     how much is directed to non-tradable investment (infrastructure,
     healthcare, education) vs. consumption?
   - Does a sovereign wealth fund exist? Is it large enough to sterilize a
     material fraction of the windfall?
   - What is the quality of institutions (tax system, rule of law)? Poor
     institutions amplify political economy distortions.

7. **Project the post-boom scenario.**
   - When the commodity boom ends (price collapse or depletion), will the
     real exchange rate reverse?
   - Which sectors will need to shrink further, and which will need to
     expand back?
   - How much unemployment and output loss will be required to re-equilibrate?
   - Can non-resource tradables be re-built, or is the atrophy permanent?

8. **Generate the prediction.**
   - Magnitude of real exchange-rate appreciation during the boom.
   - Sectoral employment shifts (flow from non-resource tradables to
     non-tradables or unemployment).
   - Duration and intensity of post-boom adjustment.
   - Whether the country will emerge with a more robust or more fragile
     tradable base.

9. **Check boundary conditions** (below). If any apply heavily, note that
   the standard model is incomplete.

## Boundary Conditions

Resource Curse / Dutch Disease models assume a **small, open, commodity-dependent
economy with slow institutional responses**. They break down or become partial under:

- **Very large, persistent, and anticipated booms.** If the commodity boom
  is known in advance (e.g., a new oil field discovered 10 years before
  production), markets price it in, real appreciation happens *before*
  revenue arrives, and the spending effect is dampened. The model assumes
  booms are surprises; if fully anticipated, agents front-load adjustment.

- **Economies with high savings rates and large, pre-existing sovereign
  wealth funds.** Norway, Botswana, and the Gulf states have large SWFs
  that sterilize much of the windfall by saving it abroad rather than
  spending it domestically. The spending effect shrinks; real appreciation
  is still present but smaller. The model's predictions are weaker in these
  cases.

- **Commodity booms paired with supply-side improvements.** If a resource
  boom is accompanied by productivity gains, infrastructure investment, or
  human capital deepening in tradable sectors, the tradable base may not
  atrophy. This requires *countervailing policy* (e.g., Chile's copper-boom
  savings discipline). The model assumes passive policy; with active
  counter-cyclical investment, outcomes improve.

- **Economies with highly substitutable non-tradable inputs.** If the
  non-tradable sector can import inputs (e.g., migrant labor, imported
  cement and steel for construction), the wage pressure and crowding-out
  effects are weaker. Economies with strict immigration controls face
  sharper real appreciation.

- **Multi-commodity booms where booms are staggered.** If multiple
  commodity cycles offset (e.g., oil booming while copper crashes), the net
  real-exchange-rate movement is smaller and the sectoral distortion is
  less acute.

- **Very large economies with diversified tradable sectors.** The model is
  designed for small, resource-dependent economies (e.g., Nigeria,
  Zambia, Venezuela). Large economies (China, India, Brazil) with large
  manufacturing bases can absorb commodity booms without de-industrializing
  in aggregate — sectoral shifts may occur but the macroeconomic identity
  is weaker.

# General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye)

## Overview

Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) describes the three-stage physiological response to any sustained stressor: an initial **Alarm** phase (acute mobilization), followed by **Resistance** (adaptation and compensation), and eventually **Exhaustion** (depletion of reserves and system failure). The model is descriptive and mechanistic—it explains *how* the body moves through these phases under prolonged stress, not *why* an individual chose to apply stress in the first place. In sports, the model predicts that an athlete's capacity, resilience, and performance follow a predictable trajectory as training stress accumulates, and that without adequate recovery, the system enters Exhaustion and performance collapses. The model is both explanatory and predictive when instantiated against a concrete training or competition timeline.

## Core Variables and Relationships

The syndrome comprises three phases, each with distinct physiological signatures:

1. **Alarm Phase** — Immediate response to stressor.
   - Sympathetic nervous system activation (catecholamine surge)
   - Cortisol elevation
   - Cardiovascular arousal (heart rate, blood pressure increase)
   - Immune function initial suppression
   - Energy mobilization from glycogen and fat stores
   - **Duration**: minutes to hours
   - **Effect on performance**: acute reduction (disorientation, jittery)

2. **Resistance Phase** — Homeostatic adaptation and compensation.
   - Sympathetic tone remains elevated but regulated
   - Cortisol remains chronically elevated but stabilized
   - Compensatory upregulation of stress-protective mechanisms (heat-shock proteins, antioxidant enzymes)
   - Cardiovascular and metabolic efficiency improve
   - Psychological habituation to the stressor (perceived effort may decline)
   - Energy stores are actively replenished if recovery (sleep, nutrition) is adequate
   - **Duration**: days to weeks (typical training block)
   - **Effect on performance**: acute reduction followed by recovery and gain; net capacity increases if stressor is removed or reduced

3. **Exhaustion Phase** — Depletion of adaptive reserves.
   - Sympathetic dysregulation (paradoxical blunting or collapse)
   - Cortisol dysregulation (flattened diurnal rhythm, insufficient acute response)
   - Immune function severely suppressed (increased infection risk)
   - Microdamage accumulation (muscle, connective tissue, bone)
   - Energy store depletion (glycogen, intramuscular stores, hepatic reserves)
   - Psychological maladaptation (mood disturbance, loss of motivation)
   - **Duration**: weeks to months if stressor is not removed
   - **Effect on performance**: sustained reduction, inability to recover even with days off

**Key relationship**: The Resistance phase is *time-limited*. If the stressor persists beyond the individual's adaptive capacity without sufficient recovery, the system *cannot* remain in Resistance indefinitely—it must transition to Exhaustion. The transition point depends on stressor intensity, individual genetic capacity, prior exposure history, and recovery quality (sleep, nutrition, psychological rest).

## Key Predictions

- **Initial performance dip followed by gain.** When a new stressor is introduced (e.g., a novel training protocol), the athlete will show acute performance loss in the first 24–48 hours (Alarm), then recover and often exceed baseline within 7–14 days if recovery is adequate (Resistance). If recovery is inadequate, the dip persists.

- **Performance plateau and creeping fatigue.** As the Resistance phase matures (typically 2–4 weeks into a fixed training block), performance gains slow despite constant effort. If training stimulus does not change, the athlete enters a plateau because the adaptive stimulus is no longer novel—yet the stressor continues to accumulate.

- **Nonlinear collapse into Exhaustion.** The transition from Resistance to Exhaustion is not gradual. Once adaptive reserves are depleted (a threshold governed by stressor intensity, frequency, sleep, and nutrition), performance and recovery capacity collapse rapidly over 3–7 days. An athlete may report feeling "fine" and then suddenly unable to perform or recover.

- **Exhaustion manifests as persistent underperformance despite reduced training.** An athlete in Exhaustion will not recover normal performance within 2–3 days of reduced training (as would occur in Resistance); instead, even light training causes disproportionate fatigue. Symptoms include elevated resting heart rate, poor sleep quality, elevated morning cortisol, and loss of motivation.

- **Recovery from Exhaustion requires a longer, deeper break.** Once in Exhaustion, the athlete cannot simply reduce load—systemic reserves must be replenished over weeks (not days). This is why "deload weeks" work during Resistance but fail once Exhaustion sets in.

- **Repeated cycles shorten the Resistance phase.** An athlete who regularly cycles through hard training (Alarm/Resistance) and recovery can improve cumulative capacity, but if the recovery window is chronically shortened (e.g., back-to-back competitions without rest), the Exhaustion threshold drops—the third or fourth successive hard effort fails to produce adaptation and instead triggers Exhaustion.

## Application Procedure

Instantiate GAS against a specific athlete, training block, or competition series.

1. **Define the stressor and its timeline.** What is the stressor (training load, competition, environmental stress, life stress)? When did it start? How intense and how frequent? Write the stressor in concrete terms: volume, intensity, frequency, and cumulative duration.

2. **Identify the athlete's current phase in the GAS cycle.**
   - Is the stressor recent (< 5 days)? → likely in Alarm.
   - Has the stressor been consistent for 1–4 weeks with adequate recovery? → likely in Resistance.
   - Has the stressor been sustained or repeated without adequate recovery for 4+ weeks? → likely approaching or in Exhaustion.

3. **Assess recovery adequacy.** For each recovery dimension (sleep, nutrition, psychological rest, training volume variance), estimate whether it is adequate for the intensity and duration of the stressor.
   - Adequate: 7–9 hours sleep, caloric/macro match to demands, regular rest days, variety in training stimulus.
   - Marginal: 6–7 hours, slight nutritional deficit, frequent hard sessions without structural recovery.
   - Inadequate: <6 hours, caloric deficit >10%, no dedicated recovery days, monotonous high-intensity stimulus.

4. **Map the athlete's trajectory across the three phases.**
   - When did Alarm onset? (should be obvious, acute symptoms)
   - When did the athlete transition to Resistance? (when acute symptoms resolved but fatigue began to stabilize)
   - Are there signs of Exhaustion onset? (persistent underperformance, elevated resting heart rate, loss of motivation, poor sleep despite reduced training)

5. **Predict the immediate outlook.**
   - If in Alarm with adequate recovery: expect rapid transition to Resistance within 5–10 days.
   - If in Resistance with adequate recovery: expect continued capacity gains for 2–4 weeks, then plateau.
   - If in Resistance with inadequate recovery: expect accelerated transition to Exhaustion within 1–3 weeks.
   - If in Exhaustion: expect 2–4 weeks minimum before capacity returns, even with substantially reduced training.

6. **Identify the recovery levers** that would most effectively prevent or reverse Exhaustion.
   - Which recovery dimension (sleep, nutrition, training volume, training variety) is the primary deficit?
   - What intervention (e.g., +1 hour sleep, +500 kcal, deload week, mental rest day) would have the highest impact given the current phase?

7. **Check boundary conditions** (see below). If any apply, note what additional factors (illness, life stress, genetic variation) would modify the GAS trajectory.

## Boundary Conditions

Selye's GAS was developed in animals under acute single stressors and applies most cleanly to sustained, uniform training stress in healthy athletes. It breaks down or requires modification when:

- **Multiple simultaneous stressors** (training + competition + life stress + illness). GAS predicts total stressor load, but the interaction of heterogeneous stressors (physical, psychological, immune) is not fully captured by a single "Exhaustion" stage. A fatigued athlete with a common cold may collapse into Exhaustion faster than training load alone predicts.

- **Individual variation in stress tolerance and recovery capacity.** GAS assumes a universal three-stage trajectory, but elite athletes show delayed Exhaustion onset, and some individuals are genetically predisposed to rapid transitions. The timing of phases varies widely (some athletes cycle every 2 weeks, others every 6). The model predicts the *structure* but not the *duration* without individual calibration.

- **Acute overtraining interventions** (e.g., a single competition or hard session during Exhaustion). GAS assumes the stressor continues; a one-off shock during Exhaustion may trigger acute Alarm symptoms overlaid on Exhaustion, producing a confusing symptom picture.

- **Chronic illness or immunosuppression.** Athletes with persistent immune challenges (chronic viral infection, autoimmune condition) may show Exhaustion-like symptoms that are not driven by training stress alone and do not fully resolve with reduced training.

- **Psychological or emotional stressors that decouple from physical training.** GAS treats stress generically, but an athlete experiencing severe psychological stress (injury, grief, relationship crisis) may enter Exhaustion without elevated physical training load, or the Resistance phase may be shortened. A complementary emotional-state or life-stress assessment is needed.

- **Late-stage Exhaustion with tissue damage.** If an athlete has accumulated microdamage (stress fractures, tendon rupture) during Exhaustion, the damage persists independently of stress reduction; physiological recovery does not repair structural injury. A complementary injury assessment is needed.

# Product-Market Fit (Sean Ellis 40% Rule)

## The Rule

If fewer than 40% of surveyed users report they would be "very disappointed" without your product, you do not yet have product-market fit and should expect to pivot or shut down before sustainable growth is possible.

## When It Applies

- Evaluating a new product or feature after the first 100–500 active users have used it for at least 2–4 weeks.
- Deciding whether to double down on user acquisition or to pause and refactor the core offering.
- Assessing whether a post-launch pivot is needed or whether the product can sustain word-of-mouth growth.
- Comparing two product directions to see which is closer to genuine product-market fit.
- Justifying to investors or stakeholders why growth has stalled despite increasing marketing spend.

## When It Misleads

- The 40% threshold is empirically derived from SaaS and B2B products; it does not transfer reliably to entertainment, consumer hardware, or winner-take-most platforms where engagement patterns differ fundamentally.
- A product with 35% "very disappointed" responses may still be growing healthily if cohort retention is strong and NPS is high — the rule conflates one signal with the whole picture.
- Timing matters: users surveyed at day 3 of onboarding will answer differently than users at day 30. A low score early does not falsify the rule; a low score after users have had time to form habits does.
- In B2B, a small number of power users may drive outsized value despite a low "very disappointed" percentage, making the rule a misleading average.

## Common Misuse

The heuristic is often applied mechanically: "We got 38%, so we pivot." In practice, teams misread the signal by surveying the wrong cohort (inactive users, sign-ups who never returned), by confusing the question ("I'd miss it" ≠ "I'd be very disappointed"), or by treating a single snapshot as a binary gate when the rule is a trend indicator — the question is not "Do we have 40% today?" but "Are we moving toward 40% week-on-week?"

Another common misuse is using the rule to *justify* inaction: "We're not at 40% yet, so marketing is premature." Ellis's framework is specifically about deciding to pivot *or* to commit to acquisition; it is not permission to stall indefinitely in exploration mode.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in product-market fit assessment lenses, as the quantitative check step after user interviews and retention metrics are analyzed. Survey a random sample of active users (not churned; not sign-ups) with the exact Ellis question, tabulate "very disappointed" responses, and record the percentage and trend.
- **Sanity-gate**: on any recommendation to accelerate growth spend, user acquisition, or feature expansion, ask: "What is the current 'very disappointed' percentage, and is it trending toward or away from 40%?" If below 30% and flat, flag the finding as premature unless other signals (cohort NPS, expansion revenue, referral rate) are exceptionally strong.

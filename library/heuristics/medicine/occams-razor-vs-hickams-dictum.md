# Occam's Razor vs Hickam's Dictum

## The Rule

When multiple independent pathologies can each explain observed findings, do not force them into a single explanation simply for elegance; Occam's Razor applies only when competing theories have equal explanatory scope.

## When It Applies

- A patient presents with symptoms that require invoking multiple coincidences if attributed to a single disease (e.g., respiratory symptoms, elevated liver enzymes, and a rash; each could be separate).
- A complex system failure shows evidence trails pointing in different directions, and the single-cause story requires downweighting some signals as "false positives."
- Diagnosis is stalling because commitment to "find the one thing" is preventing exploration of two or three independent problems.
- A clinician or analyst feels the pressure to be elegant — to find "the diagnosis" — at the cost of acknowledging that two unrelated conditions can coexist.

## When It Misleads

- In systems with a true hierarchical failure (one upstream cause triggering a cascade), forcing a multi-cause story creates spurious complexity and misdirects treatment.
- When "multiple independent causes" becomes an excuse to avoid committing to any diagnosis. Hickam's is not a shield against diagnostic decision-making.
- In low-complexity cases where a single, straightforward explanation fits all the data cleanly. Invoking Hickam's adds no value and obscures clarity.
- When the temptation to invoke Hickam's comes from insufficient investigation of the single-cause hypothesis, not from genuine evidence of independence.

## Common Misuse

The most common failure is treating Hickam's as a blanket permission to list every possible diagnosis without weighing them. The principle earns its keep only when the evidence *actively resists* a single-cause narrative — when forcing parsimony requires ignoring or discounting observations. Conversely, citing Occam's to shut down discussion of multiple causes without checking whether the single-cause story truly has equal explanatory power is equally misleading.

Another misuse is confusion about what "independent" means. Two findings are not independent just because they belong to different organ systems; they are independent only if no single upstream cause would naturally produce both.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in differential diagnosis lenses, after the hypothesis list is built, explicitly test each top hypothesis for whether it accounts for *all* observed findings or only some. If the single-cause hypothesis requires downweighting or explaining away multiple findings, flag the multi-cause alternative.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each final diagnosis or root-cause finding framed as singular, ask: "Would two or three independent conditions explain the evidence more naturally than forcing all findings into one diagnosis?" If yes, present both pathways before finalizing.

# Hindsight Bias

## The Rule
Once an outcome is known, people systematically overestimate how predictable that outcome was before it occurred.

## When It Applies
- Reviewing a failed project or incident and hearing "it was obvious this would happen" from people who did not predict it beforehand.
- Evaluating a past decision: judging whether the decision-maker was negligent by comparing their choice against the actual outcome, rather than against the information available at decision time.
- Reading a historical account and feeling that the events "had to" unfold that way, when the contemporaries faced genuine uncertainty.
- Assigning blame in post-mortems, where the outcome is now certain but was not when the decision was made.

## When It Misleads
- When the outcome *was* actually foreseeable with the information available at the time, and someone did in fact predict it. Hindsight bias is a description of a *bias*, not an excuse to dismiss all claims of foresight.
- In domains with strong causal mechanisms or historical precedent, where patterns do repeat and experienced practitioners can legitimately forecast. Discounting their judgment as "hindsight bias" mutes real expertise.
- When applied to shield decision-makers from accountability: "you can't judge me by the outcome because hindsight bias" is not a defense if the decision was negligent under the *ex ante* standard.

## Common Misuse
Using hindsight bias as a rhetorical shelter: "everyone says it was obvious now, but actually it wasn't predictable." This conflates the *cognitive distortion* (feeling of inevitability) with the *epistemic question* (was it actually foreseeable). A decision can be both genuinely hard *and* defensible, without needing to invoke bias. The heuristic is misused when it becomes an argument that no one should be held responsible because all outcomes feel inevitable in hindsight.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in post-mortem and incident-review lenses, as a mandatory step after the timeline is reconstructed. Before accepting a narrative of "obvious failure," force the agent to list the information and uncertainty that existed at decision time and compare against hindsight knowledge.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each "this was predictable" or "the decision-maker should have known" claim in a finding, ask: "What specific information available *before* the event would have made this prediction actionable, and do we have evidence it was available?"

# SAID Principle

## The Rule

The body adapts specifically and only to the demands imposed upon it; adaptation outside the imposed stimulus domain does not occur.

## When It Applies

- Prescribing training loads: a runner seeking speed gains must train at speeds above race pace, not just run long and easy.
- Troubleshooting performance plateaus: if strength is stalling, check whether the imposed demand (load, range, velocity, density) has stopped progressing; the body may have already adapted to the current stimulus.
- Evaluating transfer claims: if a training method worked for one athlete but not another, verify that both athletes actually received the same imposed demand; program names are not demands.
- Assessing why adaptation stopped: generic, varied training maintains fitness but does not drive further gains; continued improvement requires increasing specificity or intensity in the direction of the goal.

## When It Misleads

- In very-high-volume, low-specificity training (base building, general physical preparation), where broad adaptation matters more than specificity. Early-stage athletes benefit from non-specific work because they have large adaptation margins; SAID assumes the stimulus is already sufficient in quantity.
- When confusing "specific to the goal" with "identical to the goal." A 200m sprinter does not need to run 200m repeats every week; shorter, faster repeats and longer tempo work both impose demands that drive speed adaptation, just along different dimensions.
- In injury rehabilitation or deconditioning, where the constraint is tolerance, not adaptation drive. A runner returning from injury needs tolerable stimulus first, specific stimulus second; applying SAID to justify high-specificity loads too early invites re-injury.
- When the athlete is new to the movement or has large residual strength deficits, non-specific strengthening (general hypertrophy, movement quality) often unlocks more gains than premature specificity.

## Common Misuse

Treating SAID as a ban on variety or non-specific work. The principle says the body does not adapt *outside* the imposed domain, not that training *must* be specific every day. A strength coach may use general accessory work because it builds tolerance, movement quality, or addresses imbalances — all legitimate reasons that do not violate SAID; they fall outside the specificity claim.

Another common mistake is assuming that because two workouts *look* similar, they impose the same demand. Two 5km runs at different intensities, terrains, or paces are not the same imposed demand. Specificity is mechanical and metabolic, not visual.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in training program review or load prescription lenses, as a check step after goals are stated. For each goal, explicitly name the imposed demand (load, velocity, range, density, metabolic pathway) and verify it appears in the program.
- **Sanity-gate**: on any performance plateau finding, ask: "What is the specific imposed demand associated with the goal, and has that demand progressed in the last 4–6 weeks?" If not, the stall is expected, not mysterious.

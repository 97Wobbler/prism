# FITT Principle

## The Rule
A sustainable training effect requires simultaneous attention to frequency, intensity, duration, and type of exercise; optimizing one while neglecting the others produces adaptation plateaus or injury.

## When It Applies
- Designing a new training program where the athlete or coach is tempted to maximize intensity alone while keeping other variables constant.
- Evaluating why an athlete has plateaued despite months of consistent work — often one FITT variable is being held fixed while the others are overstressed.
- Periodizing a season: FITT variables shift deliberately across phases (building volume in base, intensity in peak, etc.).
- Returning from injury: all four variables must scale together; holding intensity high while reducing frequency and duration is a recipe for re-injury.
- Programming for populations with limited time, equipment, or resources — understanding which FITT variable is the real bottleneck sharpens the tradeoff.

## When It Misleads
- In very advanced or elite athletes, the principle becomes a ceiling rather than a floor. FITT is prescriptive for beginners and intermediate trainees; elite performers often optimize far beyond simple four-variable balance.
- When applied as dogma to novelty-seeking work (skill acquisition, sport-specific technique). Early technical learning does not follow FITT progression; rep structure and error correction matter more than frequency × intensity × duration.
- In rehabilitation or prehab, where the goal is not adaptation but movement quality or pain management. A therapy protocol optimized by FITT metrics may worsen outcomes if it forces the athlete into positions that trigger compensation patterns.
- When confusing "all four matter" with "all four must increase equally." Periodization explicitly reduces some variables while raising others; FITT is not a call for uniform progression.

## Common Misuse
The heuristic is often cited as justification to tweak all four variables simultaneously without a clear hypothesis about which one is the bottleneck. A coach says "we're applying FITT" and changes the sport, session length, weekly count, and load all at once — then cannot isolate why the outcome was better or worse. The principle is sharpest when one variable is chosen as primary and the others are adjusted to support it.

Another misuse: treating FITT as sufficient for program design. It is a necessary check, not a complete framework — it says nothing about exercise selection, movement quality, recovery modality, or sport-specific demands.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in a training-design lens, FITT is a mandatory four-cell review gate before finalizing any program recommendation. Each cell should be justified against the stated goal and previous cycle; any cell left unspecified or defaulted is a red flag.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each program or periodization recommendation, ask: "Which FITT variable is changing, which is constant, and why?" If the answer is "all of them" or "unclear," the recommendation is underspecified.

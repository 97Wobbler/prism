# Spaced Repetition

## The Rule

Spreading practice of the same material across time intervals produces stronger long-term retention and transfer than massing the same practice into a single session.

## When It Applies

- Designing curricula or training programs where the goal is durable recall beyond the test date.
- Choosing between a single intensive workshop and multiple short sessions spaced over weeks.
- Evaluating whether a learner's poor performance on a delayed assessment reflects weak learning or insufficient spacing of practice.
- Scheduling review sessions for high-stakes material (language acquisition, safety procedures, medical knowledge).

## When It Misleads

- When the goal is short-term performance (e.g., performance on an exam 24 hours after instruction), massed practice often produces higher immediate scores, and spacing may actually reduce short-term scores.
- In domains where the material is genuinely simple and requires only shallow encoding, the cognitive benefit of spacing may be too small to justify the administrative overhead.
- When learners have extremely high prior knowledge or expertise, additional spacing may produce diminishing returns; the benefit is largest for novice-to-intermediate learners.
- If spacing is implemented as mere repetition of identical tasks without interleaving or variation, the benefit collapses — spacing of rote drills is weaker than spacing of varied problem types.

## Common Misuse

Spacing is often cited as a magic bullet without attention to *what* is being spaced. Repeating the same fact in isolation over time produces far weaker gains than spacing retrieval practice across different contexts or problem types. Additionally, practitioners often mistake "spacing" for "giving more time"; the critical element is *retrieval under difficulty* (forgetting and re-learning), not just elapsed time.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in curriculum design or learning intervention lenses, as a check step: "Does the design distribute practice over meaningful time intervals, or concentrate it into single sessions? If concentrated, what justifies the choice?"
- **Sanity-gate**: when evaluating a learning program or training schedule, ask: "Are high-stakes retention goals being pursued with massed or spaced practice? If massed, has cost or logistics genuinely ruled out spacing, or is it an unreexamined default?"

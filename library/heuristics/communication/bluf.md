# BLUF

## The Rule
State your conclusion, recommendation, or main point in the first sentence; make the reader wait for detail only if they ask for it.

## When It Applies
- Writing a memo or email to a decision-maker with limited attention.
- Briefing a senior leader who needs to act before reading the full analysis.
- Communicating across organizational levels where the audience's time cost is inversely proportional to their rank.
- Crisis or incident communication, where clarity of the immediate problem and proposed action overrides narrative flow.
- Presenting findings to audiences who may not read past the first paragraph.

## When It Misleads
- In persuasive contexts where the reader's resistance is high, leading with the conclusion before building trust or credibility can trigger dismissal before the argument is heard.
- When the conclusion depends entirely on narrative or context that cannot be summarized — audiences will reject "here is what I concluded" without the reasoning that moved you.
- In cultures or settings where indirectness is a marker of respect or where leading with a bold claim appears aggressive or presumptuous.
- When the reader is genuinely exploring options and needs to understand the full landscape, not just your pick; BLUF filters out alternatives.

## Common Misuse
Treating BLUF as a universal law rather than a reader-dependent heuristic. A peer doing a code review may need the reasoning before the verdict; a CEO calling for a decision in ten minutes does not. The misuse is sending the same BLUF structure to both.

Another common failure: using BLUF as cover for lazy analysis. "Bottom line: we should do X" without the ground underneath it is not BLUF — it is abdication. BLUF requires that the detail exists and is available on demand; it is a *reordering*, not an omission.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in report-writing and memo-drafting lenses, as a structural rule: draft the opening paragraph as a standalone statement, then verify it would make sense if the reader stopped there.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each written communication artifact, ask whether the first paragraph answers "What do you want me to do?" or "What did you find?" If not, reorder to lead with the payload.

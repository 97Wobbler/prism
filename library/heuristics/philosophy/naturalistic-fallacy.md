# Naturalistic Fallacy

## The Rule

Do not assume that what is natural, instinctive, traditional, or evolved must therefore be good, right, or desirable.

## When It Applies

- Bioethical arguments: "humans naturally form pair-bonds, therefore monogamy is morally correct" or "we evolved to crave sugar, so sugar consumption is fine."
- Design and UX reasoning: "users naturally gravitate toward X, so X should be the default" — conflating observed behavior with endorsement.
- Policy arguments that treat existing social arrangements as inevitable consequences of human nature rather than contestable choices.
- Historical or evolutionary explanations of a practice, which someone then cites as a reason to preserve or replicate it.
- Arguments about "natural" boundaries, hierarchies, or roles as if their naturalness settles whether they should persist.

## When It Misleads

- In domains where "natural" function is actually a reliable guide to health or flourishing (nutrition, sleep, biomechanics). Evolution produced working solutions to real constraints; dismissing them as "natural" and therefore suspect can be costly.
- When used to shut down any appeal to human nature or evolved psychology, treating all such appeals as fallacy. Human nature is real and relevant; the error is *inferring value from it*, not *acknowledging it exists*.
- As a shield against uncomfortable truths: "you can't derive ought from is" does not mean human instincts, capacities, and vulnerabilities are morally irrelevant.
- In contexts where "natural" is doing actual descriptive work (ecosystem management, agricultural yield) and someone dismisses it by citing the fallacy without engaging the substance.

## Common Misuse

The fallacy is often invoked as a rhetorical trump card to dismiss an opponent's entire position without addressing the underlying claim. Someone says "humans are naturally tribal," and the reply is "that's the naturalistic fallacy" — but the fallacy is about *inference*, not about whether tribalism is real. The correct response is to accept the premise and dispute the conclusion: "Yes, we are tribal, and tribalism in this context is a bug, not a feature."

Another misuse is to treat all appeals to nature as equivalent. "This is natural" is not the same as "this evolved," which is not the same as "this is instinctive." Each has different evidentiary weight and different vulnerabilities to the fallacy.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in ethical or value-reasoning lenses, as a checkpoint after any premise that cites evolution, instinct, tradition, or prevalence. Force separation of the empirical claim (humans do X) from the normative claim (therefore X is good).
- **Sanity-gate**: on each recommendation with a normative component, ask whether the reasoning includes an unstated slide from "this is how things are" to "this is how things should be." If the slide is present, require an explicit argument for why the natural state is the desirable one.

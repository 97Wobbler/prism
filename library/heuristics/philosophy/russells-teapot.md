# Russell's Teapot

## The Rule
The burden of proof rests on the person making the affirmative claim; absence of evidence for an extraordinary claim is not evidence of absence, but it is reason to withhold assent until evidence is provided.

## When It Applies
- Evaluating unfalsifiable or untestable claims (supernatural beings, undetectable entities, hypothetical mechanisms with no observable consequence).
- Deciding whether to invest resources investigating a hypothesis when the claimant has offered no positive evidence.
- Separating "I cannot prove this is false" from "you should act as if this is true."
- Disputes over the default state: should we assume something exists until proven otherwise, or assume it does not until evidence arrives?

## When It Misleads
- When the claim is *practically* important even if unfalsifiable. "God exists" may be unfalsifiable, but the social and personal consequences of belief are real and demand engagement on their own terms, not dismissal via burden-of-proof.
- When the teapot *has* left detectable traces. If the invisible teapot orbits Mars and somehow affects satellite telemetry, the invisibility does not save the claim from scrutiny — the evidence does demand response.
- When applied asymmetrically: "I don't believe in X" is also a claim, and if that disbelief shapes action (e.g., ignoring a rare risk), it too carries burden. The heuristic applies to all claims equally, not just the ones you dislike.
- In domains of genuine uncertainty where multiple unfalsifiable priors are in play (metaphysics, axiology, some branches of epistemology itself). The heuristic is a *procedural* rule, not a substitute for philosophical argument.

## Common Misuse
Using Russell's Teapot as a conversation-ender rather than a diagnostic. Saying "that's unfalsifiable, so we can dismiss it" shuts down inquiry without actually clarifying what would count as evidence, what the claimant is and is not asserting, or why the claim matters. The heuristic is at its best when it *forces* both sides to name their priors and their evidence standards, not when it licenses hand-waving.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in epistemological audits or claims-analysis lenses, as a check on whether a claim is being treated as true without justification, and whether the burden has actually been discharged or merely assumed.
- **Sanity-gate**: for each assertion in a finding, ask: "Who made this claim, what evidence supports it, and has the burden been explicitly met or quietly shifted to the reader to disprove it?"

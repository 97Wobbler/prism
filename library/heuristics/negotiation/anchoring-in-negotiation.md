# Anchoring in Negotiation

## The Rule

The first substantive number offered in a negotiation exerts a disproportionate pull on the final settlement, even when both parties know the anchor is arbitrary or strategic.

## When It Applies

- Deciding your opening bid or asking price when you move first; a higher anchor correlates with higher settlements even after accounting for objective value.
- Defending against an extreme opening offer from the other side; anchors work even when their irrationality is transparent.
- Evaluating whether a final agreement is "reasonable" — if the anchor was extreme, your sense of what is reasonable has likely drifted toward it.
- Multi-round negotiation where the first round sets the frame for all subsequent rounds.

## When It Misleads

- When objective constraints are very tight and non-negotiable (regulatory limits, hard capacity ceilings, bankruptcy lines). The anchor still shifts perception, but the settlement space is pinched enough that the anchor's effect is visibly bounded.
- When one party has dramatically better information and can credibly signal it. If you can prove the anchor is impossible (e.g., "that price is below cost by a factor of three"), the anchor loses some power, though not all.
- In repeated or ongoing relationships where anchoring too aggressively in one round poisons trust and increases the cost of future deals. The anchor's short-term benefit can be outweighed by long-term damage.
- When the negotiation is asymmetric in power, and the weaker party has walked away as an option. Anchors work on the margin; they do not overcome a genuine reservation price.

## Common Misuse

Confusing "anchors are powerful" with "anchors are deterministic." Agents often treat the anchor as a law of physics — "I opened at $X, so I'll get $X plus half the gap" — when in reality anchors are one force among many (BATNA, information, relationship, alternatives, legitimacy). The heuristic is most misapplied when an agent anchors aggressively, then treats the resulting drift as inevitable rather than as a negotiation outcome that still had to be negotiated.

Another failure: assuming your anchor works equally well in all contexts. Anchors have less grip in contexts where the other side has strong alternatives, or where the anchor is so extreme it triggers reactance rather than adjustment.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in a pre-negotiation strategy lens, at the "opening position" step — force explicit comparison of two anchor scenarios and their expected effect on the settlement range.
- **Sanity-gate**: on a negotiated settlement, ask whether the final number is closer to the opener's anchor than to objective value would predict, and if so, whether the drift is explained by the other side's information advantage or simply by the anchor's pull.

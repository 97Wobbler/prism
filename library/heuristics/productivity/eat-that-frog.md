# Eat That Frog

## The Rule
Start your day by tackling the single hardest or most important task before anything else; momentum from that win carries the rest of the day.

## When It Applies
- You are stuck in procrastination on a high-stakes deliverable and smaller tasks are becoming a distraction.
- Your day is full of unequal tasks — some difficult and consequential, others routine and low-stakes.
- You have energy and clarity early in the day and you know it will erode if you waste it on low-friction work.
- You need psychological permission to move forward; completing the biggest thing first resets your sense of progress.
- Your team or project is stalled waiting for a decision or artifact that you've been delaying.

## When It Misleads
- The "frog" is actually dependent on prior work that hasn't been done yet. Starting it first wastes energy on the wrong task.
- The hard task requires context or collaboration that won't be available until later in the day; forcing it first means context-switching back to it.
- You are in a domain where sustained deep work takes 90+ minutes to enter flow state, and your real constraint is uninterrupted time, not task order. Eat That Frog buys psychological momentum at the cost of fragmentation.
- The "hardness" is actually complexity that will be better understood after you've done 3-4 easier, clarifying tasks. Eating the frog first can mean re-eating it.
- Your energy curve does not match the heuristic's assumption. Some people genuinely hit peak clarity at 10am or 3pm, not at 9am.

## Common Misuse
Confusing "hard" with "urgent" or "hard" with "important." A difficult task that does not move the needle is still a frog, and eating it first is procrastination disguised as productivity. The heuristic only works if the frog is actually the most consequential thing you will do that day — not just the most unpleasant.

Also treating Eat That Frog as a law that applies to every day, regardless of dependencies, context, or your actual energy pattern. The heuristic is a tiebreaker for days when you have genuine choice; it is not a schedule.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in daily planning or prioritization lenses, after the agent has identified candidate high-impact tasks, as a tiebreaker to set task order.
- **Sanity-gate**: when reviewing a daily plan or sprint commitment, ask whether the top 1-3 items are genuinely the hardest/most important, or whether they are hard because they are vague or blocked. If they are blocked, flag that Eat That Frog does not apply until the blocker is cleared.

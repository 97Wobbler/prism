# Theme Days

## The Rule
Assign a different primary role or work mode to each weekday, concentrating all tasks of that type on the same day to minimize context switching and maximize depth.

## When It Applies
- A leader or individual contributor rotates between disparate responsibilities (e.g., engineering, people management, strategy) that each require different mental modes.
- Deep work on a single class of problem requires 90+ minutes of uninterrupted focus, and the calendar is fragmented by unrelated obligations.
- A team's output suffers because task-switching overhead (loading context, rebuilding state, reorienting) compounds across many small tasks per day.
- A manager wants to protect open office hours or 1:1s for one day, leaving other days free for heads-down work.

## When It Misleads
- Emergencies and unplanned high-severity incidents do not respect theme boundaries. A rigid theme-day system with no escape hatch becomes a liability during crises.
- Some roles genuinely require distributed presence across all days (on-call, customer-facing support, time-sensitive decisions). Forcing them into a single day creates bottlenecks.
- Collaborators in different time zones or schedules cannot align with theme days without expensive coordination. The heuristic works best in co-located or highly synchronized teams.
- The work itself resists batching: debugging a production issue that spans Monday and Wednesday cannot wait for a "debugging day" six days away.

## Common Misuse
Treating theme days as a rigid law rather than a heuristic. People often create a beautiful theme schedule (Engineering Monday, Meetings Tuesday) and then abandon it entirely at the first interruption, rather than using it as a guide to defend the most defensible days. Another failure mode is assigning themes at too coarse a grain: "Product Day" that still requires rapid toggling between user research, roadmap design, and spec review is not a theme at all—it is the same chaos in a new package.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in a calendar audit or time-allocation lens, as a check step to diagnose whether a person or team is context-switching excessively, and whether theme-day-style batching would reduce that cost.
- **Sanity-gate**: when a planning or scheduling recommendation proposes spreading work across many days, ask whether consolidating by role or task type onto fewer days would improve focus, and whether the emergency exceptions are explicitly named.

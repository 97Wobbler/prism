# Ultradian Rhythms

## The Rule
Cognitive performance naturally cycles on a 90–120 minute rhythm; sustained focus decays predictably within that window, and forcing work through the trough produces diminishing returns and fatigue.

## When It Applies
- Scheduling focused work sessions: committing to a 90-minute block with a 15–20 minute break afterward yields better output than trying to push through.
- Planning meetings or presentations longer than 90 minutes: attention drops measurably after that threshold unless the rhythm is consciously interrupted.
- Diagnosing why a developer or team's output craters in the afternoon on days with back-to-back meetings: the meetings are chaining across multiple rhythm cycles without recovery.
- Tuning sprint pacing: sprints designed as two 4-hour deep-work blocks with a break in between outpace those designed as a single 8-hour push.

## When It Misleads
- In high-stakes or genuinely urgent tasks, where the rhythm is overridden by adrenaline or deadline pressure; the person does not *feel* the energy dip, and the heuristic becomes an excuse to rest when rest is not possible.
- For routine, low-cognitive-load work (email, data entry, checklists) where the rhythm is much weaker or absent; forcing a break becomes artificial friction.
- When the individual's actual rhythm is much longer or shorter — some people consistently show 120+ minute focus, others plateau at 60; the 90-minute claim is an average, not a law.
- In creative exploration or flow states, where breaking at a predetermined time interrupts a fragile momentum that took time to build.

## Common Misuse
Treating the 90-minute window as a hard rule and rigidly enforcing breaks even when work is proceeding well. The heuristic is a *default expectation* to plan around, not a signal that breaks are mandatory the moment the timer dings. Equally common: citing the rhythm as justification for a nap when the real problem is misaligned sleep, caffeine timing, or meeting overload — the rhythm is not an excuse, it is a pattern to respect.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in work-planning or sprint-design lenses, as a structural constraint on session length and meeting cadence before other factors are optimized.
- **Sanity-gate**: when a recommendation includes blocks of sustained focus longer than 120 minutes or chains multiple focus sessions with breaks shorter than 15 minutes, flag whether the rhythm is being consciously managed or assumed away. Ask: "Does this schedule assume people will maintain peak output beyond the 90-minute window?"

# Maker's vs Manager's Schedule

## The Rule

A maker's productivity depends on uninterrupted blocks of several hours; a manager's consists of one-hour slots; the two schedules are structurally incompatible, and forcing makers onto a manager's schedule destroys output.

## When It Applies

- Assigning deep-work roles (engineering, writing, design) to teams with frequent all-hands or sync meetings.
- Evaluating why a senior engineer's output dropped after a promotion into a managerial span.
- Designing communication norms for organizations with mixed maker and manager roles.
- Diagnosing friction in retros where one side complains of constant interruption and the other of poor responsiveness.

## When It Misleads

- In organizations where the "maker" work is actually highly collaborative and interrupt-driven (customer support, real-time operations, embedded-systems debugging). Some deep work is in the maker category; not all code work is.
- When applied as a blanket permission to ignore meetings and feedback loops. Some makers need to check in; the heuristic explains why *frequent* interruption is costly, not why all interruption is.
- When used to justify permanent maker isolation from decision-making. The incompatibility is about *scheduling*, not about participation; a maker can attend 90-minute monthly planning without losing flow, but not 30 minutes of meetings every day.
- In genuinely time-zone or geographically distributed teams, where some interruption is the cost of async coordination and "uninterrupted blocks" may be a luxury, not a right.

## Common Misuse

Using the heuristic to dismiss legitimate requests for status updates or collaboration as "manager talk," treating it as a cultural battle rather than a constraint-matching problem. The insight is that you cannot have both frequent interruption and sustained output from the same person; it does not mean interruption is illegitimate, only that it has a cost.

Another misuse: conflating "maker's schedule" with "introversion" or "isolation preference," then dismissing the heuristic when a maker enjoys pairing or collaborative work. The heuristic is about block structure and context-switching cost, not personality.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in workload assessment or role design lenses, when evaluating whether a role's calendar structure matches its output demands. Ask: "What is the minimum uninterrupted block this role requires, and does the calendar allow it?"
- **Sanity-gate**: when a finding recommends assigning a maker-heavy role (engineering, research, technical writing) to someone on a manager's schedule, force the agent to explain how output will not degrade, or recommend calendar restructuring.

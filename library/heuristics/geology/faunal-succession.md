# Faunal Succession

## The Rule
Fossil assemblages change in a consistent, directional way through geological time; identifying the characteristic fauna of a rock layer allows you to correlate it to other sections and assign a relative age, even if the sections are separated geographically.

## When It Applies
- Matching rock layers across separated outcrop areas where physical continuity is broken by erosion, faults, or burial.
- Assigning relative ages to strata before radiometric dating is available or practical.
- Validating stratigraphic interpretation by checking whether the fossil sequence matches expected biostratigraphic zones.
- Detecting unconformities or gaps in a section by noting abrupt shifts in fauna that skip expected intermediate forms.
- Paleontological surveys where species identification allows quick age placement of a rock without measuring the full section.

## When It Misleads
- In sections with rapid facies change (e.g., fluvial to marine switching back and forth), the same age can produce wildly different faunas; matching fauna alone can lead to false correlation of rocks that are actually coeval but lived in different environments.
- When fossils are reworked (eroded from older rocks and redeposited into younger ones), the fauna records the age of the source rock, not the host layer — age assignments based on the reworked fauna are backwards.
- In sections with strong provincial or endemic faunas (isolated basins, polar regions), the characteristic fauna may not be recognizable in globally-correlated zones, leaving the method inapplicable.
- Extinction and origination are not instantaneous worldwide; lag times between regions mean the "first appearance" of a species is diachronous — using a single species as a tie-point across large distances is risky.

## Common Misuse
Assuming that because a fossil species appears in a layer, the layer is the same age everywhere that species occurs. The heuristic correlates faunal assemblages (many species in concert), not single species. A single species can range across millions of years and multiple environments; only the *association* sharpens the age. Another failure mode is treating the method as infallible without examining the taphonomy — does the fauna represent the live community, or is it a winnowed lag deposit? Heavily burrowed horizons mix fauna from multiple time levels.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in stratigraphic correlation procedures, after fossil collection and identification, as the step that maps local fauna to regional biostratigraphic zones and checks for unexplained gaps.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each age assignment derived from fossils, ask whether the inferred fauna is consistent with the depositional environment, whether reworking is visible, and whether the fauna is a full characteristic association or a few scattered species.

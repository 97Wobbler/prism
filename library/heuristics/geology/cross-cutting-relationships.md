# Cross-cutting Relationships

## The Rule

A rock body that cuts across another rock body is younger than the rock body it cuts through.

## When It Applies

- Determining the relative age of an igneous intrusion (dike, sill, or pluton) relative to the host rock it invades.
- Dating a fault or joint set by observing whether it truncates bedding, earlier folds, or other rock bodies.
- Distinguishing younger lava flows that breach across older layered sequences.
- Establishing the sequence of events in metamorphic terranes where later shearing or intrusion cuts earlier structures.
- Correlating cross-cutting relationships across exposures to build a consistent relative chronology.

## When It Misleads

- When a later rock body is passively enclosed but does not actually cut across grain — e.g., a younger sill intruded along a bedding plane can be younger but does not "cross-cut" bedding in the sense that requires clear truncation.
- In highly deformed terranes where folding and metamorphism obscure the original cross-cutting geometry; what appears to cut across may be a refolded truncation whose original age sense is ambiguous.
- When the apparent "cutter" is not actually an intrusion but a fault breccia, hydrothermal vein, or other infill that may have formed during deformation of already-existing contacts — age determination then depends on the age of the host, not the vein.
- In mylonitic or heavily sheared zones where the sense of shear movement can flip the apparent age relationship if the shear zone itself is older than the feature it now appears to offset.

## Common Misuse

Over-applying the rule to features that do not actually achieve clear cutting — treating every contact or slight overlap as a definitive cross-cutting relationship. Field observation must show genuine truncation; proximity alone is not cross-cutting. Another common error is assuming that the principle applies equally to all scales; a microscopic vein may cut a crystal face, but the timing of vein emplacement relative to regional metamorphism is not thereby settled.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in a relative-age chronology lens, as a mandatory check step after each contact is examined in the field or hand sample. The agent must verify that the "cutting" feature actually truncates, not merely touches, the host.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each age-sequence conclusion from field mapping, ask whether the alleged younger body truly cuts across grain or merely abuts; if the geometry is ambiguous, flag the pair for radiometric dating or structural re-examination before finalizing the chronology.

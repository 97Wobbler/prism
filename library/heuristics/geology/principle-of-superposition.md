# Principle of Superposition

## The Rule

In an undisturbed sequence of sedimentary rocks, each layer is younger than the layer beneath it and older than the layer above it.

## When It Applies

- Establishing the relative age of rock strata in a vertical section where no structural disruption is evident.
- Interpreting depositional sequences in basins, deltas, and marine environments where layers accumulated in normal order.
- Cross-correlating ages between nearby drill cores or outcrops by matching the sequence of lithologies.
- Dating fossils or minerals within a stratum by constraining them to the age interval defined by radiometric dates of bracketing layers.
- Reconstructing environmental history from a succession of sedimentary facies where each layer documents a depositional event.

## When It Misleads

- In structurally deformed regions where faulting, folding, or thrusting has inverted, repeated, or omitted sections. Age order is scrambled and cannot be read from present vertical position.
- When a younger unit has eroded downward and filled a channel or cavity in an older unit, creating apparent inversions that the naked stratigraphic sequence does not resolve.
- In metamorphic and igneous terranes where original layering has been obliterated, rotated, or melted. The principle applies only to sedimentary sequences.
- When unconformities or hiatuses are present but unrecognized, causing a major age gap to be read as continuous deposition. Superposition gives order, not duration.
- In heavily bioturbated or homogenized sequences where layer boundaries are gradational or absent, making "layer" itself ambiguous.

## Common Misuse

Assuming superposition holds without first verifying that the section is undeformed. Agents often apply the rule to folded or faulted successions without examining the structural geometry, leading to false age assignments. Another common failure is conflating stratigraphic order with chronological continuity — just because layer B is above layer A does not tell you how much younger B is or whether any time elapsed between them.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in stratigraphic correlation and relative-dating lenses, as the foundational rule for reading a vertical section. Before assigning ages, verify that the sequence shows no evidence of structural inversion, major erosional surfaces, or metamorphic overprint.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each age assignment derived from stratigraphic position alone, ask whether structural deformation, erosion, or metamorphism could have scrambled the layer order, and whether field evidence explicitly rules out those scenarios.

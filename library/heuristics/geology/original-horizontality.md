# Original Horizontality

## The Rule
Sedimentary layers are deposited in horizontal or nearly horizontal positions; significant departures from horizontal indicate post-depositional deformation.

## When It Applies
- Identifying tilted or folded strata in outcrop to infer tectonic activity or folding events.
- Distinguishing primary depositional geometry from structures imposed after lithification (faulting, folding, compaction).
- Reconstructing paleodepositional environments: undeformed horizontal strata suggest quiet, low-energy settings; steeper angles suggest proximity to active margins or submarine fans.
- Reading cross-sections: horizontal reference datum allows confident assignment of age to adjacent layers and detection of unconformities.

## When It Misleads
- In prograding delta systems and submarine fans, foresets and bottomsets are deposited at angles up to 20–30° and represent primary (not deformed) geometry; applying the rule strictly forces misinterpretation of depositional dip as tectonism.
- In syndepositional deformation zones (growth strata along active faults, salt-related subsidence), tilting occurs during deposition; horizontal assumption erases the signal of contemporaneous structural activity.
- On continental margins and near rift shoulders, primary depositional dips of 3–8° are common; declaring all such dip "post-depositional" creates a false dichotomy between "truly horizontal" and "deformed."
- In paleomagnetic or magnetostratigraphic studies, the rule assumes a stable depositional surface; tilting during diagenesis or burial can occur without structural deformation.

## Common Misuse
Treating any departure from true horizontal as diagnostic of folding or faulting, without first documenting paleocurrent indicators, grain-size trends, or seismic evidence of the deforming structure itself. Agents often invoke Original Horizontality as a *conclusion* ("it's tilted, so it was deformed") rather than a *starting assumption* to be tested against local geometry.

Another trap is retroactively invoking it to explain away data that contradicts a favored tectonic model—declaring problematic dips "primary" only when they fit the narrative and "deformation" when they don't.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in stratigraphic correlation and cross-section interpretation lenses, as a check step after measuring layer attitudes. The agent should confirm that significant departures from horizontal are supported by independent structural evidence (fault traces, fold limbs, seismic dip) before attributing them to deformation.
- **Sanity-gate**: for each structural interpretation claim, ask whether the dip observed is consistent with known depositional environments and paleocurrent systems in the study area, or whether it requires post-depositional tilting to explain. If dips match progradational or syndepositional patterns documented elsewhere in the basin, flag the interpretation as potentially over-reading the rule.

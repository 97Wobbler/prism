# SMART Goals

## The Rule
A well-formed goal is specific about the outcome, measurable so you know when it is met, achievable given available resources, relevant to the broader strategy, and time-bound with a concrete deadline.

## When It Applies
- Drafting team OKRs or quarterly goals where the initial phrasing is abstract ("improve performance," "get better at X").
- Vetting a goal that has survived weeks of discussion but still lacks a clear success condition.
- Evaluating whether a goal is actionable or merely aspirational.
- Aligning a team's work to a larger strategic intent where drift happens silently.

## When It Misleads
- The goal is complex or requires real-time adaptation, in which case time-boxing too tightly forces either gaming the metric or abandoning the goal mid-quarter when the landscape shifts.
- Measurability is confused with "quantified," leading teams to optimize for the metric rather than the outcome ("we hit the KPI but broke the thing it was supposed to improve").
- The framework is applied as a gate that blocks all goals that don't fit the formula, when some genuine work (research, exploration, relationship-building) resists quantification without becoming worthless.
- Achievability is assessed in isolation from dependencies, organizational context, and competing priorities — a goal can be achievable in theory but not in practice when other teams are blocked or context changes mid-quarter.

## Common Misuse
SMART is often invoked as "pick a number and commit," bypassing the real work of specifying *why* the goal matters and *what happens if we miss it*. Teams treat the checklist (S-M-A-R-T) as proof of rigor when they've only filled in blanks. A goal can be perfectly SMART and still misaligned, irrelevant, or set in ignorance of a hard constraint.

The heuristic is also weaponized to dismiss goals as "too vague" without the effort to clarify them — the framework should be a **tool for sharpening**, not a reason to kill an idea.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in goal-setting or OKR-definition lenses, as a formatting checkpoint after initial drafting and before socialization — check each goal against the five criteria and surface which are missing.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each goal in a proposed set, ask whether you could defend to a stakeholder *exactly* how you will know the goal is met, and whether the deadline is firm or soft. If either is fuzzy, flag for clarification before commit.

# CFR Ritual Cadence

## The Rule

Conversations, feedback, and recognition lose their operational force if they happen ad hoc; they must be bound into a regular cadence tied to the work cycle, or they collapse into one-off gestures that feel performative to everyone.

## When It Applies

- Designing OKR execution rhythms where goal momentum depends on belief in progress, not just metrics.
- Team environments where performance feedback currently happens only at annual review, leaving weeks of drift unaddressed.
- Orgs where recognition exists but is sporadic, so it reads as favor-dispensing rather than norm-setting.
- Scaling teams where informal "just talk to people" breaks down and leaders default to broadcast updates.
- Turnarounds where the bottleneck is not clarity of goals but belief that the org actually cares about individuals hitting them.

## When It Misleads

- In genuinely urgent, short-burst projects (hotfix, one-week sprint) where adding a "conversation ritual" slows execution more than it accelerates it.
- When the cadence becomes the point: teams holding weekly CFR meetings that follow a script, hitting the ritual without any actual conversation, feedback, or recognition happening.
- In high-autonomy environments where over-structuring the human contact drives talented people to leave. Cadence should enable the work, not displace it.
- When feedback and recognition are used to paper over resource starvation or misalignment — ritual cannot substitute for actual fixes.

## Common Misuse

The most common failure is confusing frequency with substance. An org that holds weekly 1:1s but uses them to funnel status updates, quarterly feedback sessions that are form-filling, and recognition that is randomly awarded does not have a CFR ritual — it has theater. The cadence only lands if the conversation is about the person's progress against their work, feedback is specific and actionable, and recognition is tied to concrete contribution.

Another frequent trap is treating CFR as an HR overhead rather than a core part of execution. When leaders view the ritual as "something we have to do" rather than "how we actually stay aligned," teams sense the resistance and reciprocate with minimum-viable compliance.

## How Agents Use It

- **Embedded**: in OKR design lenses, after the goal statement is finalized, as a check step that names the cadence (weekly sync, bi-weekly feedback, monthly celebration) that will keep the goal visible and the team accountable. If the cadence is missing or vague, the goal design is incomplete.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each recommendation that involves a new goal or team alignment work, ask: "Is there a named cadence for conversation, feedback, and recognition tied to this goal, or will it drift into ad hoc gestures?"

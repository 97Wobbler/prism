# FAST Goals

## The Rule
Goals must be Frequent (checked regularly), Ambitious (stretch targets), Specific (quantified and measurable), and Transparent (visible across the organization) to drive accountability and real results.

## When It Applies
- Setting quarterly or annual OKRs at team or org level.
- Diagnosing why goals are not moving despite apparent agreement.
- Designing a culture of transparency where goal progress informs prioritization.
- Evaluating whether a goal is likely to stay top-of-mind for the team.

## When It Misleads
- When frequency becomes performative: meeting weekly to restate the same unachieved goal without changing tactics or context is theater, not accountability.
- In domains where the meaningful feedback loop is longer than the review cadence (research, infrastructure stability, multi-year projects). Forcing FAST onto a 12-month goal by breaking it into "transparent" monthly slices can obscure real progress.
- When "transparent" means "visible to leadership" but not "understood by the people doing the work." A goal broadcast to the org without context or ownership is noise, not transparency.
- Ambitious goals are not the same as unrealistic ones. Chasing a number that is demonstrably unachievable regardless of effort burns credibility and produces gaming of metrics, not motivation.

## Common Misuse
The most frequent failure is treating FAST as a format checklist rather than a discipline. Teams create "Frequent" check-ins that do not actually adjust the plan; write "Ambitious" goals that were never actually ambitious; define "Specific" metrics that are unactionable; and broadcast goals that nobody understands. The heuristic earns its keep only when frequency drives real re-planning, ambition is balanced against realistic capacity, specificity enables diagnosis, and transparency changes behavior.

Another common trap: using FAST to cull goals. The temptation is to say "if we can't measure it weekly and broadcast it, drop it." This produces a catalogue of highly visible vanity metrics while load-bearing work (technical debt, system resilience, internal quality) goes dark.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in an OKR design lens, as a validation pass on each drafted goal — test each dimension (frequency, ambition, specificity, transparency) against the team's actual context and capacity. Reject goals that fail the test or rewrite them.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each top OKR recommendation, ask: "What specific metric will we check weekly? Is that metric actually within the team's control? Is the target ambitious but achievable? And will the org see the progress alongside the plan?" If any answer is unclear, the goal is not ready.

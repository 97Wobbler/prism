# Whig History Critique

## The Rule
Do not reconstruct past events as an inevitable march toward the present outcome, reading backward from what won to explain why it had to win.

## When It Applies
- Tracing the "rise" of a technology, nation, or ideology as if earlier actors were striving toward a known endpoint (the internet "had to" emerge from ARPANET; democracy "was always" progressing).
- Attributing foresight or strategic necessity to historical figures for choices that made sense only in their immediate context.
- Dismissing dead-end paths, abandoned technologies, or defeated ideas as "obviously inferior," when contemporaries genuinely saw them as live alternatives.
- Explaining institutional or cultural change by identifying a single causal chain from past to present, ignoring roads not taken.

## When It Misleads
- Long-term structural trends are real: some outcomes *did* become more probable as conditions changed, even if not inevitable. The heuristic warns against *false* inevitability, not against recognizing genuine shifts in likelihood.
- Some historical actors *did* intend the outcomes we now see. Whig critique is not permission to erase intentional design; it is a warning against reading intent backward into actors who had no such intent.
- In domain-specific technical or scientific history, where cumulative progress is genuine (later physics builds on earlier physics), the backward gaze is partly legitimate.

## Common Misuse
Using the heuristic to dismiss *any* continuous narrative as automatically suspect. The warning is about *teleology* (the assumption that present = destiny), not about narrative itself. A coherent story about contingent choices is not automatically Whiggish — Whiggish stories are ones where the contingency disappears.

Treating "multiple paths were possible" as the final verdict. The heuristic is a corrective, not a conclusion; once you recognize roads not taken, you still have to explain why one was taken and what its consequences were.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in historical or retrospective causal lenses, as a mandatory check before finalizing the causal chain. Force the agent to name at least one plausible alternative outcome that actors could have achieved given the information and constraints *they* faced, not the information we have now.
- **Sanity-gate**: on each historical claim or origin story, ask: "Would an actor in 1920 (or 1980, or whenever) have seen this as the inevitable outcome, or are we reading backward?" If the answer is backward-reading, test whether the claim still holds after removing the assumption of inevitability.

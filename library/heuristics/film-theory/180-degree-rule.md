# 180-Degree Rule

## The Rule
Never cut across an imaginary 180-degree line established by the principal sight-line or action vector; crossing the line without a clear transition shot will flip the spatial geography and disorient the viewer.

## When It Applies
- Dialogue scenes between two actors: after establishing the 180-degree line, all reverse-angle shots must stay on the same side or introduce a transition that reorients the viewer.
- Action sequences with a clear direction of movement: a character moving left-to-right in one shot should continue left-to-right in the next, unless a cutaway resets the geography.
- Over-the-shoulder coverage during interviews or interrogations where the viewer expects stable positions.
- Establishing shots followed by closer framings: the line set by the wide shot constrains how you can frame the detail.

## When It Misleads
- In intentionally disorienting scenes (dream logic, psychological trauma, spatial confusion) the rule becomes a ceiling on meaning-making. Violating the 180 can signal instability as deliberately as following it signals clarity.
- When the geography is abstract or ambiguous from the start, there is no line to protect. Imposing a false 180-degree constraint stiffens what should be fluid.
- In montage or music-video editing, where rhythm and graphic composition take priority over spatial continuity. The rule assumes the viewer is tracking *space*; montage editing assumes they are tracking *energy*.
- In close-up dialogue where the spatial relationship is irrelevant and eye-line matching alone governs the cut. Strict adherence to the 180 can force awkward framings when the 180 itself is uninformative.

## Common Misuse
Treating the 180-degree rule as a law of nature rather than a *default prior for clarity*. Young editors often apply it mechanically and blame "bad coverage" when a violation creates discomfort, without considering whether the discomfort is a bug or the shot's intention. The rule is also misapplied retroactively: claiming a violation "breaks the 180" when the real problem is a weak transition or an unmotivated cut.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in edit-sequence and shot-coverage lenses, as a check on the spatial consistency of proposed cut sequences. Flag any 180-degree violation and require either a reframing or an explicit justification (e.g., "this is a disorient-on-purpose moment").
- **Sanity-gate**: for each sequence of dialogue or action, ask: "Is the 180-degree line stable throughout, or does every violation serve the story?" If violations accumulate without intention, the geography has failed.

# Kuleshov Effect

## The Rule
The meaning attributed to a shot is determined not by the shot's internal content, but by the shot that precedes or follows it.

## When It Applies
- Editing a sequence where a neutral or ambiguous shot is surrounded by contextual shots, and the sequence will be interpreted as depicting a causal or emotional relationship between them.
- Analyzing audience interpretation of a scene and realizing the emotional response originates from juxtaposition, not from what any single shot shows.
- Evaluating whether a shot order change will alter the viewer's reading of a character's motivation, emotion, or intent without changing the shots themselves.
- Designing a montage where you need the same footage to carry different meanings depending on what it is paired with.

## When It Misleads
- When applied to shots that are not genuinely ambiguous or neutral — a shot of an actor's face registering disgust will read as disgust regardless of what surrounds it. The effect requires interpretive slack in the shots themselves.
- In sequences with explicit on-screen text, dialogue, or causal markers that override the juxtaposition — audience understanding follows the logic, not the edit pattern.
- When the surrounding context is so overwhelming that it obliterates the shot's internal meaning rather than reframing it — the effect assumes both the shot and its context have semantic weight to combine.
- In pre-literate or non-narrative viewing, where audiences don't yet have the convention of reading shot sequences as a unified statement.

## Common Misuse
Treating the Kuleshov Effect as a universal law rather than a domain of application. Editors sometimes cite it to argue that any two shots can mean anything when juxtaposed; this ignores the requirement that the central shot must be *ambiguous enough* for the surrounding context to reframe it. A shot of a character fleeing in terror will not reframe as joy just because it is followed by a shot of a celebration. Additionally, the effect is often invoked to justify poor editing choices ("the juxtaposition carries the meaning") when the real work — building enough ambiguity in each shot — has not been done.

## How Agents Use It
- **Embedded**: in film analysis or editing-critique lenses, when evaluating whether a sequence's intended meaning relies on shot juxtaposition rather than individual shot content. The agent should verify that the central shot(s) contain enough visual or emotional ambiguity to support the reframing.
- **Sanity-gate**: for each claim about what a sequence "means," ask whether that meaning would survive if the surrounding shots were replaced or removed. If the meaning collapses, the Kuleshov Effect is doing the work; if it persists, the meaning is intrinsic to the shot itself.

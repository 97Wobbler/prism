# Marxist Criticism

## Overview

Marxist criticism is an interpretive stance, not a procedure. Its core
commitment is that cultural artifacts — novels, films, curricula, product
designs, organizational norms — **are not free-floating expressions of
individual creativity but are shaped by the material conditions and class
relations of the society that produces them**, and in turn participate in
reproducing those relations. The stance's job is to make visible the
class interests encoded in the artifact, the labor that produced it, and
the social relations it treats as natural. It is not a judgment of the
artifact's "quality" in the usual aesthetic sense; it asks a different
question and returns findings that other stances systematically miss.

## Foundational Commitments

Marxist criticism proceeds from a set of commitments it does not
re-argue in each use — these are the axioms that make the stance
coherent.

- **Material production shapes culture and ideology.** What people make,
  how they make it, and who controls the means of production are not
  background conditions but the generative ground of thought, art, and
  institutions. This is the "base / superstructure" intuition, though
  later Marxists (Raymond Williams, Althusser, Jameson) complicate it
  away from simple one-way causation.

- **Class is structural, not attitudinal.** Class is defined by
  relationship to the means of production (who owns, who must sell
  labor, who manages), not by income bracket, taste, or self-
  identification. A "middle-class aesthetic" is a structural position
  first, a taste second.

- **Cultural artifacts encode class interests, usually tacitly.** Every
  cultural product takes positions — about what is worth doing, who
  deserves what, what counts as a problem — and those positions serve
  some class locations over others. The artifact rarely announces this;
  reading the stance's job is to surface it.

- **Ideology works by naturalizing the historical.** The most powerful
  move of an ideology is to make its contingent arrangements appear
  obvious, inevitable, or "just how things are." Marxist criticism
  treats "it's always been this way" as a red flag.

- **Contradictions are informative.** Where an artifact has to work
  hardest to paper over a tension, it is pointing at its own
  class-political unconscious. Jameson's phrase: "always historicize."

- **Labor is often invisible in the artifact.** The people whose work
  the artifact depended on are usually not its named producers. Recovering
  their labor is part of reading the artifact honestly.

## Guiding Questions

Apply these questions to any artifact under a Marxist reading. They
replace the Analytical Procedure of a lens — there is no fixed order;
they are the repertoire the stance deploys.

**Production**
- Whose labor produced this artifact? Whose labor is named; whose is
  unnamed?
- What material conditions had to obtain for it to be produced at all?
- Who owns or controls the means through which it reached its audience?

**Class position and interests**
- From whose class position does the artifact make sense as "common
  sense"?
- Whose material interests does it serve if its premises are accepted?
- Which class locations are flattered, which are absent, which are
  represented as problems?

**What is naturalized**
- What social relations does the artifact present as given, inevitable,
  or natural? (Property? Hierarchy? Markets? The family form? Work
  itself?)
- What alternatives does it rule out of consideration before the
  argument even starts?
- Where does the artifact use the passive voice, abstraction, or
  technical vocabulary in a way that hides an agent?

**What is occluded**
- Whose voices, interests, or experiences are absent from the artifact?
- What work had to be offstaged for the artifact's story to cohere?
- If class conflict is named in the artifact, is it named as
  dysfunction or as structure?

**Contradictions**
- Where does the artifact strain against itself? Where are its
  internal tensions?
- What does the artifact *have to* avoid saying in order to keep its
  own frame intact?

**Historicize**
- What specific historical moment made this artifact possible?
- Is the artifact offering a universal claim that only makes sense
  under particular historical arrangements?

## What It Reveals

A Marxist reading surfaces findings that other stances routinely miss:

- **Invisible labor** behind a polished product.
- **Class interests** dressed as neutral principles ("merit,"
  "efficiency," "best practice").
- **Historical contingencies** presented as universal facts about
  human nature or markets.
- **The conditions under which a "common sense" became common** —
  showing that it is a position, not a default.
- **Whose problem is named as "the problem"** and whose is never
  named at all.

## What It Obscures

The stance has blind spots and applying it naively produces errors.

- **Non-class axes of power.** Gender, race, colonial history,
  disability, sexuality — these are not reducible to class, though
  they are entangled with it. A strict class-only reading misses
  their distinct dynamics. (Use a feminist, postcolonial, or
  intersectional stance alongside when appropriate.)
- **Aesthetic and formal specificity.** A Marxist reading sometimes
  treats the artifact as if its form were transparent to content.
  Formalist, structuralist, or close-reading stances recover what
  Marxism flattens here.
- **Individual agency and contingency.** Over-deterministic readings
  lose the actual room for maneuver of specific actors; later
  Marxists (Thompson, Williams) push back against this tendency,
  but the risk persists.
- **Non-Western production regimes** don't always map neatly onto the
  European industrial-capitalist template the classical theory was
  built around.
- **Its own conditions of production.** A Marxist reading that does
  not turn its questions on its own institutional and economic
  setting is incomplete by its own standards.

## Output Format

```
## Marxist Reading: <artifact name>

### Production conditions
- Visible producer(s): <named labor>
- Invisible labor: <whose unnamed work is required>
- Means of production / distribution: <who controls them>
- Historical moment: <specific conditions that made the artifact possible>

### Class position encoded
- The artifact "makes sense" from the position of: <class location>
- Interests served by accepting its premises: <what benefits whom>
- Class locations flattered / absent / represented as problems: <list>

### What is naturalized
- Social relations presented as given: <list>
- Alternatives ruled out before argument: <list>
- Language moves that hide agents: <passive voice / abstraction / technical
  vocabulary, with concrete examples from the artifact>

### What is occluded
- Missing voices or experiences: <list>
- Work that had to be offstaged: <list>

### Contradictions and tensions
- Internal strains the artifact has to manage: <specific passages /
  features and what they are covering for>

### Conversation with other stances
- What a feminist / postcolonial / formalist reading would add or
  push back on: <brief>

### Confidence: high | medium | low
<basis: how directly the artifact exposes its production conditions;
how tightly class interests map to what is naturalized; how much the
reading depends on contested inference>
```

# Foucauldian Power/Knowledge

## Overview

Foucault's stance is not a method of finding who "has" power but a
commitment to treating **power and knowledge as a single entangled
formation (pouvoir/savoir) that produces subjects, truths, and normal
behaviors — rather than a force that acts on pre-existing free individuals
from the outside**. The stance's purpose is to denaturalize practices
that present themselves as inevitable, scientific, or neutral, and to
show how the conditions under which something *can be said* or *can count
as true* are already shaped by a particular configuration of
power-knowledge. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure; its
findings are the ones that only become visible when you stop asking "who
is the oppressor?" and start asking "how did this arrangement become
self-evident?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **Power is productive, not only repressive.** Power does not only
  forbid, censor, and constrain — it also produces bodies, subjects,
  categories, and forms of knowledge. The subject is not *beneath*
  power but *made by* it.

- **Power and knowledge are inseparable.** There is no "pure" knowledge
  that stands outside power relations, and no power that does not
  simultaneously organize a field of knowledge. "Power/knowledge"
  (pouvoir/savoir) is a single hyphenated thing.

- **Power is relational and capillary, not possessed.** Power is not a
  thing that some people have and others lack. It circulates through
  relationships, practices, and institutions at every level. "The king"
  is one location; the family, the clinic, the school, the office, the
  database are equally live sites.

- **Discourse sets the conditions of the thinkable.** What can be
  *said* in a given field, by *whom*, in what *form*, under what
  conditions of acceptability — this is what discourse means. The
  discourse is prior to individual statements; it makes some
  statements possible and others literally unthinkable.

- **The subject is produced, not pre-given.** "The criminal," "the
  patient," "the student," "the user," "the employee" — these are not
  names for pre-existing natural kinds. They are positions that
  particular power-knowledge configurations call into being.

- **What is naturalized is the primary object of critique.** If it
  "goes without saying," that is where the work of power is most
  invisible and therefore most important to examine.

- **Resistance is internal to power, not external to it.** Power
  relations are never total; they always produce points of refusal,
  reversal, and counter-conduct. The stance looks for where resistance
  becomes visible and what form it takes.

## Guiding Questions

**Discourse**
- Who can speak with authority in this field? Whose speech is
  disqualified before it can be heard?
- What *counts* as a legitimate statement here? What procedures
  validate truth claims? (Credentials? Measurements? Citations?
  Databases? Testimonies?)
- Which vocabularies are available, and which are missing?
- What gets named as a "problem" in this discourse, and who gets the
  standing to name it?

**Production of the subject**
- What kinds of subject does this institution, practice, or technology
  produce? (A docile body? A risk to be managed? A self-monitoring user?
  A "normal" developer? An "at-risk" student?)
- What must the subject do, say, or measure about themselves to count as
  legitimate within the field?
- What new categories of person did this practice bring into existence?
  (The delinquent. The homosexual. The active user. The high-performer.)

**Practices, techniques, and dispositifs**
- What concrete techniques are in use — examinations, rankings,
  timesheets, dashboards, A/B tests, performance reviews, code reviews,
  leaderboards? Treat these as instruments of power-knowledge, not as
  neutral measurements.
- What is made visible (and to whom) by these techniques? What becomes
  invisible?
- What "dispositif" (assemblage of discourses, institutions, laws,
  architectures, technologies) holds this arrangement in place?

**The naturalized**
- What in this arrangement is presented as obvious, scientific, natural,
  or inevitable?
- What was the historical route by which it became so? (Foucault's
  "genealogical" move: reconstruct the contingent history of something
  that now seems self-evident.)
- What alternatives were foreclosed, and when?

**Where resistance is visible**
- Where does the arrangement produce friction, counter-conduct, or
  refusal?
- Who or what is positioned as a problem *by* the arrangement, and
  therefore reveals its edges?
- What forms of knowledge are being disqualified by the dominant
  discourse? (Foucault called these "subjugated knowledges.")

## What It Reveals

- The **productive** work of a practice (what subjects and truths it
  generates), which most critical stances miss because they look only
  for repression.
- The **historical contingency** of arrangements that present themselves
  as natural, scientific, or universal.
- The **capillary** operation of power inside practices that claim to
  be benign — schools, clinics, HR systems, recommendation algorithms,
  code-review norms.
- The **link between visibility and control**: who gets to see whom,
  under what conditions, with what records?
- The **production of subjects** through self-monitoring,
  self-optimization, and self-reporting — "technologies of the self."
- **Subjugated knowledges** that dominant discourse has disqualified as
  unscientific, anecdotal, or merely local.

## What It Obscures

- **Material economic structure.** Foucault famously brackets classical
  political economy; a pure Foucauldian reading can miss how capital
  accumulation, labor markets, and wage relations shape the field.
  (Pair with a Marxist reading when the material base is load-bearing.)
- **Agency and intentional action.** The stance is strong on how
  subjects are produced and weak on how they choose; the "who is
  responsible?" question is hard to put clearly inside the stance.
- **Macro-level explanation.** Foucauldian analysis resists tidy
  causal stories; it often returns dense description rather than
  simple explanation.
- **Normative grounding.** The stance denaturalizes normative claims
  but hesitates to make its own — which can make it hard to use for
  "what should we do?" decisions without supplementing.
- **Quick answers.** A Foucauldian reading typically wants more
  historical reconstruction than a fast-moving project can afford;
  applied in a hurry, it turns into vocabulary without substance.

### Discursive conditions
- Who can speak with authority here; whose speech is disqualified
- What counts as a legitimate truth claim; which validation procedures
  operate
- What gets named as "the problem" and by whom

### Subjects produced
- Kind(s) of subject this arrangement brings into being
- What self-monitoring or self-reporting is required
- New categories of person introduced or stabilized by this arrangement

### Techniques and dispositif
- Concrete techniques in use (examinations, rankings, dashboards,
  reviews, audits, telemetry, …)
- What is made visible and to whom; what is made invisible
- The assemblage holding the arrangement in place

### The naturalized
- What presents itself as obvious / scientific / inevitable
- Genealogy sketch: how did it become self-evident?
- Alternatives foreclosed along the way

### Points of resistance
- Where the arrangement produces friction or counter-conduct
- Subjugated knowledges being disqualified here

### Conversation with other stances
- What a Marxist / feminist / postcolonial reading would add or
  push back on

### Confidence: high | medium | low
<basis: how direct the evidence is; how much historical
reconstruction the reading depends on; whether the stance's blind
spots (material base, agency) are load-bearing for this artifact>

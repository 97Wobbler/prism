# Law and Economics (Posner)

## Overview

Law and Economics is an interpretive stance that **treats law not as a system of formal rights and duties but as a mechanism for allocating resources and managing incentives to minimize waste and maximize efficiency**. The stance's core commitment is that legal rules, whether or not their framers articulated it this way, can be understood as implicit solutions to economic problems — how to allocate scarce resources, reduce transaction costs, encourage productive investment, deter harmful conduct, and resolve disputes at minimum social cost. It is not a description of *why* rules exist, but a framework for reading what *work* they do in the economy. The stance asks what incentives a rule creates, who bears the costs of non-compliance or dispute, and whether an alternative allocation would waste fewer resources. Its findings are the economic logics and distributional effects that doctrinal legal reasoning passes over in silence.

## Foundational Commitments

- **Legal rules implicitly price behavior.** Every rule creates incentives and disincentives by allocating the cost of an action or harm. A tort rule that assigns liability to the defendant makes the defendant bear the cost of risk-creating behavior; a rule that assigns it to the plaintiff makes the injured party absorb the loss. These are not neutral framings — they price the behavior and shape whether it will be done more or less often.

- **Efficiency is the operative criterion, whether or not courts say so.** Posner's central claim is that common law doctrines — in contract, tort, property, and criminal law — tend over time to evolve toward efficiency even when judges do not explicitly invoke economic language. The "common law has a logic" and that logic is economic rationality, not formal legal categories.

- **Transaction costs are real and shape what law must do.** If exchange were costless, parties could bargain to any efficient outcome regardless of initial legal assignment. Because bargaining, monitoring, and enforcement are costly, law must step in to approximate what the parties would have contracted for if transaction costs were zero. This is the "Coase theorem" intuition — and the point of law is to do the work that markets cannot do cheaply.

- **Property rights must be clear and exclusive to function economically.** Ambiguous or non-exclusive property rights create a tragedy of the commons (no individual owner bears the full cost of depletion, so overuse results). Law's role is to define property in a way that gives someone the incentive to conserve and invest.

- **Wealth effects are allocative effects.** When law shifts who bears a cost (liability, default rule, entitlement), it shifts wealth and therefore incentives. These effects are not separate from "the law" — they are what the law does. A reading that ignores the distributional consequence is incomplete.

- **Rational-actor assumption is a working premise, not a claim about psychology.** The stance treats actors as responding to incentives (price, punishment, default rules) in a way that can be modeled as utility-maximizing, even if actual human psychology is messier. This is a heuristic for reading what the law presumes, not a claim about human nature.

- **Waste is the enemy.** The normative force of the stance is that legal rules should be judged by their tendency to minimize waste — uncompensated cost, redundant transaction, preventable harm, lost value. A rule that is formally just or doctrinally coherent but wastes resources is, under this stance, suspect.

## Guiding Questions

**Incentive structure**
- What does this rule reward, and what does it penalize? Who has to pay (directly or in risk) for non-compliance?
- Would a rational actor change behavior under this rule? In what direction, and by how much?
- Does the rule create perverse incentives — e.g., encouraging overdeterrence, fraud, or collusion?
- What is the rule implicitly assuming about what parties *would* do if transaction costs were zero?

**Allocation and efficiency**
- Who bears the cost of the harm or risk that the rule addresses?
- Is the cost borne by the party best positioned to prevent it (the "cheapest cost avoider")?
- Would an alternative assignment of liability, default, or entitlement result in less total waste?
- What would happen under a rule of strict liability vs. negligence vs. no liability? Which minimizes total social cost?

**Transaction costs and information**
- What does the rule assume about parties' ability to bargain, monitor, and enforce?
- Does the rule reduce information asymmetries, or does it leave them unaddressed?
- What transactions does the rule make more or less costly? For whom?
- Could parties contract around this rule if they had reason to? What does the answer tell you?

**Property and entitlements**
- How does the rule define the bundle of rights? Is it exclusive and transferable?
- Does the rule encourage investment and conservation, or does it invite exploitation or abandonment?
- Who can exclude whom from what? What incentives follow?

**Distributional consequences**
- Who gains and who loses under this rule compared to alternatives?
- Is wealth transferred from one class to another, and is that transfer a "side effect" or the operative point?
- Does the rule entrench market power or reduce it?

**Doctrinal incoherence as a signal**
- Where does the law use different standards for analogous situations (strict liability in one context, negligence in another, no liability in a third)?
- Do these differences correlate with differences in who can cheaply prevent harm, who has information, or who bears risk?
- Or is the incoherence a sign of doctrinal drift, political compromise, or a rule that has become obsolete?

## What It Reveals

- **The economic logic of doctrinal rules** that courts present as formal or moral principles — that defenses in contract law track whose breach is least costly to prevent, that tort standards track whose information is best, that property rules cluster around efficient bundling.

- **Hidden wealth transfers** — a rule framed as "correcting injustice" or "preventing harm" is simultaneously allocating costs and thus redistributing wealth from one party to another.

- **The function of default rules** — that many legal rules serve not to prohibit behavior but to set the baseline expectation (what happens if parties don't contract), and changes to defaults change behavior even when parties are free to contract around them.

- **Why certain liability assignments persist** — not because they are morally foundational but because they minimize the total cost of preventing harm and resolving disputes.

- **Regulation as implicit pricing** — that safety regulations, environmental rules, employment standards all work by making certain conduct more expensive, and the allocative effect depends on whether the rule targets the party best positioned to adjust.

- **Where law fails to track incentives** — categories that seem doctrinally coherent but that create perverse incentives or leave cheap-cost preventers with no skin in the game.

## What It Obscures

- **The distribution of wealth and power.** The stance brackets the question of *who* should bear costs and focuses on *how much* cost is incurred total. It can declare a rule "efficient" even if it systematically benefits the powerful and harms the vulnerable. (Pair with a distributive justice or critical race theory stance when equity is load-bearing.)

- **Non-economic values and motivations.** Dignity, autonomy, community, belonging, fairness as a felt intuition — these shape what law is *for* but don't reduce to efficiency. A person might sue not to maximize expected value but to assert a right or recover dignity. The stance flattens these motivations.

- **Measurement and proof.** The stance often asks "which rule minimizes total social cost?" but courts rarely have the data to answer this with precision. Efficiency arguments in applied jurisprudence often rest on guesswork dressed as analysis. (A behavioural economics or empirical jurisprudence stance qualifies this.)

- **Path dependency and institutions.** The stance assumes that the "best" rule emerges or that rules can be redesigned costlessly. It underweights how legal institutions, politics, and history lock people into suboptimal equilibria. (Institutionalist and historical stances recover what efficiency flattens here.)

- **Whose utility counts.** The stance treats all preferences as equivalent — it does not distinguish between harm done to a person and dissatisfaction in an observer's preferences. It is formally indifferent to distribution *among* the parties, which can allow radically unequal arrangements to pass as efficient.

- **Its own conditions of enunciation.** A pure law-and-economics reading that does not acknowledge its dependence on market price as the measure of value — and thus on a particular economic system — leaves its own normative ground invisible.

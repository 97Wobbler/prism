# Textualism/Originalism/Purposivism/Living Constitutionalism

## Overview

This is not a single stance but a constellation of four stances that have become the primary battleground in American constitutional law. Each makes a different commitment about where meaning lives in the Constitution and how change over time should be treated. **Textualism** insists that the Constitution means what its words mean, nothing more. **Originalism** insists that it means what those words meant when ratified — the original public meaning, not the ratifiers' private intent. **Purposivism** insists that meaning tracks the animating purpose or principle behind the text, which can migrate across cases. **Living Constitutionalism** insists that the Constitution's meaning evolves with the nation's values and circumstances, and that pretending it does not is both dishonest and dangerous. These are not technical disagreements about evidence or inference. They are fundamentally different commitments about what a Constitution *is* — what it binds, who gets to decide how, and whether the document can grow. The stance's purpose is to denaturalize the appearance that one method is obviously correct, and to show what each reveals and what each is forced to suppress.

## Foundational Commitments

- **The "original" is not a fact but an interpretive construction.** All four stances claim fidelity to something outside the present — to text, to original meaning, to purpose, or to living growth — but each constructs what it claims to find. Originalism's "original public meaning" required the entire apparatus of linguistic theory, historical corpora, and semantic intent-fixing; it did not simply announce itself in the document.

- **No interpretive stance is self-applying.** Every stance encounters hard cases where its central commitment does not dictate an outcome. When textualism cannot decide what "commerce" means, or originalism cannot recover the original meaning of an 18th-century phrase in a modern context, the stance must supplement itself with secondary moves. These moves are where real power accumulates — in the method for choosing among equally available meanings.

- **Each stance is built to solve a problem that another stance creates.** Textualism arose to constrain purposivism; originalism arose to constrain the wiggle room in textualism; living constitutionalism arose to constrain the cage that originalism built; purposivism arose to escape both textualism's literalism and living constitutionalism's apparent unitedness. Understanding why a justice adopts a stance requires understanding what outcome it forecloses that an opponent might reach.

- **Fidelity to the Constitution and constraint on judicial power are not the same thing.** A justice might believe sincerely that their method honors the document best, while observers might see that the method gives the judge discretion they need to reach a preferred result. The stance cannot simply assume that originalism is more constraining, or living constitutionalism more flexible, without examining the cases.

- **The stakes are not epistemic but political.** These are not primarily debates about the best method for recovering historical meaning. They are debates about whether a 1788 document can speak to 2026 without amendment, who gets to say what it says, and whether constraint or responsiveness is the higher good.

## Guiding Questions

**The text and its language**
- What does the relevant text actually say? (This is not trivial; courts often misquote or elide passages.)
- What are the available meanings of the key terms in the text? Are they monosemous, or does ambiguity inhere in the language itself?
- When the text says "Congress shall make no law…respecting an establishment of religion," does it constrain only Congress, or all delegated power? Does it ban all aid, or only *coercive* aid? (The text does not say.)
- What does the text *not* say? What silence, omission, or gap is the court filling in?

**Original public meaning**
- What did the relevant words mean in the mouths of the people who would have encountered them in 1788 (or 1868, or whenever)?
- To whom is the "public meaning" pitched? (Educated lawyers? Ratifying conventions? The broadest literate population? Usage guides?)
- When originalism appeals to dictionaries, legal treatises, or newspaper usage from the era, is it selecting sources to confirm a result reached first by other means?
- Can original meaning migrate across new factual scenarios (does "arms" originally meant mean muskets, or does original meaning encompass firearms the Framers could not imagine)?

**Purpose and principle**
- What is the *animating purpose* the text is trying to accomplish? (Not the purpose of a single clause, but the larger constitutional principle it instantiates.)
- Does the purpose inhere in the text, or must it be inferred from structure, history, and tradition?
- When a purpose is stated broadly (e.g., "equal protection"), does it retain its grip as circumstances change, or does it need reinterpretation in light of new social facts?
- If purpose is your anchor, what prevents you from sliding into whatever purpose you think best?

**Living meaning and tradition**
- How does the Constitution's meaning evolve? Through judicial acknowledgment of social change? Through amendment? Through new understanding of the document's own logic as society learns?
- What is the role of precedent in stabilizing meaning, and when does precedent become an obstacle to truth?
- If the document's meaning shifts, what makes it still *the same document* and not simply the judge's preferences in robes?
- Is there a "living" meaning that a reasonable modern person would accept, or does "living constitutionalism" just mask the fact that different judges want different outcomes?

**Constraint and discretion**
- Does the chosen interpretive method actually constrain the judge, or does it simply move the discretion to a different stage (in choosing which historical source to credit, in naming the purpose, in deciding how tradition evolves)?
- Where is the judge forced to decide contrary to her policy preference because the method requires it?
- Where does the method permit multiple outcomes, and what does the judge do then?

**Change and amendment**
- If the Constitution is not living, how can it possibly govern 2026? Does it govern only by amendment, or does it govern in some other way?
- If the Constitution is living, what prevents its meaning from inverting? Why can't a future generation "evolve" the First Amendment to mean the opposite?
- Is the amendment process obsolete? Or is it the *only* legitimate form of constitutional change, and everything judges do is usurpation?

**Precedent and overruling**
- What is the relationship between interpretive method and precedent? Can an originalist overrule a living constitutionalist precedent? On what grounds?
- When a precedent was wrongly decided (in the current justice's view), does the original method demand overruling, or does respect for law demand keeping it?

## What It Reveals

Each stance, if taken seriously, exposes what the others are forced to hide:

- **Textualism reveals** what words actually say, as opposed to what they are read to mean. It can make visible the degree to which purposivist courts impose meanings the text does not bear.

- **Originalism reveals** the historical contingency of what seems natural now. By insisting that meaning was fixed at ratification, it shows what choices the Framers made, and raises the question: on what grounds should we still be bound by their choices?

- **Purposivism reveals** that constitutional meaning is not a static object but a principle that can travel across contexts. It shows that textualism's fetish with original meaning often produces absurd results when the text encounters facts the Framers never imagined.

- **Living constitutionalism reveals** the impossibility of pure fidelity to a dead document and the ways that every interpretive method smuggles in contemporary values. It insists on transparency about what the Constitution must become to remain relevant.

- **All four together reveal** that the Constitution's meaning is underdetermined by any single method, and that the choice of method is itself a choice with real political consequences.

## What It Obscures

Each stance has blind spots that apply pressure on it in ways it cannot fully absorb within its own frame:

- **Textualism obscures** the fact that the text often does not speak. When "Congress shall not regulate commerce…among the several states" must be squared with the Internet, the text does not dictate the answer; textualism must supplement itself with pragmatism or history, which then drives the result. The claim to be "interpreting" rather than "amending" masks an act of construction.

- **Originalism obscures** that original meaning is not an object lying in the historical record waiting to be found. It must be constructed through choices about what counts as "public," what counts as the relevant public, which sources are trustworthy, and how to translate 18th-century concepts into modern questions. Different originalists make different choices and reach different results, which suggests the method is less constraining than advertised. (Pair with a feminist or postcolonial reading to see what voices originalism systematically excludes when "the public" is reconstructed from founding-era sources.)

- **Purposivism obscures** that a principle stated broadly enough to accommodate change is nearly content-free. "Equal protection" has been read to require segregation (in *Plessy*) and to forbid it (in *Brown*), and both readings invoked the same principle. Purposivism can become an exercise in choosing a principle first and deriving results from it. The method offers little guidance for choosing between competing principles that the text might encompass.

- **Living constitutionalism obscures** the relationship between change and constraint. If meaning evolves, what prevents the Constitution from meaning whatever is currently popular? The stance struggles to explain why *Roe v. Wade* should survive a public mood shift, or conversely, on what grounds it should be overruled. Without an anchor, the stance risks becoming indistinguishable from "the Constitution means what the current Court says it means."

- **All four obscure** the role of power, institutional politics, and material interest in the selection of interpretive method. A justice does not adopt originalism because historical linguistics proved it correct; she adopts it because it generates the outcomes her judicial philosophy requires. The choice of stance is often a choice of means to reach an already-determined end. (Pair with a critical legal studies or structural reading to examine which outcomes each method produces across a docket, and whether the method is doing the justificatory work, or serving it.)

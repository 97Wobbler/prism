# Deleuze's Movement-Image / Time-Image

## Overview

Deleuze's stance treats cinema not as an art form that represents reality or illustrates ideas, but as a **new regime of thought in which images themselves think through time and movement**. The stance's core commitment is that cinema produces two distinct kinds of images — the movement-image (where time is subordinate to action and sensorimotor schemas) and the time-image (where time becomes autonomous and acts directly on consciousness). This is not a method for interpreting films but an interpretive position that makes visible the **ontological structure encoded in editing, framing, and duration** — the philosophical commitments a film's formal choices embody. The stance asks: what kind of time does this film produce? What relations between action, perception, and affect does it naturalize? What temporal regimes become impossible within its form?

## Foundational Commitments

- **Cinema is a temporal and kinetic medium, not a representational one.** The essence of cinema is not its ability to record the world but its capacity to produce **movement and time as images**. A film does not represent action; it *is* action, unfolding in duration. This distinguishes Deleuze sharply from semiotics or narrative analysis.

- **The movement-image subordinates time to sensorimotor action.** In classical Hollywood cinema and most narrative films, time is the container for action; the plot *uses* time to unfold a sequence of movements, decisions, reactions. The sensorimotor schema (perception → action → reaction) is the logic of the movement-image, and it naturalizes a particular relation between subject and world: the subject acts because it perceives, the world responds to action.

- **The time-image liberates time from action.** In post-war European cinema (Bresson, Ozu, Godard, Duras), time becomes **autonomous** — it is no longer the backdrop for action but the primary material of cinema. In a time-image, we encounter duration, waiting, memory, and temporal disjunction *as* the substance of the film, not as interruptions of narrative.

- **Montage is not a technique; it is an ontological choice.** The way a film cuts — or refuses to cut — encodes a philosophy of time. Soviet montage (Eisenstein, Vertov) produces one kind of time; continuity editing produces another; the long take produces yet another. Montage is not stylistic variation; it is a choice about what time *is* in the film.

- **Cinema after 1945 underwent an epochal transformation.** World War II and its aftermath broke the sensorimotor link. Post-war cinema could no longer assume that action follows from perception, that subjectivity is rational and responsive. Time-images emerged as cinema's way of thinking the unthinkable of modernity — trauma, loss of agency, the opacity of the present.

- **Thought *happens in images*, not in concepts applied to images.** The stance is hostile to the idea that a film illustrates a theory or that analysis reduces the film to its "meaning." A time-image *thinks* directly through its formal operations; the thinking is not separable from the cinematic form.

## Guiding Questions

**The temporal regime**
- What is the relation between time and action in this film? Does action subordinate time (movement-image) or does time exceed and interrupt action (time-image)?
- How is duration experienced in the film? Are shots cut to the rhythm of action, or do they contain durations that have no narrative function?
- What kinds of temporal discontinuity appear — ellipsis, flashback, present-tense uncertainty, loops? Are these subordinate to narrative clarity (movement-image) or do they become autonomous (time-image)?

**Montage and editing**
- What montage regime is in operation? Classical continuity? Soviet collision montage? The refusal of montage (long take)?
- Where are the cuts, and what work do they do? Do cuts *produce* meaning (as in montage) or do they *break* meaning (as in alienation effects)?
- What is off-screen, and how does the film treat what is not shown? Is off-screen space narratively functional or does it insist on its own opacity?

**The sensorimotor link**
- Is the protagonist oriented toward the world through action? Does perception lead to decision, decision to movement?
- Where does the film break or complicate this schema? Are there moments where the character perceives but cannot act, or acts without clear motivation?
- What is the status of the body in the film — is it an instrument of will, or is it opaque, foreign, subject to affects it does not choose?

**Memory and the past**
- How does the film treat pastness? Is memory subordinate to present action, or does it intrude into the present, complicating it?
- Are flashbacks motivated by a character's recall, or do they appear without causal justification?
- What is the relation between what happened and what is shown — is the past recoverable, or is it irretrievable?

**Affect and the image itself**
- What affections does the image produce that are not reducible to narrative? (Dread, bewilderment, reverie, tedium, ecstasy?)
- Does the camera have a subjectivity, or is it withdrawn? Does the film invite identification or resist it?
- What is given to vision directly, without the mediation of a character's point of view?

**The world and the visible**
- What is the status of the world in the film — is it a coherent space to be acted in, or is it fragmented, illegible, resistant to meaning?
- Does the film present a world that the protagonist understands, or a world that exceeds understanding?
- How does the film show what is ordinary, banal, or resistant to narrative interest — waiting rooms, empty streets, faces at rest?

## What It Reveals

- **The temporal structure of a film as philosophical claim**, not as neutral form. Every edit, every duration, every temporal rupture is a commitment about what time *is* — revealed only when you stop seeing montage as "technique" and start seeing it as ontology.

- **The collapse of sensorimotor certainty in post-war cinema.** Films that seem "slow" or "plotless" are not failing at narrative — they are enacting a philosophical catastrophe, the breakdown of the link between perception and action. They make visible what modernity broke.

- **Time as a medium independent of movement.** Most criticism treats time as the duration of a sequence of actions. This stance surfaces time as *material* — as something cinema shapes and renders visible in itself.

- **The autonomy of affect from narrative motivation.** A face held in close-up beyond narrative justification, a location lingered in without plot necessity, a duration that stretches time — these become visible as primary rather than decorative when you read through this stance.

- **The politics of editing.** Soviet montage, classical continuity, and long-take cinema do not just *represent* different worlds — they *produce* different relations between subject and world, agency and passivity, clarity and opacity. This politics is only visible from within the stance.

- **Cinema's capacity to think what language struggles with** — duration, pastness, the opacity of the present, the body's refusal of intention. The stance recovers cinema as a philosophical practice, not a supplement to philosophy.

## What It Obscures

- **Narrative and genre as independent forces.** The stance is so committed to temporal and kinetic analysis that it can flatten the work of story, character psychology, and generic convention. A film noir or melodrama has narrative and affective logic that cannot be reduced to its montage regime; pair with a narrative or genre stance when plot is load-bearing.

- **The viewer's embodied experience and historical position.** Deleuze writes as if the time-image speaks directly to consciousness; he underestimates how a viewer's own history, body, cultural formation, and the historical moment of viewing all shape what registers as "time" in the film. Phenomenological or historically-situated readings recover this.

- **The material and economic conditions of cinema's production.** The stance treats cinema as a pure philosophical practice; it brackets the studio system, labor, technology, and capital that made particular forms of cinema possible. A Marxist or institutional reading would recover what is occluded here.

- **Non-European cinema and temporal regimes outside Deleuze's canon.** The stance was built on European (especially French) art cinema; it can miss or misread how cinema in other traditions — Japanese, Iranian, West African, Indigenous — produces time differently, not as a movement toward time-images but as autonomous systems of temporal thought.

- **The film's address to pleasure, desire, and identification.** The stance privileges temporal and kinetic thinking over the libidinal economies films mobilize. A psychoanalytic or feminist stance would push back on what is neutralized by this emphasis on time.

- **The adequacy of the movement-image / time-image binary.** Some films do not fit neatly into either category; some oscillate; some produce hybrid regimes Deleuze's framework struggles to accommodate. The stance can force films into its grid rather than letting them exceed it.

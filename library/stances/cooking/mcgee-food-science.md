# McGee Food Science

## Overview

McGee's stance is a commitment to **treating cooking as a legible field of chemical reactions, physical transformations, and measurable conditions — not mystery, intuition, or unexamined tradition**. The stance's purpose is to denaturalize kitchen "wisdom" (salt draws out moisture; room-temperature butter creams better; acid "cooks" fish) by asking what actually happens at the molecular level, which steps produce the claimed result and which are folklore, and where precision matters versus where cooks have been overthinking. It is an interpretive position applied to recipes, techniques, and ingredient behavior — not a procedure that generates new dishes, but a way of reading what is happening when a dish works or fails. Its findings are the ones that become visible when you stop asking "how was this done?" and start asking "why would that transformation occur, given what we know about proteins, starch, emulsions, and heat?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **Cooking is chemistry and physics, not magic or intuition.** Every transformation in food — browning, curdling, emulsifying, gelling, fermentation — obeys chemical and physical laws. There is no heat-independent reason a sauce breaks or a soufflé falls. The stance refuses mystery as an explanation.

- **Tradition is evidence of effect, not evidence of mechanism.** A technique that has worked for centuries signals that *something real happens*, but not necessarily what cooks have believed causes it. The mechanism must be tested against what chemistry and physics actually predict.

- **Precision is not always necessary.** The stance is not committed to exact measurement in every case. Some transformations are robust across a range of conditions; others are sharp transitions where small changes produce large effects. Knowing which is which is the point.

- **Sensory experience is data, not authority.** What cooks can taste, smell, see, and feel is accurate information about what has happened. But sensory experience does not explain *why* it happened or whether the explanation cooks give is correct.

- **Ingredient composition matters more than ingredient names.** "Cream" or "butter" or "egg" can vary wildly in water, fat, and protein content depending on source and production. The stance reads through the common name to the actual composition and asks what difference it makes.

- **Heat is not monolithic.** Dry heat, moist heat, radiant heat, conductive heat — they produce different results at the same temperature because they transfer energy to food at different rates and in different patterns. The stance distinguishes between them.

- **Timing and sequence are mechanisms, not just duration.** Why does *when* you add acid matter? Why does *how fast* you heat oil change what it does? The stance looks for the chemical reason the sequence produces the observed effect.

## Guiding Questions

**Mechanism and transformation**
- What is actually changing in the food at the molecular level? (Protein unfolding? Water evaporation? Fat oxidation? Starch gelatinization? Maillard browning or simple caramelization?)
- At what temperature, pH, or time does this transformation occur? Is it gradual or does it have a threshold?
- What evidence would show whether the stated mechanism is correct?

**Tradition versus necessity**
- Does the recipe require this step, or does tradition demand it?
- Which elements of the technique produce the claimed effect? Which are superstition or habit?
- If the mechanism is understood, can the technique be simplified or improved?

**Ingredient composition**
- What are the actual percentages of water, fat, and protein in this ingredient as typically sold?
- How much does this ingredient vary depending on source, season, or production method?
- Would a different ingredient with similar composition work the same way?

**The role of precision**
- Is this transformation sensitive to exact quantities, or robust across a range?
- What happens if you deviate from the recipe by 10%? 20%? At what point does the dish fail?
- Where in the technique is measurement critical, and where is eyeballing acceptable?

**Heat and energy**
- What kind of heat is being applied here? (Dry? Radiant? Conductive? Through steam or water?)
- How fast is energy actually transferring to the food?
- Could a different heat source or intensity produce the same result faster or more reliably?

**Why sequence and timing matter**
- Why does the *order* of operations produce a different result than doing them simultaneously?
- Why does *how fast* you heat or cool change what happens?
- What chemical reason explains why "wait until it reaches this stage" matters?

## What It Reveals

- **The actual chemistry and physics of cooking transformation** — what is literally happening, divorced from received wisdom.
- **Where tradition has learned something real through long trial and error, even if cooks misunderstood the mechanism.**
- **Which recipe instructions are essential and which are cargo-cult repetition** — unnecessary steps that persist because "that's how it's done."
- **Why some dishes are fragile (sensitive to precision) and others are robust** — which can inform whether you can cook by feel or need to measure.
- **Ingredient substitution that would actually work**, based on understanding what property of the original ingredient is load-bearing.
- **Common failures and why they happen** — why the sauce breaks, the cake sinks, the emulsion separates — with the chemistry visible, not mysterious.

## What It Obscures

- **Flavor as a primary category.** McGee's stance is strong on mechanism and weak on sensory pleasure; reducing cooking to chemistry can obscure why people *like* certain combinations and techniques that seem suboptimal by pure mechanism. (A gustatory or phenomenological stance recovers what chemistry flattens.)

- **Cultural and historical meaning.** A dish does not exist only as a set of chemical reactions; it is embedded in memory, tradition, social context, and identity. A purely reductionist reading misses *why* people cook and eat the way they do. (A historical or ethnographic stance is needed to recover this.)

- **Variation as feature, not bug.** The stance tends toward reproducibility and standardization; it can miss how variation — in ingredient sourcing, in technique, in intentional "mistakes" — is itself a form of knowledge and creativity. Regional and home cooking often *rely* on variation that a reductionist stance reads as imprecision.

- **Craft knowledge that has not yet been chemically explained.** There are techniques in professional kitchens and home traditions that work reliably but whose mechanisms are not yet well understood. Reducing "it works" to "we don't know why yet" can suppress investigation of what skilled practitioners know but have not yet formalized.

- **The embodied and situated nature of skill.** Knowing chemistry does not make someone a better cook; technique requires practice, feel, muscle memory. The stance can marginalize the non-verbal, embodied knowledge that experienced cooks develop. (A phenomenological or apprenticeship-based stance recovers this.)

- **Pleasure and meaning in eating.** If cooking is "just chemistry," the stance offers no grip on why eating together matters, why certain dishes are comforting, or why flavor memory is not reducible to volatile compounds. (A sensory or cultural stance is required for these questions.)

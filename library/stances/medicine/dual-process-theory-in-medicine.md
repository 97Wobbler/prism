# Dual Process Theory in Medicine

## Overview

This stance commits to **reading diagnostic reasoning and clinical judgment as the product of two irreducible cognitive systems operating in tension: System 1 (fast, intuitive, pattern-based, often called "gut feel") and System 2 (slow, deliberative, rule-following, analytical)**. The stance's purpose is to make visible *which system is in control at which moments* and *where the transition between them fails or is suppressed*. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure; its findings surface only when you stop asking "was the diagnosis right?" and start asking "which cognitive mode was active, and what made the clinician stay in or exit it?" The stance is suited to reading clinical cases, diagnostic interviews, reasoning transcripts, and institutional practices that shape when doctors slow down or trust their first impression.

## Foundational Commitments

- **Two systems operate in all clinical reasoning, and they have fundamentally different operating characteristics.** System 1 is fast, associative, low-effort, and runs in parallel; System 2 is slow, rule-governed, effortful, and sequential. Neither is intrinsically superior; both are necessary. But they compete for control of behavior, and the stakes in medicine are high.

- **System 1 is pattern-recognition powered by experience — and experience is a double-edged commitment.** Experienced clinicians have rich, accurate pattern libraries built from seeing thousands of cases. But those same libraries can be overwritten by recency bias, availability bias, anchoring, and stereotype. High experience does not guarantee correct System 1 output; it can calcify into false confidence.

- **System 1 does not know its own limitations.** System 1 produces feelings of confidence, fluency, and coherence — the "ring of truth" — whether the output is correct or not. A clinician can be absolutely certain and absolutely wrong, and System 1 will not warn them. This is the core problem the stance reads for.

- **System 2 override is effortful and rare.** Triggering deliberation (System 2) requires cognitive load, time, institutional permission, and often an explicit challenge or doubt. In busy clinical settings, System 2 is chronically starved of these resources. Most clinical reasoning stays in System 1.

- **The transition between systems is not automatic.** A clinician must notice that something is off — that the case doesn't quite fit, that they're uncertain, that the standard diagnosis seems too tidy — before they shift to System 2. Many errors occur because no such notice was triggered.

- **Cognitive biases are not character flaws; they are the shadow side of how System 1 works.** Anchoring, availability, representativeness, premature closure, overconfidence — these are not mistakes the clinician is making despite proper reasoning; they are *how pattern recognition necessarily works*. They cannot be willed away; they must be structured against.

- **The clinical environment shapes which system dominates.** Time pressure, fatigue, information overload, emotional stakes, institutional culture (teaching hospital vs. rural clinic), and the availability of colleagues for consultation all affect whether System 1 stays in charge or System 2 gets deployed. The stance reads the *conditions* that favor error, not just the error itself.

## Guiding Questions

**System 1 activation and dominance**
- How quickly did the clinician settle on a diagnosis? What triggered the confidence that they had "got it"?
- What pattern did the clinician match the case to? Was the match tight or loose? What features were used to clinch it?
- What explicit *feeling* accompanies the diagnosis — ease, fluency, a sense of coherence? Or doubt, friction, surprise?
- How much does the case resemble high-frequency conditions the clinician sees regularly (availability bias risk)?

**System 2 deployment — or its absence**
- Did the clinician slow down and reason through the case step-by-step, or did they stay in intuitive mode?
- What would have *forced* them to engage System 2? (A colleague's question? An atypical feature? A bad outcome that triggered a review?)
- Was there institutional time, space, or permission to deliberate? Or was System 1 the only option given the environment?
- How much effort would deliberation have cost, and was it worth it given what was at stake?

**Failure modes of System 1**
- Is there evidence of anchoring — clinging to an early diagnosis and filtering new information through it?
- Is there premature closure — the clinician stopped searching for alternatives after generating the first satisfying explanation?
- Is there overconfidence — high certainty despite low information or atypical presentation?
- Are there signs of availability bias — the clinician reaching for diagnoses they see often, regardless of base rate?
- Is there representativeness bias — matching the patient to a stereotype and missing the individual presentation?

**The case itself**
- Which features are typical/atypical for the suspected diagnosis?
- Which features go unexamined or are explained away as "not important"?
- What differential diagnoses were considered and explicitly rejected, and which were never generated?
- If the diagnosis turned out to be wrong, what would the correct diagnosis have revealed that this one missed?

**Metacognition and doubt**
- Where did the clinician express uncertainty? Where did they suppress it?
- What would have made them *think about their thinking* — i.e., engage metacognition?
- Is there evidence they were aware of their own biases, or does the reasoning flow as if bias were not a risk?

**Environment and structure**
- What institutional or informational constraints kept System 2 offline?
- Were there decision supports, checklists, or protocols that could have forced deliberation?
- Was there a culture of respectful disagreement that would have invited a colleague's challenge?

## What It Reveals

- **The *moment* of diagnostic error** — not just that an error occurred, but the precise cognitive event: the premature termination of search, the pattern that was overweighted, the signal that was filtered out, the feeling of certainty that was unwarranted.

- **Errors in experienced clinicians** — why highly trained doctors make mistakes that a careful apprentice might not make. The stance reveals that experience can *increase* certain biases (anchoring, overconfidence, availability).

- **The role of System 1 in good outcomes** — that intuition is not the enemy, and that many correct diagnoses are made fast, correctly, and without conscious reasoning. The stance reads which conditions make System 1 reliable versus dangerous.

- **Where the system failed to switch modes** — moments where System 2 *should* have been triggered but wasn't, and what prevented it. This is often more tractable for improvement than trying to eliminate bias from System 1.

- **The institutional shaping of cognition** — how fatigue, time pressure, staffing, information systems, and hierarchy determine whether a clinician can afford to slow down.

- **Overconfidence as a predictable failure mode** — the stance makes visible the disconnect between subjective certainty and actual diagnostic accuracy, particularly in cases where System 1 has made a strong but wrong pattern match.

## What It Obscures

- **The content of expertise itself.** Dual process theory is *mute* on what distinguishes a correct pattern library from a poor one, or how a clinician builds one. It says System 1 can fail; it does not say how to make System 1 better at what it does. (Pair with a domain-specific clinical epistemology stance when you need to ask what makes a diagnosis *correct*, not just *confidently reached*.)

- **The social and emotional dimensions of diagnosis.** Why a clinician *wants* to anchor on a diagnosis, or fears being wrong, or needs to appear confident to patients and teams — these are not purely cognitive. Dual process theory flattens motivation into "effort allocation." (Use a stance that reads clinical relationships, power, and emotion for what the theory misses here.)

- **The role of the body and sensory perception.** System 1 includes embodied knowing — the clinician who "feels" something is wrong before they can articulate why. Dual process theory, imported from lab psychology, tends to treat all of System 1 as if it were categorical pattern-matching, missing the non-propositional, felt dimension. (A phenomenological or somatic stance recovers this.)

- **Statistical and base-rate reasoning.** The theory is good at pointing out when System 1 overrides base rates; it is weaker at diagnosing when System 1 is *correct to do so* — when the patient *in front of you* takes priority over population statistics. The theory can lead to over-deliberation.

- **Systemic and structural failures.** Dual process theory focuses on individual cognition; it can miss the point that some errors are not cognitive mistakes but failures of systems, training, or resources. A clinician with no access to imaging or lab support is not making a System 1 error by guessing; they are working under constraint. (Use a systems or critical-organizational stance when the failure is upstream of the individual's cognition.)

- **What happens after diagnosis.** The stance reads how diagnoses are *reached* but is relatively silent on how they are *acted on* — treatment decisions, escalation, follow-up. A clinician may reach the correct diagnosis via System 2 and then fail to act on it for entirely different reasons.

## Output Format

```
## Dual Process Reading: <case / decision / clinical scenario>

### System 1 activity
- Pattern matched: <what the clinician recognized>
- Speed and confidence of match: <immediate / gradual; high certainty / tentative>
- Biases at work: <anchoring / availability / representativeness / premature closure / overconfidence>
- Felt sense: <ease, fluency, doubt, friction, surprise>

### System 2 engagement
- Was System 2 activated? <yes / no / partially>
- What would have forced deliberation? <atypical feature / colleague challenge / time available / institutional protocol>
- What was the cost of deliberation vs. the cost of error? <analysis>

### The case itself
- Typical features: <list>
- Atypical / missed features: <list>
- Differentials explicitly considered: <list>
- Differentials never generated: <list>

### Failure mode (if applicable)
- Which bias or transition failure drove the error? <specific diagnosis>
- What System 1 output was overvalued? <which pattern, which confidence)
- What System 2 discipline was missing? <which check, which alternative>

### Environment and structure
- Time / fatigue / information load: <was System 2 affordable?>
- Institutional culture: <could the clinician speak doubt? ask for help?>
- Decision supports available: <protocols, checklists, consultants>

### Conversation with other stances
- What a clinical-epistemology / phenomenological / systems stance would add
  or push back on: <what is missed by staying in pure cognitive mode>

### Confidence: high | medium | low
<basis: how clearly the case exposes the system 1/2 transition; how much
is inference about the clinician's internal state; whether the diagnosis
was eventually confirmed or remains contested>
```

# History from Below

## Overview

History from Below is a commitment to treating **ordinary people—workers, peasants, women, colonized subjects, the poor—not as passive recipients of historical forces but as active makers of their own history, whose agency, consciousness, and cultural practices shaped the world even when official records ignored them**. The stance rejects the assumption that "history" means the documented deeds of elites, politicians, and institutions. Its purpose is to recover the agency, experience, and intentionality of people whom dominant historiography rendered voiceless—not to add them as a footnote to a story already told, but to reorganize what counts as historical evidence and what questions matter. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure; its findings are the ones that become visible only when you stop asking "what did the powerful intend?" and start asking "how did ordinary people understand their own situation, and what did they do about it?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **Ordinary people are historical agents, not historical objects.** Workers, peasants, slaves, colonized peoples, women—these are not populations to be acted upon by history but people who made deliberate choices, organized collectively, created meaning through culture and ritual, and reshaped their world in consequential ways. Agency is not the monopoly of elites.

- **Experience is evidence.** The lived experience of ordinary people—reconstructed through letters, diaries, court records, folklore, memory, artifacts, bodily practices—is legitimate historical data, not merely illustration of structures. What people felt, valued, and understood about their own situation is not decoration but constitutive of history itself.

- **Consciousness emerges from practice, not the reverse.** People do not acquire a ready-made ideology and then act on it. Consciousness—class consciousness, political awareness, moral conviction—develops through struggle, work, and collective action. The consciousness and the practice are entangled; you cannot understand one without the other.

- **Culture and custom are not residual; they are political.** The rituals, songs, customs, and moral economies of ordinary people are not quaint folklore or mere tradition. They encode values, resistance, and claims about the legitimate order of things. To read culture is to read a kind of politics that official records miss.

- **Silence in the archive is significant.** What is absent from official records, what had to be reconstructed from fragmentary evidence or read against the grain of hostile sources—this absence itself is telling. The gap between what the powerful documented and what ordinary people lived is where the stance works.

- **The crowd is not a mob; it is a collectivity with its own logic.** When ordinary people gather, riot, strike, or protest, their actions are not irrational outbursts but responses to perceived violations of a moral economy—a shared sense of what is fair and what is owed. Reading the event requires understanding the crowd's own frame, not dismissing it.

- **History is contingent; it could have been otherwise.** The outcome that actually occurred was not inevitable. Ordinary people's choices, struggles, and resistance shaped the course of history; they were not simply swept along. The stance insists that alternatives existed and were sometimes chosen.

## Guiding Questions

**What the powerful documented and what they did not**
- What appears in official records—laws, statistics, official correspondence, institutional minutes? Who do these records treat as significant?
- What is conspicuously absent or distorted in these sources? What would those who lived it have testified?
- How can you read official documents *against the grain*—treating hostile accounts of "disorder" or "crime" as evidence of agency rather than deviance?

**Recovery of experience and consciousness**
- What can be reconstructed about how ordinary people understood their own situation? (Through letters, diaries, testimony, folklore, court records, memorials, oral history?)
- What evidence reveals what people valued, feared, or considered legitimate and illegitimate?
- How did people's consciousness change through the events they lived through? What did they learn from struggle?

**Agency and intentionality**
- What deliberate choices did ordinary people make, given their constraints? What did they try to do?
- Where did they organize collectively, and around what claims or values?
- What alternatives did they imagine or attempt, even if they failed?

**Culture, custom, and moral economy**
- What rituals, customs, songs, or traditions encoded the values and claims of ordinary people?
- What was the "moral economy"—the shared sense of fairness and obligation—that made an action seem justified or outrageous?
- How did people use existing cultural forms to resist or claim legitimacy?

**The crowd and collective action**
- When ordinary people gathered in numbers—rioting, striking, protesting—what was the *logic* of their action? What grievance or violation did it address?
- What rules or codes governed the crowd's behavior? (It was not chaos; it had its own structure.)
- How did authorities misrepresent or criminalize action that had internal justification?

**What was foreclosed or won**
- What did ordinary people achieve through their struggle, even if incomplete or partial?
- What did they lose or have taken from them?
- What alternatives were open at that moment, and which path was actually taken?

## What It Reveals

- **The agency of ordinary people** in shaping history—not as passive victims or beneficiaries of progress, but as deliberate actors whose choices mattered.
- **The internal logic of popular action**—the sense of justice, fairness, or grievance that motivated resistance, even when authorities dismissed it as mob hysteria or crime.
- **The cultural and moral resources** that subordinate groups used to understand and contest their situation—traditions, rituals, popular religion, craft knowledge.
- **The gap between official history and lived history**—what dominates narratives miss because they ask only about the powerful and the documented.
- **Contingency and alternative possibilities**—showing that the actual course of history depended on real struggles and choices, not inevitable progress or decline.
- **How subordinate groups made themselves** through collective action and culture—not as a unified category waiting to be "represented," but as a people constituted through practice and struggle.

## What It Obscures

- **Structural constraints and material conditions.** History from Below can emphasize agency and choice so strongly that it underplays the real limits—poverty, illiteracy, colonial domination, legal disability—within which choice was made. (Pair with a structural-materialist or postcolonial stance when the constraints are load-bearing.)

- **Scale and aggregate patterns.** Close attention to individual experience and local action can obscure larger patterns: continental trade, demographic trends, technological change, long-term institutional transformation. A microhistorical stance sometimes loses sight of the macro-forces it operates within.

- **Power asymmetries and failure.** The stance is drawn toward recovering agency and resistance; it can be less clear about the overwhelming weight of domination, the systematic suppression of alternatives, and the fact that most popular movements failed. (Pair with a stance attentive to power's weight—Foucauldian, Marxist—to avoid romanticizing resistance.)

- **Non-human factors.** Disease, climate, ecology, animal behavior—these are not "agency" in the stance's sense, but they constrained and shaped what people could do. A purely human-centered history misses these co-shapers.

- **The testimony of the defeated.** Ordinary people left traces, but those traces were often shaped by interrogators, court clerks, reformers, and collectors with their own agendas. Reading experience "off the record" is harder than it appears; interpretation is always at risk of projection.

- **Abstraction and long-term change.** The stance excels at the concrete and the local; it is weaker at explaining large-scale transitions, nation-making, or structural change that no one deliberately chose. (Pair with a genealogical or systems-oriented stance when the artifact requires explaining large-scale transformation.)

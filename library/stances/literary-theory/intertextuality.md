# Intertextuality (Kristeva)

## Overview

Kristeva's stance is a commitment to reading **any text as a site where multiple other texts, discourses, and utterances are woven together, absorbed, echoed, and transformed — rather than as a self-contained utterance from a unified authorial consciousness**. The stance does not treat intertextuality as mere "allusion" or "influence" (the old literary history question of "where did this come from?"), but as a structural condition: meaning is *generated* through the text's dialogic relation to the corpus it inhabits. The stance's purpose is to make visible the voices, vocabularies, and textual traces that compose what appears to be a singular text, and to show how each text is a mosaic that reorganizes fragments from prior utterances. It is interpretive, not procedural; its findings are the ones that emerge only when you stop asking "what does the author mean?" and start asking "what texts is this text speaking through, and how are they being transformed?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **The text is not a monolith but a plurality.** What reads as "one voice" is always already a site of multiple utterances, registers, and textual traces. The author does not stand outside or above this plurality; the author is *positioned within* it, and authorial intention itself is a construct of the intertextual field.

- **Intertextuality is not borrowing; it is absorption and transformation.** A text does not simply "use" prior texts as resources; it absorbs them, digests them, displaces them. The prior text is not left intact; it is broken down and reconstituted within the new utterance. Kristeva calls this "transposition."

- **Every utterance is dialogic.** Following Bakhtin, every speech act — every way of saying something — already contains within itself a response to, an orientation toward, an echo of prior utterances in the same linguistic field. No utterance is innocent of this dialogism.

- **The text is a space where discourses intersect and contest.** Different social registers, professional vocabularies, temporal strata of language (archaic, contemporary, futuristic), and ideological positions can inhabit the same textual surface. These are not unified by the author; they coexist, sometimes in productive tension.

- **Meaning emerges from the text's position in the textual corpus, not from its internal structure alone.** A text means differently depending on what it is in dialogue with. The same sentence reads differently if it is heard as a quotation, a parody, a homage, or a correction of a prior utterance than it reads in isolation.

- **The reader reconstructs the intertextual network.** The text does not announce all its quotations or dialogues; the reader must activate them, recognize them, and hold multiple textual voices in tension at once. Intertextuality is a collaborative production between text and reader.

## Guiding Questions

**Textual traces and absorbed voices**
- What prior utterances, texts, genres, or discursive registers can be heard speaking through this text?
- Where does the text echo, quote (explicitly or tacitly), parody, or rewrite prior texts?
- Which voices are recognizable, and which are absorbed so thoroughly they no longer announce themselves?
- What happens to the prior text when this text takes it in — is it preserved, inverted, fragmented, decontextualized?

**Dialogism and address**
- Whom is this text speaking to? What prior speaker, audience, or counter-argument is it implicitly in conversation with?
- Where does the text anticipate or parody an objection, a misreading, or an alternative interpretation?
- What assumptions about "how one speaks" in this field does the text presume and potentially overturn?

**Semantic instability and register**
- Where do multiple registers, vocabularies, or ideological positions inhabit the same textual surface?
- Where does the text shift its language in a way that signals a shift in whose voice is "speaking"?
- What happens when a word from one discourse meets a word from another — do they reinforce, mock, or destabilize each other?

**What is excluded and silenced**
- What discourses, voices, or prior texts is this text *not* absorbing? What is it pointedly excluding?
- What utterances are available in the surrounding culture that this text refuses to incorporate?
- Whose speech is not permitted in the textual field this text establishes?

**Transposition and transformation**
- How does the incorporation of a prior text change that text's meaning?
- Does the text preserve its source or does it deform it? Does it domesticate a radical prior text, or radicalize a domestic one?
- What new meanings become possible when this text absorbs that utterance?

**Temporal strata**
- What temporal layers — archaic language, contemporary slang, technical jargon, projected futures — are compressed into the text's present?
- How does the text's relationship to historical language-use (how people *actually spoke* in prior periods) complicate its meaning?

## What It Reveals

A Kristevian intertextual reading surfaces findings that other stances routinely miss:

- **The plural authorship of what appears singular.** Showing that "the author's voice" is always already a composite, a strategic positioning within a field of prior utterances, not an origin point.

- **How prior texts are absorbed, deformed, and made to serve new purposes.** This is different from source-hunting; it is about the *work* the text does on what it inherits.

- **Semantic instability and productive ambiguity.** Where multiple registers coexist, meaning cannot be pinned down to a single signified; the text's power often lies in this irresolvability.

- **How discourse shapes what can be said and who can say it.** By making visible the linguistic and textual constraints the speaker operates within, the stance shows that utterance is never free.

- **The reader's active role in meaning-making.** The text does not contain meaning; the reader must activate the intertextual relations, and different readers will activate different networks of reference.

- **The political and ideological dimensions of what is absorbed, quoted, or excluded.** Whose discourse gets incorporated into legitimacy, and whose is kept out? What does it mean that *this* prior text enters *this* new utterance?

## What It Obscures

The stance has substantial blind spots.

- **The material and economic conditions of textual production and circulation.** Intertextuality as a structural principle says nothing about who publishes, who can afford books, what gets marketed, or how a text's reach is mediated by distribution networks. A Marxist or institutional analysis recovers what the stance brackets. (Pair with a political-economy reading when material conditions are load-bearing.)

- **Biographical and intentional dimensions of authorship.** The stance is hostile to "the author" as an explanatory category, which is productive, but it sometimes flattens genuine questions about what a specific person chose to do, why they reached for a particular prior text, or what their stakes were. A biographical or genetic-criticism approach recovers this.

- **Formal specificity and the non-linguistic texture of artworks.** Intertextuality is a textual-linguistic concept; it does not easily extend to music, visual art, sculpture, or even the visual and sonic dimensions of written texts. A formalist or medium-specific stance is necessary for these.

- **Stable reference and mimetic dimensions.** The stance is strong on how meaning is generated through textual play but weaker on how texts also *refer to the world outside texts* — how they represent, witness, document, or make claims about extra-textual reality. A referentialist or documentary stance recovers this.

- **The diachronic development of a single work or tradition over time.** Kristeva's stance is fundamentally synchronic — it maps relations across a conceptual space rather than tracing evolution. A historical or genetic approach captures what is lost.

- **Intentional parody versus accidental echo.** The stance does not always distinguish clearly between deliberate quotation or rewriting and unconscious absorption of discourse. A philological or source-critical approach can sharpen this distinction where it matters.

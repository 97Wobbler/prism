# Ecocriticism

## Overview

Ecocriticism is an interpretive stance committed to treating **the nonhuman world — animals, plants, ecosystems, geological formations, atmospheric conditions — not as backdrop or resource but as active participants in the artifact's meanings and the world it describes**. The stance refuses the assumption that humans are the only meaningful subjects in a text, and that nature exists primarily to be represented *by* humans *for* human purposes. Its work is to denaturalize human exceptionalism — to show where texts have buried the nonhuman agency they depend on, where they have treated ecological destruction as inevitable or invisible, and where they have rendered entire worlds mute. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure; its findings emerge when you stop asking "what does this text say about nature?" and start asking "what does this text do *with* and *to* the nonhuman worlds entangled in it?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **The nonhuman world has agency and voice, not just presence.** Nonhuman beings — animals, plants, fungi, rivers, weather — act, communicate, and shape the worlds they inhabit. They are not inert matter waiting for human interpretation or use. The text's job is not to "give voice" to the voiceless (a Human act) but to attend to voices already present in ways humans typically ignore.

- **Humans are not separate from ecology; they are embedded in it.** The stance refuses the nature / culture binary that treats nature as a separate realm outside history. Humans and nonhuman nature are entangled; what happens to ecosystems happens to human worlds, and vice versa. The text either acknowledges this entanglement or it obscures it through a fantasy of separation.

- **Ecological destruction is not inevitable or background.** Where a text treats environmental ruin, species extinction, soil depletion, or climate change as a given condition rather than the result of specific histories of extraction and accumulation, it is participating in naturalization. The stance asks: whose actions produced this state? What alternatives were foreclosed?

- **Scale matters, and it is rarely what it appears.** A single pesticide molecule, a cleared forest, a shopping mall, industrial agriculture, atmospheric CO₂ — these operate at different scales but are connected. Texts often flatten or obscure these connections, treating the local as merely local and the planetary as too large to affect meaning.

- **Nonhuman temporality is not human time.** Trees, soils, geological formations, species evolution operate on timescales that dwarf human lifespans and institutional planning horizons. A text that narrates only within human temporal frames has already made a choice about what matters.

- **Attention itself is an ethical and political act.** What a text makes visible or invisible about the nonhuman world is never neutral. Where it directs the reader's gaze — what it names, what it leaves unnamed — shapes what can be thought as real, valuable, or worth preserving.

## Guiding Questions

**Nonhuman presence and agency**
- Which nonhuman beings, ecosystems, or weather systems appear in the text? Which are absent?
- Where does the text treat nonhuman entities as actors with their own purposes and effects? Where does it treat them as inert objects, resources, or metaphors?
- What forms of nonhuman communication or expression does the text notice or foreclose?
- How do nonhuman beings or forces constrain, enable, or redirect what happens in the text?

**Human exceptionalism and separation**
- Does the text assume humans are separate from nature, or does it show human entanglement with ecological systems?
- What human needs, desires, or values does the text treat as universal or natural? What are being naturalized that are actually historical?
- Where does the text invoke "nature" as a timeless stable thing, and where does it show nature as dynamic and contested?

**Ecological destruction and its causes**
- What environmental damage, species loss, or ecosystem degradation appears in the text? Is it named as such or treated as background?
- Who is responsible? Does the text assign causality to specific human actions and systems, or does it leave destruction as an unmarked condition?
- What forms of extraction, accumulation, or consumption structure the world the text inhabits? Are these made visible?

**Scale and connection**
- At what scales does the text operate — individual, local, regional, planetary? What happens at scales it ignores?
- Does the text show how local places are connected to distant supply chains, atmospheric circulation, migratory routes, or colonial histories?
- What appears "local" and self-contained that is actually dependent on ecological systems at other scales?

**Temporal frames**
- What time horizons does the text use? Does it narrate only within human generations, or does it reckon with geological time, species evolution, or ecosystem cycles?
- Are futures imagined? If so, whose futures — human, nonhuman, both?
- What deep past does the text acknowledge or erase?

**Labor, commodification, and use**
- How are nonhuman beings, plants, and ecosystems valued? As resources? As commodities? As subjects with their own worth?
- What labor — visible and invisible, human and nonhuman — produces the world the text assumes as given?
- Where does extraction occur in the text's world, and who benefits?

## What It Reveals

- **Nonhuman agency and intelligence** operating in texts that appear to be about humans only — animal intelligence, plant communication, ecosystem self-regulation, geological or atmospheric force.
- **The buried history of extraction** — where did the materials, energy, labor come from? What ecosystems were cleared? The text often obscures this genealogy.
- **Human dependence on nonhuman systems** — air, water, soil, pollinators, decomposers — that are either taken for granted or actively destroyed in pursuit of human ends the text naturalizes.
- **Temporal incommensurability** — the collision between human timescales (seasons, years, careers, centuries) and the actual timescales of ecological processes (soil formation, species adaptation, climate regulation).
- **The politics of naming** — what species, ecosystems, or forms of suffering get named as real and important? Whose loss is mourned in the text, and whose is made invisible?
- **Consumption and its nonhuman costs** — the texture of the world the text assumes as background reveals what was killed, displaced, or toxified to make that world possible.

## What It Obscures

- **Complex nonhuman interiority.** The stance resists treating animals or ecosystems as mere extensions of human meaning-making, but this caution can produce a reading paralysis — if we cannot fully know nonhuman experience, how do we read it? Some ecocritical work overcorrects by projecting human-like consciousness onto nonhuman beings (romanticization), which is its own erasure.

- **The role of human culture and difference.** An ecocritical stance focused only on the human / nonhuman binary can flatten differences among humans — Indigenous land practices, peasant ecologies, or racialized environmental justice movements get submerged under "humanity" as a unified category. (Pair with postcolonial, Indigenous, or environmental justice readings to recover these.)

- **Complicity and difficulty of exit.** The stance is strong at identifying what is destroyed and good at asking who benefits, but it offers limited resources for thinking about how to act differently when one is already embedded in the systems being critiqued. It can produce guilt or despair rather than possibility.

- **Aesthetic and formal pleasure.** Over-attention to what is destroyed or exploited can eclipse what the text is *doing formally* with beauty, wit, difficulty, or craft. A purely thematic ecocritical reading sometimes flattens the text's own complexity.

- **Economic and technological specificity.** The stance can treat "industrial capitalism" or "technology" as monolithic; it misses the actual diversity of economic forms and their different ecological effects. Agrarian systems, gift economies, and knowledge systems outside the Western frame require their own analysis.

- **Its own position.** An ecocritical reading that does not acknowledge the paper, energy, and ecological footprint of its own production — the university, the publishing industry, the carbon cost of reading — is incomplete by its own light.

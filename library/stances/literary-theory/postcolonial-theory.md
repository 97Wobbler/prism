# Postcolonial Theory (Said/Spivak/Bhabha)

## Overview

Postcolonial theory is an interpretive stance committed to **treating colonialism not as a historical event that ended but as a system of representation, knowledge-production, and subject-formation whose effects persist in how the colonized world is written, imagined, and governed**. The stance does not ask "was this writer anti-colonial?" but rather: "what does this text *assume* about the colonized world, and how do those assumptions encode colonial power relations?" Its core move is to show that knowledge itself—scientific classification, history, anthropology, the novel form—was implicated in colonization, and that what appears to be neutral description or universal truth is actually a colonial arrangement presented as natural. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure; its findings become visible when you stop reading the colonized world on the terms the empire set for it and start asking what those terms *accomplished*.

## Foundational Commitments

- **Colonialism is a system of representation, not only of military force.** The conquest and governance of colonized territories depended on producing a body of knowledge about those territories and their peoples—knowledge that positioned them as backward, feminine, irrational, or child-like, and therefore justified being ruled by the rational West. This representational system did material work: it organized trade, law, education, and the colonized subject's own self-perception.

- **The colonized subject is produced, not naturally existing.** The category "the native," "the Oriental," "the savage," "the coolie"—these are not names for pre-existing kinds of people but positions that the colonial apparatus called into being. Colonialism did not simply conquer territory; it made certain kinds of subjectivity possible and others illegible.

- **Western culture claims universality while encoding particularity.** When a European novel, historical account, or scientific taxonomy presents itself as speaking for humanity, it is actually speaking from a particular position—the metropolitan center—while hiding that particularity behind claims to objectivity or timelessness. This is what Said calls the "worldliness" of texts: they are always implicated in real power arrangements.

- **The subaltern cannot speak (or speaks only through the master's voice).** Spivak's provocation: the colonized subject's own voice, interests, and agency are systematically rendered illegible by the colonial episteme. When we "recover" the subaltern's voice through colonial archives or metropolitan texts, we are often recovering the colonizer's ventriloquism, not the subaltern themselves.

- **Hybridity and mimicry are not fusion but ambivalence.** Bhabha's intervention: the colonized do not simply accept or reject the colonizer's culture. They inhabit it, mock it, repeat it with a difference—and this mimicry is not a failed copy but a site of both power and undecidability. The colonizer cannot fully control what the colonized make of colonialism.

- **The past is not past; colonial relations shape the present.** Colonialism formally ended in most places by the mid-20th century, but its effects persist in international structures, languages, literatures, categories of knowledge, and the colonized world's continued positioning as raw material and market. Reading postcolonially means treating the present as still colonial in its structures.

## Guiding Questions

**Representation and knowledge**
- Who is authorized to speak about, describe, or interpret the colonized world? Whose voice counts as expertise?
- What knowledge forms did colonialism establish and naturalize? (Racial classification. Anthropological "types." Economic categories. Historical periodization.)
- What assumptions about the colonized world are being made, and presented as observation rather than interpretation?
- Where does the text use the passive voice or abstraction to hide colonial agency? ("The colony was developed." "Traditional society gave way to modernity.")

**The colonized subject**
- What kind of subject does the colonial text produce or assume? (Passive? Feminine? In need of civilization? Grateful for rule?)
- What self-understanding is the colonized subject forced to adopt in order to be legible within colonial discourse?
- What aspects of the colonized subject's own understanding are rendered invisible by colonial categories?

**Worldliness and implication**
- What material interests does this text serve? (Trade? Land appropriation? Cultural legitimacy?)
- What political work is done by presenting a colonial arrangement as natural, scientific, or inevitable?
- From what metropolitan position is this text written, and what does it take for granted as "the world"?

**Resistance and mimicry**
- Where does the colonized subject refuse, subvert, or inhabit-with-a-difference the colonial framework?
- How does the text register—and how does it suppress—colonial violence and resistance?
- What is being mocked, quoted, or repeated by the colonized in a way the colonizer cannot fully contain?

**The archive and the unspoken**
- What would need to be suppressed for this narrative to cohere? (Competing claims to the land. Indigenous knowledge systems. The violence of conquest.)
- Whose absence is most consequential? (What voice or experience is most fundamentally excluded?)
- What does the text *not say*, and what does that silence protect?

**Continuities and persistence**
- What colonial structures persist in the postcolonial present? (Languages imposed. Economic extraction continuing. Education systems organized around metropolitan knowledge.)
- How do supposedly "postcolonial" texts, institutions, or nations still reproduce colonial relations?

## What It Reveals

A postcolonial reading surfaces findings that other stances routinely miss:

- **How knowledge systems were colonizing.** Scientific racism, anthropology, historiography, the novel form itself—these were not neutral vessels but instruments that organized the colonized world into intelligibility for the West. The claim to objectivity was itself a colonial move.

- **The representational logic that justified empire.** Not just that colonizers were cruel, but that they had to produce a whole system of representation—the Oriental as sensual, the African as savage, the Indian as feminine—in order for colonization to seem reasonable, even civilizing.

- **The production of the colonized subject.** How colonialism did not merely oppress pre-existing people but made certain kinds of subjectivity mandatory: the assimilated colonial subject, the grateful native, the native informant, the interpreter between worlds.

- **Hybridity as a site of power and undecidability.** Not a harmonious mixing but a space where colonial control is both asserted and fails—where the colonized subject's mimicry produces meanings the colonizer did not intend.

- **Temporal violence.** How the colonized world is placed in an evolutionary narrative where it is always "behind"—not yet modern, not yet rational, not yet developed. This temporal ordering justifies present domination as "bringing them forward."

- **The persistence of colonial structures in the postcolonial moment.** Independence did not end colonialism; it transformed it. The postcolonial state, education system, and international order still bear the marks.

## What It Obscures

The stance has significant blind spots.

- **Difference within the colonized world.** The stance can treat "the colonized" as a unified category, flattening the interests of colonized elites, competing nationalisms, and internal oppressions (gender, caste, class) that do not map neatly onto the colonizer/colonized binary. (A materialist or intersectional stance recovers what postcolonial theory homogenizes.)

- **Material economics and extraction.** At its strongest, postcolonial theory is about representation, knowledge, and subjectivity. But the motor of colonialism was economic extraction, labor coercion, and resource appropriation. A pure representational reading can miss what the knowledge-system was organized to *do*—how it served accumulation. (Pair with historical materialism when the economic structure is load-bearing.)

- **The subaltern's own agency and knowledge.** Spivak's "the subaltern cannot speak" is a powerful critique of metropolitan recuperation, but taken too far, it can render the colonized as wholly constituted by colonialism, with no alternative consciousness or practice. (Subaltern Studies historians and decolonial thinkers push back here.)

- **Non-Western colonialisms.** The stance is largely organized around European colonialism. Ottoman, Japanese, or Chinese imperial projects did not work the same way; the framework can flatten difference. (Attending to specific historical formations recovers what the theory generalizes away.)

- **What comes after.** The stance is strong on critique of the colonial episteme but hesitant about what alternative knowledge systems look like or how to build them. It can produce a kind of critique-without-exit.

- **Its own institutional location.** Postcolonial theory as practiced often depends on the academy, metropolitan publishers, and English-language circulation—the very structures that extend colonial intellectual dominance. The stance rarely turns its gaze on itself in this way.

- What categories does the text use to describe the colonized world? (How are they positioned: passive? feminine? irrational? in need of civilization?)
- What counts as legitimate knowledge about the colonized world in this text? Whose knowledge is disqualified?
- What is naturalized as inevitable, scientific, or universal that is actually a colonial arrangement?

### The colonized subject produced
- What kind of subjectivity does the text assume or demand of the colonized?
- What self-understanding must the colonized adopt to be legible within this framework?
- What alternatives to this subjectivity does the text foreclose?

### Worldliness and material work
- From what metropolitan position is this text written?
- What material interests does the representational system serve? (Trade routes? Labor coercion? Land appropriation? Legitimacy?)

- Whose voices, experiences, or knowledge systems are most fundamentally excluded?
- What continuities with colonialism does the "postcolonial" text reproduce?

- What a Marxist / feminist / subaltern-studies reading would add or push back on

# Merton's CUDOS

## Overview

Merton's stance is a commitment to treating science not as a method or epistemology but as **a set of institutional norms — Communalism, Universalism, Disinterestedness, and Organized Skepticism (CUDOS) — that shape what counts as legitimate scientific conduct, whose claims are believed, and how priority and credit are distributed**. The stance does not ask whether these norms are "good" or whether science "lives up to them." Instead, it asks: which norms are actually operative in a given field or institution? Which are invoked rhetorically but violated in practice? Where do the official norms break down, and what hidden norms replace them? This is an interpretive position, not a procedure; its findings become visible when you stop asking "is this good science?" and start asking "what institutional logic is actually running here?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **Science is an institution with norms, not just a method.** The practice of science is governed by shared rules about what counts as legitimate knowledge-making, who can make claims, how credit is awarded, and what obligations scientists have to each other and to the public. These norms are socially enforced and historically variable, not inscribed in the logic of reasoning itself.

- **The official norms and the operating norms may diverge sharply.** Scientists may invoke Communalism (shared knowledge) while fiercely defending priority claims. They may preach Universalism (judgment by merit alone) while operating within networks of patronage, nationality, and institutional prestige. The gap between rhetoric and practice is not hypocrisy to be condemned but a primary object of analysis.

- **Norms are enforced through reward and penalty systems.** Credit, recognition, funding, position, and collaborative inclusion are the currencies through which the scientific community enforces its norms. Who gets cited, who gets hired, who gets believed when they make a claim — these decisions enforce which norms actually matter in a field.

- **Priority disputes reveal the norms in operation.** When scientists argue over who discovered something first, what they are actually fighting about is the definition of discovery in that field — what counts as a legitimate claim, how you prove you had the idea before someone else did, and why priority matters. These disputes are not pathological; they are the norm-system made visible.

- **Different fields have different norm-stresses.** Some fields are Communalism-heavy (expect rapid data-sharing); others are Disinterestedness-heavy (independence from funding interests is paramount); others run on priority-consciousness that subordinates Communalism. The stance reads the field's norm-configuration as a whole.

- **What is naturalized as "good science" is itself normative.** The scientist who invokes "the scientific method" or "rigor" is not describing a neutral procedure but is enforcing a particular institutional standard that benefits some actors over others.

## Guiding Questions

**The official norm and its enforcement**
- Which of the CUDOS norms does this field or institution explicitly endorse? Which does it claim are central to science?
- How are these norms taught to newcomers (students, trainees, postdocs)? What penalties attach to violations?
- Who has standing to claim a norm violation? Whose complaint is taken seriously?

**The operating norm**
- What norms actually govern behavior in practice, whether or not they are acknowledged?
- Where do the official norms constrain action, and where are they systematically disregarded?
- What unspoken rules do successful practitioners actually follow?

**Priority and credit**
- How is priority determined in this field? (First publication? First presentation? First conception? Patent filing? Institutional claim?)
- What counts as "proof" of priority? (Lab notebooks? Correspondence? Witnesses? Institutional records?)
- How is credit distributed when multiple actors contribute? Who is first author, who is acknowledgment, who is invisible?
- What happens to someone who claims priority but cannot prove it? What happens if they prove it but the community is invested in another story?

**Communalism under pressure**
- When does the norm of sharing knowledge get enforced, and when does it break down?
- What data or code is actually shared vs. hoarded? (Look at what practices are sanctioned and what are tolerated.)
- How do researchers balance publishing priority with collaborative transparency?
- Where does access to knowledge depend on institutional affiliation, nationality, language, or prior relationships?

**Universalism in practice**
- Who is judged by merit, and who is judged by other criteria?
- What happens when evaluation criteria are applied inconsistently across groups?
- Does funding, institutional prestige, or network position affect whose claims are believed?
- Where do demographic or institutional differences correlate with different standards for what counts as adequate evidence?

**Disinterestedness and its exceptions**
- What counts as a disinterested stance in this field? (Independence from funding? Lack of patent interest? Absence of personal stakes?)
- When is it acceptable to have financial stakes in the outcome of research? When is it grounds for disqualification?
- How are conflicts of interest defined and policed? By whom? With what penalties?

**Organized skepticism under strain**
- What claims face rigorous skeptical scrutiny, and what claims are accepted without it?
- Who has the standing to raise skeptical objections? Whose doubts are heard, whose are dismissed as impossible or niche?
- When does skepticism get reframed as obstructionism or bad faith?

## What It Reveals

A Merton reading surfaces findings that other stances miss:

- **The implicit norm-system** that actually governs a field, often radically at variance with what scientists say science is about.
- **How institutional rewards** (funding, positions, citations, collaborative inclusion) enforce norms far more effectively than explicit rules.
- **Priority disputes as norm-work**: showing that fights over "who discovered it first" are really fights about what definition of discovery the field will accept.
- **The distribution of credibility and skepticism**: which actors' claims are believed without extensive replication, and which face fortress-like scrutiny.
- **How "objectivity" or "rigor" functions as a norm** rather than a neutral standard — what it costs to appear to satisfy it, who can afford the cost, who cannot.
- **The historical contingency of the norm-system**: that what looks like the timeless structure of science is actually a configuration that emerged in a specific place (20th-century Anglo-American academic science) and is not universal.

## What It Obscures

The stance has consequential blind spots:

- **Material conditions and power asymmetries that pre-date the norm-system.** Merton's framework treats the scientific community as internally normed but brackets the political economy of science — funding concentration, labor markets, access to equipment, who can afford to do "basic" research. A pure Merton reading can miss how material scarcity shapes which norms get enforced against whom. (Pair with political-economy or Marxist readings when resources are load-bearing.)

- **The norm-system's origins and contestation.** Merton identifies what the norms are but is weaker on why they took this form (not another), whose interests they served, and how they got institutionalized. Historical or genealogical work is needed to recover that.

- **Individual motivation and ethical choice.** The stance is strong on institutional enforcement and weak on why individuals internalize norms or when they choose to violate them. It risks portraying scientists as norm-dupes or norm-players without room for genuine ethical commitment.

- **Non-epistemic values and politics inside science.** Gender, race, empire, nationalism, religious conviction — these are not reducible to norm-deviation; they structure the field itself. A pure Merton reading can treat them as "departures from Universalism" rather than as constitutive of how the norm-system actually works. (Use intersectional or postcolonial stances alongside.)

- **What the norms produce: whose knowledge counts, whose is disqualified.** Merton brackets the question of epistemic justice — whether the norm-system is just in its distribution of who gets believed. It is a sociology of norms, not a politics of knowledge.

- **Quick answers about compliance.** Applying Merton in a hurry produces "they violated Disinterestedness" without mapping the actual norm-configuration of the field. The stance requires empirical specificity about how norms work in a particular field; it breaks down if used as a checklist.

## Output Format

```
## Merton Reading: <field / institution / practice>

### Scope
- Field or institution being read: <specific domain>
- Why a Merton reading: <what norm-tensions or norm-questions are visible here>

### Official norms (explicit endorsement)
- Which CUDOS norms are claimed as central to this field
- How they are taught and transmitted (documents, induction, institutional rhetoric)
- Stated penalties for violation

### Operating norms (what actually runs the field)
- Norms that actually govern behavior (may differ sharply from official norms)
- Unspoken rules successful practitioners follow
- Where official norms are enforced strictly; where they are systematically bypassed

### Priority and credit system
- How priority is determined in this field (first publication / patent / conception / presentation)
- What counts as proof of priority
- How credit is distributed when multiple actors contribute
- Consequences of priority disputes (who wins, what is lost)

### Communalism under pressure
- What knowledge is genuinely shared; what is hoarded or gated
- Access conditions (affiliation, language, time, relationships)
- Tensions between publication priority and collaborative transparency

### Universalism in practice
- Whose claims are judged by what criteria
- Where consistent standards apply; where they diverge
- Role of institutional prestige, funding, network position in credibility

### Disinterestedness and conflicts of interest
- What counts as a disinterested stance here
- How and by whom conflicts of interest are defined
- Whose financial stakes are tolerable; whose disqualify

### Organized skepticism
- What claims face rigorous scrutiny; what claims are accepted
- Who has standing to raise doubts; whose skepticism is heard
- Where skepticism gets reframed as obstruction

### Conversation with other stances
- What a political-economy / feminist / postcolonial / genealogical reading
  would add or push back on

### Confidence: high | medium | low
<basis: how directly the artifact (field, institution, practice) exposes its
norm-system; how visible the gap between official and operating norms; how
much inference is required to map the actual reward structure>
```

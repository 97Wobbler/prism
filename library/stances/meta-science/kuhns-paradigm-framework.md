# Kuhn's Paradigm Framework

## Overview

Kuhn's stance is a commitment to treating **scientific fields not as cumulative progress toward truth but as organized by shared exemplars, rules, and commitments (a "paradigm") that determine which questions count as real problems, which solutions are acceptable, and which observations even register as anomalies**. The stance's purpose is to denaturalize the appearance that science simply "discovers" pre-existing facts; instead, it shows how a field's current paradigm makes certain findings possible and renders others literally unthinkable or unobservable. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure. Its findings emerge when you stop asking "which theory is correct?" and start asking "what does this field's shared consensus make it impossible to see?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **A paradigm is a shared commitment to exemplars, not just a theory.** A paradigm is not simply a set of propositions; it includes canonical cases, problem-solutions, instrumentation practices, and exemplary papers that show practitioners how to work. Scientists learn by apprenticeship into the paradigm, not by learning axioms. The paradigm is often tacit and harder to articulate than any explicit theory it contains.

- **Normal science is puzzle-solving within a framework.** Most scientific work does not question the paradigm; it assumes the paradigm and works to extend, refine, and apply it to new cases. These are "puzzles" — solvable problems whose solution is certain to exist because they fall within the paradigm's scope. Normal science does not search for novelty; it searches for predicted solutions to predicted kinds of problems.

- **Paradigms determine what counts as an anomaly.** Data that does not fit the paradigm can be ignored, shelved, attributed to experimental error, or explained away — because the paradigm is the standard against which observations are measured. An anomaly is not simply a bad fit; it is a failure to fit something the paradigm says should fit. Observations that fall completely outside the paradigm's purview are not registered as anomalies; they are simply not data.

- **Crises arise when anomalies accumulate and cannot be explained away.** As long as the paradigm can absorb anomalies (through auxiliary hypotheses, refined measurements, or postponement), normal science continues. But when anomalies multiply, persist, and begin to look like the paradigm itself is broken, a field enters crisis. Crisis is rare and uncomfortable; fields resist it fiercely.

- **Scientific revolutions are gestalt shifts, not rational replacements.** When a new paradigm emerges, it does not "win" through argument or evidence alone. The old paradigm and the new one are partly incommensurable — their standards of proof, their definitions of key terms, their sense of what counts as a problem, even their observations, do not map directly onto each other. The shift from one paradigm to another is more like a conversion or a gestalt switch (seeing the rabbit instead of the duck) than a logical deduction.

- **The history of science is not linear progress.** Once you see through the paradigm lens, you see that each paradigm had anomalies it could not solve, blind spots it could not overcome, and future practitioners found problems in what their predecessors thought was settled. "Progress" is real *within* a paradigm but not *between* them; there is no vantage point outside all paradigms from which to judge which is "really" closer to truth.

- **Communities hold paradigms, not individuals.** A paradigm is maintained by a community of practitioners who teach it, defend it, and police its boundaries. Outsiders and mavericks are often ignored; the young are trained into conformity; attempts to work outside the paradigm are seen as not-really-science. The paradigm is a social and institutional fact, not merely an intellectual one.

## Guiding Questions

**The paradigm's explicit content**
- What are the canonical texts, experiments, or cases that define how this field does work? (The exemplars.)
- What counts as a well-formed problem in this field? What makes a problem "real"?
- What counts as an acceptable solution? What methods, standards of rigor, and forms of evidence are treated as normal?

**What the paradigm makes invisible**
- What kinds of observation or data fall outside the paradigm's framework and are therefore not registered as anomalies or even as data?
- What questions are not asked because they fall outside the paradigm's scope?
- Which phenomena are treated as not-yet-solved puzzles versus as threats to the paradigm itself?

**Anomalies and their handling**
- What problems does the current paradigm face that practitioners acknowledge but explain away?
- How are these explained away — through auxiliary hypotheses, appeals to experimental error, claims that more data will resolve it?
- Are there observations that don't fit and that practitioners know don't fit, but that have not yet triggered crisis?

**Training and boundary maintenance**
- How are practitioners initiated into the paradigm? (What textbooks? What apprenticeships? What problems are assigned to trainees?)
- How does the field police what counts as legitimate work? Who is listened to; who is dismissed?
- What penalties attach to working outside the paradigm?

**Crisis and incommensurability**
- Is the field in crisis? What signals would tell you?
- If alternatives are emerging, are they truly incompatible with the current paradigm, or can the paradigm absorb them?
- What would have to change for the field to switch to a different paradigm?

**Historical ruptures**
- What problems did the previous paradigm have that the current one solved? What did it lose?
- What problems does the current paradigm have that it does not yet treat as fatal?
- Are there phenomena that matter to practitioners now that earlier paradigms ignored entirely?

## What It Reveals

A Kuhnian reading surfaces findings that other stances systematically miss:

- **The paradigm's own anomalies** — problems the field acknowledges but keeps at arm's length because solving them would require abandoning the frame itself.
- **What falls outside the paradigm's vision entirely** — the questions not asked, the observations not made, the phenomena treated as noise rather than signal.
- **The historical contingency of what seems obvious** — showing that the field's current commitments are not demanded by nature but chosen by a community at a particular moment.
- **How institutional and social structures hold the paradigm in place** — training regimes, journal gatekeeping, hiring, prestige hierarchies, and the penalties for dissent.
- **The incommensurability of frameworks** — why competing paradigms can look like they are not even arguing about the same thing, and why a paradigm switch is not a simple matter of evidence.
- **Why a field can be utterly confident about something later fields will discard** — showing confidence is not an indicator of truth but a sign of paradigm stability.

## What It Obscures

The stance has real blind spots and limitations.

- **The role of empirical evidence and experimental precision.** Kuhn emphasizes paradigm-dependence so heavily that he can make it sound as though paradigms are arbitrary or purely social constructs. In fact, experiments do push back; nature does constrain. A stance that over-reads Kuhn can miss how evidence and technical refinement actually do steer research. (Pair with philosophy of experiment and careful history of measurement for this.)

- **Rational evaluation between paradigms.** If paradigms are incommensurable, how do we judge? Kuhn's own answer (that the shift is ultimately a matter of conversion) leaves the question open. This can look like relativism. A fuller picture requires engagement with later work (Kuhn's own "correspondence rules," or Laudan's problem-solving model) or other epistemological stances.

- **Micro-level decisions and contingency.** Kuhn works at the level of fields and large-scale shifts. A Kuhnian reading can miss local decisions, individual brilliance, accident, and the actual messiness of how people work. (Use ethnographic or biographical stances alongside.)

- **Non-Western and alternative knowledge systems.** The paradigm concept was built from 20th-century European and American physics. It does not map cleanly onto fields with different institutional structures, cumulative practices, or epistemic values. (Pair with postcolonial or comparative epistemology when appropriate.)

- **What paradigms do *for* their practitioners beyond truth-seeking.** A paradigm provides identity, community, resources, and career paths. Kuhn acknowledges this but does not make it central. A reading that emphasizes only paradigm-as-lens misses paradigm-as-livelihood and paradigm-as-belonging.

- **The possibility of genuine novelty within a paradigm.** Kuhn's strict line between normal science (extensions of the paradigm) and revolutionary science (replacement) can flatten the actual creativity and unexpected discoveries that happen within normal science.

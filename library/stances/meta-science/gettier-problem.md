# Gettier Problem

## Overview

The Gettier stance is a commitment to treating **justified true belief (JTB) as a necessary but not sufficient condition for knowledge — and to using counterexamples as the primary tool to expose what is missing from any definition that assumes JTB is sufficient**. This stance does not propose a positive account of knowledge; it is purely diagnostic. Its job is to take a proposed analysis of knowledge and find the case where a subject has justified true belief but lacks knowledge, thereby showing that the analysis is incomplete. The stance assumes that these gaps are not accidental failures of an analysis but revelatory — they point toward what genuinely makes something knowledge rather than lucky true belief. It is an interpretive position suited to reading epistemological claims and theoretical accounts of knowledge, expertise, and epistemic warrant.

## Foundational Commitments

- **JTB is necessary but not sufficient.** Justified true belief is minimally required for knowledge, but something further is required to rule out cases where belief is justified and true yet does not constitute knowledge. This "something further" is what any adequate account must capture.

- **The counterexample is the primary diagnostic tool.** A single case where JTB obtains but knowledge does not defeats a proposed analysis. The stance treats such cases as windows into the structure of knowledge, not as curiosities to be dismissed. What the counterexample makes visible is what the analysis missed.

- **Justification and truth can come apart from knowledge.** A subject can be justified in a belief (by all the standards available to them) and the belief can be true (in the world) yet the subject's cognitive position can be epistemically defective in a way that disqualifies it as knowledge. This gap is the primary object of investigation.

- **There is a difference between epistemic luck and epistemic virtue.** Some cases of justified true belief involve luck — the subject's belief being true is not appropriately connected to their justification or cognitive agency. Knowledge requires that this luck be absent. Different analyses cash out this absence differently, but the stance insists on the distinction.

- **The problem is not solved but clarified.** The Gettier problem does not argue that knowledge is impossible or that JTB is adequate. Rather, it maps the space of possible solutions (reliability conditions, causal constraints, safety and sensitivity requirements, safety from veritic luck, performance conditions) and shows that each solution has its own counterexamples. The stance's work is to keep the problem sharp.

- **Intuitions about cases are evidence.** The stance treats the judgment "this person does not know, even though they satisfy all the conditions we laid down" as data that a theory must accommodate. The intuition may be wrong in any given case, but the burden is on the analysis to explain why it is wrong, not to dismiss it as unreliable.

## Guiding Questions

**The case structure**
- What is the subject's belief, and on what grounds do they hold it?
- What is the justification for the belief, and is it strong enough to make the subject justified in holding it by ordinary epistemological standards?
- Is the belief in fact true? (The setup must ensure truth without the subject's knowledge.)
- What is the "twist" or hidden fact that makes the case problematic — the fact that would disqualify the belief from counting as knowledge if the subject knew it?

**The intuitive judgment**
- Does the subject know? What do our ordinary judgments say?
- If the answer is "no knowledge," what is missing? What does the subject lack that would elevate their justified true belief into knowledge?
- Is the intuition stable across different versions or variations of the case?

**Testing a proposed analysis**
- Does the proposed condition (causal, reliability, safety, sensitivity, virtue-based, or other) rule out the counterexample?
- If it does, does it do so without over-reaching — without excluding cases that should count as knowledge?
- Can a counterexample to the proposed condition itself be constructed? (What makes a subject reliably right about something, but still not know it?)

**The underlying structure of the gap**
- Is the gap between justification and knowledge a gap between *internals* (what the subject can access) and *externals* (how the world actually is)?
- Is it a gap between *epistemic standing* (being justified) and *epistemic achievement* (actually knowing)?
- Is it a gap between the subject's *cognitive process* and the *world's cooperation* with that process?

**Variations and stability**
- How does the case behave when details change? (When the subject is more justified; when the hidden fact is different; when the mechanism producing truth is different?)
- If the intuition shifts across variations, what does that shift reveal about what actually carries the epistemic weight?

## What It Reveals

- **The insufficiency of the JTB formula itself.** Most proposed conditions for knowledge — even those with long philosophical pedigrees — are vulnerable to counterexamples that show they do not ensure genuine knowledge.

- **The distinct structure of different types of epistemic failure.** Gettier cases expose different failure modes: cases where the subject's justification is based on a false lemma, cases where the mechanism connecting belief to truth is broken, cases where luck is involved in the truth but not the justification, cases where the subject is reliable but not safe.

- **The role of luck in distinguishing knowledge from justified true belief.** Many analyses converge on the idea that veritic luck — the subject's belief being true by accident rather than by design or tracking — is what disqualifies JTB from knowledge. The Gettier problem makes this visible.

- **The importance of the cognitive subject's entitlement to the analysis.** The stance reveals whether a proposed analysis is capturing something the subject could actually know or justifiedly believe, or whether it is imposing external structure the subject has no access to.

- **Differences between what makes belief justified and what makes it knowledge.** Justification appears to be more internalist, more accessible to the subject; knowledge appears to require external conditions (causal relationships, reliability, safety, appropriate connection to the world) that the subject may not have access to.

## What It Obscures

- **Positive accounts of knowledge.** The Gettier stance is purely negative — it shows what knowledge is not, but does not itself propose what knowledge is. When applied without supplementation, it leaves the analyst in the position of having ruled out one after another proposed sufficient condition without offering a coherent alternative. (A constructive epistemology — virtue epistemology, reliabilism, contextualism, safety-based approaches — is needed to build what the Gettier problem tears down.)

- **The diversity of knowledge beyond propositional knowledge.** Gettier cases typically concern "knowledge that" — propositional knowledge — while "knowledge how," "acquaintance knowledge," and practical or embodied knowledge may have different structures entirely. A pure Gettier reading can miss these heterogeneous forms.

- **Social and testimonial dimensions of knowledge.** Most Gettier cases are individualist — they concern a lone subject and their justification. They can obscure how knowledge is socially distributed, how testimony and institutional credentials function, and what role collective epistemic practices play in warranting belief.

- **The pragmatic and contextual variability of knowledge standards.** Gettier analysis tends toward abstractness; it can miss how what counts as knowing is sensitive to practical context, conversational stakes, and what is at risk if the belief is wrong. (Contextualism and pragmatism recover this.)

- **The relationship between justification and truth.** By treating JTB as a starting point, the stance assumes justification and truth are independent variables that can come apart. But some epistemologies argue that justification just *is* truth-aptness, or that the conditions that make a belief justified are the same as those that make it true. The stance's framing rules out this debate.

- **Meta-level questions about epistemology itself.** The Gettier problem is a meta-scientific move — it questions the adequacy of existing analyses by finding their boundaries. But it does not ask: Why do we care about this particular definition? What are we trying to do when we analyze knowledge? Is the problem itself well-formed? These questions require stepping outside the stance.

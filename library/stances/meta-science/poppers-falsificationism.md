# Popper's Falsificationism

## Overview

Popper's stance commits to a single, austere principle: **a statement has scientific standing only if it is logically possible to prove it false through empirical observation or logical refutation**. The stance is not about how science *works* (descriptively) but about what *counts* as science (normatively). Its interpretive power lies in the ability to distinguish genuine scientific claims from speculative, metaphysical, or tautological statements that *appear* scientific but cannot be falsified. It is fundamentally a stance about the demarcation problem — where to draw the line between science and non-science — and applies that criterion to any artifact that makes claims about the world: theories, methodologies, statistical arguments, predictions, and the structure of institutions that claim scientific authority.

## Foundational Commitments

- **Falsifiability is the criterion of demarcation.** A statement is scientific if and only if there exists a logically possible observation or experiment that would prove it false. If no such observation could ever exist, the statement is not a scientific claim, no matter how sophisticated its language or institutional backing.

- **Verification is logically impossible; falsification is logically possible.** No amount of positive evidence can confirm a universal statement with certainty (the "problem of induction"), but a single authentic falsifying instance can decisively refute it. Asymmetry is the key: the logic runs one way.

- **Ad hoc modifications destroy falsifiability.** A theory that can always be patched to accommodate any counterevidence by adding auxiliary hypotheses, redefining terms, or shifting the scope is unfalsifiable and therefore unscientific, even if the patches are internally consistent.

- **Prediction must be independent of the data used to construct the theory.** A theory that is built to fit existing data and then tested on the same data is not being tested at all; the test is genuine only when the theory makes predictions about new cases not yet observed.

- **The problem of induction has no solution.** We cannot prove that the future will resemble the past, that unobserved cases match observed ones, or that a law holds universally. Science must proceed without proof; falsificationism accepts this and turns it into strength rather than weakness.

- **Simplicity and parsimony have epistemic weight.** When two theories are equally consistent with the data, the simpler theory is preferable not because it is "true" but because it makes stronger predictions and is therefore more falsifiable — it rules out more possibilities.

- **Error and refutation are constitutive of progress.** Science advances not by accumulating confirmations but by bold conjectures that are ruthlessly tested and, when they fail, replaced by better theories. The scientist must actively *seek* refutation, not shelter from it.

## Guiding Questions

**Logical structure of the claim**
- Is there any possible observation or experiment that would count as evidence *against* this claim?
- If no such observation could exist even in principle, is the claim scientific or metaphysical?
- Has the claim been formulated precisely enough that its negation is clear?
- What exactly would have to be observed or measured for the claim to be false?

**Ad hoc modification and auxiliary hypotheses**
- If the claim has been challenged by counterevidence, how has it been patched?
- Are the patches adding explanatory power, or merely protecting the theory from refutation?
- Could the theory accommodate *any* observation without revision? If so, it is unfalsifiable.
- Have the original terms been quietly redefined to escape refutation?

**Prediction and novel evidence**
- Are the predictions made *before* seeing the data, or constructed *after* to fit observations?
- Is the theory being tested on the same data used to build it, or on genuinely new cases?
- Are the predictions specific enough to be wrong?
- Does the theory forbid certain observations, or is it compatible with all possible outcomes?

**Simplicity and explanatory reach**
- Between competing theories equally consistent with current data, which rules out more possibilities?
- Does this theory trade simplicity for ad hoc flexibility? At what cost?
- How much explanatory apparatus (unobservables, entities, parameters) does the theory require?

**Risk and refutability**
- What would the scientist *least* want to find?
- Is the research designed to refute the hypothesis, or to confirm it?
- Are alternative explanations being actively sought, or only the favored theory tested?

**Institutional gatekeeping disguised as science**
- Does authority or credentials substitute for logical refutability?
- Are claims treated as "beyond question" because of their source rather than their logical structure?
- Is the burden of proof on the challenger to refute, or on the claimant to specify conditions of falsity?

## What It Reveals

- **Metaphysical claims masquerading as science.** Popper's stance makes visible statements that are logically unfalsifiable but use scientific language and institutional authority — psychoanalytic dogma, teleological history, statistical claims with infinitely movable goalposts.

- **Auxiliary hypothesis creep.** When a theory is patched repeatedly to escape refutation rather than being abandoned, the stance shows how "flexibility" becomes loss of testability. Each patch can be rational individually; the accumulated pattern reveals unfalsifiability.

- **Circularity in argument structure.** The stance detects when a theory is being tested on the same data used to construct it, making apparent confirmation illusory. It also reveals when a claim defines away its critics (e.g., "anyone who disagrees is not truly following the method").

- **The difference between precision and vagueness disguised as nuance.** Popper's stance distinguishes between a claim made more specific (and therefore more falsifiable) and a claim made vaguer (and therefore less falsifiable) under the guise of sophistication or contextual sensitivity.

- **Where institutional authority replaces logical argument.** The stance exposes appeals to expert consensus or institutional prestige that substitute for specifying what would count as evidence against the claim.

- **Prediction failure treated as irrelevant to the theory.** The stance surfaces when a theory makes failed predictions but the failure is written off as a measurement error, external interference, or methodological problem rather than as a genuine refutation.

## What It Obscures

- **The practice of actual science.** Popper's normative criterion (what science *ought* to be) has limited descriptive power for how science *actually* works. Scientists routinely proceed with unfalsifiable background assumptions, protective belts of auxiliary hypotheses, and incommensurable frameworks. Kuhn and Lakatos documented this extensively, and the stance does not easily accommodate it. (Pair with a historical or sociological stance when understanding scientific practice is the goal.)

- **Values, interpretation, and framework dependence.** What counts as a "genuine" falsifying observation is itself theory-laden. Different frameworks interpret the same data differently; "the data" is not independent of the theory allegedly being tested. The stance treats observation as if it is transparent and objective. (Hanson, Kuhn, and later philosophy of science push back hard here.)

- **Probabilistic and statistical claims.** Falsificationism was built for classical physics; it struggles with statistical mechanics, quantum mechanics, and modern statistical inference, where "falsification" is not binary and auxiliary hypotheses about measurement error, sample size, and degrees of freedom are load-bearing. The stance tends to dismiss statistical claims as less scientific or to demand impossible precision.

- **Underdetermination of theory by evidence.** Multiple logically incompatible theories can fit the same data. Popper's stance emphasizes which theories are simpler or more falsifiable, but this does not uniquely determine which is "correct." The stance works best when theories genuinely differ in their predictions, less well when they are empirically equivalent.

- **The irreducibility of context.** Claims that make sense and are falsifiable *within* a disciplinary framework may not be falsifiable outside it. Biology, anthropology, and history deal with singular events and complex systems where the logic of laboratory falsification does not transfer. The stance privileges experimental science over non-experimental science without acknowledging this as a choice.

- **Social and institutional dimensions of refutation.** A theory is only falsified when the scientific community *accepts* that refutation as valid. That acceptance is partly logical, partly social and institutional. Pure Popperism ignores the question of how disagreement about whether a refutation "really" happened gets resolved.

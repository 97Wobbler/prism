# Critical Geopolitics

## Overview

Critical Geopolitics is a stance committed to denaturalizing the spatial assumptions embedded in political discourse and strategic reasoning. Its core claim is that **geography is not a neutral container of politics but an active, contested field where representations of space, territory, borders, and the "natural" relationships between peoples and places are produced to serve particular power interests**. The stance rejects the notion that geopolitical claims rest on obvious facts about maps or resources; instead, it reads how geographical imaginaries — narratives about who belongs where, which regions are "strategic," what constitutes a "region" at all — become sedimented into policy, military doctrine, and international relations. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure; its findings surface the political work that geography performs when it presents itself as description rather than prescription.

## Foundational Commitments

- **Geography is represented, not given.** Maps, regions, and spatial categories (the "Middle East," the "China Threat," "the homeland") are not natural divisions that exist prior to naming them. They are produced through discourse, cartography, education, and policy — and they can be otherwise. What counts as a "region," which boundaries matter, and what relationships are "natural" are all contingent, not inevitable.

- **Spatial claims carry strategic intent.** Every assertion about what territory belongs to whom, which space is "ours" or "theirs," which areas are vital or peripheral, is a move in a political game. Geography becomes legible as a weapon once you treat geographical claims as claims, not facts.

- **The nation-state has no natural geopolitical logic.** The state naturalizes itself by claiming that its borders are "natural" — dictated by geography, history, or culture — when in fact state boundaries are political achievements, often recent and always contested. Critical geopolitics exposes how geographical arguments are used to make contingent state power look inevitable.

- **Power operates through spatial representation and control.** Ruling a territory requires not only military or administrative control but also the ability to make a particular representation of that space — its boundaries, resources, risks, inhabitants — the one that "everyone" accepts. This is why maps, atlases, textbooks, and media depictions are geopolitical weapons.

- **Geopolitical reasoning has a genealogy, not a logic.** Geopolitical concepts (balance of power, spheres of influence, heartland theory, containment, pivot points) are historically produced ways of thinking about space and strategy, not timeless truths about how the world works. Understanding their provenance reveals their contingency.

- **Scale is a contested political achievement.** What counts as "local," "regional," "national," or "global" is not a natural fact about size; it is a framing that serves some interests over others. The scale at which a problem is posed is itself a geopolitical move.

- **Geopolitical identities are performatively constituted.** Actors are not born geopolitical subjects ("the West," "the East," "rogue states," "strategic partners"). These identities are reproduced through repeated invocation in discourse, policy, and military doctrine. Once you see this, the apparent solidity of geopolitical divisions cracks.

## Guiding Questions

**Representation and discourse**
- How is this space being represented? Through what maps, narratives, metaphors, and scales?
- Who produced these representations? Under what institutional auspices (state cartography, academic geography, policy think tanks, military planning)?
- What alternative representations are being silenced or made to seem illegitimate?
- What work does the representation do? What claims does it make possible or foreclose?

**Geographical claims and their justifications**
- What geographical arguments are being made (about territory, resources, proximity, natural affinity, historical ownership)?
- What do these claims naturalize? What do they present as geographical fact that is actually political claim?
- What history is being invoked, and what is being left out? (Geopolitical arguments often cherry-pick history to justify contemporary claims.)
- How does the claim use the language of necessity ("we must control this area," "this region is vital")?

**Scale and boundary-making**
- At what scale is this geopolitical problem being framed? Why this scale and not another?
- Who benefits from the boundaries being drawn this way? Who is separated from whom?
- What would change if the scale or the boundary were redrawn?
- Are "regions" or "zones" being naturalized? (The Middle East, Southeast Asia, the Arctic — these are contested frames, not given geographical units.)

**Subjects and identities**
- What geopolitical identities are being invoked or constructed ("the West," "the Global South," "rising powers," "rogue states")?
- How are these identities naturalized as geographical or civilizational facts rather than political productions?
- Who is included and excluded by these identity claims?
- What roles or attributes are assigned to each geopolitical subject?

**Strategic narratives and geopolitical reasoning**
- What strategic logic is being invoked (balance of power, containment, pivot, encirclement, sphere of influence)?
- Where did this logic come from? Is it being applied universally or selectively?
- What does it assume about how power moves through space and who can legitimately claim territory?
- What alternative strategic logics are rendered invisible by accepting this one?

**Power, control, and materiality**
- What material resources or military positions are being claimed as strategically vital?
- Who benefits from treating them this way? Who loses?
- What forms of control (military, administrative, epistemic) are implied by the geographical claim?

## What It Reveals

- **The political geography inside "common sense."** What appears as natural geopolitics — the idea that certain territories "must" be controlled, that certain regions are "inherently" rivals — is always a politically interested narrative dressed as description.

- **How state legitimacy rests on geographical claims.** States justify themselves by claiming that their borders are dictated by geography, history, or culture; Critical Geopolitics shows how much work goes into making that claim stick.

- **The role of cartography and representation in enabling power.** Maps are not neutral; they are instruments that make certain territorial claims visible and legitimate while rendering others unthinkable. Who controls the map controls part of what becomes "possible" in geopolitics.

- **The genealogy of geopolitical concepts.** Terms like "pivot," "containment," "the West," "the heartland" are not timeless observations about the world; they are inventions with histories. Recovering that history exposes their contingency.

- **Scale-based manipulation in strategy.** Defining a problem at the regional scale rather than the local scale (or vice versa) is a geopolitical move that serves some actors over others. The stance makes visible what scale work is being done.

- **Geopolitical Others.** The stance surfaces how particular actors are represented as threats, rivals, or irrelevant through geopolitical discourse — and how that representation enables certain actions (containment, encirclement, intervention).

## What It Obscures

- **Material resource conflict.** Critical Geopolitics is strong on discourse and representation and weaker on the genuine material stakes of territorial control — water, minerals, arable land, energy reserves. A reading that attends only to representation can miss that there is sometimes real scarcity driving geopolitical behavior. (Pair with political ecology or resource-based analysis when material scarcity is load-bearing.)

- **The durability of geographical constraints.** While the stance is right that regions and boundaries are produced, it risks overstating the malleability of geography. Climate, topography, distance, and resource location do impose real constraints; they are not purely discursive. An overconfident Critical Geopolitics reading can treat all geography as ideology.

- **Non-state actors and transnational dynamics.** The stance was developed primarily to critique state-centric geopolitics and the "great power" narrative. It is weaker on how non-state actors (corporations, diasporas, networks, NGOs) navigate geopolitical space, and on dynamics that do not fit the state-vs.-state frame.

- **Embodied and lived geography.** By focusing on high-level discourse, maps, and policy narratives, the stance can miss how geography is lived — how borders actually constrain mobility, how territorial claims are experienced by people on the ground, how the everyday production of space happens below the level of strategy.

- **Geopolitical asymmetry.** The stance works well for reading how powerful states naturalize their dominance; it is less sharp on how weaker states and non-state actors use geographical claims defensively or creatively. Not all geopolitical discourse is equally positioned to succeed.

- **Its own institutional position.** Critical Geopolitics analysis often happens in universities and think tanks funded by the very powers it critiques. The stance does not systematically ask where it itself is produced and what interests its critique might serve.

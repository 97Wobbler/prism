# Conflict Theory

## Overview

Conflict theory is a commitment to reading **social arrangements not as consensus-based or functionally integrated systems, but as provisional settlements between groups with fundamentally opposed interests over scarce resources, status, and power**. The stance rejects the assumption that institutions exist because they are functionally necessary or broadly agreed-upon; instead, it asks which groups benefit from the current arrangement and what mechanisms keep subordinate groups compliant. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure. Its findings become visible when you stop asking "how does society function?" and start asking "whose interests does this arrangement serve, and how is the arrangement maintained in the face of conflict?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **Material interests generate structural conflict.** Groups in a society have genuinely opposed interests rooted in their access to resources, status, and power. These conflicts are not aberrations or failures of integration; they are the normal state. Conflict is not a sign that something is broken but a sign that distribution is unequal.

- **Power is unequally distributed and actively defended.** Power is not a neutral variable that circulates equally; dominant groups accumulate control over resources, institutions, and coercive means, and they use these tools to maintain their position. What appears as "consensus" is often the silence of people with no realistic alternative.

- **Institutions are tools of domination and stabilization.** Schools, law enforcement, wage systems, professional credentials, workplace hierarchies — these are not neutral mechanisms for allocating talent or resources. They are arrangements that legitimate and reproduce the advantages of dominant groups and the subordination of others. They naturalize inequality.

- **Subordinate groups resist.** Even when dominated groups have limited power, they resist, adapt, organize, and make competing claims. Resistance takes many forms — some open, most covert; the stance looks for where the system is actually unstable, not just where it claims to be stable.

- **Change comes through conflict, not evolution toward equilibrium.** Societies do not naturally progress toward greater integration or justice. Change happens when subordinate groups gain resources, organization, or opportunity to make competing claims powerful enough that the system must shift. Stability is maintained through force, incentive, and ideology — not because the arrangement is inherently balanced.

- **Legitimation is political work, not inevitable.** When dominant groups make their privileges appear justified (as "meritocratic," "natural," "for everyone's benefit"), this is active ideological labor, not a reflection of reality. The stance treats justifications as claims to examine, not as facts.

## Guiding Questions

**Distribution and who benefits**
- Who gets access to what resources — money, time, education, safety, dignity, voice?
- Which groups have grown richer, more secure, or more powerful under this arrangement? Which have lost ground?
- What would change if resources were distributed differently, and who would lose?
- What mechanisms ensure that dominant groups' advantages are passed to the next generation?

**Control and coercion**
- Who controls the institutions that matter — law enforcement, credit, hiring, physical space, information flow?
- What happens to people who refuse to comply? What are the costs of exit or resistance?
- What overt and covert coercion holds the arrangement in place? (Threat of job loss, homelessness, arrest, social exclusion, loss of credibility?)
- What rules apply differentially depending on group membership?

**Legitimation and naturalization**
- What story does the system tell about why the distribution is fair or necessary?
- Who gets to tell this story and under what conditions is it believed?
- What is framed as individual failure or merit when it is actually structural advantage or disadvantage?
- What would have to be true for the current arrangement to be actually just, and is it true?

**Where conflict is suppressed or visible**
- What grievances exist but are not articulated publicly? Why not?
- Where is there organized resistance, and what form does it take?
- Where is conflict breaking through the surface of "normal" operation?
- What do people do informally to work around or subvert the system?
- Which claims for change are labeled "radical" or "unrealistic," and what interests does that labeling serve?

**Boundaries and membership**
- Who counts as a legitimate participant in decisions that affect them, and who does not?
- How are group boundaries drawn and maintained? (Credentials, hiring, access to networks, language, dress, geography?)
- What keeps subordinate groups divided, and what would build solidarity among them?

**Historical contingency**
- How did this particular distribution of power come to exist? Was there a moment when it could have been different?
- What alternatives were foreclosed, and by whom?
- What maintains the arrangement now, and what would be needed to destabilize it?

## What It Reveals

A conflict-theory reading surfaces findings that stances committed to equilibrium or consensus systematically miss:

- **Who wins and loses materially** under the current arrangement — not in the abstract, but in concrete terms of wealth, safety, time, dignity, and opportunity.
- **Mechanisms of subordination** that operate below the surface of "how things are done" — gatekeeping, surveillance, credentialing, access control, the differential application of rules.
- **Where the system is actually fragile** — where compliance is maintained through coercion rather than consent, where resistance is being managed rather than absent.
- **The political work of legitimation** — how dominant groups make their privileges appear justified, natural, or beneficial to all, and where this story breaks down.
- **Subordinate groups' own resources and agency** — the ways people resist, adapt, organize, and make claims even in conditions of limited power.
- **How institutions reproduce inequality across generations** — not through biology or culture, but through structures that accumulate advantage for some and disadvantage for others.

## What It Obscures

The stance has real blind spots; applying it without acknowledging them produces incomplete readings.

- **Non-conflictual social goods.** Some institutions and norms create genuine value for most people — language, reciprocity, arts, some forms of knowledge. A pure conflict reading can flatten these into "just ideology" and miss what is actually worth preserving. (Pair with functionalism or ritual theory when coordination and meaning-making are load-bearing.)

- **Axes of conflict beyond resource distribution.** Gender, sexuality, race, disability, religious affiliation — these generate real conflicts that do not always map neatly onto class or material-resource frameworks. A reading that reduces all conflict to economic interests misses their distinct dynamics. (Use feminist, postcolonial, disability-theory, or intersectional stances alongside.)

- **Agency and choice under constraint.** Conflict theory can overstate the degree to which people are trapped and understate the room for individual maneuver, creativity, and genuine ethical choice. People do sometimes cooperate beyond what material interests strictly require.

- **Stability and its sources.** Over-emphasizing conflict can miss how much of social life is stable because people genuinely share goals, find meaning in participation, or hold commitments that transcend material interest. (A functionalist or institutionalist stance recovers this.)

- **Its own conditions of production.** A conflict analysis that does not examine its own funding, institutional position, and political stakes is incomplete by its own lights. Who is able to make this argument, and what interests are served by making it?

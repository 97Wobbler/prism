# Intersectionality (Crenshaw)

## Overview

Intersectionality is a commitment to recognizing that **systems of oppression (race, gender, class, sexuality, disability, nation) do not operate independently but mutually constitute and amplify each other at their points of intersection — and that the experience of someone positioned at multiple axes of subordination is not the sum of separate oppressions but a distinct and often invisible formation**. The stance's purpose is to make visible what single-axis frameworks (race alone, gender alone, class alone) systematically erase: the specific harms, strategies, and knowledge that emerge when multiple structures of subordination converge on the same body or community. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure; its findings appear only when you stop asking "which oppression is primary?" and start asking "what becomes possible to see and do when you start here — from the margins where multiple systems meet?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **Oppressions are not additive; they are constitutive.** The experience of a Black woman under racism and patriarchy is not racism + sexism. Rather, racism and patriarchy each shape how the other operates. Gender oppression takes a racialized form; racial oppression takes a gendered form. They remake each other.

- **The margins reveal what the center obscures.** Those positioned at the intersection of multiple subordinations develop knowledge and strategies that are invisible from single-axis perspectives. This is not a metaphor; it is epistemologically consequential — different knowledge is available from different social locations.

- **Single-axis frameworks reproduce the very dominations they critique.** A feminist framework that speaks only of "women" without specifying which women can make women of color disappear from the analysis itself, reproducing their marginalization inside the supposedly liberatory space.

- **Institutions and policies have disparate impacts along intersecting lines.** A law or practice may appear "neutral" or even progressive on one axis while inflicting specific harm on those who are subordinated on multiple axes simultaneously. This harm is not incidental; it is structural.

- **The intersectional location is a site of both specific vulnerability and distinct strategic knowledge.** Those at the intersection are not simply "most oppressed" (a flattening that competes oppressions); they are positioned to see and articulate dynamics that single-axis movements cannot.

- **Intersectionality is about structure, not identity.** The stance is not about how individuals experience multiple identities. It is about how systems of power distribute resources, safety, visibility, and voice along multiple axes, and how those distributions are entangled.

## Guiding Questions

**Mapping the intersection**
- Which axes of power are operating in this institution, practice, policy, or artifact?
- Where are people positioned at the *convergence* of multiple systems of subordination? Which specific people or communities?
- What becomes *impossible* or *invisible* when analysis stays within single-axis frames?

**What disappears**
- Whose experiences, needs, or strategies are erased when the framework focuses on one axis alone?
- Which community at the intersection is being spoken for by single-axis movements, and what are they losing in the translation?
- What harms are being named as "not really about X" because they don't fit the single-axis account?

**How oppressions remake each other**
- How does racism shape the form that gender oppression takes for this community?
- How does patriarchy reshape racial oppression? (E.g., how are Black women's bodies racialized differently than Black men's bodies; how is immigration enforcement gendered?)
- What does class oppression look like *through* the lens of race and gender at once?
- How do sexuality, disability, or nation-status intersect with race and gender to create distinct formations of power?

**Policy and institutional impact**
- Does this policy appear "neutral" or progressive on one axis while causing specific harm on another?
- Who benefits from the single-axis framing? Whose interests does it serve to ignore intersectionality?
- What "trade-offs" or "competing interests" are being invoked, and who does the trade-off leave behind?

**Knowledge from the margin**
- What do people at this intersection know about how systems actually work that single-axis frameworks miss?
- What strategies, resistances, or innovations emerge from this location that movements centered elsewhere have not yet articulated?
- Whose intellectual and organizing work is being credited or erased?

**The stakes of visibility**
- What becomes possible to claim, demand, or organize around once this intersection is centered rather than marginalized?
- What resources, safety, or recognition are at stake in whether the intersection is recognized?

## What It Reveals

An intersectional reading surfaces findings that single-axis stances systematically miss:

- **Distinct harms that appear at the intersection.** A harm that affects women at the intersection of gender and race is not reducible to either axis alone; denying its specificity is a form of erasure.
- **The mutual constitution of oppressive systems.** Showing how racism and patriarchy (or class and sexuality, or disability and nation-status) remake each other, rather than operating as separate tracks.
- **The strategic knowledge produced by those at the intersection.** Communities positioned at multiple axes of subordination develop understanding of power dynamics that single-axis movements have not yet articulated — and these insights are often discredited or absorbed without attribution.
- **How "universal" claims actually apply only to some women (or some workers, or some racial groups).** Exposing the unmarked position from which single-axis frameworks speak.
- **The disparate impact of seemingly neutral policies.** Showing how a policy coded as progressive on one axis can inflict specific harm on those subordinated on multiple axes.
- **The structural interdependence of oppressions.** Revealing that addressing one axis of oppression while ignoring others leaves the system of domination intact, even strengthened.

## What It Obscures

The stance has genuine blind spots that produce errors if applied without care.

- **Individual complexity and agency.** Intersectionality is a structural stance; it attends to systems and positions, not to the actual choices, negotiations, and resistances of specific people. A reading that exhausts itself in mapping structure can flatten the human texture of how people navigate and refuse subordination. (Pair with narrative or biographical stances when individual agency is load-bearing.)

- **Axes beyond the canonical few.** Most intersectionality work focuses on race, gender, class, and sometimes sexuality. Disability, immigration status, nation, religion, caste, age, weight, and other axes are often marginalized *within* intersectional analysis itself — reproducing at the theoretical level the very problem the stance is designed to reveal.

- **Power between and among subordinated groups.** Intersectionality clarifies how multiple systems of oppression converge on the same body, but it can flatten the distinct powers and hierarchies *among* different communities of color, or among women across racial lines. Patriarchy operates within racialized communities; colorism, anti-Blackness, and ethnic hierarchies operate within communities of color. These are not reduced by centering the intersection.

- **Non-relational forms of power.** The stance is built on structural and relational thinking (how systems interact, how positions are produced in relation to each other). Non-relational forms — accumulated ecological damage, pandemic, trauma — are harder to theorize through intersectionality without supplementation.

- **The problem of scale and generality.** Intersectionality pushes toward the specific: *these* communities, *this* moment, *these* axes. It resists grand theory. But this specificity can make it hard to identify patterns that hold across contexts, or to make claims that travel. (Pair with comparative or structural-theoretical stances when large-scale patterns matter.)

- **Its own institutional position.** Intersectionality has been absorbed into diversity bureaucracies, human resources, and corporate equity initiatives, often drained of its critical edge. The stance can be deployed to claim progressivism while leaving power structures intact. The stance itself does not address how its own insights become domesticated.

## Output Format

```
## Intersectional Reading: <institution / policy / practice / artifact>

### Scope and location
- What is being read: <institution / policy / practice / text / artifact>
- Whose intersectional location(s) are at stake here

### Axes in operation
- Primary axes of power operating in this site: <race, gender, class, sexuality, disability, nation, …>
- How these axes are constituting and remaking each other: <specific examples>

### What single-axis frames erase
- What disappears when analyzed through race alone / gender alone / class alone: <specific erasures>
- Which communities at the intersection are invisible or misrepresented in single-axis accounts
- What harms are being named as "not really about X"

### Disparate impact and the "neutral" facade
- How the policy / practice appears progressive or neutral on one axis
- Who it harms specifically at the intersection of multiple axes
- What "trade-offs" are being invoked and who bears the cost

### Knowledge and strategy from the margin
- What people at this intersection know and have articulated that single-axis frameworks miss
- Whose intellectual or organizing work is being credited or erased
- What becomes possible to demand or organize around once the intersection is centered

### Structural interdependence
- How the system of power benefits from keeping these axes separate in analysis
- What would have to change if this intersection were genuinely centered, not just acknowledged

### Conversation with other stances
- What a single-axis feminist / antiracist / labor stance would emphasize or obscure
- What axes are still being marginalized even within this intersectional reading

### Confidence: high | medium | low
<basis: how directly the artifact exposes its intersectional operation; how clear the disparate impact is; which axes are most visible and which remain obscured; whether the reading risks flattening the complexity it claims to reveal>

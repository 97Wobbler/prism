# Actor-Network Theory (Latour)

## Overview

Actor-Network Theory (ANT) is not a procedure for predicting outcomes but a **commitment to treating social order as the product of heterogeneous networks in which human and nonhuman actors are symmetrically engaged in making things hold together**. The stance rejects the idea that society is made of "social" forces that shape "technical" or "natural" ones; instead, it asks how associations between humans, objects, documents, institutions, and forces become stabilized enough to be taken for granted. Its purpose is to make visible the work of translation—the negotiations, alignments, and investments—that goes into producing any durable arrangement. It is suited to reading organizations, technologies, scientific claims, material systems, and institutions where you want to understand not *why* something succeeded but *how* it was assembled and what keeps it from falling apart.

## Foundational Commitments

- **Humans and nonhumans are symmetrically implicated.** A network is not a human group that uses tools; it is an assembled collective in which a bridge, a computer, a disease organism, a document, a law, and a person all do work of association. The "social" is not a domain that contains the "technical" or "natural"—they are entangled from the start. Treating them symmetrically does not mean humans and nonhumans have identical capacities; it means their capacities are mutually configured by the network.

- **Stability is not the default; it is an achievement.** Things do not stay together because of inherent properties or underlying structures. They stay together because heterogeneous actors are continuously enrolled, aligned, and enrolled again. The moment this work of alignment stops, the network begins to unravel. What looks like a stable "institution" or "fact" is a durable translation.

- **Power is relational and emergent, not possessed.** No actor has power "in itself." Power emerges from the ability to enroll others—to make them go along, to pass through you, to stay associated. A CEO has power to the extent that subordinates, documents, contracts, technologies, and expectations consistently align to amplify their decisions. The moment that alignment fractures, the power is gone.

- **Translation is the primary operation.** Translation means that each actor in a network modifies the interests and investments of others to align with its own interests. A scientist translates the interests of a funding agency, a laboratory, peer reviewers, and future users into a research design. A technology translates the activities of multiple stakeholders into a single functioning system. Translation is not deception; it is the work of making networks hold.

- **Facts and artifacts are network effects.** A scientific fact is not "true in itself"; it is true to the extent that a network of laboratories, instruments, journals, colleagues, and funding agencies treats it as stable and reliable. A technology "works" not because of its intrinsic design but because it has been able to enroll the humans, materials, and institutions that keep it operating.

- **Inscription and documentation stabilize networks.** Written accounts, diagrams, databases, standards, and other inscriptions are not representations of a network—they *are* part of the network. They allow the network to travel, endure, and scale. Losing the documentation is losing the network.

- **Failure, breakdown, and controversy are informative.** When a network breaks down—a technology fails, a scientific claim is disputed, an organization collapses—the hidden actors and alignments that usually stay silent become visible. Controversy is when the black box opens.

## Guiding Questions

**Enrollment and actors**
- Who or what are the actors in this network? (Include objects, documents, regulations, institutions, as well as people.)
- How were they enrolled into this arrangement? What interests were they promised?
- Who is made essential to the network's functioning, and who is rendered dispensable?
- Which actors are visible in the official narrative, and which stay hidden?

**Translation and alignment**
- What work of translation holds this network together?
- How does each actor modify the interests and investments of others?
- What is gained and lost in each translation?
- What happens when one actor refuses to go along?

**Stabilization and durability**
- What keeps this network from falling apart? What are the points of stress?
- What inscriptions (documents, standards, databases, designs) stabilize it?
- How much continuous labor is required to maintain the appearance of stability?
- What would break if one key component were removed?

**Power and asymmetry**
- Who or what emerges as "obligatory passage point"—necessary to go through to get things done?
- How do some actors accumulate the ability to enroll others while others lose it?
- Where are the asymmetries in the network, and how are they naturalized?

**Failure and breakdown**
- Where does this network show stress or resistance?
- What actors or interests are being excluded or overridden?
- If it broke down, what would the breakdown reveal about hidden dependencies?
- When does "stability" show itself to be precarious?

**Heterogeneity and mixing**
- How does this arrangement depend on mixing of human and nonhuman actors?
- What would be lost if we tried to "purify" it into purely social OR purely technical OR purely natural components?

## What It Reveals

- **The active role of nonhuman actors**—objects, documents, technologies, organisms, and materials—in holding together what we call "social order." Most sociological stances treat these as passive tools; ANT makes them protagonists.
- **The work of translation and alignment** that keeps institutions and systems running. What appears effortless on the surface requires continuous negotiation and re-enrollment behind the scenes.
- **The contingency and fragility** of apparently stable arrangements. Facts, technologies, organizations, and institutions are held together by heterogeneous actors, not by "logic" or "structure." Change any actor, and the whole thing shifts.
- **The role of inscription and documentation** in making networks durable and scalable. A network travels through writing, not despite it.
- **Power as relational and emergent**, not as possession. The CEO's power is only as strong as the last alignment that fed it.
- **What breaks down as a window onto hidden dependencies.** Controversy and failure make visible what "working smoothly" keeps hidden.

## What It Obscures

- **Structural inequality and domination over time.** ANT is strong on how alignments form and weaken but often weak on how some actors' capacity to enroll others is systematically enabled or constrained by historical accumulation of resources, status, or violence. The stance can read as "neutral" about asymmetries it documents. (Pair with a political economy or intersectional stance to recover the question of structural embedding.)

- **Intentionality and strategic action.** By treating all actors symmetrically and focusing on emergent effects, ANT can flatten or obscure cases where powerful actors *deliberately* design systems to exclude or subordinate. Intentional domination is sometimes present, not just emergent effect.

- **Individual experience and lived contradiction.** The stance privileges the network's coherence and the work of alignment; it is less attentive to the friction, suffering, and incommensurable interests of people caught inside the network's logic.

- **Macro-historical forces and path dependency.** ANT's focus on local translations can make it hard to see how earlier histories of colonialism, industrialization, or institutional development set the stage for what networks can form now. The stance treats each network as freshly assembled.

- **The question of who benefits and who pays.** ANT can be used to describe a network neutrally without forcing the question of whose interests the translation serves and whose are foreclosed. Without supplementing, it can avoid the normativity question.

- **Its own conditions of production.** An ANT reading that does not turn its gaze on the institutions, funding, and peer networks that produced the reading itself is incomplete by its own standards. The stance often falls short here.

## Output Format

```
## Actor-Network Reading: <system / artifact / institution>

### Scope and entry
- What network is being traced: <system, artifact, institution, or controversy>
- Why this is a productive site: <what makes it visible through ANT>

### Actors and enrollment
- Human actors: <people, roles, organizations>
- Nonhuman actors: <objects, documents, technologies, organisms, forces, institutions>
- How each was enrolled: <what interests were promised or coerced>
- Visible vs. hidden actors: <who is acknowledged, who is offstaged>

### Translation and alignment
- Key work of translation: <how interests of different actors are bent to align>
- Points of negotiation: <where actors' interests had to be modified or reconciled>
- What is gained and lost in each translation: <trade-offs visible in the network>

### Stabilization mechanisms
- Inscriptions and documents: <what records stabilize the network>
- Obligatory passage points: <which actors or checkpoints are essential>
- Continuous labor required: <what work maintains stability>
- Fragility: <what would break if key components failed>

### Power and asymmetry
- Who or what emerges as able to enroll others: <obligatory passage point(s)>
- Asymmetries in the network: <who benefits, who is subordinated>
- How asymmetries are naturalized: <what makes them seem inevitable>

### Moments of breakdown or resistance
- Where the network shows stress: <tensions, incompatibilities, refusals>
- What failure would reveal: <hidden dependencies surfaced by breakdown>

### Conversation with other stances
- What a political economy / intersectional / power/knowledge reading would add or push back on: <brief>

### Confidence: high | medium | low
<basis: how directly you can trace the network and its actors; whether you have access to moments of translation or breakdown; how much inference about hidden actors the reading depends on>
```

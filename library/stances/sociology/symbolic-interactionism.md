# Symbolic Interactionism

## Overview

Symbolic interactionism is a commitment to **reading social order as something *made continuously through the exchange and interpretation of symbols between actors*, not as the playing-out of pre-scripted roles or the enforcement of fixed norms**. The stance rejects the idea that society is a structure that sits *above* individuals, constraining them from the outside. Instead, it treats society as an accomplishment — something that exists only in and through the actual, moment-to-moment work of interpretation, negotiation, and meaning-making. It is an interpretive position for reading face-to-face interaction, organizational encounters, identity performances, and any site where actors must coordinate action by reading each other's conduct and adjusting their own. The stance's findings are invisible to approaches that assume social order is either given (structural) or purely invented (individualist); symbolic interactionism reveals the intricate third terrain: the ongoing, interactive *negotiation* of meaning that holds any social world together.

## Foundational Commitments

- **Humans act on the basis of meanings they assign to things.** People do not respond directly to stimuli or to objective facts; they respond to their *interpretation* of those stimuli and facts. The meaning of an object — a gesture, a silence, a uniform, a question, a code review comment — is not inherent; it emerges from how an actor construes it.

- **These meanings arise through social interaction, not individual contemplation.** A symbol only means something because people have learned to treat it as meaning that thing in shared contexts. Language, gesture, status markers, professional identities — all are shared conventions, learned and perpetually adjusted in interaction. No meaning is purely private.

- **Meanings are continuously adjusted and renegotiated in interaction itself.** Social order is not handed down and then passively received. It is accomplished through people reading each other's conduct, inferring intentions, adjusting their own actions, and mutually interpreting what is happening. This happens in real time, continuously, often without explicit discussion.

- **The self is a social object, not a pre-given entity.** A person does not have a fixed, internal "self" that she carries into interaction and expresses. Instead, the self emerges as a reflexive process — the person takes the role of the other, imagines how she appears to them, and adjusts herself accordingly. The self is interactive, not introspective.

- **Interaction is negotiation, even when it appears to be rule-following.** Even when people seem to be "following rules," they are actually interpreting what the rule means in *this* context, for *these* particular others, in light of ongoing contingencies. The rule does not execute itself; it is taken up and implemented through judgment.

- **Order is local and emergent, not imposed from above.** Large-scale structures (law, bureaucracy, economy) only "work" because they are perpetually enacted at ground level through countless small interactions where people interpret what the structure means and what it requires of them here and now. If interaction stops doing this work, the structure ceases to exist.

- **What is implicit is as important as what is explicit.** Much of the meaning-making in interaction happens through things that are *not* said — through tone, timing, eye contact, what counts as obvious, what is taken for granted. Reading interaction means attending to what people do *with* and *without* words.

## Guiding Questions

**Symbols and their meanings**
- What symbols (verbal, gestural, material, sartorial) are in circulation in this interaction?
- Which meanings for these symbols are *shared* among the actors, and which are contested or private?
- How do actors signal which meanings they are assigning to an object or gesture?
- Where do meanings shift in the course of the interaction?

**Definition of the situation**
- How are the actors collectively establishing what kind of situation *this* is? (A casual conversation? A performance? A crisis? A negotiation?)
- What cues — spatial, temporal, verbal, bodily — establish and maintain the definition?
- What happens when different actors have competing definitions of the situation?
- How is the situation redefined if something unexpected occurs?

**Taking the role of the other**
- What is each actor inferring about the other's perspective, interests, or intentions?
- How does each actor adjust their own conduct based on imagining how they appear to the other?
- Where do actors misread each other's intentions, and what repair work follows?
- How does the presence of an audience change what actors do?

**Negotiated order**
- What agreements (explicit or tacit) are being negotiated in this interaction about how things will be done, who has what standing, what counts as fair?
- Where are the pressure points in the negotiation? What are actors reluctant to concede?
- What history of prior interactions shapes what can be negotiated here?
- What happens when the negotiated order breaks down?

**Performance and impression management**
- What is the actor trying to make appear about themselves, the situation, or what is at stake?
- What might they want to *conceal* or downplay?
- What contradictions arise between what they want to present and what the interaction itself is revealing?
- Who is the intended audience for this performance?

**What is taken for granted**
- What background assumptions are the actors *not* stating but are relying on to make sense of each other?
- What would have to be made explicit if these shared assumptions broke down?
- Where do actors appeal to "common sense" or "obviously"?
- What counts as normal, expectable conduct in this context, and what would violate it?

## What It Reveals

A symbolic interactionist reading surfaces findings that other stances miss:

- **The constant, active work of meaning-making** in what looks like passive role-playing or norm-following. What structural stances see as obedience, the interactionist sees as creative interpretation and negotiation.

- **How order is continuously accomplished at ground level** — not descending from above but being perpetually enacted through countless micro-decisions about what something means and what to do about it.

- **Contradictions and tensions between how actors present themselves** and what their conduct reveals about them; the gap between performed identity and what is implicit in behavior.

- **The power of implicit understanding** — how much coordination happens through things not explicitly stated, through shared history, through reading tone and timing.

- **How actors reflexively monitor themselves** and adjust their conduct in real time as they read others' responses. The interaction is not mechanistic; it is responsive and adaptive.

- **The fragility of social order** — that it depends entirely on actors continuing to participate in the meaning-making process. If they stop cooperating with the definitions and symbols in circulation, it collapses.

- **Negotiated compromises embedded in seemingly neutral procedures** — how a "policy" or "standard" is actually the sedimented result of prior struggles about what is fair or necessary.

## What It Obscures

The stance has significant blind spots when applied without supplementation:

- **Structural inequality and constraint.** Symbolic interactionism excels at showing how order is made through interaction, but it can minimize how much room actors actually have for renegotiation. Power differentials — based on class, race, gender, institutional position — constrain what definitions of the situation are even *available* to propose. A pure interactionist reading can treat structural inequality as merely a matter of different interpretations rather than material constraint. (Pair with a structural or Marxist stance to recover what is fixed in place.)

- **How symbols get their weight in the first place.** The stance explains how actors use shared symbols to coordinate, but it says less about *why* certain symbols carry so much force — why a uniform commands deference, why a credential opens doors. This often traces to historical power and accumulation outside the frame of a single interaction. (A historical or Foucauldian reading recovers this genealogy.)

- **The role of affect and embodiment in meaning-making.** Symbolic interactionism focuses on meaning, but much of what shapes how actors interpret each other is pre-symbolic — fear, desire, fatigue, habituation, the visceral response to another body. Not everything in interaction is *meaningful* in the symbolic sense; some of it is just happening in the body.

- **Large-scale institutional logics.** While the stance can show how people enact a bureaucracy through interaction, it is less equipped to analyze the logic *of* bureaucracy as a system — why certain forms of coordination, surveillance, and sorting appear across different bureaucracies. A systems or organizational theory lens is needed here.

- **Quick reduction to "it's all negotiated."** Applied carelessly, the stance can flatten important differences between contexts where negotiation is genuinely open and contexts where one party has monopoly on definition. Not all interactions are equally negotiated; the stance risks missing where power has already foreclosed the range of meanings available.

# Lateral Thinking (de Bono)

## Overview

Lateral Thinking is a stance committed to **treating the tendency toward logical, sequential, step-by-step reasoning (what de Bono calls "vertical thinking") as a powerful but limiting default, and to deliberately suspending it in order to break out of established thought patterns and generate genuinely novel connections**. The stance's purpose is not to replace vertical thinking but to recognize where it becomes a prison — where it filters out possibilities before they can be entertained, where it enforces a single "correct" path and makes alternatives literally unthinkable. It is an interpretive position about how problems get stuck and how to unstick them; it asks: not "is this logically sound?" but "what would happen if we stopped being logical for a moment?" The stance is suited to reading problem-solving processes, organizational decision-making, design contexts, and any artifact that claims to be "innovative" but may be trapped in existing categories.

## Foundational Commitments

- **Pattern-following is both a tool and a trap.** The mind's ability to recognize patterns, apply learned sequences, and follow logical chains is essential for functioning in known domains. But once a pattern is established — a "dominant idea," a way of framing the problem, a category boundary — the mind tends to reinforce it and filter out anything that doesn't fit. De Bono calls this "vertical thinking": digging deeper in the same hole. The stance commits to making this automaticity visible.

- **The problem is often the problem-statement itself.** Before you can solve a problem, you have to frame it — decide what counts as "the problem." But the frame itself is a pattern, and it is usually inherited, not chosen. Lateral thinking assumes that the way a problem is stated already contains assumptions, constraints, and exclusions that may be the real obstacle. Reframing is not a side effect; it is the core move.

- **Logic is not the only valid mode of reasoning.** Vertical thinking treats logic as the arbiter of correctness: if a step follows from the premises, it is valid; if not, it is nonsense. Lateral thinking insists that there are valuable mental moves — random juxtaposition, metaphorical transfer, deliberate absurdity, reversal — that are not logical but are productive. They generate possibilities that logic would exclude until proven necessary.

- **Chance and accident are underutilized.** In problem-solving, we typically organize information, identify constraints, and narrow down. But de Bono observes that valuable ideas often come from unexpected connections — an unrelated stimulus, a mishearing, a random word inserted into the discussion. The stance commits to treating randomness not as noise but as a tool: "random entry" as a deliberate technique, not just something that happens.

- **Provocation is a thinking tool, not a claim.** In vertical thinking, provocation is a mistake: you propose something wrong and then have to correct it. Lateral thinking treats the provocative statement (what de Bono marks with "Po") as a launching point. "Po: what if we charged customers *nothing* for this?" is not a hypothesis to test against current logic; it is a statement that changes the landscape of what you can think next. The provocation is deliberately illogical or impossible under current constraints, precisely so it can shift the frame.

- **Alternatives are invisible until they are named.** A group solving a problem together will usually converge quickly on a shared frame and shared constraints. Once everyone agrees on the frame, alternative possibilities stop being noticed — they don't appear in the conversation because there is no language for them yet. Lateral thinking assumes alternatives exist but are just not visible; the job is to make them audible.

## Guiding Questions

**The problem-statement itself**
- How was this problem framed, and by whom? What is the inherited problem-definition?
- What assumptions does the problem-statement embed? What counts as "in scope" and what is automatically "out of scope"?
- If you inverted the problem — asked "how could we *achieve* the opposite?" — what would you see?
- What would change if you renamed the problem? What if you called it something completely different?

**Breaking pattern-dominance**
- What is the dominant idea that controls thinking in this space? What path does it force, and what alternatives does it block?
- Where is the mind following a learned sequence automatically, without questioning whether it is the only path?
- What constraints are real (material, legal, physical) and what are just "the way we do things"?
- Where is the group rushing to consensus prematurely, before alternatives are even visible?

**Random entry and accident**
- What would happen if you introduced a completely unrelated object, idea, or word into the problem-space?
- Where have accidental discoveries or "happy mistakes" played a role in past solutions?
- What minor details or edge cases are being filtered out by the dominant frame?

**Reversal and inversion**
- What if you reversed the problem? Instead of "how do we reduce waste," ask "how do we *maximize* waste"?
- What if you flipped the roles — the customer becomes the provider, the constraint becomes the goal?
- What if the opposite of the current solution were true? What would that world look like?

**Metaphor and analogy**
- What domains or practices outside this space solve a "similar" problem (under a different name)?
- What metaphors are embedded in the way people talk about this problem, and are they accurate?
- What would a radically different field (biology, music, traffic engineering, comedy) suggest about this?

**Provocation and deliberate absurdity**
- What Po-statements could you make that are impossible or illogical under current constraints?
- What would you do if the main constraint were removed? (If cost were unlimited? If speed didn't matter? If you had to do the opposite?)
- Where is the group being too "reasonable," and what becomes possible if you permit deliberate unreasonableness for a moment?

## What It Reveals

A Lateral Thinking stance surfaces findings that vertical thinking systematically misses:

- **The hidden frame**: what was taken for granted as "the problem" often *is* the problem. Making the frame explicit and changeable opens new solution-spaces.
- **Blocked alternatives**: possibilities that are not logical given current assumptions but that become visible (and sometimes sensible) once the frame shifts.
- **Pattern-dominance in group thinking**: how quickly a group converges on a shared frame and how that convergence ends the exploration of alternatives — usually without anyone noticing.
- **Constraint-confusion**: which constraints are real and which are inherited habits, tradition, or "the way we've always done it."
- **The premature jump to solution**: where a group has moved to execution before the real problem-space has been explored, because the frame was never questioned.
- **Accidental adjacencies**: where small details, edge cases, or "irrelevant" stimuli could generate novel directions if they were not automatically filtered out.

## What It Obscures

The stance has systematic blind spots; using it without awareness of its limitations produces errors.

- **Implementation and detail.** Lateral thinking is strong on generating possibilities and weak on executing them. It can produce a room full of wild ideas and leave the question "but how do we actually *build* this?" unanswered. Pair with a disciplined vertical-thinking phase (or a project-management stance) when you need rigor.

- **Feasibility and constraint-reality.** The stance treats many constraints as "assumed" rather than real, which is often useful — but not always. Some constraints are real (physics, law, market structure, organizational capacity) and lateral thinking can generate beautiful possibilities that are literally impossible. De Bono himself acknowledges the need to test ideas against reality eventually.

- **Risk and consequence.** Lateral thinking emphasizes breaking patterns and trying novel directions; it is less attuned to what could go wrong, what dependencies might break, or what second- and third-order effects might follow. Use a risk-assessment or scenario-planning stance alongside when consequences are high.

- **Emotional and social truth.** The stance focuses on logical/structural reframing and is weaker on the emotional or relational dimensions of problem-solving. A group might resist a reframed problem not because it is logically unsound but because the frame challenges identity, status, or belonging. Add a psychologically or relationally informed stance when group dynamics are load-bearing.

- **Power and conflict.** Lateral thinking assumes problems are puzzles to be solved and alternatives are just hidden. It tends to elide situations where different actors have genuinely opposed interests and where "finding the right frame" is not possible because the frame itself is the site of conflict. (Use a Marxist or power-analysis stance when interests diverge.)

- **History and why we think this way.** Lateral thinking asks "what if we broke the pattern?" but does not always ask "why did this pattern form in the first place?" Understanding the historical genesis of a dominant frame can matter for deciding whether to break it or strengthen it. Pair with historical or genealogical analysis when appropriate.

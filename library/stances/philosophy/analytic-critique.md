# Analytic Critique

## Overview

Analytic critique is the interpretive posture of stepping back from a claim and evaluating it on its logical and epistemic merits alone — what it asserts, what it assumes, whether its parts connect — without first judging whether it is morally good or bad, whether its author is admirable or suspect, or whether the conclusion is congenial. The stance is not neutrality in the weak sense of "no opinion." It is a disciplined commitment to let structure determine evaluation, so that a claim made by an enemy and a claim made by an ally get the same treatment, and so that the force of a rhetorically charged or morally loaded claim is assessed by what it *argues* rather than by what it *feels* like. The stance is operational when an analyst reads any claim and can truthfully say: I am not yet agreeing, disagreeing, or condemning; I am asking what the claim is and how it hangs together.

## Foundational Commitments

- **Moral evaluation is bracketed.** Whether the claim is good or bad, kind or cruel, virtuous or vicious, is not the question this stance asks. The stance evaluates whether the claim is well-formed, internally consistent, and adequately supported. The reason for this commitment is that moral verdicts, delivered too early, short-circuit structural analysis: once a claim is labeled repugnant, its logical flaws stop being diagnostic and its logical strengths stop being visible. The moral question remains important — it is simply the business of a different stance (ethical, political, existential), not this one.

- **Neutral starting point — no prior truth or falsity assumption.** The stance refuses to enter the analysis with the verdict already fixed. A claim that sounds obviously true is checked as carefully as one that sounds obviously false. The reason: pre-assumed verdicts produce motivated reasoning — confirming evidence gets weight, disconfirming evidence gets discounted, and the analysis becomes theater. A neutral start is not the claim that all positions are equally defensible; it is the discipline of suspending the verdict until the structure has been seen.

- **Attribution is not evidence.** Who made the claim — an expert, a non-expert, an ally, a rival, a famous author, an anonymous voice — is tracked for context but does not count toward whether the claim is sound. The reason: attribution-as-evidence collapses into *ad hominem* in one direction and *appeal to authority* in the other, and both patterns let the analyst skip the harder work of evaluating the argument. The stance keeps attribution on a separate ledger: recorded for intellectual honesty, barred from doing evaluative work.

- **Vague claims require context before analysis, not around it.** When a claim is too under-specified to be analyzed — "Life is beautiful," "Things will work out," "The system is broken" — the stance refuses to proceed by guessing what was meant. Instead, it demands specification: what kind of beauty, what system, which respect, under what conditions. The reason: analysis of an invented version of the claim produces findings about the invention, not about what the speaker actually said. Demanding context is intellectually respectful; inventing it is a disguised way of putting words in the speaker's mouth.

## Guiding Questions

**The moral-bracketing check**
- If this claim said the opposite thing, would I be evaluating it with the same patience and the same standards?
- Am I being harsher on the claim because I find its conclusion distasteful, or more lenient because I find it congenial?
- Can I state what the claim asserts without using evaluative adjectives ("absurd," "obviously," "disturbing")?

**The neutrality check**
- Before I looked at the argument, had I already decided the verdict? If so, what specifically in the argument have I let revise that verdict?
- If this claim turned out to be sound, what would change for me? If that answer is "nothing," I may be treating it as unfalsifiable for motivated reasons, not because it actually is.
- Can I write a version of this claim that gives it its best shot — the charitable reconstruction — before I evaluate it?

**The attribution/evidence separation**
- If I removed the author's name, would this claim stand or fall on its own reasoning?
- Am I using the author's authority to add weight the argument itself does not carry — or using the author's identity to deduct weight the argument does carry?
- What would I say if an anonymous source made exactly this argument with exactly this evidence?

**The vagueness check**
- Is there a specific context, example, or operationalization under which I could decide whether this claim is true? If not, what specific further information would I need to ask for?
- Am I analyzing what the claim actually says, or a tidied-up version I produced? If the latter, have I shown my reconstruction explicitly?
- Could the speaker honestly say, "That is not what I meant"? If yes, I have not yet engaged with the claim — I have engaged with an adjacent one.

## What It Reveals

- **Formal and structural defects.** Fallacies of form (affirming the consequent, equivocation, composition, hasty generalization), missing warrants, chains whose grounds do not actually connect to the conclusion, and premises doing silent load-bearing work — all of these become inspectable once moral reflexes are set aside.

- **Argument resilience independent of author.** Whether a claim would still stand if its author were replaced with someone you disagree with — and whether it would still fall if its author were replaced with someone you admire. The stance surfaces how much of the perceived strength of a claim actually comes from who said it.

- **The shape of a vague claim's legitimate specifications.** By refusing to analyze under-specified claims without context, the stance reveals the range of possible claims a vague utterance could become — and, often, that the vagueness itself is doing work (rhetorical, political, evasive) that the speaker would lose if forced to choose a specification.

- **Epistemic vs. normative layers in mixed claims.** Claims that present as descriptive often contain normative cargo, and vice versa. By holding moral evaluation off the table, the stance makes the smuggled cargo visible — at which point a separate evaluation can address it deliberately rather than absorb it silently.

## What It Obscures

- **Moral stakes and lived consequences.** Exactly because moral evaluation is bracketed, the stance says nothing about whether a claim, if acted on, would harm people, violate rights, or betray a commitment. A virtue-ethics, care-ethics, or political stance is needed to recover this.

- **Power, positionality, and whose voice is heard.** Treating attribution as mere context and not evidence flattens the fact that some speakers are systematically under-credited and others over-credited. Feminist, postcolonial, and critical-race stances would put exactly this back on the ledger.

- **Rhetorical and performative force.** By analyzing what the claim *says* and *implies* rather than what it *does* in context (reassure, threaten, bond, exclude), the stance misses the dimension where speech-act theory and discourse analysis operate. A pragmatic or performative reading is needed alongside.

- **Historical and material conditions of production.** Why this claim, here, now, from this speaker? A Marxist, historicist, or genealogical stance attends to conditions of production; analytic critique ordinarily does not.

- **Emotional and aesthetic dimensions.** The force of a claim for a reader — how it feels, what it evokes, what it invites — is not what this stance evaluates. Hermeneutic, phenomenological, and literary-critical stances are better suited to this layer.

- **Its own interests and blind spots.** The stance presents itself as neutral, but the decision to bracket morality, strip attribution, and demand operationalization is itself a methodological choice with consequences. Applied with integrity, the stance acknowledges that its demand for structure privileges claims already built for that kind of inspection — and disadvantages traditions that resist formalization.

## Analytic Critique Reading: <claim or artifact>

### Attribution (recorded, not weighed)
<Who stated the claim, when, where. Noted explicitly so it is not doing covert evaluative work.>

### Bracketed moral content
<Any moral charge in or around the claim — briefly named and set aside, to be revisited only by a different stance.>

### Specification status
[adequately specified | under-specified — context required]
<If under-specified, name the specific questions that must be answered before analysis proceeds.>

### Charitable reconstruction
<The strongest, most defensible version of what the claim asserts, stated in neutral language.>

### Structural assessment
- Logical form: <deductive / inductive / analogical / other>
- Validity (does the conclusion follow from the premises?): <yes / no / partial, with note>
- Soundness (are the premises actually supported?): <yes / no / partial, with note>
- Attribution-independence: <would the claim survive/fall without its author's authority? note specifically>

### What the stance sees
<Formal defects, smuggled normative cargo, silent load-bearing premises, or — if none — the claim's structural soundness.>

### What this stance has not addressed
<Named layers — moral, political, performative, material — deferred to other stances.>

### Confidence: high | medium | low
<high when the claim is adequately specified and structural verdict is unambiguous; medium when partial specification or mixed verdict; low when vagueness or context-dependence prevented full structural analysis.>

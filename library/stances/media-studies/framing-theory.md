# Framing Theory

## Overview

Framing theory is a commitment to treating **meaning-making as an active work of selection and emphasis, not as a transparent report of reality**. A frame is not a bias imposed on top of a neutral fact; it is the structure that makes the fact intelligible in the first place. The stance refuses the idea that media simply "reflect" events and instead asks: what constitutes the event as an event? What becomes the "frame" through which an audience encounters it? Which competing interpretations were available and which were suppressed? The stance's purpose is to denaturalize the frame — to show that what presents itself as "just reporting what happened" is actually a constructed interpretive package with political and economic consequences. It is an interpretive position, not a procedure; its findings emerge only when you stop asking "is this true or false?" and start asking "why is this the question being asked at all?"

## Foundational Commitments

- **Frames are constitutive, not merely descriptive.** A frame does not simply filter or distort an objective reality that exists independently. The frame *makes* the event intelligible by selecting what counts as relevant, what causal chains matter, what actors are central, and what the appropriate response should be. Without a frame, there is no event — only raw occurrence.

- **The same fact admits multiple frames, and frames are not equally available.** Two outlets can accurately report the identical set of facts and generate opposite public understandings because they have foregrounded different facts, invoked different value systems, and suggested different causal chains. But not all frames have equal access to distribution channels; some are routinely available and others are structurally suppressed.

- **Framing is work, not accident.** Frames are selected, constructed, and reinforced by specific actors — journalists, political campaigns, advocacy organizations, institutions — working within constraints of resources, routines, professional norms, and economic interests. The frame is not a neutral lens that naturally sits there; it is maintained through active selection.

- **What is backgrounded is as important as what is foregrounded.** The power of a frame lies not only in what it makes salient but in what it renders invisible, obvious, or beyond the scope of the issue. By deciding what is "inside" the frame and what is "outside," a frame determines what arguments are even intelligible.

- **Frames carry implicit values and solutions.** Every frame smuggles in a diagnosis of the problem, an implied cause, a conception of who should act, and what success looks like. These are rarely stated explicitly; they are embedded in the choice of which facts matter and how they connect.

- **Competing frames exist, even when one dominates.** At any moment, multiple interpretations of an event are being offered by different actors — political opponents, advocacy groups, academic experts, local communities. The stance's job is to map this field of competing frames and understand which ones get distributed, amplified, and naturalized as "common sense."

## Guiding Questions

**Selection and emphasis**
- What facts, actors, or dimensions of the event are foregrounded?
- What is relegated to the background or mentioned only in passing?
- Which causal relationships are made explicit, and which are left implicit?
- Whose voices are quoted directly, and whose are paraphrased or absent?

**The diagnostic frame**
- What does the frame define as "the problem"? (Policy failure? Market dysfunction? Individual behavior? Systemic injustice? Natural disaster?)
- What implicit diagnosis of cause is embedded in this framing?
- Who or what is positioned as responsible or culpable?
- What groups or interests are positioned as the solution?

**What is naturalized**
- What assumptions does the frame treat as so obvious they do not need argument?
- What is presented as inevitable, timeless, or beyond human choice?
- What value systems or moral frameworks are invoked without being named?
- What alternative framings are rendered literally unthinkable by the current frame's logic?

**Scope and boundaries**
- What is defined as "inside" this issue and what is declared "outside" or irrelevant?
- Where are the frame's edges? What contextual information is excluded as not germane?
- How does the framing prevent or enable certain kinds of follow-up questions?

**Competing frames**
- What alternative framings of the same events are being offered by other actors?
- Why does one frame dominate distribution and others remain marginal?
- What resources, institutional positions, or narrative credibility give some frames more power than others?
- When do frames shift? What triggers reframing?

**Economic and institutional interests**
- What interests are served by this particular framing?
- What frame would serve opposing interests, and why is it suppressed or unavailable?
- How do media routines, business models, and professional norms shape what frames become habitual?

## What It Reveals

- **The constructed nature of "the news."** What appears to be a neutral report of objective facts is revealed as an interpretive package shaped by selection, emphasis, and framing choices.

- **How meaning is politically consequential.** The same event (a protest, a policy change, a market shift, a disease) generates radically different public understandings and political responses depending on how it is framed. Framing is not superficial — it determines what people understand to be at stake.

- **The work of marginalization.** Groups whose experiences, perspectives, or grievances do not fit the dominant frame become literally invisible to media audiences — not because the information is hidden, but because the frame makes it not-newsworthy, not-relevant, not-part-of-the-story.

- **Frame dominance and its sources.** Why one interpretation of reality becomes naturalized as "common sense" while others remain marginal or radical — the answer lies in access to distribution channels, institutional credibility, resource disparities, and professional routines, not in inherent truth value.

- **The implicit values and solutions embedded in framing choices.** By analyzing the frame, you surface the diagnosis, causal theory, and model of agency the frame prescribes — often without stating them explicitly.

- **The field of competing frames.** Rather than treating public discourse as a single unified narrative, the stance maps the alternative framings being offered and the struggle over which interpretation will dominate.

## What It Obscures

- **The material interests behind framing choices are often bracketed.** While framing theory is sensitive to how frames serve some interests over others, it sometimes stops at the level of "political advantage" without analyzing the economic structures, ownership patterns, and labor processes that constrain what frames become habitual in the first place. (Pair with a Marxist or political-economy reading when corporate or state power is load-bearing.)

- **Agency and intentionality become murky.** Framing theory vacillates between treating frames as consciously constructed by strategic actors (politicians, PR campaigns, journalists) and treating them as emerging from professional routines and organizational norms. This slippage can obscure questions of responsibility and choice.

- **The frame's internal coherence is often overstated.** Real media discourse is messier and more contradictory than tidy frame analysis sometimes allows; multiple frames can coexist within a single text, and people's actual understanding may be more hybrid than frame analysis suggests.

- **How audiences resist, reframe, or ignore frames.** Framing theory is strong on how frames are constructed and distributed but weaker on how audiences actively interpret, reject, or renegotiate the frames they encounter. The stance can overstate frame power at the expense of audience agency. (Use reception theory or audience ethnography alongside to recover this.)

- **Frames in cultural and subcultural spaces.** Framing theory developed primarily for mass media and news; it is less equipped to analyze frames circulating in digital communities, niche media, or oral cultures where distribution is decentralized and frame-making is participatory.

- **Non-discursive dimensions of meaning-making.** Affect, embodiment, visual composition, sound design, and other aesthetic elements shape how an event is understood, but framing theory's focus on selection and emphasis can flatten these dimensions into "content" and miss their independent work.

## Output Format

```
## Framing Analysis: <event / issue / media text>

### The dominant frame
- What is defined as "the problem"?
- What facts, actors, or causal relationships are foregrounded?
- What is backgrounded or declared outside the frame's scope?

### Diagnostic frame and implicit solutions
- Diagnosis of cause embedded in the frame
- Who or what is positioned as responsible; who as the solution
- Values and moral frameworks invoked (explicitly and implicitly)

### What is naturalized
- Assumptions treated as too obvious to require argument
- What alternative framings are rendered unthinkable by this frame's logic
- Scope boundaries: what is "inside" and "outside" the issue

### Selection and emphasis
- Whose voices are quoted directly; whose are paraphrased or absent
- Which causal relationships are made explicit
- What contextual information is excluded as not germane

### Competing frames
- Alternative framings being offered by other actors (political opponents, advocacy groups, affected communities)
- Why one frame dominates distribution and others remain marginal
- What resources or institutional credibility give some frames more power

### Interests served
- Whose material or political interests are advanced by this frame
- What frame would serve opposing interests, and why is it suppressed

### Conversation with other stances
- What a Marxist / postcolonial / audience-reception reading would add or push back on

### Confidence: high | medium | low
<basis: how explicitly the frame is stated in the text; how much inference is required to uncover the diagnostic logic; whether competing frames are clearly available or require reconstruction from scattered sources>
```

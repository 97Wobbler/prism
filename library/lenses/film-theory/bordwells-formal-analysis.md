# Bordwell's Formal Analysis

## Overview

Bordwell's method treats film as a system where narration (how story information is revealed), style (visual and sonic technique), and form (structural organization) collaborate to produce meaning. Rather than interpreting a film as an expression of authorial intent or cultural symbolism, this lens asks: what *procedures* does the film use to guide the viewer's attention and comprehension? Practitioners apply it to understand how a film works mechanically — the decisions that make a particular story intelligible — and to identify gaps between what the film appears to claim and what its formal choices actually accomplish.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Identify the Narrative Skeleton

1. **Extract the story in plot order.** Write a three-sentence summary of what happens: opening state, central conflict, resolution. Do not interpret; report only causality.

2. **Identify the narrative gaps.** What information is *withheld* from the viewer? What is revealed out of chronological order? List each instance (e.g., "The protagonist's past is withheld until act 3," "The antagonist's motivation is revealed only after their first scene").

3. **Map the narration mode.** For each major scene, note whether the viewer is:
   - **Knowledgeable** (knows more than the protagonist)
   - **Aligned** (knows what the protagonist knows, when they know it)
   - **Restricted** (knows less than the protagonist; the protagonist hides information from camera)
   - **Omniscient** (has access to multiple characters' perspectives/knowledge simultaneously)

### Phase 2 — Analyze Stylistic Systems

4. **Camera and blocking.** For 3–5 key scenes, note:
   - Shot scale and angle (close-up, medium, wide; high, level, low angle)
   - Camera movement (static, pan, track, handheld)
   - Depth of field (shallow or deep focus)
   - Framing choice (what is included/excluded from the frame edge)
   - How blocking positions characters relative to each other and the camera

   Ask: *Does this choice guide attention toward or away from narrative information? Does it align with or contradict the narration mode?*

5. **Editing rhythm and continuity.** For the same scenes:
   - Shot duration (average length of individual shots)
   - Cut pattern (cuts on action, dialogue, or motivated by shot content?)
   - Transitions (cuts, dissolves, fades, or other bridges between shots)
   - Match cuts or spatial discontinuities

   Ask: *Does the editing pace match the scene's informational density? Do cuts clarify or obscure spatial relationships?*

6. **Lighting and color.** Note:
   - Key light position and intensity
   - Color palette (saturated, desaturated, warm, cool, monochromatic)
   - Practical lights vs. motivated lighting
   - Changes in lighting across the film or within scenes

   Ask: *How does lighting affect visual hierarchy? Does it reinforce or complicate narrative meaning?*

7. **Sound and music.** Document:
   - Diegetic sound (exists in the story world: dialogue, ambient sound, radio, etc.)
   - Non-diegetic sound (exists only for the viewer: score, voice-over narration)
   - Dialogue delivery (naturalistic, stylized, overlapped, sparse)
   - Sonic motifs or leitmotifs
   - Silence and its narrative function

   Ask: *What information does sound provide that image does not? Does the score align with or undercut the visual narrative?*

### Phase 3 — Formal-Narrative Integration

8. **Identify formal reinforcement.** Find moments where style and narration align:
   - The camera mirrors a character's knowledge or ignorance.
   - Editing pace matches emotional or plot intensity.
   - Lighting or color marks a narrative turning point.
   - Sound design reveals what the image conceals.

9. **Identify formal contradiction.** Find moments where style undermines or complicates narrative:
   - The camera shows something the narration claims is hidden.
   - Editing creates ambiguity where the plot is clear (or vice versa).
   - Style suggests meaning the dialogue denies.
   - Non-diegetic elements (score, voice-over) impose an interpretation the images resist.

10. **Trace the meaning effect.** For each contradiction or reinforcement, write: *If the film had made the opposite formal choice, what would the viewer understand differently?*

### Phase 4 — Synthesis

11. **State the film's formal strategy.** In 2–3 sentences, describe the dominant approach: Does the film clarify story information through style, or does it withhold and complicate? Does it guide the viewer toward a stable interpretation, or does it encourage alternative readings?

12. **Identify what formal analysis reveals that thematic interpretation misses.** Thematic reading might claim "the film is about isolation." Formal analysis asks: *How does the film *show* isolation through camera placement, editing, and sound?* Write the delta.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Story skeleton is causal and under 150 words | Y/N |
| All narrative gaps are identified (at least 3) | Y/N |
| Narration mode is mapped for at least 3 major scenes | Y/N |
| 3–5 key scenes are analyzed across all five stylistic systems (camera, editing, lighting, sound, blocking) | Y/N |
| At least one formal reinforcement and one formal contradiction are identified | Y/N |
| Each finding includes a counterfactual ("if the opposite choice were made...") | Y/N |
| Formal strategy statement is distinct from thematic interpretation | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All narration modes are "aligned." This suggests the analysis missed scenes of restricted or omniscient narration, or the film genuinely offers no stylistic variation (rare).
- Stylistic analysis becomes interpretive ("the blue lighting symbolizes sadness"). Record what you see; save interpretation for Phase 3.
- Formal contradictions are explained away as "intentional ambiguity" without specifying what the ambiguity *does* to viewer comprehension.
- The formal strategy is identical to the thematic statement. Formal analysis should reveal *how* the theme is constructed, not just *that* it exists.
- No notation of narration mode changes. If a scene shifts from aligned to restricted, or restricted to omniscient, note it — these shifts are often crucial.
- Sound analysis is absent or generic. Many analysts default to visual analysis and treat sound as decoration.

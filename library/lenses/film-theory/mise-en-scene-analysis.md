# Mise-en-scène Analysis

## Overview
Mise-en-scène — literally "putting on stage" — is the sum of all visual elements within a frame: setting, props, costume, lighting, blocking (actor placement and movement), and camera position. Rather than treating these as decoration, this lens treats them as a primary language through which a film communicates. Practitioners use it when they need to explain *why* a shot feels a certain way or what a director is asserting through visual composition alone, independent of dialogue or plot.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Identify the Frame

1. **Select a single shot.** Not a scene—a shot. Define its boundaries: where it begins (cut-in or transition) and where it ends (cut-out). Write the timecode or scene marker.

2. **Watch it three times.** First, without note-taking. Second, note what draws your eye. Third, note what *doesn't* draw your eye (negative space, off-screen area, elements in soft focus).

3. **Describe the frame in neutral terms.** Avoid interpretation. Avoid metaphor. Answer these questions in order:
   - What is the physical space? (Interior/exterior, scale, recognizable location or abstract?)
   - What light sources are visible or implied? (Natural, practical, key light direction, color temperature?)
   - What objects are in the frame? List them. Include their placement (foreground, mid-ground, background; left, center, right).
   - How are actors positioned? (Standing, sitting, facing which direction, distance from camera, distance from each other?)
   - What is in focus and what is soft? (Depth of field, plane of focus, what is sharp?)
   - Is the camera moving? (If yes: pan, tilt, dolly, crane? In which direction? At what speed relative to the action?)

### Phase 2 — Analyze Spatial Relationships

4. **Map power and hierarchy in the frame.** Identify:
   - **Dominant figure or object.** What occupies the most visual weight? (Size, brightness, centrality, sharpness, color saturation.)
   - **Secondary figures.** In what order do the eye move after the dominant?
   - **Vectors.** Trace the lines created by actor gaze, body orientation, arm direction, edges of set pieces. Do they lead toward or away from the dominant figure? Do they create tension (crossing vectors) or harmony (parallel vectors)?
   - **Depth layers.** How many planes of action are there? Are foreground and background balanced, or is one privileged?

5. **Examine the color and tonal palette.** Without naming the emotions it evokes, answer:
   - What colors dominate? (Warm, cool, saturated, desaturated, monochromatic, high contrast?)
   - What is the brightest element? What is darkest?
   - Is there a color that appears only in the protagonist's costume or only in the setting? (Isolation vs. integration.)
   - Is the image overall high-key (bright, even, open) or low-key (dark, shadowed, closed)?

6. **Document blocking and movement.** If actors move:
   - What is the trajectory? (Toward camera, away, lateral, curved, hesitant, purposeful?)
   - Does movement enter or exit the frame, or stay within it?
   - Do multiple actors move in relation to each other? (Convergence, divergence, mirroring, opposition?)
   - At what moment in the shot does movement occur? (Immediate, delayed, at cut-out?)

### Phase 3 — Interpret Intentionality

7. **Ask: What does this arrangement *show* that the story could have shown differently?** For each major choice identified above (lighting, object placement, blocking, camera position), ask why *that* choice and not an alternative:
   - If the light is from the side, why not frontal? What does side-lighting reveal that frontal would hide?
   - If the actor is small in the frame, why not close? What does distance assert?
   - If the background is soft, why not sharp? What is the director protecting us from or directing us toward?
   - If the actor's gaze is off-screen, why not at the camera or at another character?

8. **Identify what is absent or obscured.** What is:
   - Off-screen but implied (by gaze direction, sound, actor reaction)?
   - Out of focus (deliberately soft, not accidental)?
   - In shadow or at frame edge?
   - Never shown in this shot but shown in adjacent shots (cutaway denial)?
   Write a sentence about each. Absence is intentional too.

9. **Connect spatial choices to narrative or thematic claim.** Based only on the visual analysis (not dialogue, not plot), write one sentence: "This arrangement asserts that..." Examples:
   - "This arrangement asserts that the protagonist is trapped by the space around them."
   - "This arrangement asserts dominance through scale and lighting, not through centrality."
   - "This arrangement asserts that the two characters are emotionally distant despite physical proximity."

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Single shot selected and timecode noted | Y/N |
| Frame described in neutral terms (no interpretation in Phase 1) | Y/N |
| All elements in Phase 1 (space, light, objects, actors, focus, camera) answered | Y/N |
| Dominant figure/object identified with visual justification | Y/N |
| At least three vectors (gaze, orientation, movement) traced | Y/N |
| Color/tonal palette described without emotional language | Y/N |
| For each major choice in Phase 3, an alternative was posed and rejected | Y/N |
| At least two elements identified as intentional absence or obscuring | Y/N |
| Final assertion relates visual choice to a claim (not just description) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Phase 1 description includes words like "lonely," "tense," "hopeful." These are interpretations, not observations. Start over with neutral language.
- Every element in the frame is analyzed as equally important. Real mise-en-scène has hierarchy. If everything is dominant, nothing is.
- The final assertion in Phase 3 restates plot ("the character is sad because their friend left") rather than what the frame *shows* independent of narrative ("the frame isolates the character through scale and lighting").
- No absent elements identified. Every frame hides something. If you only describe what is visible, you are not reading mise-en-scène.
- Alternatives in Phase 3 are not genuine ("Why not close-up? Because the scene requires a wide shot"). Reframe: "Why wide instead of close? Because distance asserts isolation where close-up would assert vulnerability."
- The analysis contradicts itself between Phase 1 (neutral) and Phase 3 (interpretation). If the light is truly even and frontal, you cannot later claim side-lighting creates drama.

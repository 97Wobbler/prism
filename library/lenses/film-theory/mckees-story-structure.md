# McKee's Story Structure

## Overview
McKee's framework treats story as a chain of causally linked events that force a protagonist to change. A story consists of an inciting incident (the disruption), progressive complications (rising stakes and reversals), climax (the protagonist's final choice under maximum pressure), and resolution (the new normal). Practitioners use this when a script feels shapeless, when audiences disengage midway, or when a story's emotional arc doesn't land—it forces the analyst to ask whether the events actually *caused* the protagonist to transform, or whether change was imposed externally.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Extract the Story's Events

1. **Watch or read the full artifact (script, film, novel section).** Do not summarize or rely on memory; work from the text itself.

2. **List every event that materially changes the protagonist's situation or knowledge.** An event is something that happens *to* the protagonist or something the protagonist *does* that has a consequence. Minor character beats, exposition, or scenes that repeat earlier information do not count. Write each as a complete sentence with subject and consequence.
   - Bad: "Mary is sad."
   - Good: "Mary learns her partner is leaving, forcing her to choose between following or staying."

3. **Arrange these events in chronological order.** Number them. You should have 6–15 core events for a feature-length narrative; fewer suggests structural sparseness; more suggests unfocused or repetitive plotting.

### Phase 2 — Test Causality

4. **For each event pair (N → N+1), write the causal link.** Ask: "Did event N *force* event N+1 to happen, or did N+1 happen anyway?"
   - Tight causal link: "Because Mary learned her partner is leaving, she decided to confront them." (N causes N+1)
   - Loose causal link: "Mary learned her partner is leaving. Meanwhile, her boss offered her a promotion." (N and N+1 are parallel; no causal pressure)
   - Write whether the link is tight, loose, or missing.

5. **Mark each event as one of:**
   - **Inciting Incident** — the disruption that destabilizes the protagonist's world (usually event 1–3)
   - **Complication** — events that raise stakes, introduce new obstacles, or reverse prior assumptions
   - **Crisis** — the point where the protagonist's options narrow to an impossible choice (no good answer exists)
   - **Climax** — the protagonist's final, irreversible choice or action under maximum pressure
   - **Resolution** — the new equilibrium showing what the protagonist's choice cost and what it enabled

6. **Identify the "controlling idea" (theme).** This is the answer to the question: "What must the protagonist learn or sacrifice to survive?" State it as a single sentence. 
   - Example: "Courage means acting despite fear, not without it."
   - If you cannot state the controlling idea, the story lacks thematic coherence.

### Phase 3 — Measure Protagonist Agency

7. **For each complication, note whether the protagonist *caused* it or *suffered* it.**
   - Caused: the protagonist made a choice that escalated the conflict
   - Suffered: external circumstance, another character's action, or luck determined the event
   - Write the ratio (e.g., "4 caused, 6 suffered").
   - Stories with >75% suffering tend to feel passive; <25% suffering can feel implausible (no external resistance).

8. **At the climax, ask: Does the protagonist have a real choice, or is the outcome predetermined?**
   - Real choice: two or more options exist, each with genuine consequences, and the protagonist selects one
   - Predetermined: the protagonist's action is the only logical move, or the outcome is already decided
   - If predetermined, the story is not a climax but a confirmation.

### Phase 4 — Check Thematic Alignment

9. **Reread the climactic choice.** Does it prove or disprove the controlling idea?
   - Strong alignment: the climax directly tests the controlling idea; the choice resolves it
   - Weak alignment: the climax happens but feels unrelated to the thematic question
   - Misalignment: the climax contradicts the controlling idea (rare; usually unintentional)

10. **Examine the resolution.** Does the protagonist's world reflect the cost of the climactic choice?
    - Earned resolution: the protagonist changed; the world or their circumstance changed to match that change
    - Unearned resolution: the protagonist is restored to the original state despite the climax, or the cost is glossed over
    - Hollow resolution: external forces (rescue, luck, retcon) undo the climax's consequences

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| 6–15 core events extracted and numbered | Y/N |
| Each event is written as a complete sentence (subject + consequence) | Y/N |
| Causal links between all consecutive event pairs are identified (tight/loose/missing) | Y/N |
| All five structural beats (inciting incident, complication, crisis, climax, resolution) are present | Y/N |
| Controlling idea is stated in one sentence | Y/N |
| Protagonist agency ratio is calculated (caused vs. suffered events) | Y/N |
| Climax contains a real (non-predetermined) choice | Y/N |
| Resolution reflects the cost of the climactic choice | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Causal links are mostly "loose" or "missing." The story is a sequence of incidents, not a chain; events feel arbitrary.
- The climax occurs but the protagonist did not cause it (external rescue, coincidence, or another character's action resolves the conflict). The protagonist is a passenger in their own story.
- No controlling idea can be stated. The story has plot but no thematic spine; it answers "what happened?" but not "what does it mean?"
- The protagonist's ratio of suffered to caused events is >90%. The story is a complaint or lament, not a narrative arc.
- The resolution restores the protagonist to their original state despite the climax. The story teaches nothing and costs nothing; it is escapism pretending to be drama.
- Crisis and climax are absent or collapse into a single scene. Without the narrowing of options (crisis) before the choice (climax), there is no pressure and no catharsis.
- The protagonist's climactic choice directly contradicts their established values without explanation. Inconsistency, not growth, signals a structural failure.

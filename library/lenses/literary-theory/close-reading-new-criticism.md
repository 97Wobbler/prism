# Close Reading / New Criticism

## Overview

Close reading suspends external context (author biography, historical moment, reader psychology) and treats the literary text as a self-contained linguistic object. Meaning emerges from the interplay of form, structure, diction, imagery, and rhetorical devices—not from what the author intended or what happened outside the page. Practitioners use this when they need to account for how language actually works in a poem, story, or passage, and when received summaries ("this poem is about love") obscure the specific verbal evidence that creates the effect.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Prepare the Text

1. **Transcribe or obtain a clean, modernized copy of the text.** Use a reliable scholarly edition if available. Note typography, punctuation, line breaks, stanzas, or paragraph divisions exactly as printed. Do not "correct" archaic spelling or grammar without recording the original.

2. **Mark the text in layers.** Use separate passes, one per layer:
   - Layer 1: Circle or highlight all words you do not recognize or that seem unusual in context.
   - Layer 2: Mark all repetitions of words, sounds, or images.
   - Layer 3: Bracket all shifts in tone, perspective, or tense.
   - Layer 4: Underline any direct address, questions, or command forms.

3. **Establish sentence and syntactic boundaries.** Number sentences or major clauses. Note inversions, fragmentary constructions, or sentences that span multiple lines or stanzas. Record the *shape* of the syntax, not just the grammar.

### Phase 2 — Map Formal Elements

4. **Identify the governing form.** Is this free verse, blank verse, rhymed couplets, a sonnet, prose? State the rule (if any) that the text follows. Note where the text obeys or breaks that rule.

5. **Catalog sound devices.** For each passage, record:
   - Alliteration (repeated initial consonants: "bare, broke, born")
   - Assonance (repeated vowel sounds: "time, find, mind")
   - Consonance (repeated internal/final consonants: "book, back")
   - Rhyme (end-rhyme, internal rhyme, slant rhyme)
   - Meter (if regular): mark stressed and unstressed syllables across 3-4 representative lines
   - Record *any* deviation from established meter as a local event to explain

6. **Map imagery and symbol chains.** Create a table:
   - Column 1: Image or symbol (e.g., "water," "light," "stone")
   - Column 2: Every occurrence in the text (cite line or page)
   - Column 3: Context (what surrounds it, what modifies it)
   - Column 4: Mutation or shift (does the image's valence, scale, or quality change across occurrences?)

7. **Identify tension and paradox.** Scan for:
   - Oxymoron (contradictory terms: "living death," "sweet sorrow")
   - Paradox (apparently impossible but logically true: "He died to save us")
   - Irony (statement whose literal meaning contradicts its intended meaning)
   - Ambiguity (genuine multiplicity of meaning, not just vagueness)
   For each, record the exact words and the readings it permits.

### Phase 3 — Trace Argument and Structure

8. **Outline the logical or emotional progression.** Divide the text into sections (stanzas, paragraphs, scenes). For each section, write a single sentence describing its primary assertion, image, or emotional state. String these together to see the arc.

9. **Identify structural turning points.** Where does tone, scale, perspective, or imagery shift sharply? What formal or linguistic feature marks the turn (e.g., "But," a blank line, a change from question to assertion, a switch from first to third person)?

10. **Trace syntax across line or paragraph breaks.** Does a sentence end mid-line, forcing the reader forward? Does a line-break interrupt a phrase, creating suspense or emphasis? Record the *effect* of each enjambment or pause.

### Phase 4 — Synthesize and Argue

11. **Propose a thesis about how form creates meaning.** Do not argue "the poem is about X" (external meaning). Instead argue "the text creates the *experience* of X through the deployment of Y formal features." Example:
    - Weak: "This poem expresses the speaker's grief."
    - Strong: "The poem enacts grief through syntax: short, fragmented sentences in the first half give way to a single 12-line sentence in the second half, forcing the reader to hold accumulated images before release, mirroring the speaker's movement from paralyzed shock to acceptance."

12. **Identify the governing paradox or irony.** Most literary texts enact a tension that does not fully resolve. Name it: What does the text *say* versus what does it *do*? What does it seem to *want* versus what it *achieves*?

13. **Test counter-readings.** For each major claim, try its opposite. If the text supports both readings, you have found ambiguity, not error. Record ambiguities without collapsing them.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| All formal layers (lexicon, sound, image, syntax, structure) were marked | Y/N |
| Image chain table populated with ≥3 images and ≥10 total occurrences | Y/N |
| At least 2 paradoxes, ironies, or ambiguities identified and quoted | Y/N |
| Thesis connects form to meaning without reference to author intent or historical context | Y/N |
| Syntax and enjambment analyzed in at least 2 passages | Y/N |
| At least one alternative reading proposed and assessed | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All analysis is thematic ("this is about love"). No formal features (word choice, line break, sound) are cited.
- The thesis invokes author biography ("the poet was sad, so...") or historical fact ("in 1945, when this was written..."). Close reading requires suspension of external context.
- Paradox or irony is mentioned but treated as a flaw: "The speaker says X but doesn't mean it." In literary analysis, contradiction is often the *point*.
- Every image is treated as a symbol with a fixed meaning ("water = rebirth"). Images mutate. The analysis must track *how* and *why*.
- Sound devices are listed but never connected to meaning. "Alliteration occurs on line 5" is observation, not argument.
- The counter-reading was a strawman, not a genuine alternative. A real counter-reading changes which formal features you weight or which passages you emphasize.

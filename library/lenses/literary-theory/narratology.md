# Narratology (Genette)

## Overview

Narratology separates the *events that occur* (story) from the *manner and sequence in which they are presented* (discourse). The gap between these two layers reveals how an author manipulates time, viewpoint, and information to shape meaning. Practitioners use this lens when a narrative feels evasive, when its timeline seems deliberately obscured, or when the emotional impact seems disconnected from the plot events themselves. The discipline is distinguishing *what happened* from *what we are shown and in what order*.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Extract Story and Discourse

1. **State the narrative artifact.** Identify the text, film, game, or other work you are analyzing. Write the medium and form in one sentence.

2. **Extract the story (fabula).** Arrange all plot events in chronological order, from earliest to latest, stripping away narrative voice, flashbacks, and gaps. If events are ambiguous or unknown (by design), mark them as `[unclear]`. The result should be a timeline that makes logical sense regardless of how the work presents it.
   - Example: In *The Great Gatsby*, the story includes Gatsby and Daisy's past affair (5 years before the novel opens), arranged before the novel's present-day events.

3. **Extract the discourse (sjuzhet).** Record the exact sequence in which information reaches the reader/viewer. Note where narrative time slows down (scene, dialogue, description) versus speeds up (summary, ellipsis). Mark any event that is *withheld entirely* as `[omitted]`.
   - Example: *The Great Gatsby* opens in the narrative present and gradually reveals the past affair through dialogue and exposition, not in chronological order.

### Phase 2 — Measure Temporal Manipulation

4. **Map order deviations.** For each major event, note whether it appears in the discourse *before*, *after*, or *simultaneously* with its chronological position in the story. Create a simple table:
   
   | Event | Story position | Discourse position | Type of shift |
   |---|---|---|---|
   | <event> | <when it really happened> | <when we learn of it> | Analepsis (flashback) / Prolepsis (flashforward) / In-order |

5. **Measure duration.** For 3-5 significant scenes, compare story-time (how much time passes in the events) to discourse-time (how many pages/minutes of text). Mark as:
   - **Isochronous:** 1 hour of events = 1 page of text (roughly proportional)
   - **Stretched:** 1 hour of events = 5 pages of text (scene, slowed down)
   - **Compressed:** 1 hour of events = 1 sentence (summary, skipped)

6. **Identify gaps and ellipses.** What periods of story-time are *not shown* in the discourse? Why might the narrator have skipped them? Mark whether each gap seems:
   - **Necessary** (too mundane to narrate; everyone accepts the skip)
   - **Suspicious** (information is hidden; creates mystery or unreliability)
   - **Structural** (the gap marks a narrative boundary or time jump)

### Phase 3 — Analyze Focalization

7. **Identify the focalizer for each section.** Who has access to information at each moment? Mark sections as:
   - **Zero focalization:** Omniscient narrator knows everything, including characters' private thoughts (example: *Pride and Prejudice*)
   - **Internal focalization:** Limited to one character's perspective and knowledge (example: *The Great Gatsby*, mostly through Nick Carraway)
   - **External focalization:** Narrator sees only externals, never enters any character's mind (example: *The Iceman Cometh*)

8. **Track focalization shifts.** Does the narrative perspective shift between characters or chapters? Create a timeline:
   
   | Section | Focalizer | Knowledge limit | Emotional distance to events |
   |---|---|---|---|
   | Ch. 1-3 | Nick Carraway | What Nick witnesses or is told | Observational, growing skeptical |
   | Ch. 4 | Omniscient | Gatsby's past (Nick wasn't there) | Analytic, objective |

9. **Assess reliability.** For sections with internal focalization, answer:
   - Does the character have reason to lie or misperceive?
   - Are the character's interpretations later contradicted by events?
   - Does the narrator acknowledge their own uncertainty or bias?
   Mark each focalizer as **reliable**, **partially unreliable**, or **unreliable**.

### Phase 4 — Trace Rhetorical Effect

10. **For each major temporal or focalization choice, write its effect:** What does the reader *not know* at each point? What emotional or intellectual state does that create?
    - Example: Withholding Gatsby's past until Chapter 6 keeps him mysterious and alluring; revealing it later deflates the romance.

11. **Identify the author's intent.** Based on the pattern of disclosure, gaps, and focalization, write one sentence describing what the narrative structure is trying to make you *feel or believe* about events and characters.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Story and discourse are extracted separately and non-identical | Y/N |
| At least 3 order deviations (analepsis/prolepsis) are identified with placement notes | Y/N |
| Duration of ≥3 scenes is measured (compressed, stretched, or isochronous) | Y/N |
| All major gaps and ellipses in discourse are noted with justification tags | Y/N |
| Focalization is mapped per section with knowledge limits specified | Y/N |
| At least one focalization shift is documented with its effect | Y/N |
| Reliability assessment tags each focalizer (reliable / partially unreliable / unreliable) | Y/N |
| Rhetorical effect of ≥2 major structural choices is traced | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Story and discourse look nearly identical. Either the narrative has no temporal play (possible but rare) or the extraction was incomplete — you missed flashbacks, frame narration, or shifts in scope.
- Focalization is marked as "omniscient" throughout without noting perspective shifts. Most texts shift between characters or between omniscience and limitation. Look harder.
- Gaps are not marked or justified. Every narrative has omissions; missing them means the analysis is surface-level.
- Reliability assessment is absent or identical for all focalizers. If every source of information is equally reliable, you likely didn't interrogate whether characters have motive or access to the truth.
- The rhetorical effect is generic ("makes the story dramatic" or "builds suspense"). Trace *how* — what specific information is withheld, and what does that absence accomplish?
- No prolepsis or analepsis marked. Pure chronological narratives are rare; flashbacks and foreshadowing are the tools of nearly every text.

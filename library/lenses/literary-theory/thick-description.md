# Thick Description (Geertz)

## Overview
Thick description is the practice of recording not just what someone *does* but the layers of meaning, intention, context, and cultural reference that make the action intelligible within its community. Geertz contrasted this with "thin description" — the mere fact of behavior stripped of significance. A wink is thin description; a wink coordinated with a confederate to mock a third party is thick. Practitioners use this when they encounter an action that seems opaque, contradictory, or culturally foreign, and they need to recover the web of meanings that makes it coherent to its participants.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Capture the Action

1. **Record the action in the thinnest possible terms.** Write only what a video camera would see: "A person raised one eyelid while keeping the other open." No interpretation. No intent. No context yet.

2. **Identify the primary action type.** Is this a gesture, a speech act, a ritual, a material practice, a social interaction, or something else? This determines what kinds of meanings to look for.

3. **Note the participants.** Who performed the action? Who witnessed it? Who was the action directed at or about? This social positioning matters for meaning.

### Phase 2 — Layer 1 — Immediate Context

4. **What was happening immediately before and after this action?** Write 2-3 sentences describing the surrounding activity. Was this action continuous with what came before, or did it break the flow? Did it change what came after?

5. **What is the stated or assumed purpose of this action?** Not "what does the person intend" — but "what is this action recognized as *for* in this setting?" Is it a greeting, a correction, a signal, a compliance, a violation?

6. **What rules or norms is this action following, breaking, or testing?** List at least 3. Examples: "This greeting uses a formal register when peers typically use casual speech" or "This interruption violates turn-taking norms but follows a hierarchy rule."

### Phase 3 — Layer 2 — Cultural and Historical Reference

7. **What prior actions, narratives, or cultural artifacts does this action invoke or echo?** Is this action a quotation, a parody, a re-enactment, a response to an earlier action? Geertz called this "thick" — the action carries resonance from elsewhere.

8. **What social categories does this action invoke?** Does it mark someone as belonging to (or excluded from) a group, status level, gender, occupation, or faction? Write the categories explicitly.

9. **What emotional or evaluative stance does the action embody or signal?** Is the action respectful, mocking, urgent, casual, defiant, compliant? Is this stance consistent with the actor's usual behavior or a departure? Note both.

### Phase 4 — Layer 3 — Systems of Meaning

10. **Map the web of meanings this action participates in.** Create a small table:
    - **Key symbol/concept the action invokes:** <...>
    - **What this symbol means in this cultural context:** <...>
    - **What values or beliefs it expresses:** <...>
    - **Who shares this understanding (and who might not):** <...>

11. **Identify what the action assumes the audience already knows.** What shared knowledge, history, or identity is being taken for granted? A thick description often fails if the audience doesn't share this background — so make it explicit.

12. **Test for dissonance.** Is this action perfectly continuous with the stated norms and meanings, or does it bend, stress, or reinterpret them? If there is tension, what does that reveal about how meanings actually work in this setting (as opposed to how people say they work)?

### Phase 5 — Triangulation and Verification

13. **How do participants themselves explain or justify this action?** Find at least one account from someone who performed or witnessed the action. Compare it to your interpretation. Where do they align? Where do they diverge? Divergence is often meaningful — it reveals competing interpretations or tacit knowledge the participant couldn't articulate.

14. **Does this action fit into a *pattern* you've observed elsewhere?** If this action appears unique, its meaning is local. If it repeats across contexts, it may signal a deeper cultural logic. List 1-3 similar actions you've observed and note what they share.

15. **What would an outsider (someone from a different culture, era, or group) misunderstand about this action?** Geertz's point was that meaning is not self-evident. Write what a naive observer might conclude and why that conclusion would be wrong.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Thin description (Phase 1) is free of interpretation | Y/N |
| At least 3 norms or rules are identified (Phase 2, Step 6) | Y/N |
| At least 2 prior actions or cultural references are named (Phase 3, Step 7) | Y/N |
| The web of meanings table (Phase 4, Step 10) has 4 populated rows | Y/N |
| Participant account collected and compared to interpretation (Phase 5, Step 13) | Y/N |
| At least one point of dissonance or divergence is noted | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The "thin description" in Phase 1 already contains interpretive language ("assertively," "reluctantly," "cleverly"). This means you are skipping the decomposition and will miss thin meanings.
- No cultural references or prior actions are named. The action is treated as isolated. Thick description requires showing how it echoes or responds to other meanings.
- The participant account matches the interpretation perfectly. Either the interpretation is so obvious it needs no verification, or it's so embedded in your own cultural assumptions that you're not actually learning from the participant's account.
- The meanings are described as universal ("all humans understand respect this way"). Geertz's entire point was that meanings are culturally specific. Universal descriptions are thin.
- No dissonance or tension between stated norms and actual practice. Most cultures have gaps between the ideal and the real; missing this is a sign the analysis is too surface-level.

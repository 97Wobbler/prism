# Form Criticism

## Overview
Form criticism examines a biblical or religious text by identifying its literary form (genre, structure, rhetorical patterns) and reconstructing its original life-setting — the historical, social, or liturgical context in which it was composed and transmitted. Rather than asking only "what does this mean?", form criticism asks "what was this *for* before it was written down?" and "who kept changing it, and why?" Practitioners use this lens when a text seems composite, when its meaning shifts depending on context, or when they suspect oral tradition layers beneath the written surface.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Identify the Literary Form

1. **Delimit the unit.** Mark where the text begins and ends. Note if boundaries are sharp or blurred. Check whether the text is:
   - A complete, self-contained piece (e.g., a parable, a psalm, a blessing)
   - A fragment embedded in a larger narrative
   - A composite of smaller units
   Write down your reasoning.

2. **Classify the genre.** Compare the text to known literary categories in its tradition. For biblical material, common forms include:
   - **Narrative forms:** myth, legend, novella, historical account, etiological tale
   - **Poetic forms:** psalm (lament, praise, thanksgiving), hymn, dirge, victory song
   - **Sayings:** proverb, aphorism, wisdom saying, prophetic utterance, parable
   - **Legal/ritual forms:** law code, commandment, liturgical instruction, blessing, curse
   - **Epistolary forms:** letter, doxology, confession
   
   Do not force a classification if none fits cleanly. If the form is mixed or anomalous, note that explicitly.

3. **Map the internal structure.** Outline the text's logical or rhetorical progression:
   - Opening formula or situation-setter
   - Middle development (narrative climax, argument, elaboration)
   - Closing formula or resolution
   
   Note repeated phrases, refrains, or parallel structures. These are often editorial seams or oral-tradition signatures.

4. **Compare to parallel texts.** Search the tradition for texts with the same or similar form. List:
   - Exact parallels (same form, same content)
   - Formal parallels (same form, different content)
   - Thematic parallels (same content, different form)
   
   What is constant across parallels? What is variable? The constant elements are likely core to the form.

### Phase 2 — Reconstruct the Original Life-Setting (Sitz im Leben)

5. **Infer the social function.** Based on the form, propose the original purpose:
   - Was this a **ritual text** (prayer, blessing, curse at a ceremony)?
   - A **teaching text** (wisdom saying, parable, law taught orally)?
   - A **polemic** (defense against an external claim or rival group)?
   - A **commemoration** (retelling to mark an event or maintain group memory)?
   - A **therapeutic text** (healing, comfort, assurance)?
   - An **entertainment** (story told for pleasure or prestige)?
   
   Write a one-sentence hypothesis about what the text *did* in its original context.

6. **Identify the implied audience.** Who is addressed or assumed?
   - Speaker and addressee relationship (priest to congregation, elder to youth, insider to outsider)
   - Implied knowledge level (specialized jargon vs. plain speech)
   - Emotional state (troubled, joyful, confused)
   
   A text composed for priests reads differently than one composed for peasants.

7. **Locate the historical moment.** What conditions would have made this text *necessary*?
   - Political crisis (invasion, exile, restoration)
   - Religious crisis (loss of temple, competing priesthoods, doctrinal change)
   - Social crisis (famine, plague, migration)
   - Routine occasion (daily prayer, weekly gathering, annual festival)
   
   Do not invent a crisis if the text does not demand one. Many texts arise from weekly liturgy, not emergency.

### Phase 3 — Detect Layers and Later Modification

8. **Mark seams and doublets.** Locations where the text appears to have been spliced or revised:
   - Duplicate phrases or narrative elements (same thing told twice slightly differently)
   - Abrupt shifts in voice, tense, or vocabulary
   - Parenthetical notes or explanations that interrupt flow
   - Theological or ideological contradiction within the same passage
   
   For each seam, propose: Is this an editorial addition, an oral-tradition variant, or a translator's annotation?

9. **Assess the text's stability.** If multiple manuscript versions exist (as in biblical texts), compare them:
   - Are some versions much longer? Shorter? Reordered?
   - Do variants cluster in specific sections?
   - Are variants theologically motivated (e.g., later version makes the text more monotheistic)?
   
   Variants often reveal what later scribes thought the text *should* say.

10. **Distinguish layers by function.** Propose a developmental hypothesis:
    - **Layer 1 (Core):** The minimal form that satisfies Phase 2. What is the irreducible kernel?
    - **Layer 2 (Expansion):** Material added to elaborate, explain, or apply the core. Mark the function of each addition.
    - **Layer 3 (Frame):** Contextualizing material (introduction, heading, concluding formula) added to integrate the text into a larger collection.
    
    Write this as a numbered sequence: "Original form likely was [Layer 1]. Later [function] led to addition of [Layer 2]. When included in [collection], [Layer 3] was added."

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Text unit is clearly delimited with reasoning provided | Y/N |
| Literary form is identified and compared to parallels | Y/N |
| Life-setting hypothesis includes social function, audience, and historical moment | Y/N |
| At least one seam or doublet is identified and explained | Y/N |
| Proposed layers are sequenced and justified by their function | Y/N |
| Analysis does not depend on external historical sources alone | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The form is named but never compared to other texts with the same form. You have a label, not evidence.
- The life-setting is invented without reference to the form. ("This text must be from exilic times" — but why does the form imply that?)
- No seams are identified. Either the text is genuinely seamless (possible but rare) or you are not reading closely enough.
- The core layer is larger than 1–2 verses and still described as "original." You have likely included later elaborations in your kernel.
- Layers are proposed based only on theological preference ("earlier version is more authentic") rather than textual, formal, or functional evidence.
- The analysis concludes with "original meaning was X" without acknowledging how later layers transformed or re-contextualized that meaning.

# Lectio Divina

## Overview

Lectio Divina ("divine reading") is a four-stage method for engaging sacred texts in a way that aims at transformation rather than comprehension alone. It moves from *lectio* (careful reading of the words as they are), through *meditatio* (turning the text over mentally to discover personal resonance), *oratio* (speaking to God in response to what emerged), and finally *contemplatio* (resting in silence with what has been given). Practitioners use this when a text resists conventional analysis, when they seek integration of meaning into lived practice, or when intellectual understanding has plateaued and a shift in posture is needed.

## Analytical Procedure

### Stage 1 — Lectio (Reading)

1. **Select a discrete passage.** Choose 3–20 verses (or equivalent unit length for non-biblical texts). It must be small enough to return to repeatedly without fatigue.

2. **Read aloud slowly, word by word.** Do not skip or abbreviate. Notice where your attention catches, where you stumble, where a single word lingers. Do not interpret. Write down any word or phrase that arrests you, without explanation.

3. **Read again, this time silently.** Let your pace slow further. Reread sentences you instinctively want to hear twice. Notice the rhythm, weight, and texture of the language. What images arise? What sensory qualities does the text carry?

4. **Note the literal content.** Write a 2-3 sentence factual summary of what the passage says, independent of meaning. This anchors the work to the text itself and prevents pure projection.

### Stage 2 — Meditatio (Meditation)

5. **Identify the word or phrase that resonated most from Stage 1.** This is typically the one you marked first or returned to. If multiple phrases pulled at you, choose one; you will work with the others in separate cycles.

6. **Hold this word or phrase in silence for 1–2 minutes.** Do not think about it deliberately. Let it move in the background of your awareness. Notice what memories, feelings, questions, or images it evokes. These associations are not distractions — they are the material of meditatio.

7. **Ask: "What does this word invite me to see about myself, my practice, or my situation?"** Write whatever surfaces, without censoring or organizing. Contradictions, half-formed insights, and emotional reactions are all valid. The goal is not coherence but honest response.

8. **Connect the resonance to your lived experience.** Where have you encountered this reality in your own life? Is there a relationship, decision, or interior struggle to which the text speaks? Be concrete — name the situation, the person, the feeling.

### Stage 3 — Oratio (Prayer/Response)

9. **Formulate a response to God (or to the ultimate truth the text carries, in secular contexts).** This is not analysis or theology. It is direct address: gratitude, grief, question, commitment, petition, or resistance — whatever the previous stages have opened.

10. **Speak or write your response as if in dialogue.** Use second-person address ("You show me..." or "I ask you..."). If words fail, express the response as a gesture, a drawing, or an extended silence with intention. Duration: 2–5 minutes of actual speaking or writing.

11. **Mark what you are asking for or offering.** Name one concrete change, prayer, or commitment that flows from this dialogue. This tethers oratio to intention rather than sentiment.

### Stage 4 — Contemplatio (Contemplation)

12. **Release the work of the previous three stages.** Set down the text, your notes, and your effort. Sit in silence for 5–10 minutes with no agenda. Do not try to deepen the experience or consolidate the insights. Simply rest with what has been given.

13. **After the silence, write a single sentence about the quality of that rest.** Was it restful, dry, agitated, spacious, empty? Do not explain — name the texture. This signals completion and marks the transition out.

14. **Close the practice.** Return to ordinary awareness. If you experienced clarity, hold it lightly. If the practice felt barren, know that contemplatio sometimes runs empty and that is not failure.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Passage selected is discrete and re-readable (≤20 verses or equivalent) | Y/N |
| At least one word/phrase was marked in lectio without immediate interpretation | Y/N |
| Meditatio connected the text to a lived situation or interior reality | Y/N |
| Oratio was written or spoken as direct address, not analysis | Y/N |
| A concrete commitment or prayer emerged from oratio | Y/N |
| Contemplatio was undertaken (even if felt empty) with no forced outcome | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Lectio became theological commentary instead of noticing where the text caught. The reader is analyzing rather than reading.
- Meditatio stayed abstract ("this teaches about faith") instead of personal ("this unsettles my certainty about..."). The text was not allowed to challenge the reader.
- Oratio was intellectual assent ("this is true") rather than response. No actual dialogue occurred.
- The four stages were rushed or combined. Each stage requires its own pace and posture; collapsing them collapses the method.
- Contemplatio was skipped or replaced with "thinking about what I learned." The method lost its contemplative anchor.
- No word or phrase ever arrested the reader. Either the selection was too long, the reading was too fast, or the reader's attention has narrowed to the intellectual only.

# Historical-Critical Method

## Overview

The Historical-Critical Method analyzes a biblical text as a composite artifact — not a unified composition but a document shaped by multiple sources, genres, and redactional hands across generations. Rather than treating the text as synchronic (all written at once), practitioners decompose it diachronically (by layers and origin). The discipline is the discipline of seams: tension points, repetitions, shifts in vocabulary or theology that signal where sources meet or where a redactor spliced material. Scholars use this when a text's internal incoherence or genre-mixture demands historical explanation rather than harmonization.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Textual Analysis (Isolate seams and tensions)

1. **Read the passage aloud in the original language (Hebrew, Aramaic, or Greek).** Mark every instance of:
   - Duplicate or near-duplicate material (same event narrated twice with variations)
   - Abrupt shifts in vocabulary, grammar, or style
   - Theological inconsistencies (e.g., God portrayed as both distant and intimate, or as merciful then vengeful within the same narrative)
   - Changes in narrative perspective (third person → first person, or vice versa)
   - Grammatical anomalies (subject-verb disagreement, tense shifts, unusual pronouns)

2. **List each seam and its type.** Example:
   - Genesis 1:27 and 2:21–22: Two creation accounts with contradictory sequence (spirit/body vs. dust/rib) and different divine names (Elohim vs. YHWH)
   - 1 Samuel 15:35 and 16:1: Sudden contradiction — Samuel said he would not see Saul again, then immediately does

3. **For each seam, determine whether it is:**
   - **A duplicate** — same event told twice with divergent details
   - **A contradiction** — claims that cannot both be true in the same narrative world
   - **A stylistic break** — vocabulary, grammar, or tone shift with no content repetition
   - **A theological tension** — the same character or event portrayed in theologically incompatible ways

### Phase 2 — Source Identification (Assign material to hypothetical sources)

4. **Collect all material of the same type (duplicates, contradictions, or stylistic groups) into provisional source documents.** Use traditional scholarly sigla if applicable:
   - **J** (Jahwist) — uses YHWH, earthy theological perspective, interests in covenant
   - **E** (Elohist) — uses Elohim, focus on prophecy and divine revelation through dreams
   - **P** (Priestly) — genealogies, laws, ritual detail, systematic theology
   - **D** (Deuteronomic) — characteristic phrases ("you shall do what is right in the sight of the LORD"), covenant law, exhortatory style)

5. **For each source, mark:**
   - Geographic and temporal markers (e.g., "J seems to know South Judah politics; E knows North Israel politics")
   - Theological vocabulary and worldview (P's repeated `yadaʿ` / "knowing" → recognition vs. J's narrative immediacy)
   - Presumed historical context (e.g., when would J's view of kingship have been plausible?)

6. **Note what cannot be assigned to a known source.** These may be later accretions, glosses, or evidence of a source not yet identified. Flag these as "unassigned" with a reason (e.g., "contradicts all identified sources").

### Phase 3 — Form-Critical Analysis (Identify genre and oral prehistory)

7. **For each passage or source layer, classify its literary form:**
   - Narrative / Epic (continuous story)
   - Law / Apodictic (absolute rule: "You shall not...") or Casuistic (conditional: "If... then...")
   - Poetry / Psalm / Wisdom saying (identify meter, parallelism, rhetorical device)
   - Prophecy / Oracle (direct divine speech, threat / promise)
   - Genealogy / List (repetitive structure)
   - Legend / Etiological (explains a place name or custom)

8. **For narrative and poetry, identify the original oral or written unit before incorporation into the larger text.** Oral traditions often have:
   - Repetitive structures (formulae, repeated phrases for memorization)
   - Brief anecdotes with a single point
   - Episodic rather than cumulative plot
   - Inconsistency with neighboring material (suggesting independent origin)

9. **Estimate the Sitz im Leben ("life setting") — the original social and institutional context in which this form was used.** For example:
   - A legal code's original setting: an assembly for dispute resolution
   - A hymn's original setting: a sanctuary liturgy
   - A genealogy's setting: a tribal council asserting lineage rights

### Phase 4 — Redaction Analysis (Reconstruct the editing process)

10. **Identify editorial seams — places where a redactor(s) combined sources.** These often show:
    - Formulaic connectives ("And it came to pass..." or repetitive "the LORD said to...") linking disparate material
    - Insertion of clarifying details that break syntax or logic
    - Framing devices (an introduction and conclusion to a source block that are not in the original source)
    - Harmonization attempts (a phrase or clause inserted to make two sources cohere)

11. **For each seam, determine the redactor's editorial principle:**
    - **Conflation** — merged both sources side-by-side (e.g., "and Pharaoh's magicians did so too, but Aaron's rod swallowed theirs")
    - **Subordination** — nested one source within another
    - **Replacement** — chose one version and discarded the other (visible only by comparing parallel texts)
    - **Supplement** — added interpretive comments or transitions

12. **Estimate the date and intention of the redactional layer.** Consider:
    - Which sources appear to be known to the redactor? (If P is present alongside J and E, the redactor worked after all three sources existed.)
    - Does the redaction show concerns typical of a particular period? (Exilic redactors often add promise-of-restoration endings.)
    - Is there evidence the redactor was trying to reconcile theology, law, or history?

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Every seam and tension in the text was identified and typed (duplicate / contradiction / stylistic / theological) | Y/N |
| Material was grouped into source layers with consistent vocabulary and theological perspective | Y/N |
| Each source has a hypothesized Sitz im Leben and date range | Y/N |
| Forms were classified and checked against known oral-tradition patterns | Y/N |
| At least one redactional seam was identified with evidence and editorial principle named | Y/N |
| Unassigned material was flagged with a reason | Y/N |
| The analysis produced a plausible diachronic narrative (source → form → redaction) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Every seam is explained as "later addition" or "redaction" without identifying a coherent source behind the base text. This is analytic drift — every fragment becomes a layer.
- Sources are identified by isolated vocabulary matches (e.g., "J uses YHWH, so every YHWH passage is J") without checking whether the same passage contains E-characteristic theology. Vocabulary alone is unreliable.
- The redaction analysis assumes a single redactor and ignores the possibility of multiple editorial phases. Real texts are often edited multiple times across centuries.
- Form-critical classification is vague ("this is a narrative") without identifying the specific form (etiological legend, heroic epic, covenant renewal ceremony text) or its characteristic features.
- No attempt to date sources or redactional layers. "Source A is older than Source B" requires evidence (historical references, theological development, mention in other datable texts).
- The final diachronic narrative is presented with high confidence but relies on reconstructions that cannot be verified (since the "original sources" no longer exist as standalone documents). Confidence statements must reflect this.

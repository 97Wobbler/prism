# PaRDeS

## Overview
PaRDeS (an acronym for the Hebrew words Peshat, Remez, Derash, and Sod) is a four-level hermeneutical system for extracting meaning from a text. Peshat is the literal, grammatical sense; Remez is the allegorical or symbolic layer; Derash is the interpretive, homiletical layer (often midrashic); Sod is the mystical or hidden layer. Practitioners use this lens when a single reading of a text feels thin, when traditional and mystical readings seem to conflict, or when they need to defend why a text admits multiple coherent interpretations without collapsing into relativism.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish the Text

1. **Select a passage or phrase.** It should be between one sentence and one paragraph — long enough to sustain multiple readings, short enough to analyze in depth. Write it verbatim.

2. **Identify the immediate linguistic context.** What precedes and follows the passage? Are there repeated words, parallel structures, or grammatical anomalies? Note these without yet interpreting.

3. **List all dictionary definitions or grammatical possibilities for ambiguous words.** For each key term in the passage, record all plausible meanings, not just the most obvious one. Example: the Hebrew word *tzaddik* can mean "righteous person," "justified person," "aligned person," or (in Kabbalistic use) "emanation."

### Phase 2 — Peshat (Literal Layer)

4. **Construct the plain reading.** Using the most common definitions from step 3 and following grammatical rules, state what the passage says if taken at face value. A child should be able to understand this reading without specialized knowledge.

5. **Mark any jarring or unusual elements.** Does the literal reading seem redundant, awkward, or at odds with surrounding text? Write these friction points down — they often signal that other layers are at work.

6. **Document what the Peshat reading claims, asserts, or commands.** Do not yet ask whether it is "true" or "spiritual" — just map the content.

### Phase 3 — Remez (Allegorical/Symbolic Layer)

7. **Extract symbols from the literal reading.** Each entity, action, or attribute in the Peshat can stand for something else. Example: if the literal text describes a wall, Remez might read "wall" as an obstacle to understanding, or as a boundary between the material and spiritual worlds.

8. **Apply standard allegorical correspondences from the tradition.** If working within Jewish hermeneutics, consult known symbol systems: the four worlds (Assiyah, Yetzirah, Briyah, Atziluth), the ten Sefirot, the divine names, and the human body (microcosm). Do not invent private symbols; stay within the interpretive tradition you are using.

9. **Restate the passage as an allegory.** Replace each literal element with its allegorical referent and read the passage as a spiritual or psychological mapping. Example: "David's victory over Goliath" becomes "the rational soul's triumph over the bestial appetite."

10. **Ask: Does this allegorical reading honor the literal text, or does it ignore it?** A sound Remez reading should not require the Peshat to be false. It should deepen the Peshat, not contradict it.

### Phase 4 — Derash (Interpretive/Homiletical Layer)

11. **Generate midrashic questions.** Derash is the layer of "what can we derive from this text?" rather than "what does it say?" Ask: What does this teach us about human conduct? What does it reveal about God's relationship to creation? What law or ethical principle is embedded here?

12. **Mine the passage for wordplay, numerical values (gematria), or grammatical oddities.** Derash often builds on linguistic features: repeated letters, words that appear nowhere else in Scripture, or structural parallels to other passages. Record these.

13. **Propose an interpretation that uses Derash techniques.** This interpretation is allowed to be creative and expansive. It can draw analogies, collapse time frames, and treat the text as a springboard rather than a boundary. Example: "The fact that Jonah is swallowed by a *great* fish, not just any fish, teaches us that even suffering from God's hand is immense in mercy."

14. **Verify that the Derash interpretation is grounded in linguistic or textual evidence**, not pure invention. It should feel like a discovery, not an imposition.

### Phase 5 — Sod (Mystical/Hidden Layer)

15. **Access the Sod layer via the framework's depth rules.** In Jewish mysticism, Sod often involves:
    - Contemplation of the divine names and their permutations
    - The presence of the infinite (Ein Sof) within finite forms
    - The interplay of contraction and expansion in consciousness
    - The text as a map of the inner worlds or psyche
    Consult the Kabbalistic tradition (e.g., Lurianic Kabbalah, Hasidic philosophy) or the mystical tradition native to the framework you are using.

16. **Read the passage as a description of transformation or union.** Sod often asks: What inner state or cosmic process does this enact? What shifts in consciousness is the reader invited into?

17. **Write the Sod reading.** It should feel abstract, non-dualistic, and focused on unity or hidden patterns. Example: "The breath (ruach) that animates the text is the same breath that animates the reader; in reading, the boundary between subject and object dissolves."

### Phase 6 — Coherence Check

18. **Create a four-row table:**
    - Row 1: Peshat reading (what it says)
    - Row 2: Remez reading (what it symbolizes)
    - Row 3: Derash reading (what it teaches)
    - Row 4: Sod reading (what it enacts or unveils)

19. **For each pair of rows, identify points of coherence and tension.** Where do they reinforce each other? Where do they seem to contradict? Write these explicitly.

20. **Judge whether the four readings form a unified whole or separate, incompatible interpretations.** A strong PaRDeS reading shows the four layers as different angles on a single reality, not as competing stories.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Peshat reading is jargon-free and would be understood by a literal-minded reader | Y/N |
| Remez uses symbols from the tradition, not invented personal symbolism | Y/N |
| Derash is grounded in linguistic or textual evidence (wordplay, structure, etc.) | Y/N |
| Sod invokes the mystical framework coherently and is not mere poetry | Y/N |
| All four layers reference the same passage, not drift into separate texts | Y/N |
| Coherence table shows at least one explicit connection between non-adjacent layers (e.g., Peshat-Derash) | Y/N |
| No layer was skipped or left underdeveloped | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Peshat reading sounds spiritual or symbolic. PaRDeS requires that the literal layer be genuinely literal first.
- Remez uses private or invented symbols not grounded in tradition. This collapses the distinction between Remez and pure personal association.
- Derash has no textual or linguistic hook. It reads like free association, not midrash.
- Sod is indistinguishable from Remez. Both layers should operate at different depths of abstraction or consciousness.
- The four readings are completely isolated — no reinforcement or tension noted in the coherence check. This suggests they were generated independently, not as four angles on one text.
- Coherence is claimed but not shown. "All four readings agree the text is about mercy" is assertion, not analysis.
- The passage was chosen because it obviously supports a pre-existing theological agenda. PaRDeS works best on surprising or ambiguous texts.

# Liberation Theology Method

## Overview
The Liberation Theology Method is a three-phase lens for analyzing a pastoral, social, or spiritual problem by (1) observing its material reality and structural roots, (2) interpreting that reality through theological categories, and (3) designing concrete action toward liberation. It operates on case studies, community situations, policy proposals, or pastoral dilemmas where injustice appears to be systemic. Practitioners use it when a problem resists charitable interpretation and when existing solutions have failed to address why the problem recurs.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — See (Observe the Reality)

1. **Identify the community or population experiencing the problem.** Name them specifically: not "the poor" but "agricultural workers in region X" or "detained migrants." Vagueness here blocks later analysis.

2. **Document the material conditions.** For each of these categories, write down what you observe or can verify:
   - **Economic:** wages, assets, debt, access to credit, what they own vs. what they owe
   - **Social:** family structure, health outcomes, literacy, access to education, social mobility barriers
   - **Political:** representation, voice in decisions that affect them, legal protections, enforcement
   - **Physical:** housing, water, food security, exposure to hazard
   Do not rely on abstract statistics alone — if possible, include testimonies, interviews, or direct observation notes.

3. **Trace the structural causes.** For each condition you documented, ask: "Why does this persist?" Write down:
   - Who benefits from this condition persisting?
   - What institutions, laws, or norms maintain it?
   - What would have to change for the condition to change?
   Distinguish between individual choices and systemic barriers. "They are poor because they don't work hard" is not structural analysis.

4. **Identify the ideology masking the problem.** What narrative or belief system makes this injustice seem natural, inevitable, or deserved? Examples:
   - "This is how things have always been."
   - "These people lack the capacity to do better."
   - "God wills this inequality."
   - "The market works fairly if we let it."
   Write down the ideology plainly, then identify who benefits from it being believed.

### Phase 2 — Judge (Interpret Theologically)

5. **Consult the tradition.** Review what your theological tradition says about justice, liberation, the poor, and God's bias. This may be Scripture, doctrine, or pastoral teaching. Write down at least 3 passages, principles, or teachings that speak to the situation. Examples:
   - "The Spirit of the Lord is upon me... to preach good news to the poor... to set the oppressed free" (Luke 4:18)
   - "If a brother or sister is naked and destitute of daily food, and one of you says to them, 'Go in peace, be warmed and filled,' without giving them the things needed for the body, what good is that?" (James 2:15–16)
   - "I have come that they may have life, and have it to the full" (John 10:10)

6. **Analyze the conflict between doctrine and practice.** Compare what the tradition teaches with what Phase 1 revealed about the community's actual condition. Where is there contradiction? Examples:
   - The doctrine says "all are made in God's image," yet the practice denies dignity to specific groups.
   - The teaching calls for justice, yet institutions endorsed by the church perpetuate injustice.
   Write this contradiction clearly.

7. **Identify whose God is being served.** Examine what theology is actually being preached or implied by the status quo:
   - Does the current theology console the afflicted or console the afflictors?
   - Does it justify the existing order or challenge it?
   - Who is it good news for?

8. **Articulate a liberating theological claim.** In light of the tradition and the contradiction, write one sentence that asserts what God's action or desire is with respect to the oppressed community. Example:
   - "God's liberation of the oppressed is not a future promise alone — it is a call to action now."
   - "The restoration of dignity to those treated as objects is central to redemption."

### Phase 3 — Act (Design Liberating Response)

9. **Specify the aim.** What would freedom/liberation look like for this community? Be concrete:
   - Bad: "End poverty."
   - Good: "Enable agricultural workers to own cooperative land, negotiate fair wages collectively, and have voice in local governance within 18 months."

10. **Design interventions at three levels:**
    - **Immediate relief:** What stops the acute suffering now? (food, shelter, safety)
    - **Structural change:** What institutions, laws, or power relationships must shift? (land reform, wage laws, voting rights, credentialing barriers)
    - **Consciousness raising:** How will the community understand their own condition and capacity for change? (education, reflection, testimony, organizing)
    Write at least one action under each level.

11. **Identify allies and obstacles.**
    - Who in the community has agency and leadership?
    - Which external actors (church, NGO, government, business) could be partners?
    - What resistance will you face and from whom?
    - What resources exist locally?

12. **Commit to accountability to the community.** How will the community affected evaluate whether this action is working? Who decides success? What structures exist for course correction?

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| The community is named specifically, not abstracted | Y/N |
| Material conditions are documented across Economic/Social/Political/Physical categories | Y/N |
| Structural causes identify institutions/norms, not individual failings | Y/N |
| The ideology masking the problem is named and its beneficiaries identified | Y/N |
| At least 3 theological sources are cited and applied | Y/N |
| A genuine contradiction between doctrine and practice is articulated | Y/N |
| The liberating theological claim is distinct from the status quo theology | Y/N |
| Aim is concrete and falsifiable, not abstract | Y/N |
| All three intervention levels (relief/structural/consciousness) have at least one action | Y/N |
| Accountability structures center the community's voice, not external judgment | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Phase 1 uses abstract language ("the vulnerable," "developing nations"). Structural analysis requires naming.
- Phase 1 attributes conditions primarily to individual failings or cultural deficits. This misses structure.
- Phase 2 cites theology but makes no connection to the reality described in Phase 1. The judgment becomes decorative.
- Phase 2 theology validates the status quo. If the theological conclusion is "this is how things are meant to be," the framework has failed.
- Phase 3 interventions focus only on relief (food, medicine, charity) with no structural component. Charity without structural change repeats the cycle.
- Phase 3 actions are designed without consultation with or leadership by the affected community. This reproduces the power imbalance it claims to oppose.
- The theological claim in Phase 2 and the action in Phase 3 contradict each other. If theology says "liberation now" but action is "education only," something is dishonest.

# Four Senses of Scripture

## Overview
The Four Senses framework decomposes a text (typically scriptural or sacred, but applicable to any layered work) into four distinct interpretive levels: literal (what the text says), allegorical (what it symbolizes), moral (what it demands of the reader), and anagogical (what it reveals about ultimate reality or destiny). Practitioners use this when a single reading feels reductive and they suspect the text carries intentional polysemy — multiple meanings operating simultaneously, each true at its own level. The discipline is distinguishing these layers without collapsing them into one another.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish the Literal Sense

1. **Read the passage as plainly as possible.** Identify what is explicitly stated: actors, actions, settings, direct claims. Write 2–3 sentences describing only what the text literally says, using the text's own language where possible. Do not interpret, allegorize, or theologize yet.

2. **Mark any ambiguities in the literal reading.** Note places where grammar, reference, or context is unclear. These are hinges where other senses may later turn. Example: "Did the king build the city, or did the city already exist?" Record both possibilities.

3. **Establish the genre and original context.** Is this a law, a narrative, a poem, a parable? What historical or cultural reference frame does it assume? This grounds the literal sense — a metaphor is still literal if the audience would have recognized it as one.

### Phase 2 — Extract the Allegorical Sense

4. **Ask: What does this represent beyond itself?** Do not invent meanings arbitrarily. Look for:
   - Internal symbolic systems (the city of Jerusalem standing for the people of Israel; water for purification)
   - Typology (a past event prefiguring a future one, or a figure representing a category: Adam as the first human, Jesus as the New Adam)
   - Metaphorical equivalences that the culture or the text itself endorses elsewhere
   Write at least one allegorical reading, with its justification.

5. **Check for coherence with the literal sense.** The allegorical reading should not require the literal reading to be false. Example: if the text says "the city was surrounded," the allegorical reading "the city represents the soul" does not contradict the literal fact of the siege — it adds a layer. Record any tension.

### Phase 3 — Discern the Moral Sense

6. **Ask: What does this demand or teach about how to live?** Do not drift into personal application or modern relevance; instead, identify the ethical principle the text embeds. Examples:
   - "Do not covet" (directly stated law becomes a principle about desire and contentment)
   - "The prodigal son's father forgives" (narrative embodies the principle of paternal mercy and restored relationship)
   - "You are the salt of the earth" (metaphor becomes an imperative about the reader's role)
   Write the moral principle in one sentence.

7. **Distinguish moral sense from allegorical sense.** Allegorical sense says "this represents that." Moral sense says "this teaches you to do or be this." They often coexist but are not identical. Record both separately.

### Phase 4 — Perceive the Anagogical Sense

8. **Ask: What does this reveal about ultimate things — God, eternity, salvation, cosmic destiny?** The anagogical sense points beyond present life to the end of history or the nature of the divine. Examples:
   - A flood narrative may literally recount judgment but anagogically signify the end of the age
   - A wedding feast may represent human community but anagogically point to union with God
   - A pilgrim's journey may be a literal voyage but anagogically image the soul's ascent
   State the anagogical meaning in one sentence.

9. **Verify that it builds on, not contradicts, the lower senses.** Anagogy is not a layer unto itself; it should complete the literal, allegorical, and moral readings by giving them cosmic or eternal significance. If it seems to require denying the literal sense, revisit it.

### Phase 5 — Integrate and Evaluate

10. **Lay out all four senses in order.** On a single page or section, state each sense clearly. Check that each makes sense on its own and that they do not contradict.

11. **Identify which senses are most robust.** Does the literal sense rest on strong textual evidence? Is the allegorical sense supported by parallel usage elsewhere in the tradition or canon? Is the moral sense universalizable or culturally specific? Is the anagogical sense grounded in the tradition or speculative? Mark each as well-grounded or tentative.

12. **Note any sense that appears absent or weak.** Not every text will yield all four senses equally. If one is missing, decide whether the text genuinely does not support it or whether the interpreter has not yet found it. Record this uncertainty.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Literal sense is stated without interpretation or allegory | Y/N |
| Allegorical sense is grounded in the text, tradition, or typology — not arbitrary | Y/N |
| Moral sense is extracted as a principle, not a modern application | Y/N |
| Anagogical sense is framed as cosmic or eternal significance, not metaphor | Y/N |
| Each sense is stated in one clear sentence | Y/N |
| Lower senses are not contradicted by higher ones | Y/N |
| At least one sense has been marked as robust; one is marked as tentative or absent | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All four senses are present and equally strong for a banal or non-sacred text. The framework may be overapplied or meanings may be invented rather than discovered.
- The allegorical, moral, and anagogical senses all say roughly the same thing. Collapse indicates insufficient distinction — go back and sharpen each layer.
- The anagogical sense contradicts or ignores the literal sense. It may be speculation rather than exegesis. Anagogy should crown the literal reading, not replace it.
- The moral sense is a contemporary application ("this teaches us about social justice today") rather than a timeless principle. That is useful but not the moral sense in this framework.
- No tradition or textual evidence is cited for the allegorical or anagogical senses. They may be true, but they are not yet part of the interpretive record and should be flagged as speculative.
- The interpreter claims all four senses are certain. No reading is certain; mark confidence levels honestly.

# Backward Design (UbD)

## Overview

Backward Design inverts the typical instructional planning sequence. Instead of choosing content first and adding assessment at the end, you begin with the desired result (what students should understand or be able to do), design valid assessments that measure that outcome, and only then plan the instruction needed to prepare students to succeed on those assessments. Practitioners use this when they suspect their teaching is misaligned—when students pass quizzes but can't transfer knowledge, or when hours of instruction produce shallow understanding. The discipline is ruthless honesty about what students actually need to demonstrate.

## Analytical Procedure

### Stage 1 — Identify Desired Results

1. **State the enduring understanding.** This is not a topic or a standard. It is a central idea students should retain years after the class ends.
   - Bad: "The Civil War."
   - Good: "Societies are often built on unresolved moral tensions that eventually force change."
   - The test: Could a student explain this idea to someone outside the course?

2. **List the essential questions.** These are 2–4 open-ended questions that drive inquiry and point toward the enduring understanding. They should be worth asking repeatedly across the unit and the grade level.
   - Example: "Under what conditions do ordinary people resist injustice?"
   - Bad: "What year did the Civil War start?" (too narrow)

3. **Specify knowledge and skills targets.** List the discrete facts, procedures, and competencies students must master to engage with the enduring understanding. Be concrete.
   - Example: "Students will identify primary sources, distinguish causation from correlation, analyze competing narratives about a historical event."

### Stage 2 — Determine Acceptable Evidence

4. **Design summative assessment(s).** This is the capstone task or exam that demonstrates whether students have achieved the Stage 1 targets. It must directly measure the enduring understanding and essential questions, not just recall.
   - Good: "Students write a 5-page analytical essay arguing whether Reconstruction policies succeeded or failed, citing primary sources from at least three perspectives."
   - Bad: "Multiple-choice test on dates and names."
   - Criterion: A student could score high on this assessment and still not understand your enduring understanding — that's misalignment.

5. **Design formative assessments.** These are frequent, low-stakes checks (quizzes, discussions, sketches, problem sets) that reveal whether students are progressing toward the summative task. They should be designed *after* you know what the summative task is, so they practice the same skills.
   - Map each formative check to one or more Stage 1 targets.

6. **Specify the performance standard.** What does "proficiency" look like? Use a rubric with 3–4 levels (e.g., Developing, Proficient, Advanced). Each cell should be concrete, not vague.
   - Bad: "Good understanding of the topic."
   - Good: "Cites evidence from at least two sources; explains the connection between evidence and claim; acknowledges at least one alternative interpretation."

### Stage 3 — Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction

7. **Audit the instructional sequence.** List the activities, readings, discussions, and projects you plan to use. For each, write: "This prepares students to [specific formative assessment] which prepares them for [summative assessment]."
   - If you cannot complete that chain, the activity may be busywork.

8. **Check coverage against Stage 1.** Every enduring understanding and essential question should appear repeatedly throughout the unit, not just once. Enduring understandings are hard-won; one discussion is not enough.

9. **Design the first lesson and the final lesson.** The first lesson should hook engagement and surface prior knowledge against the essential questions. The final lesson should be a review and reflection: students revisit the essential questions and enduring understanding to see how their thinking has changed.

10. **Specify how formative data will be used.** Write down: "If a student shows struggle with [formative assessment], I will [reteach / small-group intervention / differentiated task]." This is the feedback loop. Without it, formative assessment is theater.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Enduring understanding is stated as a transferable idea (not a topic) | Y/N |
| Essential questions are open-ended and worth asking repeatedly | Y/N |
| Summative assessment directly measures the enduring understanding (not just content recall) | Y/N |
| Formative assessments align with summative task and Stage 1 targets | Y/N |
| Performance standard is concrete and measurable, not vague | Y/N |
| Every instructional activity can be chained backward to Stage 1 | Y/N |
| Enduring understanding and essential questions appear multiple times in instruction | Y/N |
| Data use protocol is written (what happens if a student struggles) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Enduring understanding sounds like a chapter title or standard. It should sound like a claim or principle, not a category.
- Summative assessment is a multiple-choice or fill-in-the-blank quiz. These typically measure recall, not understanding or transfer.
- Formative assessments do not resemble the summative task. (Example: summative is an essay; formatives are matching questions.)
- A unit has 10+ instructional activities and none can be dropped without affecting the chain to Stage 1. Over-coverage is also misalignment.
- The rubric uses words like "good," "adequate," or "needs improvement" without defining what those look like on a concrete task.
- Enduring understanding and essential questions appear in the syllabus but are never revisited in instruction.
- Teachers say they will "differentiate" or "scaffold" but offer no specific, pre-planned alternatives for students who struggle on formative checks.

# Crazy 8s

## Overview
Fold a single sheet of paper into 8 sections, set a timer for 8 minutes, and sketch one solution per section. The constraint forces speed over polish and breadth over depth. Practitioners use this to escape the gravity of the first idea (which is rarely the best) and to generate enough variation that patterns and recombinations emerge. The lens is not about artistic quality — it's about trading refinement for exploration.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Setup

1. **Define the challenge in one sentence.** Write it at the top of the paper where all sketchers can see it. The challenge should be specific enough to constrain thinking (not "design an app") but open enough to allow multiple valid approaches (not "add a blue button to the login screen").

2. **Fold the paper into 8 rectangles.** Fold the sheet in half lengthwise, then fold in half again widthwise, then diagonally once more. Unfold to reveal 8 sections. Each section is roughly 4 inches × 2.5 inches — small enough to force speed, large enough to hold a rough sketch.

3. **Gather all materials upfront.** Sketchers need only pens or pencils. No color, no rulers, no laptops. Analog-only for this phase.

4. **Set the timer to 8 minutes and announce it clearly.** Start when all sketchers have pens in hand. Eight minutes is strict. Do not extend it.

### Phase 2 — Sketching

5. **Each sketcher draws one solution idea per section.** The goal is one rough sketch per minute. Ideas should vary deliberately — don't iterate on the same concept across sections. Examples of variation:
   - Different user entry points (mobile vs. desktop vs. voice)
   - Different mental models (task-focused vs. object-focused vs. timeline-focused)
   - Deliberately bad/absurd ideas (sometimes adjacent to good ones)
   - Ideas you don't believe in (to escape your own biases)

6. **Sketches are rough.** Stick figures, boxes, arrows, and labels are sufficient. No shading, no typography, no time spent on aesthetics. If you spend more than 90 seconds on any sketch, you're doing it wrong.

7. **Add labels or 1-2 word descriptions** to clarify what each sketch shows. The label can be the only text — the sketch itself is the main artifact.

8. **Do not erase or cross out.** If a sketch idea doesn't work halfway through, leave it and move to the next section. Rough drafts are features, not failures.

### Phase 3 — Documentation & Review

9. **After the timer stops, each sketcher annotates their name and the date** on their sheet. If multiple people are sketching, collect all 8-sketch sheets and lay them out together.

10. **Read every sketch aloud (or in silence, then discuss).** For each sketch, one sentence: "This one shows [concept]." Do not critique or defend yet. The goal is shared understanding of what was attempted.

11. **Mark patterns and outliers.** Which ideas appeared across multiple sheets? Which were unique? Which surprised you? Mark these with a highlighter or note.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Challenge statement is specific and one sentence | Y/N |
| All 8 sections have at least one sketch | Y/N |
| Each sketch is rough (can be completed in ~1 min) | Y/N |
| Ideas vary across sections (not iterative refinement) | Y/N |
| At least 2 sketches represent genuinely different approaches | Y/N |
| Sketches are labeled or annotated | Y/N |
| No erasing or major refinement mid-session | Y/N |
| Timer was respected (no extension beyond 8 min) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All 8 sketches look like the same idea with minor tweaks. The sketcher anchored on the first thought and couldn't escape it.
- Sketches are too polished or detailed. Refinement during the session crowds out exploration.
- Fewer than 8 sketches completed, or large sections left blank. Either the challenge was too complex or the pace wasn't maintained.
- No variety in approach — all mobile, all task-focused, all serious. Forced variety (including deliberately absurd ideas) is a sign of healthy exploration.
- Challenge statement is vague ("improve the experience"). Sketchers diverged so much that comparison is meaningless.
- Sketches have no labels. Interpretation becomes subjective and collaborative learning fails.

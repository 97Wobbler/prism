# Brainwriting 6-3-5

## Overview
Brainwriting 6-3-5 is a structured ideation technique where six participants each write three ideas on a shared form, then pass it to the next person, cycling through five rounds. The result is 108 ideas (6 participants × 3 ideas × 6 forms, accounting for rotation). The discipline is parallel, asynchronous generation without wait time or dominant speakers. Practitioners use it when they need high idea volume fast, when group dynamics suppress quieter voices, or when they want to run ideation across time zones or in writing-first cultures.

## Analytical Procedure

### Setup Phase

1. **Assemble participants.** Ideally six people (the framework scales poorly with fewer; more requires multiple sessions). Mix experience levels and departments if possible — diversity of background drives idea variety.

2. **Prepare six identical forms.** Each form has three rows, six columns. Header row shows participant numbers 1–6. Each cell is a blank space large enough for 1–2 sentences. Forms may be paper or digital (shared document, spreadsheet, or dedicated tool).

3. **Brief the group on the goal.** One sentence: "We're generating ideas for <problem>. Write one idea per cell. No editing, no evaluation. Build on previous ideas if inspired, but don't have to." Set the tone as quantity-over-quality. Typical duration: 5–6 minutes per round × 5 rounds = 30 minutes total.

### Execution Phase

4. **Round 1 — Initial ideas.** Distribute Form A to Participant 1. They write three ideas in cells (1,1), (1,2), (1,3). Pass to Participant 2 at the timer signal (typically 5 minutes).

5. **Rounds 2–5 — Idea iteration.** Each participant:
   - Reads all previous ideas on the form they receive
   - Writes three new ideas in the next available column (their assigned column)
   - May be inspired by or explicitly build on earlier ideas (this is encouraged)
   - Passes to the next participant when the timer signals
   
   The form circulates: 1 → 2 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6 → (new form or 1 again). Repeat five times total. Participants write in their own designated columns to avoid overlap or duplicate counting.

6. **Post-session collection.** Gather all completed forms. Each form should show six columns filled (one per participant per round). You now have 6 forms × 18 cells (3 ideas × 6 participants) = 108 total ideas (or fewer if forms were not completed).

### Consolidation Phase

7. **Transfer ideas to a shared list.** Transcribe all ideas verbatim into a single document, preserving which round and participant they came from (optional but useful for traceability).

8. **De-duplicate.** Mark ideas that are identical or near-duplicates. Do not delete them yet; mark them for later review.

9. **Cluster by theme.** Group ideas by loose topic or type (feature ideas, process improvements, risk mitigations, etc.). Use a wall, digital board, or spreadsheet columns. Do not score or rank yet.

10. **Vote or prioritize.** Have the full group (or a subset) rate or vote on ideas. Typical methods: dot voting (each person gets N dots), ranking, or simple yes/no "worth exploring further." Record the votes.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| All six participants completed all five rounds | Y/N |
| Total idea count matches expected (6 × 3 × rounds completed) | Y/N |
| Ideas are transcribed verbatim, not paraphrased or edited | Y/N |
| De-duplication was performed and marked (not deleted) | Y/N |
| Ideas are clustered into recognizable themes | Y/N |
| Voting/prioritization was performed on the full set | Y/N |
| At least one idea is a refinement of or reference to a previous idea (showing build-on occurred) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Fewer than half of the six participants completed all five rounds. The session was interrupted or poorly facilitated.
- All ideas come from one or two participants. Either the exercise devolved into turn-taking by dominant speakers, or others wrote placeholder text. Check for actual engagement.
- De-duplicated list removes 50% or more of ideas. Either the problem was too narrow, the group converged too quickly (which can be good), or participants didn't understand the distinctiveness needed.
- No ideas show evidence of building on prior ideas. Participants treated the form as individual worksheets rather than a shared conversation. The circulation didn't add value.
- Forms were not rotated on schedule. Some participants wrote multiple ideas in the same column (column collision), making it unclear how many distinct thinkers contributed.
- Voting results show one idea with overwhelming consensus and the rest scattered or ignored. This suggests either one idea was genuinely exceptional or the voting system was biased (e.g., founder's idea was visible).

- Facilitation method: <in-person / hybrid / remote>

### Execution Quality
- Participation evenness: <high / medium / low> — <justification (e.g., "all 6 participants contributed ~18 ideas each">)

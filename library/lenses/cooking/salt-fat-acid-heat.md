# Salt Fat Acid Heat

## Overview

Every savory dish relies on four elements working in concert: salt (to amplify flavors), fat (to carry flavors and soften textures), acid (to brighten and balance), and heat (to develop flavors through cooking). When a dish tastes dull, one-dimensional, or unfinished, the problem is almost always an imbalance in these four — not a missing ingredient. Practitioners use this lens to move beyond recipe-following and diagnose what a dish actually needs, turning vague dissatisfaction ("something's wrong") into precise action ("add acid" or "use more fat").

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Taste and Identify the Problem

1. **Taste the dish.** Take a clean spoonful and eat it. Notice your immediate reaction. Is it pleasant? Flat? Greasy? Sharp? Write one or two words describing what you taste.

2. **Identify what is *not* working.** Ask yourself: Does this taste good but something feels missing? Does it taste actively bad in a specific way? Is the texture off? Separate texture problems from flavor problems — this lens addresses flavor.

3. **Describe the sensation in each category below. For each, decide if it is present, absent, or excessive:**
   - **Salt:** Do the flavors feel amplified and present, or muted and dull?
   - **Fat:** Is there richness and mouth-feel, or does the dish feel dry or too lean?
   - **Acid:** Is there brightness, liveliness, and a sense of "pop," or does it taste heavy and flat?
   - **Heat:** Are the flavors developed and complex, or raw and one-note?

### Phase 2 — Diagnosis

4. **For each of the four elements, rate it as deficient, adequate, or excessive.** Use this rubric:
   - **Deficient:** The dish noticeably lacks this element. Removing it further would make things worse. Adding it would improve the dish.
   - **Adequate:** The element is present in the right proportion. The dish would not improve if you added more.
   - **Excessive:** The element dominates and masks other flavors. Reducing it would improve the dish.

5. **Identify which deficient elements are the largest problems.** Rank them by impact. A dish that is both under-salted and under-acidic will usually benefit most from addressing the salt first (salt amplifies all other flavors, including acid), then the acid second.

6. **Consider the cooking method's role.** Ask: Has the heat been applied long enough to develop flavor? Is the dish still warm enough to taste its full flavor? (Cold dishes taste different from warm ones.) Did the cooking method require fat as a vehicle for heat? These questions help distinguish "this element is missing" from "this cooking step was incomplete."

### Phase 3 — Correction

7. **Add or adjust the deficient element in small increments.** Taste after each change. Do not add multiple elements at once — you will not know which one made the difference.
   - To add **salt:** Use kosher salt or sea salt (finer table salt can oversalt quickly). Start with 1/4 teaspoon per serving and taste.
   - To add **fat:** Use butter, oil, cream, or fat from the protein. Fat dissolves into the dish — it does not sit on top. Start with 1 teaspoon per serving.
   - To add **acid:** Use lemon juice, vinegar, or other acid. Acid is sharp and changes flavor instantly. Start with 1/2 teaspoon per serving, or a small squeeze of lemon.
   - To develop **heat:** Return the dish to heat and cook longer. This is a slower adjustment than the other three. If the dish is already fully cooked and the heat was sufficient, this element is not your problem.

8. **Taste again after each adjustment.** The goal is not a dramatic change — it is a subtle return to balance. You should notice the other flavors coming forward, not a strong taste of salt or lemon.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Dish was tasted directly (not imagined) | Y/N |
| Each of the four elements was rated independently | Y/N |
| At least one deficient element was identified | Y/N |
| Corrections were made in small increments | Y/N |
| Taste feedback was collected after each adjustment | Y/N |
| Final result shows improvement in balance and clarity | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The dish was "fixed" by adding more salt, fat, *and* acid simultaneously. You cannot diagnose the real problem this way.
- After adding an element, the dish tastes worse (sharper, greasier, saltier). This usually means that element was already present in sufficient quantity — you misdiagnosed.
- The problem was solved by cooking longer or raising the heat, but you attributed it to adding one of the four elements. The element adjustment had no effect; the heat development did.
- Every deficient element was rated as "salt." This is unlikely. The four elements play different roles; imbalance usually involves more than one.
- The dish tasted fine before adjustment and worse after. Either the diagnosis was wrong or the adjustment was too aggressive (added too much in one step).

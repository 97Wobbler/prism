# Thai Flavor Balance

## Overview
Thai cuisine rests on five taste dimensions—salty, sour, spicy, bitter, and sweet—that must work in concert rather than dominate individually. This lens provides a concrete, repeatable procedure for tasting a dish and diagnosing which dimension is out of place. Unlike subjective assessments ("this needs something"), the framework isolates the specific taste axis causing the imbalance and suggests concrete adjustments. Practitioners use it when a dish tastes wrong but the problem isn't obvious, or when developing a recipe and needing to dial in flavors with precision.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Calibration (prepare your palate)

1. **Cleanse your palate.** Eat a small piece of plain white rice or drink room-temperature water. Wait 30 seconds. This resets your taste receptors and reduces carryover from previous foods.

2. **Identify reference standards for each taste.** Taste small amounts of:
   - **Salty:** A pinch of sea salt on your tongue, or a spoonful of fish sauce (nam pla)
   - **Sour:** A few drops of fresh lime juice or a small piece of lime
   - **Spicy:** A thin slice of Thai red or green chili (adjust for your tolerance, but feel the heat)
   - **Bitter:** A tiny piece of bitter melon (bitter greens work too) or a small sip of black coffee
   - **Sweet:** A small piece of palm sugar, honey, or a ripe mango
   Write down how each feels on your palate — location, intensity, duration.

3. **Record baseline.** Before tasting the dish, note: dish temperature, main ingredients you expect, and any obvious flavors you anticipate. This prevents confirmation bias.

### Phase 2 — Systematic tasting

4. **Take a small spoonful.** 1–2 teaspoons of the dish. Do not eat it yet.

5. **Smell first.** Hold it near your nose for 2 seconds. Note the aroma. Smell accounts for ~80% of taste, so this is not secondary.

6. **Place on your tongue (center).**  Let it sit for 2 seconds without swallowing. Notice:
   - Which area of the tongue reacts first (tip = sweet/salty, sides = sour, back = bitter)?
   - Does the heat from spice build or peak immediately?
   - Is there a coating sensation (oils, richness) that masks other tastes?

7. **Chew slowly** (5–8 chews). Some tastes emerge only during chewing as flavors develop. Note what changes as you chew.

8. **Swallow and observe the finish.** What lingers after swallowing? Many dishes hide a bitter or ashy finish that dominates the memory even if the initial taste was pleasant.

9. **Rate each of the five dimensions** on a scale of 1–5, where 3 is "present and balanced" and 1 and 5 are extremes:
   - **Salty:** 1 (absent) → 2 (mild) → 3 (just right) → 4 (noticeable) → 5 (overwhelming)
   - **Sour:** 1 (absent) → 2 (subtle) → 3 (bright) → 4 (puckering) → 5 (mouth-puckeringly sharp)
   - **Spicy:** 1 (absent) → 2 (gentle warmth) → 3 (pleasant heat) → 4 (intense heat) → 5 (unbearable)
   - **Bitter:** 1 (absent) → 2 (subtle) → 3 (minor note) → 4 (noticeable) → 5 (overwhelming)
   - **Sweet:** 1 (absent) → 2 (subtle) → 3 (present, supporting) → 4 (noticeable) → 5 (candy-like)

10. **Cleanse and repeat.** Wait 30 seconds, drink water, and taste again if the first tasting was unclear. Note whether your second tasting confirms or revises your first ratings.

### Phase 3 — Diagnosis and adjustment

11. **Identify the primary imbalance.** Look for any dimension rated 1 or 5. If multiple are at extremes, treat the most offensive one first.

12. **Cross-reference the imbalance with the ingredient list.** A high salty rating should correlate with fish sauce, soy sauce, or salt added; if it doesn't, the salt may be from concentrated stock or fermented ingredients. This reveals whether the imbalance is intentional or accidental.

13. **Propose a single adjustment.** Do not fix multiple dimensions at once. For each imbalanced dimension:
   - **Too salty (5):** Reduce fish sauce or salt by 25%, or add a teaspoon of lime juice to counteract salinity perception.
   - **Not salty enough (1):** Add 1 tsp fish sauce or ½ tsp salt; retest after stirring and letting it sit 1 minute.
   - **Too sour (5):** Add a pinch of sugar or 1 tbsp cream/coconut milk to soften acidity.
   - **Not sour enough (1):** Add 1 tbsp lime or tamarind juice.
   - **Too spicy (5):** Add coconut milk, cream, or a starchy element (potato, noodles) to dilute heat.
   - **Not spicy enough (1):** Add fresh chili, chili paste, or white pepper; retest immediately.
   - **Too bitter (5):** Add sweetness (palm sugar, honey) or fat (coconut milk); check for burnt or over-extracted ingredients and remove or reduce them.
   - **Not bitter enough (1):** This is rarely a problem; bitter often emerges from long cooking or bitter greens. Leave as is unless the dish tastes flat.
   - **Too sweet (5):** Add salt, acid (lime), or spice to balance; reduce added sugars.
   - **Not sweet enough (1):** Add palm sugar, honey, or ripe fruit; 1 tsp at a time.

14. **Retest after adjustment.** Repeat the tasting procedure (steps 1–10). Record new ratings and compare to previous ones.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| All five dimensions are rated (none left blank) | Y/N |
| Ratings are justified by specific sensations (not generic) | Y/N |
| Primary imbalance is clearly identified (at least one dimension ≠ 3) | Y/N |
| Adjustment proposed is a single ingredient change, not a recipe rewrite | Y/N |
| Retest ratings are recorded and show movement toward 3 | Y/N |
| Taste was evaluated in three phases: smell, initial, finish | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All five dimensions are rated 3 (perfectly balanced). Either the dish is exceptionally well-made (rare on first iteration) or the rater was not tasting carefully.
- Ratings cluster at 1 or 5 across multiple dimensions. This usually signals a misunderstanding of the scale or palate fatigue. Cleanse and restart.
- An adjustment was proposed but no retest was performed. Without retest, you don't know if the fix worked or made it worse.
- The dish has a high bitter or salty rating but the ingredient list doesn't explain it. Suspect overcooked aromatics, burnt garlic/shallots, or old spices. These require removal, not a simple salt/acid adjustment.
- Sweet is rated 5 and sour is rated 1 simultaneously. This is almost never an imbalance to fix — it's a dessert or a dish heading in that direction. Clarify intent before adjusting.

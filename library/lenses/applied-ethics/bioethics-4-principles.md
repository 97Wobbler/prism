# Bioethics 4 Principles (Beauchamp-Childress)

## Overview
The Beauchamp-Childress framework assesses ethical decisions in healthcare and biomedical research against four prima facie duties: Autonomy (respect for persons' self-determination), Beneficence (act in others' best interest), Non-maleficence (avoid harm), and Justice (fair distribution of benefits and burdens). Practitioners use this when a medical decision, policy, or research protocol involves stakeholder disagreement or when the right course is unclear because multiple ethical values pull in different directions. The discipline is not picking a winner early — it is documenting how each principle applies, where they conflict, and what weights and exceptions you are actually claiming.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Extract the Case
1. **Write the decision or action in one sentence.** Example: "Withdraw life support from a patient in permanent vegetative state against the family's wishes."

2. **Identify the stakeholders.** List the people or groups affected: patient, family, clinicians, institution, public, etc.

3. **Note what each stakeholder wants or claims.** Write their stated interest, not your interpretation of it.

### Phase 2 — Score Each Principle

For each of the four principles, work through the questions below. Score each principle as **fully applicable**, **partly applicable**, or **not applicable** based on what the case reveals. Write your reasoning for every answer.

#### Autonomy — Respect for self-determination
- Does the patient have decision-making capacity (able to understand, retain, reason about, and communicate a choice)?
- Has the patient expressed a preference, advance directive, or value statement about this decision?
- If the patient cannot decide, is there a valid surrogate (designated agent, family member per hierarchy)?
- Does the proposed action align with the patient's known values, preferences, or prior autonomous choices?
- **Score:** Does this case require respecting someone's autonomous choice? How clearly?

#### Beneficence — Act in the patient's best interest
- What is the patient's medical state, prognosis, and realistic goals of care?
- What are the clinical benefits of the proposed action (cure, symptom relief, prolonged life, restoration of function)?
- Who defines "benefit" — the patient, the family, the clinician, or professional standards? Do they agree?
- Are there non-medical benefits relevant here (spiritual, relational, financial)?
- **Score:** Does this case call for acting to improve the patient's condition or wellbeing? How much weight does that carry?

#### Non-maleficence — Do no harm
- What are the risks, side effects, or harms of the proposed action?
- What are the harms of inaction or the alternative?
- Can harms be mitigated? At what cost?
- Does this action impose pain, loss of function, indignity, or existential suffering?
- **Score:** Does harm dominate the calculation? Is it avoidable?

#### Justice — Fair distribution and rights
- Who bears the costs and who receives the benefits of this decision? Is the distribution fair?
- Does the case involve unequal access to treatment, research enrollment, or resources?
- Are vulnerable populations (economically disadvantaged, cognitively impaired, prisoners, non-citizens) disproportionately affected?
- Does the decision reflect or challenge systemic inequities in healthcare?
- **Score:** Is fairness in play? Are rights or equity at stake?

### Phase 3 — Map Conflicts

4. **For each pair of principles, note whether they point in the same or opposite directions.**

| Principle pair | Alignment | Tension | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Autonomy ↔ Beneficence | Same / Opposite | High / Medium / Low / None | <...> |
| Autonomy ↔ Non-maleficence | | | |
| Autonomy ↔ Justice | | | |
| Beneficence ↔ Non-maleficence | | | |
| Beneficence ↔ Justice | | | |
| Non-maleficence ↔ Justice | | | |

5. **Identify the dominant conflict.** Which pair of principles creates the hardest choice?

### Phase 4 — Justify the Resolution

6. **For each conflicting pair, state your weights explicitly.** Example: "In this case, I am prioritizing Autonomy over Beneficence because the patient has clear capacity and a documented preference, and respecting that choice is a non-negotiable right even if the clinician judges a different outcome would be medically better."

7. **Cite any exceptions or overrides you're claiming.** All four principles are prima facie — they can be overridden under specific conditions. If you override one, name the condition:
   - Autonomy can be limited if the patient lacks capacity, poses danger to others, or cannot consent (emergency).
   - Beneficence can be limited if it violates autonomy or imposes undue harm.
   - Non-maleficence can be accepted if the benefit outweighs the risk (e.g., surgery).
   - Justice can be limited in emergencies, but should be restored as soon as possible.

8. **Write the ethical justification.** Do not abstract or hide the weights. Example: "I recommend withdrawal of life support because the patient's autonomous choice (clear in writing) overrides the family's hope for recovery (Autonomy > Beneficence). The prolonged suffering and negligible prognosis also favor withdrawal (Non-maleficence supports Autonomy). This respects the patient's dignity and rights despite causing grief to the family."

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Case is stated in one plain sentence | Y/N |
| All stakeholders are named | Y/N |
| Autonomy is scored with evidence (capacity, preference, surrogate) | Y/N |
| Beneficence is scored with clinical facts (prognosis, goals, benefit definition) | Y/N |
| Non-maleficence is scored with harms of action and inaction both | Y/N |
| Justice is scored with attention to distribution and vulnerable populations | Y/N |
| All six principle pairs are mapped for conflict or alignment | Y/N |
| Weights are explicit (not hidden or left to inference) | Y/N |
| Any overrides or exceptions are named and justified | Y/N |
| Justification is written in prose, not bullet points | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All four principles come out "fully applicable" with no conflicts. Either the case is trivial (unlikely) or the analysis avoided hard choices (common). Push on the tensions.
- Autonomy is scored without evidence of capacity or a documented choice. "The patient would probably want..." is not Autonomy; it is Beneficence dressed up.
- Non-maleficence is scored only for the proposed action, never for the status quo. Inaction also has harms. If only one side shows harm, the analysis is one-sided.
- Justice is empty or generic ("everyone deserves care"). Look for actual distribution of burdens: Who gets treatment? Who pays? Who bears risk?
- Weights are stated as values ("respect autonomy") without saying *how much* weight it gets relative to the others. "I value both Autonomy and Beneficence equally" is evasion. Cases force a ranking.
- No override condition is named. If a principle is set aside, the reader must know under what condition and why that condition applies here.

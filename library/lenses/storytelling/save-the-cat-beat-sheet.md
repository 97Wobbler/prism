# Save the Cat Beat Sheet

## Overview
The Save the Cat Beat Sheet maps a complete screenplay onto 15 discrete structural moments (beats), each tied to a specific narrative function and ideal page count. Rather than thinking about acts or arbitrary divisions, this lens treats a story as a sequence of emotional turning points and functional scenes. Practitioners use it to diagnose a slugging script (beats are bunching), to stage-gate revisions (does beat 5 land on page 12±2?), and to retrofit classical narrative structure onto nontraditional premises.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Identify the Beats

Read or re-read the screenplay in full. As you watch/read, mark the moment where each of the following 15 beats occurs. If a beat is unclear or missing, note it. Use the definitions below to calibrate what counts as "the moment."

1. **Opening Image (0-1 pages).** A snapshot of the protagonist's world before the story changes. Often in ordinary life, often unhappy or incomplete. This is NOT a title card — it's a scene that shows us who the protagonist is and what their problem or emptiness looks like. Write the scene descriptor and page number.

2. **Catalyst / Inciting Incident (12±2 pages).** An event enters the protagonist's world that will force them to leave their ordinary life or change their perspective. This is the moment the story *begins*, not the moment they decide to act. Write what the catalyst is and what page it lands on.

3. **Debate (12-20 pages).** The protagonist hesitates, questions, gathers courage, or argues against what the catalyst is pushing them toward. This beat answers "Why doesn't the hero just do it immediately?" Write the nature of the doubt and the page range.

4. **Break into Two (20±1 pages).** The protagonist commits to the journey. They say yes, they cross the threshold, they make the choice to pursue the goal. This is active commitment, not passive surrender. Write the moment of commitment and its page.

5. **B Story (22 pages).** Introduce a character (mentor, love interest, rival, ally) who will embody a perspective or lesson the protagonist needs to learn. This is not a full scene necessarily, but the introduction of a character who will challenge or teach the hero. Write who this character is.

6. **Fun and Games (20-50 pages).** The protagonist pursues their new goal in the upward trajectory. This is what the audience came to see — the "promise of the premise." Include setbacks and smaller victories, but the trend is upward. Write 2-3 key scenes that typify this beat.

7. **Midpoint (50±2 pages).** Either a false victory (the hero seems to have won, but hasn't) or a false defeat (the hero seems to have lost, but hasn't). The stakes are raised, the emotional temperature spikes, and the audience's understanding of the goal or the cost shifts. Write what the false victory or defeat is.

8. **Bad Guys Close In (50-75 pages).** External pressure mounts. The opposition gains ground, allies doubt or leave, the hero's method stops working, or bad luck compounds. This is the moment when the heat is truly on. Write what pressure is mounting and from where.

9. **All Is Lost (75±2 pages).** A death moment — literal or metaphorical. A character dies, a relationship ends, the hero fails publicly, a deadline passes, a secret is exposed. This is the lowest point, the moment that cannot be undone. Write what is lost.

10. **Dark Night of the Soul (75-80 pages).** The protagonist grieves, despairs, or regroups. They are not yet changed; they are processing what was lost. This is the pause before the climb. Write how the protagonist responds to the loss.

11. **Break into Three (80±1 pages).** A new perspective arrives — from within the hero or from outside (the B Story character, a clue, a realization). The protagonist sees a path forward that they couldn't see before. This is the shift from despair to determination. Write what new insight or information arrives.

12. **Finale (80-99 pages).** The final push. The protagonist acts on their new understanding, confronts the opposition, and pursues the goal with everything they have. This is the climactic sequence; track the 3-5 key moments. Write the major beats of the finale.

13. **Final Image (99-100 pages).** A mirror of the Opening Image, showing how the protagonist or the world has changed. If the opening was sad, this is hopeful; if the opening was cynical, this is earnest. Write the scene and how it contrasts with beat 1.

### Phase 3 — Assess Emotional Arc

For each beat, write a one-sentence description of the protagonist's emotional or material state:

- After beat 1: <emotion/position>
- After beat 2: <emotion/position>
- ... continuing through beat 13

Plot these on a simple graph (page numbers on x-axis, emotional state on y-axis) to see if the arc is coherent: generally up during Fun and Games, a sharp dip at All Is Lost, a recovery and climb during Finale.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| All 15 beats are identified with a specific scene or moment | Y/N |
| Each beat has an associated page number (or page range for longer beats) | Y/N |
| At least 11 of 13 major timing beats fall within ±2 pages of target | Y/N |
| The emotional arc shows upward momentum before Midpoint and recovery after All Is Lost | Y/N |
| B Story character is clearly established by page 22–25 | Y/N |
| Opening Image and Final Image show a meaningful contrast | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Beats 2 and 4 (Catalyst and Break into Two) are on the same page or within 1 page. These should be 8+ pages apart; if they're together, the Debate beat was skipped or compressed to nothing.
- No identifiable All Is Lost moment. A story without a true low point feels incomplete and robs the Finale of its power.
- Fun and Games (beat 6) is shorter than 15 pages. This is the "promise of the premise" — if it's rushed, the audience hasn't had enough time to engage.
- Midpoint (beat 7) falls outside the 48–52 page window. This beat is the fulcrum of the entire story; if it's early, the second half drags; if it's late, the first half is overweight.
- Opening Image and Final Image are nearly identical. There should be a clear before-and-after contrast that shows what the protagonist learned or how the world changed.
- Beats are bunching (e.g., All Is Lost, Dark Night, and Break into Three all happen within 3 pages). Snyder's timing creates breathing room; when beats compress, scenes feel rushed and emotional beats land flat.

# Contract Analysis

## Overview
A contract exists only when six necessary elements align: an offer was made, accepted without material alteration, in exchange for valuable consideration, with genuine mutual assent, by parties with legal capacity, and for a purpose that is not illegal. Missing or ambiguous any one element breaks the chain. Practitioners use this lens when a disputed agreement needs to be evaluated for enforceability, when a party claims they never agreed to terms, or when ambiguity about the contract's scope creates implementation risk. The discipline is checking each element against evidence in the record — not assumption, not reasonable intent, but what was actually communicated and done.

## Analytical Procedure

### Element 1 — Offer

1. **Identify the alleged offeror.** Who made the statement or conduct?

2. **Extract the proposed terms.** Write out:
   - The core obligation(s) being proposed
   - The price or exchange (money, services, goods, forbearance)
   - Any conditions precedent or subsequent
   - Any deadline or termination condition
   
   If a term is missing (e.g., price unspecified), note it explicitly.

3. **Test for definiteness.** Does the offer contain enough specificity that a court could enforce it without further negotiation? Check:
   - Is the subject matter clearly identified?
   - Is the price or consideration clear or calculable?
   - Are performance standards measurable or objective?
   
   If three or more material terms are blank, the offer is not binding; note this.

4. **Check for communication.** Was the offer actually communicated to the alleged offeree? If communication is indirect (posted, published, third-party relay), note whether the offeree had reasonable notice.

5. **Assess revocation or expiration.** Did the offeror revoke the offer before acceptance? Did the offer expire by its own terms or by lapse of reasonable time? If revoked before acceptance, no contract can form from that offer.

### Element 2 — Acceptance

6. **Identify the alleged acceptor.** Who responded to the offer?

7. **Extract the acceptance.** Write down the words or conduct offered in response. Check whether the acceptance:
   - Unconditionally agrees to the exact terms of the offer, OR
   - Contains a material modification (new or different term)
   
   If material terms differ, this is a counter-offer, not an acceptance. Note the differences.

8. **Verify the acceptance was communicated.** Was the offeror informed of the acceptance? Did the offeree use a method of communication the offeror authorized or that is customary for this type of transaction?

9. **Check the timing.** Did acceptance occur before the offer expired or was revoked?

10. **Test the mode of acceptance.** If the offer specified "by email" and the acceptor mailed a letter instead, acceptance may fail (unless the deviation is immaterial or waived). Note any mismatch.

### Element 3 — Consideration

11. **Identify what each party gave, promised, or gave up.** For the offeree (acceptor):
    - Money paid?
    - Services rendered or promised?
    - Goods delivered or promised?
    - A right or claim waived or limited?
    - Forbearance from action (e.g., agreeing not to sue)?
    
    For the offeror:
    - What do they receive in return?

12. **Test for sufficiency.** Consideration must have some value in the economic or legal sense, but it need not be equal to what the other party receives. Ask:
    - Did the acceptor receive something they did not already have a legal right to receive?
    - Did the offeror receive something of value (however small) or a legal detriment?
    
    If one party receives something they already had a duty to provide or do, that is not consideration.

13. **Watch for pre-existing duty.** If Party A promises to do something they are already legally obligated to do (by law, prior contract, or court order), that promise is not consideration for a new exchange. Note this separately.

14. **Check for past consideration.** If Party B gave something valuable in the past and Party A is now promising payment "in return," that promise is enforceable only in limited circumstances (e.g., moral obligation with some written acknowledgment, or restitution for unjust enrichment). Flag this as weak consideration.

### Element 4 — Assent (Meeting of the Minds)

15. **Reconstruct what each party understood at the moment of acceptance.** Write down:
    - What did the offeror believe they were agreeing to?
    - What did the acceptor believe they were agreeing to?
    
    Use the parties' own words from emails, notes, conversations, or testimony — not what they claim now.

16. **Check for ambiguity.** If the contract language is ambiguous, ask:
    - Could a reasonable person interpret the term in two or more substantially different ways?
    - Did the parties use the same interpretation at the time they signed or accepted?
    - Is there extrinsic evidence (prior dealings, industry custom, negotiations) that clarifies what they actually intended?
    
    If there is genuine ambiguity and the parties' understanding differed materially, mutual assent may be absent.

17. **Assess good faith.** Did one party intentionally conceal the meaning of a term or exploit the other's misreading? If yes, assent under that term may be voidable (rescissible).

### Element 5 — Legal Capacity

18. **Check the offeror's capacity.** Was the offeror:
    - Of legal age (18+ in most U.S. states)?
    - Of sound mind (not adjudicated incompetent)?
    - Not under the influence of duress, undue influence, or fraud?
    - A legal entity with authority to bind itself (e.g., a corporation, if corporate authority is in question)?
    
    Minors and adjudicated incompetents cannot form binding contracts. Duress or fraud vitiates capacity.

19. **Check the acceptor's capacity.** Apply the same test to the person or entity accepting the offer.

20. **Identify any guardianship or agency issues.** If one party was represented by a guardian, agent, or attorney, was that representative authorized to bind the principal? If authority is unclear, note it as a defect.

### Element 6 — Legality

21. **Check the subject matter and purpose.** Is the contract for:
    - An illegal act or transaction (e.g., drug sales, theft)?
    - A violation of public policy (e.g., price-fixing, enforced non-compete outside reasonable bounds)?
    - Performance that violates a statute or regulation?
    
    If yes, the contract is void and unenforceable. Write the specific statute or policy violated.

22. **Check consideration legality.** Is the consideration itself unlawful? (E.g., one party is paying the other to commit a crime.) If yes, the contract fails.

23. **Assess regulatory compliance.** Even if the contract's purpose is lawful, does performance require a license, permit, or approval that is absent or suspended? Document any gaps.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Element | Check | Pass |
|---|---|---|
| **Offer** | Offer includes definite terms (subject, price, conditions) or framework allowing enforcement | Y/N |
| **Offer** | Offer was communicated to the offeree | Y/N |
| **Offer** | Offer was not revoked or expired before acceptance | Y/N |
| **Acceptance** | Acceptance unconditionally agreed to exact terms (or counter-offer was accepted) | Y/N |
| **Acceptance** | Acceptance was communicated to offeror | Y/N |
| **Acceptance** | Acceptance occurred before offer expired | Y/N |
| **Consideration** | Both parties gave, promised, or gave up something of value | Y/N |
| **Consideration** | Consideration was not pre-existing duty or past act | Y/N |
| **Assent** | Both parties' understanding of key terms aligned at time of acceptance | Y/N |
| **Assent** | No material ambiguity left unresolved | Y/N |
| **Capacity** | Both parties had legal capacity to contract | Y/N |
| **Capacity** | No duress, undue influence, or fraud vitiates consent | Y/N |
| **Legality** | Contract purpose is lawful | Y/N |
| **Legality** | Performance does not violate statute or public policy | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- **Offer has no deadline and no revocation.** The offeror claims it lapsed, the offeree claims it is still open. Without evidence of express termination or a lapse of "reasonable time," enforceability is uncertain.
- **Acceptance contains material changes.** The acceptor added conditions, changed price, or altered scope. This is a counter-offer, not acceptance — no contract exists unless the offeror's silence or conduct accepts the counter.
- **Consideration is asserted but vague.** "We agreed in exchange for good faith efforts" or "mutual benefit" is not specific enough. Consideration requires something concrete.
- **Pre-existing duty problem unaddressed.** A contractor agrees to finish work "on schedule" — but they already have that obligation in the original contract. A new promise to do so is not consideration.
- **Assent assumed without evidence.** Testimony that "everyone agreed" or "it was understood" without documented contemporaneous evidence (email, notes, meeting record) is weak.
- **Capacity issue missed.** One party was a minor, or a representative without written authority, or a corporation acting outside its certificate of incorporation. Enforceability fails.
- **Illegality buried in performance.** The contract looks lawful on its face (e.g., "provide consulting"), but performance requires the offeree to violate an NDA with a third party or flout a regulation. The contract is void.

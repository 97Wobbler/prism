# Statutory Interpretation Canons

## Overview
Statutory interpretation canons are rules of thumb for reading legislation when the text is ambiguous or when ordinary meaning fails to produce a coherent result. They include textual principles (ejusdem generis, expressio unius, noscitur a sociis), structural principles (rule of lenity, constitutional avoidance), and purposive principles (mischief rule, golden rule). Practitioners use these when judicial review requires a defensible reading of legislative language, especially when multiple readings are plausible. The discipline is systematic application — cherry-picking canons to reach a predetermined outcome is a failure of the method.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Extract the Interpretive Problem

1. **State the statutory text in question.** Quote the exact provision. If it's part of a larger section, include immediate context (the preceding and following sentences).

2. **Identify the word, phrase, or structure that is ambiguous.** Mark it explicitly. Describe what makes it ambiguous: does it have multiple dictionary meanings? Does it create internal contradiction with another part of the statute? Does its application to a concrete case yield absurd results?

3. **Enumerate all plausible readings.** For each reading, state:
   - The literal meaning it produces
   - A concrete case where this reading would apply
   - Whether this reading creates internal tension elsewhere in the statute

### Phase 2 — Apply Textual Canons

4. **Ejusdem Generis (of the same kind).** If the text lists specific items followed by a general term ("automobiles, trucks, motorcycles, and other vehicles"), does the general term include only items of the same class as the specifics, or can it reach beyond? List which reading applies here.

5. **Expressio Unius Est Exclusio Alterius (expression of one thing excludes others).** If the statute lists categories but omits a category that one reading would include, does the omission signal intentional exclusion or mere silence? Determine which reading this principle favors.

6. **Noscitur a Sociis (a word is known by its companions).** Does the term in question appear alongside other words that illuminate its meaning? Does the context narrow or broaden its ordinary definition?

7. **Rule of the Last Antecedent.** If the ambiguous term modifies multiple antecedent clauses, which one does it most naturally modify based on proximity and grammar? Write the parsing it implies.

### Phase 3 — Apply Structural and Purposive Canons

8. **Rule of Lenity (in dubio, pro reo).** If the statute is criminal or penal in nature and the ambiguity remains unresolved by textual canons, does fairness demand that the ambiguity be read against the government (i.e., in favor of the defendant)? Note whether this principle applies.

9. **Constitutional Avoidance.** Does one reading raise serious constitutional doubts while the other avoids them? If yes, enumerate the constitutional problem and note that avoidance canon may favor the non-constitutional reading.

10. **Mischief Rule / Golden Rule.** What harm or mischief was the statute intended to remedy? Does one reading better achieve that purpose than the other? Conversely, does one reading produce an absurd result that no legislature would have intended? Document this finding.

11. **Statutory Purpose and Harmonization.** Read the statute as a whole, including its preamble, purpose clause (if any), and related sections. Does the statute's overall design favor one reading over the other?

### Phase 4 — Evaluate Canon Conflict and Weighting

12. **Map which canons favor each reading.** Create a table:
    | Interpretive Problem | Reading A | Reading B | Supporting Canons | Opposing Canons |
    |---|---|---|---|---|
    | <ambiguity> | <meaning> | <meaning> | Textual/Structural/Purposive | Textual/Structural/Purposive |

13. **Identify canon conflicts.** Do textual canons pull in one direction while purposive canons pull in another? Note this explicitly. There is no meta-rule that textual canons always win; modern jurisprudence recognizes both textual and purposive tools as legitimate.

14. **Apply the tie-breaker hierarchy (if applicable to your jurisdiction):**
    - Modern textualism (Scalia school): Textual canons first; purposive canons only if text is genuinely ambiguous after textual analysis.
    - Traditional holistic reading: Textual and purposive canons applied together; purpose informs how to read text.
    - Note your jurisdiction's preference and explain why one reading aligns better with it.

### Phase 5 — Construct the Interpretation and Defend It

15. **Write the winning interpretation.** This is the reading that the canons (weighted by your jurisdiction's methodology) most strongly support. It must be stated precisely enough that a lawyer could cite it in a brief.

16. **Acknowledge the losing reading(s).** Explain what canon(s) support it and why you found them less weighty. A weak interpretation is not a bad one — you must show you considered it seriously.

17. **Note ambiguity that persists.** If the canons do not resolve the question, say so. This is not a failure; it signals that the statute may need legislative amendment or that further evidence (legislative history, agency interpretation) may be required.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Ambiguous text quoted verbatim with surrounding context | Y/N |
| All plausible readings enumerated with concrete application examples | Y/N |
| All six textual canons (ejusdem generis, expressio unius, noscitur a sociis, last antecedent, lenity, constitutional avoidance) were considered | Y/N |
| Mischief rule / golden rule applied with identification of statutory purpose | Y/N |
| Canon conflicts identified and acknowledged where they exist | Y/N |
| Winning interpretation stated precisely enough to be cited in a legal brief | Y/N |
| Losing reading(s) acknowledged with supporting canons listed | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Only textual canons were applied; statutory purpose was never examined. Context matters, especially for broad remedial statutes.
- A canon was applied selectively to reach a predetermined outcome. Check: would the same canon logic apply consistently to other provisions in the statute?
- The "winning" interpretation rests on a single canon while stronger canons point the other way. Explain the weighting or concede the ambiguity.
- Legislative history was cited but never distinguished from the statutory text itself. The text is the law; legislative history is secondary and often contradictory.
- The interpretation, if applied broadly, would render other statutory provisions nonsensical or contradictory. The statute must be read as a coherent whole.
- No acknowledgment of the losing reading. Courts expect interpreters to engage seriously with counter-arguments.

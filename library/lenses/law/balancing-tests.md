# Balancing Tests

## Overview
Balancing tests are the primary mechanism by which courts evaluate whether laws or regulations survive constitutional scrutiny. Rather than applying a single standard, courts apply one of three graduated tests depending on the classification or right at stake: strict scrutiny (most demanding), intermediate scrutiny (moderate), or rational basis review (most deferential). Each test weighs the government's stated purpose against the means chosen to achieve it. Practitioners—including litigators, legislative drafters, and policy analysts—use this framework to predict how a court will likely review a challenged law and to identify vulnerabilities in a regulation's justification.

## Analytical Procedure

### Step 1 — Identify the Classification or Right Under Challenge

1. **Identify what the law classifies, restricts, or burdens.** Is it:
   - A suspect class (race, national origin, alienage)? 
   - A quasi-suspect class (gender, legitimacy)?
   - A fundamental right (voting, interstate travel, access to courts, privacy)?
   - A generally applicable regulation affecting speech, association, or religion?
   - A regulation affecting a non-fundamental right or non-suspect class?
   
   Write down the classification or right in plain terms.

2. **Check whether the classification/right has established precedent.** Search case law for identical or closely analogous classifications. If precedent assigns it a tier (strict, intermediate, rational basis), note that. If not, you will infer it in Step 2.

### Step 2 — Assign the Appropriate Standard of Review

3. **Apply Strict Scrutiny if any of the following is true:**
   - The law classifies on a suspect ground (race, national origin, alienage in most contexts).
   - The law burdens a fundamental right without less-restrictive alternatives.
   - The law is content-based and restricts speech.
   
   If strict scrutiny applies, proceed to Step 3.

4. **Apply Intermediate Scrutiny if any of the following is true:**
   - The law classifies on a quasi-suspect ground (gender, legitimacy).
   - The law is content-neutral but substantially burdens speech (time/place/manner).
   - The law burdens important (but not fundamental) rights without adequate fit.
   
   If intermediate scrutiny applies, proceed to Step 3.

5. **Apply Rational Basis Review if:**
   - The law affects a non-suspect class and a non-fundamental right.
   - The law is generally applicable and not directed at a protected class or right.
   
   If rational basis applies, proceed to Step 3.

### Step 3 — Articulate the Government's Purpose

6. **Identify the government's stated purpose.** Extract this from:
   - Legislative history (committee reports, floor statements, preamble).
   - Agency declarations in regulatory filings or judicial briefs.
   - The face of the statute if purpose is self-evident.
   
   Write the purpose in one sentence. If multiple purposes are claimed, list them separately and evaluate each.

7. **Assess legitimacy of the purpose:**
   - **Under strict or intermediate scrutiny:** Is the purpose compelling or important? Not merely incidental. Write whether it passes this threshold and why (or why not).
   - **Under rational basis:** Is the purpose at least conceivably legitimate? (This is nearly always satisfied; rational basis challenges typically fail on fit, not purpose.)

### Step 4 — Evaluate the Fit Between Means and Purpose

8. **Under Strict Scrutiny — Narrow Tailoring:**
   - Is the law narrowly tailored to serve the compelling interest?
   - Does it target the evil in the narrowest practicable terms?
   - Are there less-restrictive alternatives that would serve the purpose with less burden?
   - Would a smaller classification, shorter duration, or exemption preserve the purpose?
   
   For each alternative, ask: Could the government achieve its purpose without burdening this group/right? If yes, note it as a less-restrictive alternative. Strict scrutiny is passed only if no plausible less-restrictive alternative exists.

9. **Under Intermediate Scrutiny — Substantial Relation to Important Interest:**
   - Does the law materially advance the important government interest?
   - Is the fit between means and purpose direct and substantial (not remote or speculative)?
   - Could the government achieve the interest with significantly less restriction? If yes, the fit fails.
   
   Intermediate scrutiny asks whether a closer fit is available without defeating the government's purpose. Narrow tailoring is not required, but there must not be a patently obvious less-restrictive alternative.

10. **Under Rational Basis — Any Conceivable Rational Relation:**
    - Is there any rational connection between the law and the stated purpose?
    - Could the legislature rationally believe this law would advance the purpose, even if evidence doesn't support it?
    - Would a rational legislator view this as a reasonable, if imperfect, means?
    
    Under rational basis, the government need only show that the law is not wholly irrational. A weak fit usually passes.

11. **If the stated purpose appears pretextual,** flag it:
    - Is the stated purpose consistent with how the law actually operates?
    - Does the law burden the named purpose but not others similarly situated?
    - Is the purpose inconsistent with the legislative record?
    - Would a rational legislator adopt this means to achieve the stated purpose, or does the fit suggest the real purpose is unstated?
    
    Pretextual purpose undermines all standards of review.

### Step 5 — Generate the Verdict

12. **Record whether the law passes or fails under the assigned standard:**
    - Strict scrutiny: Passes only if purpose is compelling AND law is narrowly tailored with no less-restrictive alternatives.
    - Intermediate scrutiny: Passes if purpose is important AND law substantially relates to purpose with no significantly less-restrictive alternative.
    - Rational basis: Passes if purpose is conceivably legitimate AND law bears some rational relation to purpose.

13. **Document assumptions and vulnerabilities.** Note:
    - Which standard of review is most debatable (e.g., is this truly a quasi-suspect class?).
    - Which purposes are most vulnerable to challenge as pretextual or insufficiently important.
    - Which less-restrictive alternatives are most plausible.
    - Whether evidence in the record supports the fit (or contradicts it).

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Classification or right under challenge is clearly identified | Y/N |
| Appropriate standard of review (strict/intermediate/rational basis) is assigned with justification | Y/N |
| Government's stated purpose is extracted and rendered in one sentence | Y/N |
| Purpose legitimacy is evaluated against the assigned standard's threshold | Y/N |
| Fit analysis applies the correct standard (narrow tailoring / substantial relation / rational relation) | Y/N |
| At least one less-restrictive alternative is identified and evaluated (strict/intermediate) | Y/N |
| Pretextuality analysis is conducted if purpose/operation mismatch is apparent | Y/N |
| Final verdict is stated and justified with reference to all components | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The standard of review is never explicitly named or is assigned without justification. The entire analysis depends on getting this right.
- The government's "purpose" is vague, multiple, or inconsistent. Demand a single, clear statement. If the government offers multiple purposes, evaluate each separately.
- Under strict or intermediate scrutiny, no less-restrictive alternative is considered or discussed. This is the crux of the analysis; omitting it is analysis failure.
- The fit analysis conflates the three standards. "The law rationally relates to the purpose" is not a strict scrutiny conclusion; it will fail strict scrutiny.
- Pretextuality is not examined even though the law's actual operation contradicts its stated purpose. If a law ostensibly serves one interest but burdens only a specific group unrelated to that interest, pretextuality must be addressed.
- The verdict is stated without tracing it back to the elements (purpose + fit) of the standard applied. A bare "the law will likely be upheld" is not analysis.
- Evidence or legislative history that contradicts the stated purpose is ignored or minimized.

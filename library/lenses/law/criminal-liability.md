# Criminal Liability Assessment

## Overview

Criminal liability requires five concurrent elements: a voluntary act (actus reus), a culpable mental state (mens rea), causation linking the act to harm, the harm itself, and concurrence between act and intent at the moment of conduct. A charge succeeds only if all five are present and proven beyond reasonable doubt. Practitioners use this lens to pressure-test prosecutorial cases, identify defensible vulnerabilities, or teach doctrine. The discipline is interrogating each element separately before reaching a conclusion.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish the Alleged Conduct

1. **Identify the specific criminal statute.** Write its name, jurisdiction, and the statutory language verbatim. If the statute has subsections, note which applies.

2. **Describe the factual narrative in plain language.** Who did what, to whom, when, and with what outcome? Remove legal jargon. Write 2-3 sentences maximum.

3. **Identify the alleged harm.** What injury, loss, or danger does the statute aim to prevent? (e.g., death, bodily injury, property loss, bodily autonomy violation.)

### Phase 2 — Interrogate Actus Reus (Guilty Act)

4. **Isolate the required act or omission.** What physical or omissive conduct does the statute require? List the elements:
   - Is a specific act required (e.g., "strike," "take," "enter")? Or is any act that produces the result sufficient?
   - Does the statute include omission liability (failure to act)? If so, under what duty?
   - Is the act required to be "voluntary"? Apply the test: could the defendant have chosen not to do it?

5. **Check for actus reus defects.** For each of the following, answer Y/N and justify:
   - Was the act (or omission) voluntary, or was it reflexive, coerced, or involuntary?
   - If omission, did the defendant owe a legal duty to the victim? (Common sources: statute, contract, special relationship, prior conduct creating danger.)
   - If the statute defines a result crime (e.g., "causing death"), was the prohibited result actually produced?

### Phase 3 — Interrogate Mens Rea (Guilty Mind)

6. **Identify the required mental state.** Consult the statute. Common formulations:
   - **Specific intent:** the defendant intended both the act and its result (e.g., theft requires intent to permanently deprive).
   - **General intent (recklessness/negligence):** the defendant acted with awareness of risk (recklessness) or should have been aware (negligence).
   - **Strict liability:** no mental state required (rare; usually regulatory).
   - **Transferred intent:** intent to harm one person satisfies the requirement even if a different person was harmed.

7. **Assess the evidence for mens rea.** List direct and circumstantial evidence:
   - Direct: confessions, statements showing intent.
   - Circumstantial: motive, preparation, knowledge, prior similar conduct, silence or flight, concealment.
   - Ask for each piece: does it tend to prove intent, recklessness, or negligence? Or is it ambiguous?

8. **Check for mens rea defects.** For each of the following, answer Y/N and justify:
   - Is the evidence sufficient to infer the required mental state beyond a reasonable doubt?
   - Did the defendant lack capacity to form the intent (infancy, insanity, intoxication if relevant)?
   - Is there an honest but unreasonable mistake of fact that negates intent? (Depends on jurisdiction and offense type.)

### Phase 4 — Interrogate Causation

9. **Trace the causal chain from act to harm.**
   - What is the immediate result of the defendant's act?
   - What intervening events, if any, occurred between the act and the final harm?
   - Was the harm a foreseeable result of the act, or was it highly extraordinary?

10. **Apply the causation tests.** For each, answer Y/N:
    - **Cause-in-fact ("but-for" test):** But for the defendant's act, would the harm have occurred? (If yes, there is no cause-in-fact.)
    - **Proximate cause (legal cause):** Was the harm a foreseeable result of the act, or was it so remote or dependent on intervening acts that holding the defendant liable would be unjust?
    - **Intervening acts:** If someone or something else acted between the defendant's conduct and the harm, was that act foreseeable or unforeseeable? (Foreseeable intervening acts do not break the chain; unforeseeable ones usually do.)

11. **Check for causation defects.** For each, answer Y/N and justify:
    - Is there a causal connection between act and harm, or is the connection speculative?
    - If causation is indirect (e.g., A tells B who tells C who injures D), is liability still fair?
    - Is the harm so far removed in time or space that proximate cause is broken?

### Phase 5 — Interrogate Concurrence

12. **Verify concurrence in time and intent.**
    - At the moment the defendant committed the act (or omission), did the defendant possess the required mental state?
    - If the mental state arose before or after the act, concurrence is broken.
    - Example: D intends to kill V, but the gun misfires harmlessly. Later, D negligently hits V with a car and kills him. The intent existed but did not concur with the negligent act.

13. **Check for concurrence defects.** For each, answer Y/N and justify:
    - Was the required mens rea present at the precise moment of the actus reus?
    - If intent shifted between planning and execution, does the final intent align with the final act?

### Phase 6 — Synthesize

14. **Create a liability matrix.** For each of the five elements, record:
    - Requirement (what the statute demands)
    - Evidence (direct and circumstantial)
    - Verdict (present / absent / unclear)
    - Confidence (high / medium / low)

15. **Reach a liability conclusion.** All five elements must be present. If any element is absent or uncertain, state which one(s) and why. If all five are present, identify the most vulnerable element (the one most likely to be challenged or overturned on appeal).

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Statute cited verbatim with correct jurisdiction | Y/N |
| Factual narrative is jargon-free and under 150 words | Y/N |
| Actus reus element interrogated: voluntary act, duty (if omission), result produced | Y/N |
| Mens rea element interrogated: type identified, evidence listed, capacity checked | Y/N |
| Causation tested: both but-for and proximate cause addressed | Y/N |
| Intervening acts (if any) classified as foreseeable or unforeseeable | Y/N |
| Concurrence checked: intent present at moment of act | Y/N |
| Each element has a confidence tag (high/medium/low) with justification | Y/N |
| Liability matrix is complete (all five elements scored) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Actus reus is assumed present without interrogating voluntariness or duty (omission cases).
- Mens rea is inferred from a single ambiguous act; no alternative inferences are considered.
- Causation is stated as obvious without addressing intervening acts or foreseeability.
- Concurrence is not explicitly checked; intent is assumed to persist throughout the relevant timeframe.
- A single element is "unclear" but the analyst proceeds to a liability conclusion anyway.
- The liability matrix assigns "high confidence" to all five elements; this is rare and suggests insufficiently adversarial interrogation.
- Defenses (insanity, duress, necessity, self-defense) are mentioned but not analyzed in the liability conclusion.

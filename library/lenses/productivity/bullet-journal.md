# Bullet Journal

## Overview
The Bullet Journal is a hybrid calendar, task list, and reflection system built on rapid logging (compact symbols + short form) and periodic migration (reviewing and discarding old entries). Rather than trying to predict what matters months ahead, you log what surfaces daily, then enforce a discipline of reviewing and carrying forward only what still deserves attention. Practitioners use this when standard to-do systems feel either too rigid (predefined categories) or too loose (no review gate), and when they suspect they're spending more energy organizing than executing.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish the Logging System

1. **Choose a medium.** Pen and paper, digital notebook, or hybrid. The medium determines friction. Write down your choice and your reason (speed, searchability, portability, aesthetics, cost of loss).

2. **Define your symbol set.** Create a key mapping at least three symbols:
   - **Task** (`—` or `•`): an actionable item with a clear done state
   - **Note** (`-`): information or context, no action required
   - **Event** (`o`): time-bound commitment (meeting, deadline)
   - Optionally add priority (`*` or `!`) and status modifiers (`>` = migrated, `<` = scheduled ahead, `x` = completed, `~` = abandoned)

3. **Establish your primary logging surface.** This is where you dump everything daily without overthinking. Set a time window (e.g., first thing, end of day, as-you-go) and commit to it. Write one sentence per entry — if it takes more, it's a note to be elaborated elsewhere, not a bullet.

4. **Test the system for one week.** Log everything that crosses your mind: tasks, ideas, meetings, interruptions. Do not migrate or review yet. The goal is to calibrate friction — if you're skipping logging because it's annoying, the medium or symbol set is wrong.

### Phase 2 — Implement the Migration Rhythm

5. **Choose a migration cadence.** Weekly is standard; daily is for high-volume contexts (ops, support); monthly is for low-context work. Document your choice.

6. **Schedule migration time.** Block 15–30 minutes, weekly, at a fixed day/time. This is non-negotiable — migration is the lens's load-bearing discipline.

7. **At migration, for each entry, apply one of four judgments:**
   - **Complete** (`x`): mark it done and leave it in the past entry.
   - **Carry forward** (`>`): copy the bullet to the current logging surface with the `>` modifier. This means it's still relevant and needs visibility.
   - **Schedule** (`<`): if the item has a specific future date, move it to that date's entry (a mini-calendar or index) with the `<` modifier. Removes clutter from the current surface.
   - **Discard** (`~`): strike through or erase. You decided it no longer matters. This is the hardest judgment — embrace it. No guilt. If you're never discarding anything, the system is storing junk, not clarifying priorities.

8. **Count the results of each migration.** Record: how many completed, how many carried forward, how many scheduled, how many discarded. This becomes a leading indicator of your filtering discipline.

### Phase 3 — Measure and Reflect

9. **After four weeks, measure three metrics:**
   - **Carry-forward ratio:** (items carried forward) / (total items in past month). Healthy is 30–50%. Above 70%, you're logging too much or avoiding discard. Below 20%, you're either very effective or not logging comprehensively.
   - **Completion rate:** (completed items) / (total items that reached migration). Healthy is 60–80%. Below 40%, tasks are vague or priorities are misaligned. Above 90%, tasks may be too small to be meaningful.
   - **Discard rate:** (discarded items) / (total items). Healthy is 20–40%. Zero discard signals you're not filtering; high discard (>50%) can signal either strong prioritization or poor initial logging.

10. **Conduct a quarterly reflection.** Look at which categories of tasks tend to carry forward repeatedly (sticky items). Ask: Are these worth doing? Am I avoiding them? Should I break them into smaller pieces? Should I delete them?

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Symbol key is defined and includes task, note, event | Y/N |
| Primary logging surface is established and used daily for ≥1 week | Y/N |
| Migration rhythm is scheduled and non-skippable | Y/N |
| All four judgments (complete/carry/schedule/discard) are applied at migration | Y/N |
| Migration metrics (carry ratio, completion rate, discard rate) are recorded for ≥4 weeks | Y/N |
| Carry-forward ratio is between 20–70% | Y/N |
| A quarterly reflection has been conducted | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- No migration occurs, or it's sporadic. Without the review gate, the journal becomes archive (useless) or recycle bin (overwhelming). This is the most common failure mode.
- Carry-forward ratio exceeds 80% consistently. Tasks are not being discarded; the system is becoming a burden, not a clarifier.
- Completion rate is below 30%. Either tasks are too large to complete in the intended interval, or priorities are unstable week to week.
- The discard rate is zero. You're not filtering; you're storing. Discard is the discipline.
- No distinction between symbols — everything is a bullet, or modifiers are ignored. Without them, you cannot distinguish signal (actionable work) from noise (information).
- Logging is irregular or skipped for days. High friction in the medium or too many simultaneous logging surfaces. Choose one primary surface.
- Quarterly reflection is skipped. You're mechanically migrating but not learning from the pattern. The lens fails without reflection.

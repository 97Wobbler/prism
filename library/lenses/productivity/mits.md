# Most Important Tasks

## Overview
MITs is a daily prioritization discipline where you identify exactly three outcomes that, if completed, would make the day successful—regardless of everything else on your list. The constraint to three is deliberate: it forces trade-offs instead of allowing false urgency to expand the list. Practitioners use MITs when they have more work than time, need to avoid burnout from perpetual task-switching, or want a daily measure of whether they're making progress on what actually matters.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Define Your Time Horizon and Constraints

1. **Establish your strategic anchor.** Write down your 3–6 month goal or the problem you're trying to solve. This is not your MIT list; this is what your MIT list serves. Example: "Ship the checkout redesign" or "Reduce cloud spend by 20%."

2. **Assess your actual available time.** Subtract meetings, interruptions, and administrative overhead from your work day. Be honest. If you have a 9–5 job with 4 hours of meetings, you have roughly 4 usable hours. Write this number down.

3. **List everything you *think* should get done today.** Brain dump. No filtering. Include carryovers from yesterday, new requests, and items that feel urgent.

### Phase 2 — Select and Validate

4. **From your brain dump, identify which 3 items are closest to your strategic anchor.** For each candidate, ask:
   - Does completing this directly unblock the anchor goal or prevent a critical blocker?
   - If I do nothing else today but finish this, will I feel the day was successful?
   - If I defer this 1 week, what breaks or slips?

5. **For each of the three, write a completion condition.** Not "work on the checkout redesign" but "validate the payment flow with 3 merchants and document blockers." You must be able to answer YES or NO at day's end.

6. **Verify feasibility against your available time.** Estimate effort for each MIT. If the three add up to more than 80% of your available time, drop the lowest-priority one and add a fourth candidate. Re-estimate and choose. Do not over-subscribe.

### Phase 3 — Commit and Review

7. **Write your three MITs down in a visible place.** Not in a master to-do app buried with 200 other items. A sticky note, a doc, a whiteboard. Visibility creates commitment.

8. **As you work, track blockers and time spent on each MIT.** If a MIT takes 4 hours of your 4-hour day and you're not done, note it. This data feeds tomorrow's plan.

9. **At day's end (or start of next day), conduct a 5-minute review:**
   - Did you complete all three?
   - Did you work on non-MITs that crowded them out? What caused the context switch?
   - Are any MITs blocked waiting on someone else?
   - What will the three MITs be tomorrow?

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Strategic anchor (3–6 month goal) is explicitly written | Y/N |
| Available time estimate is realistic (accounts for meetings/overhead) | Y/N |
| Each MIT has a completion condition (not vague) | Y/N |
| Total MIT effort fits within 80% of available time | Y/N |
| MITs are written in a visible location, not buried in app | Y/N |
| Review was conducted (blockers logged, next day's MITs drafted) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- MIT list grows to 5–6 items by midday. This indicates either misestimation of effort, loss of discipline under pressure, or unclear completion conditions.
- All three MITs get completed but you worked 12 hours. Your available-time estimate was wrong or your completion conditions were too narrow.
- Zero connection between MIT and strategic anchor. Your MITs are reactive to interruptions, not aligned to what matters.
- Same MIT appears on the list three days running without completion. Either it's mislabeled as "important" or it's blocked and nobody unblocked it.
- Review step is skipped. Without reviewing, you have no data to improve estimates or spot patterns.
- MIT contains "admin" work that fills the slot without moving the needle (e.g., "reply to Slack," "process inbox"). These should not be MITs; they are hygiene.

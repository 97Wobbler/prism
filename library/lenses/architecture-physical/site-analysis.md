# Site Analysis

## Overview
Site analysis is a disciplined survey of a location's physical, climatic, and social context to ground design decisions in observable fact rather than assumption. It operates on an actual or proposed building site and produces a structured inventory of constraints and opportunities. Practitioners use it to avoid designing for an imaginary site and to surface hidden relationships—between slope and drainage, sun angle and thermal load, street alignment and view corridors—that shape the viability of a design.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Inventory the Physical Site

1. **Establish a coordinate system and measure the boundary.** Record the site perimeter, area in square meters, and true north orientation. Note the legal property lines and any easements or restrictions.

2. **Map and measure existing topography.** Walk the site at cardinal points (N, S, E, W) and intermediate diagonals. Record elevation change in 1–2 meter intervals. Mark the highest and lowest points, valleys, ridges, and any slope faces steeper than 1:3 (33°). Photograph slope angles from ground level.

3. **Identify existing vegetation and tree canopy.** Note tree species, trunk diameter, and crown extent. Measure the height of significant trees using a clinometer or shadow method (height = shadow length × tan(sun angle)). Record the footprint of shade cast at solar noon on the site's worst heat day. Mark any native vegetation or habitat.

4. **Inventory hard structures and infrastructure.** Record all existing buildings, walls, paved areas, and roads. Note their age (if knowable), orientation, condition, and setback from property lines. Mark utility lines (water, sewer, gas, electric, fiber) and their depth. Note any structures that will remain or be demolished.

5. **Document the immediate surroundings (100–200 m radius).** Map neighboring buildings, their heights, setbacks, and primary facades. Note sight lines into and out of the site. Identify dominant view directions (toward landmarks, water, mountains, or urban skyline). Photograph key views from site corners and center.

### Phase 2 — Climate and Environmental Performance

6. **Record solar geometry for the site's latitude.** Using solar diagrams or tools, mark the winter solstice sun path (lowest arc), summer solstice sun path (highest arc), and equinox path. For winter and summer, record solar altitude at 9 AM, noon, and 3 PM. Calculate direct solar hours on the south-facing façade in winter (usually the most constrained season).

7. **Document prevailing winds.** Record the dominant wind direction(s) by season (if the site is windy, obtain a wind rose diagram from a meteorological station or regional climate data). Note any existing wind breaks (vegetation, buildings, topography). Mark dead zones where wind stagnates or funnels.

8. **Assess thermal and hydrological behavior.** Note the depth to groundwater (from property records or test pits if feasible). Mark areas of standing water, seasonal flooding, or drainage problems. Record soil type and infiltration rate. Note the presence of any springs, streams, or ponds and their direction of flow.

9. **Catalog microclimatic variations.** Identify thermal mass (exposed rock, paving, water) that absorbs and releases heat. Mark frost pockets (low-lying areas that collect cold air). Note any urban heat island effect (dark paving, lack of vegetation) on and around the site.

10. **Record noise and air quality.** Walk the site's edges and note sources of noise (traffic, industry, trains). Measure sound levels in dB if equipment is available, or describe qualitatively. Note any odors, dust sources, or visual pollution from adjacent land uses.

### Phase 3 — Circulation and Access

11. **Map pedestrian and vehicular access points.** Mark the locations and widths of existing roads, sidewalks, and curb cuts. Note the presence of controlled intersections (lights, stop signs) versus uncontrolled. Record pedestrian desire lines (visible worn paths) and how they differ from designed pathways.

12. **Document public transportation.** If applicable, record the location of bus stops, train stations, or transit nodes within walking distance (typically 400–800 m). Note the frequency and routes of service.

13. **Record site egress requirements.** Confirm the number of required exits for buildings of anticipated size. Verify setback distances from property lines and neighboring structures. Note any fire access lanes or truck routes that must be maintained.

### Phase 4 — Perception and Spatial Character

14. **Document visual quality and sense of place.** Photograph the site from multiple vantage points (entrance, center, corner, elevated position if possible). Note the perceived edges: where does "the site" feel like it begins and end? Record distinctive landmarks visible from the site or that frame it (a mountain, a church steeple, a street tree).

15. **Catalog sightlines and visual privacy.** From the intended building location, draw sightlines to the street, neighbors, and distant landmarks. Measure the angle of visibility from neighboring windows into the site. Note whether the site is visible or concealed from the surrounding street.

16. **Record seasonal and daily rhythm.** If possible, visit at different times (morning, midday, evening) and seasons. Note when the space is animated (school drop-off, market day) and when it's quiet. Observe patterns of use by adjacent residents or businesses.

### Phase 5 — Integration and Constraints Synthesis

17. **Cross-reference all findings.** For each major finding (e.g., summer solar gain, winter wind exposure, slope runoff), ask: Does this constrain the design? Does this create an opportunity? Is this finding in tension with another? Example: "South-facing slope receives maximum summer sun but also is the primary view direction; passive solar gains must be balanced against view preservation."

18. **Identify non-negotiable constraints.** These are regulatory, environmental, or structural facts that the design must accommodate without exception. Example: "Groundwater is 1 m below grade; basement construction is not viable." Mark these clearly.

19. **List latent opportunities.** These are site conditions that, if leveraged, improve the design. Example: "Slope faces south; gravity-fed terraces could create tiered usable space and reduce grading." Mark these separately from constraints.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Boundary, area, and true north are recorded | Y/N |
| Topography is mapped with contours or spot elevations at cardinal and diagonal walks | Y/N |
| Existing vegetation is documented (species, height, shadow at peak sun) | Y/N |
| Utility locations and depths are identified | Y/N |
| Solar geometry is recorded for winter, summer, and equinox | Y/N |
| Wind direction and speed (or dominant direction from data) are noted | Y/N |
| Hydrological condition (water table, drainage, flooding risk) is assessed | Y/N |
| Noise and air quality are documented (measured or qualitative) | Y/N |
| Pedestrian and vehicular access points are mapped | Y/N |
| Sightlines into and out of the site are recorded | Y/N |
| At least one visit outside standard business hours or at a different season | Y/N |
| Constraints and opportunities are explicitly listed, not just data recorded | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The site has been visited only once, during midday on a business day. Temporal variation is as important as spatial variation.
- Solar geometry was not calculated; "the sun comes from the east" is assumed without actual sun path data.
- Noise levels are guessed rather than measured or sourced from traffic counts. A site next to a highway has a different acoustic profile than data alone suggests.
- Groundwater and soil conditions are assumed without property records or test pits. Drainage failure is expensive; verify.
- Surrounding buildings are noted but their height-to-setback ratio and wall plane orientation are not. These determine shadow, wind tunnel effects, and view loss.
- No distinction between constraints and opportunities. A steep slope is both — the analysis must say which is which and why.
- The analysis stops after inventory. Constraints and opportunities are not ranked by impact on design feasibility.

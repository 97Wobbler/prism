# Parti Diagram

## Overview
A parti diagram is a stripped-to-essence abstraction of how a building or space organizes itself conceptually—the skeleton beneath the skin. It shows relationships between major zones or moves using minimal marks: a line for a path, a circle for a gathering space, a gesture for a hierarchical axis. The discipline is knowing what to strip away; most practitioners mistake a parti for a quick sketch, when the real work is choosing which relationships matter and which are noise. Architects use this when a design is stuck in decoration or detail, or when they need to verify that the core organizational idea actually makes sense before investing in form.

## Analytical Procedure

1. **Identify the primary functional zones or moments.** List the major activities or spaces the design must accommodate: entry, circulation spine, gathering, work areas, storage, threshold conditions, etc. Write each as a noun phrase, not a room name. Do not list every room—only the zones that define the overall spatial logic.

2. **For each zone, extract its geometric essence.** Ask: "If I had to show this zone to someone using only one shape or line, what would it be?"
   - A reception/threshold might be a **line** (a moment of transition)
   - A gathering space might be a **circle** or **node** (radial, convergent)
   - A gallery or processional might be a **linear path** (directional)
   - A distributed program might be a **field** or **grid**
   - A hierarchy might be a **central axis**

3. **Map relationships between zones.** Draw how zones connect:
   - Is one nested inside another (concentric)?
   - Are they along a spine (linear sequence)?
   - Are they radiating from a center (radial)?
   - Are they isolated and independent?
   - Is one a threshold between two others?

4. **Add ONE primary organizational move.** The parti is not the sum of all moves; it is the one move that, if removed, the design would lose its logic. Examples:
   - "Bilateral symmetry around a central spine"
   - "Concentric zones of increasing privacy"
   - "Linear processional path with satellite spaces"
   - "Four quadrants divided by a cross-axial move"
   Identify this move and write it as a sentence. If you find yourself writing multiple moves, you may not have a parti yet—the design may be conflicted.

5. **Verify against the program.** Go back to your original functional zones from step 1. Do they all fit within the organizational move you identified in step 4? 
   - If yes, the parti is coherent.
   - If a major zone is awkwardly accommodated or does not align with the move, flag this as a conflict: the design is forcing program into a geometric idea that doesn't serve it.

6. **Draw the diagram at high abstraction.** Use only:
   - Simple geometric forms (lines, circles, rectangles, grids)
   - Labels (2-3 words max per zone)
   - Directional arrows or implied flow where relevant
   - One or two annotations describing the organizing principle
   Do not include furniture, walls, materials, fenestration, or detail. Do not include a scale or site context unless it is essential to the move.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Primary functional zones identified (3-7 zones; not every room) | Y/N |
| Each zone reduced to a single geometric essence | Y/N |
| Relationships between zones are drawn or clearly stated | Y/N |
| One primary organizing move is identified and named | Y/N |
| All major program zones fit coherently within the move | Y/N |
| Diagram uses only abstraction (no detail, no clutter) | Y/N |
| A person unfamiliar with the design can read the move in 10 seconds | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The diagram looks like a floor plan with simplified walls. If your grid or shape is orthogonal and room-like, you've drawn a plan, not a parti. Go more abstract.
- Multiple competing organizing moves appear equally important. This signals the design is confused about its logic. A parti should have one dominant move.
- Program zones do not fit the move cleanly. If you have to rotate a zone or squeeze a function to fit the geometric idea, the parti is not working—either the geometry is wrong or the program assignment needs rethinking.
- The diagram requires a legend to interpret. If someone has to ask "what does this circle mean?" the abstraction has failed. Clarity is the point.
- No clear directional or hierarchical relationship between zones. The diagram is a collection of shapes rather than a system. Relationships are the content of a parti.
- The diagram is ornamental or formal for its own sake. A parti serves the program; if removing it changes nothing about how the space works, it is not a real move.

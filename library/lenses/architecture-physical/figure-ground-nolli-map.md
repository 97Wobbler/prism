# Figure-Ground / Nolli Map

## Overview

A Nolli Map inverts the conventional architectural drawing by treating voids (streets, plazas, courtyards) as figure and buildings as ground. Rather than drawing buildings in solid black and streets in white, the figure-ground reversal makes the spatial network the primary artifact. Practitioners use this lens when conventional floor plans and site plans fail to reveal how public and semi-public spaces actually connect, or when a development's impact on the urban fabric cannot be understood from traditional building footprints alone. The discipline is disciplined inversion — not arbitrary, but systematic and legible.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish the Drawing Baseline

1. **Acquire or create a base map of the study area.** Use a site plan or aerial photograph at consistent scale (typically 1:500 to 1:5000 depending on scope). The area should be large enough to show context — a single building block is insufficient; include at least 3-4 blocks or the equivalent spatial unit.

2. **Identify all voids explicitly.** List every connected open space:
   - Public right-of-way (streets, alleys, pedestrian passages)
   - Public gathering spaces (plazas, squares, parks)
   - Semi-public or accessible courtyards (hotel courtyards, commercial atriums, colonnaded galleries)
   - Private voids that are visually permeable (transparent lobbies, open-air loading docks)
   
   Do NOT include completely private, non-visual voids (internal office spaces, mechanical rooms, locked courtyards).

3. **Classify voids by accessibility.** For each void, mark whether it is:
   - **Fully public** — anyone can enter freely, day and night
   - **Conditionally public** — open during business hours or for paying customers
   - **Semi-public** — accessible but socially gated (requires familiarity or purpose)
   - **Visually public** — private but visible from public space

### Phase 2 — Create the Inverted Drawing

4. **Render all voids in a single solid color (traditionally black or dark gray).** All buildings, site furniture, and other non-void elements become white or background.

5. **Ensure topological continuity.** Trace the black (void) mass with your finger or cursor. Every point in the public network should be reachable without crossing a building. If it is not, you have discovered a fragmented or gated system. Mark these breaks explicitly.

6. **Add a connectivity legend.** Where voids are interrupted but conceptually connected (e.g., separated by a single pedestrian crossing, a small bridge, or a gate that is normally open), show the link as a fine gray line or dashed line. Distinguish temporary or seasonal connections.

### Phase 3 — Interrogate the Reversal

7. **Ask the inversion question: What does the void network tell you that the building footprints did not?** Examine:
   - **Void hierarchy** — Are some spaces larger, more central, or more visually prominent than others? Do they form a continuous system or isolated islands?
   - **Permeability** — Can a pedestrian move through the area without backtracking? Are there dead ends, cul-de-sacs, or forced detours?
   - **Visual relationship** — Which buildings face which voids? Are building entrances oriented toward public space or away from it? Are blank walls or loading docks exposed to the public realm?
   - **Asymmetry** — Is the void network biased toward certain parts of the site? Are some neighborhoods disconnected from the broader system?

8. **Compare the inverted drawing to the original site plan.** What is obscured by the conventional drawing that becomes obvious in the Nolli reversal? Common discoveries:
   - A major street network is actually a series of unconnected segments separated by private property
   - A "public square" is fragmented by planters, railings, or level changes that do not appear on the plan
   - Buildings that appear independent are actually knit together by colonnades, covered passages, or shared courtyards
   - A residential neighborhood has no public gathering space despite appearing dense on a footprint plan

### Phase 4 — Synthesis

9. **Generate a diagnostic statement.** In one or two sentences, describe what the inverted figure-ground reveals about spatial health:
   - Example: "The void network is linear and ribbon-like, with no gathering spaces wider than 15 meters. There is a sharp permeability break at the district boundary."
   - Example: "Despite low building density, the void network is highly fragmented. Three neighborhoods are isolated from the central plaza by private property."

10. **Identify intervention points.** Where does the void network have:
    - **Closure** — a space that feels enclosed, safe, and defensible (good)
    - **Permeability** — a space that invites movement and connection (good)
    - **Ambiguity** — a space that is neither clearly public nor private, or that dead-ends unexpectedly (problematic)
    - **Orphaning** — a void that is surrounded by building but has no visible function or access (often an opportunity for reactivation)

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Study area includes at least 3–4 blocks or equivalent spatial unit | Y/N |
| All voids (public, semi-public, visually public) are identified and listed | Y/N |
| Inverted drawing is legible — voids are one color, non-voids are clearly distinct | Y/N |
| Topological continuity is traced; breaks are marked | Y/N |
| Void hierarchy and permeability patterns are explicitly described | Y/N |
| Comparison between conventional plan and inverted drawing reveals at least one non-obvious spatial fact | Y/N |
| At least one intervention point (closure, permeability, ambiguity, or orphaning) is identified | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The inverted drawing looks nearly identical to the conventional plan. The reversal did not reveal new relationships — either the area is genuinely simple (rare) or the void definition was too narrow (common).
- Voids are classified as "public" without evidence of actual permeability or access. A plaza with a gate or a courtyard with signage that says "Private" is not public.
- Topological continuity is not checked. A network drawn as black may actually be a series of isolated islands if the map resolution is too coarse.
- The diagnostic statement is generic ("The area has streets and buildings"). It does not reference the specific void pattern revealed by the inversion.
- No intervention points are identified. The analysis describes what is but never asks what could change.
- The study area is too small (single block) or too large (entire district). The scale should allow the entire void network to be held in working memory.

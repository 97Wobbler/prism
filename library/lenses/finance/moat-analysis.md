# Moat Analysis (Buffett)

## Overview
A moat is a durable structural advantage that allows a company to maintain above-average returns and resist competitive pressure over years or decades. The analysis asks: what prevents competitors from copying this business, and how wide is that moat? Practitioners use this when evaluating whether a business can sustain high margins, market share, and pricing power, or whether competitive advantages are temporary. The discipline is distinguishing structural moats from cyclical strengths or management skill.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Identify the Economic Machine

1. **State the company's core business in functional terms.** Not "a software company" but "sells project management software to mid-market companies on a per-seat annual subscription model." Include pricing mechanism, customer acquisition, and repeat/churn patterns.

2. **Measure current competitive advantage.** Compare the company to direct competitors across:
   - **Gross margin** — revenue minus cost of goods/services. Higher than peers of similar scale?
   - **Operating margin** — after all operating expenses. Sustained above 15% or 20%, or degrading?
   - **Return on invested capital (ROIC)** — annual profit divided by total capital deployed. Consistently above 15%?
   - **Price/cost ratio** — can the company raise prices without losing customers? Is it doing so?
   - **Customer churn/retention** — percentage of customers who renew annually. Above 85% signals pricing power.
   
   If none of these outperform peers, there is no moat yet — stop and reassess the business.

### Phase 2 — Categorize the Moat (if present)

For each candidate moat below, ask: *Does this advantage exist today and would it take 5+ years for a competitor to replicate or neutralize it?* If no, it is not a moat.

#### Brand & Pricing Power
- Customers actively choose this product over cheaper alternatives and show willingness to pay premium prices.
- Examples: luxury goods (LVMH), soft drinks (Coca-Cola), financial brands (American Express).
- **Test:** Would a 10% price increase lose <5% of volume? (If yes, pricing power exists.)
- **Durability check:** Is the brand defended by network effects, habit, or switching cost? Or is it just current preference (fragile)?

#### Cost Advantage (Absolute)
- Company produces the same output as competitors but at lower unit cost — not due to temporary scale, but structural.
- Examples: vertically integrated mining (lower transport cost), proprietary manufacturing process, long-lived asset with depreciated carrying value.
- **Test:** Can a new entrant match the cost structure by copying the business model? If yes, it's not a moat.
- **Durability check:** Is the cost advantage tied to a fixed resource (ore deposit), proprietary knowhow (trade secret), or accumulated scale (hard to replicate)?

#### Switching Cost
- Customer switching to a competitor requires economic or technical effort (not just inconvenience).
- Examples: enterprise software (migration data, retraining staff), pharmaceutical (formulary locks), credit card networks (merchant adoption).
- **Test:** If a competitor offered 20% better service, what % of customers would switch within 12 months? If <10%, switching cost is substantial.
- **Durability check:** Is switching cost enforced by contract, technical integration, or regulation? Or is it merely convenience (fragile)?

#### Network Effects
- The product becomes more valuable as more users adopt it.
- Examples: payment networks (Visa, Mastercard), social networks (Meta), B2B exchanges, operating systems.
- **Test:** Does the platform have a chicken-and-egg problem? If a new entrant could launch and grow from zero, network effects are weak.
- **Durability check:** Is the network proprietary (can't switch) or open (can fork)? Are there natural limit conditions (single-homing vs multi-homing)?

#### Intangible Assets (Patents, Licenses, Regulatory Approval)
- Company holds intellectual property or regulatory permission that competitors cannot easily obtain.
- Examples: pharmaceutical patents, FDA approvals, spectrum licenses, broadcast licenses.
- **Test:** When does the patent or license expire? If within 5 years, it is not a durable moat.
- **Durability check:** What happens after expiration? Are there follow-on patents or regulatory barriers to competitor entry?

#### Scale Economics
- Fixed costs are high; unit costs drop sharply with scale. Incumbent has cost advantage by size alone.
- Examples: microchip fabrication, airline networks, utilities.
- **Test:** If a new competitor could match the incumbent's production volume, would the cost advantage disappear? If yes, it is scale economics but not a moat (vulnerable to new investment).
- **Durability check:** Are there hard capital constraints (billions to enter) or technology barriers that sustain the scale advantage? Or just current size?

3. **Assign a moat type to each advantage identified.** A company may have multiple moats (e.g., brand + switching cost).

### Phase 3 — Assess Moat Width and Durability

4. **For each identified moat, estimate its width.** Width = the size of the competitive advantage in terms of profit or margin.
   - **Narrow:** 2–4% margin advantage over best competitor.
   - **Medium:** 4–8% margin advantage.
   - **Wide:** 8%+ margin advantage.
   
   Use the margin comparison from Phase 1, Step 2.

5. **For each moat, assess durability by asking:**
   - **Is the moat expanding or shrinking?** Are customers becoming more or less dependent on the moat source? Is competitive intensity increasing or decreasing?
   - **Are there credible threats?** New technology, regulation change, new competitor, customer consolidation, or loss of key asset?
   - **How has the moat survived past disruptions?** If the company faced threats before, did the moat hold or did it narrow?
   - **What is the time horizon of durability?** Conservative assumption: 5–10 years. Optimistic: 15+ years.

6. **Rate overall durability:**
   - **Durable:** Moat has widened in past 5 years; no credible threats; structural advantage (e.g., network, brand) not dependent on any single person or regulation expiring soon.
   - **Reasonable:** Moat stable in past 5 years; threats exist but are not imminent; requires monitoring.
   - **At risk:** Moat narrowed in past 5 years; credible threat present within 3 years; dependent on regulatory protection or leadership.

### Phase 4 — Valuation Implication

7. **Determine the investment horizon implied by moat durability.** A durable moat justifies a longer holding period and higher valuation multiple. A narrow or eroding moat does not.
   - **Durable moat (10+ years):** Reasonable to assume sustained above-average returns; supports premium valuation (e.g., 1.5–2× market P/E multiple).
   - **Reasonable moat (5–10 years):** Assume returns above market but declining; market multiple or modest premium.
   - **At-risk moat (<5 years):** Assume returns converge toward market; discount valuation; treat as value trap unless price is very low.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Company's core business model stated in functional terms (pricing, customer type, churn) | Y/N |
| All four competitive metrics measured: gross margin, operating margin, ROIC, price/cost | Y/N |
| At least one competitor directly compared on each metric | Y/N |
| Each identified moat assigned to one of six categories | Y/N |
| Moat width estimated as narrow/medium/wide with supporting margin data | Y/N |
| Durability assessed against expansion/threat/history/timeframe criteria | Y/N |
| Investment horizon and valuation implication stated explicitly | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All competitive metrics are "in line with peers." If there is no measurable advantage today, no moat exists yet. Do not infer one.
- Moat identified is "great management" or "visionary CEO." Individual skill is not a moat; it is a risk (key person dependency). Moats are structural.
- Pricing power is claimed but not tested. If the company has never raised prices significantly, willingness to pay is untested — do not assume it.
- Moat durability rated as "durable 20+ years" based on current strength alone, with no threat assessment. Every moat is under attack; missing competitive threats is analysis failure.
- Switching cost is claimed but customer actually multi-homes. Enterprise software used alongside three competitors is not locked in; switching cost is low.
- Patent moat identified with expiration date within 5 years and no follow-on patent pipeline. This is not a durable moat; it is a ticking clock.
- Scale economies claimed but new entrant or competitor has successfully entered with lower scale. Scale advantage may exist but is not impregnable.

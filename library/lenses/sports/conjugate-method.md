# Conjugate Method (Westside)

## Overview
The Conjugate Method trains maximum strength, dynamic effort, and repetition effort in parallel on separate days, rotating exercise variations every 2–3 weeks to prevent accommodation and manage cumulative fatigue. Instead of specializing in one energy system or movement pattern, the lifter cycles through variations of the same lift that emphasize different rates of force development. Practitioners use this when a single rep-max has stalled despite consistent training, when injury demands periodic unloading, or when the sport demands explosive strength *and* work capacity together.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish the Lift Architecture

1. **Identify the competition lift or primary strength goal.** This is the movement you will never abandon: squat, bench press, deadlift, or a sport-specific analog. Write it as a single lift with no ambiguity.

2. **Define three concurrent training qualities and their frequency:**
   - **Maximum Effort (ME)** — 1–3 reps at 90%+ of 1RM; develops absolute strength. Frequency: 1–2 days/week.
   - **Dynamic Effort (DE)** — 3–5 reps at 50–70% of 1RM moved *as fast as possible*; develops rate of force development and neuromuscular coordination. Frequency: 1–2 days/week.
   - **Repetition Effort (RE)** — 6–20 reps at moderate intensity (60–85% of 1RM); develops work capacity, hypertrophy, and metabolic stress. Frequency: 1–2 days/week.

3. **Confirm that all three qualities are trained in the week.** A valid week includes at least one session targeting each. If any is missing, the method is not being applied; rebalance.

### Phase 2 — Design the Rotation Schedule

4. **Select exercise variations for each quality.** The point of variation is to change the load curve, range of motion, or mechanical advantage while training the same fundamental pattern. Examples:
   - Bench press variations: board press (short ROM, heavy), chains, bands, decline, pin press (bottom-up).
   - Squat variations: pause squat, box squat, safety-bar squat, front squat.
   - Deadlift variations: sumo, trap bar, deficit deadlift, rack pull.
   - Non-variations (same movement, different loading): raw vs. with wraps, narrow vs. wide stance — these are suboptimal for rotation.

5. **Assign one primary variation per quality per rotation cycle (typically 2–3 weeks).** Record the movement, rep scheme, intensity, and target volume:
   - ME day: "Incline bench press, 3×2 @ 92% 1RM"
   - DE day: "Bench press with chains, 8×3 @ 65% + chains"
   - RE day: "Close-grip bench press, 3×8 @ 75%"

6. **Plan the rotation schedule for 6–12 weeks ahead.** Document which variation will be used for each quality in each 2–3 week block. Ensure no variation is used more than twice consecutively. If you cannot specify the next two rotations, the plan is vague; make it concrete.

### Phase 3 — Validate Complementarity

7. **Check that the three qualities are biomechanically distinct.** If ME, DE, and RE all use the same variation, rotation is not happening. If the intensity profiles overlap (e.g., both DE and RE at 70%), you are not training three distinct adaptations; adjust loads.

8. **Confirm that variations load different joints or ranges.** A board press (partial ROM, heavy) and a pin press (bottom-loaded) are complementary. A close-grip bench and a standard bench are not; they create the same demand. Replace one.

9. **Verify that no single variation appears in the same cycle for two qualities.** You should not squat box squats on ME day and pause squats on RE day in the same week; it creates redundancy. Spread variations across the rotation.

### Phase 4 — Monitor and Adjust

10. **Track performance metrics each session:** reps completed, rate of perceived exertion, bar speed (if measured), and form breakdown. Record these in a structured log.

11. **After each 2–3 week rotation, compare the preceding cycle to the one before it.** Did the variation elicit a training effect (improved reps, speed, or load tolerance)? If performance flatlined, the variation did not work; flag it and swap it out in the next cycle.

12. **Monitor fatigue weekly.** If cumulative soreness, sleep debt, or injury signals appear, it is usually a sign that one quality is overloaded relative to the others. Reduce volume on the highest-fatigue day or shorten the rotation cycle.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Three distinct qualities (ME, DE, RE) are trained in the week | Y/N |
| Each quality is assigned a specific variation and rep/intensity scheme | Y/N |
| Variations across the cycle are biomechanically distinct (not the same lift) | Y/N |
| No variation repeats consecutively across two rotation blocks | Y/N |
| Next two rotation cycles are specified in advance | Y/N |
| Performance tracking includes reps, RPE, and form notes | Y/N |
| A comparison metric exists to assess variation efficacy | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All three qualities (ME, DE, RE) use the same lift in the same week. This is periodization failure; you are not rotating.
- The "variation" between cycles is changing the weight, not the exercise. True variation requires changing the movement, ROM, or loading curve.
- Maximum Effort sessions are done at a single rep max every week without variation. This creates excessive CNS fatigue and accommodation; it should rotate.
- Dynamic Effort is performed slowly (6–7 RPE instead of explosive). At that intensity, it is not training rate of force development; it is just a lighter warm-up.
- No performance comparison exists between rotation blocks. You cannot tell if a variation worked; the method is running blind.
- Fatigue accumulates across all three qualities, but load is never reduced. Parallel training is only sustainable with adequate management; if one quality is always maximal, accommodation and overtraining result.

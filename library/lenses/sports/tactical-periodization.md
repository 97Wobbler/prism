# Tactical Periodization

## Overview
Tactical Periodization inverts the conventional training hierarchy: instead of building physical capacity first and then layering tactical work on top, it makes the team's tactical model the organizing principle of every session. Every drill, possession pattern, and intensity prescription flows from the structure of how the team plays in matches. Practitioners use this when they sense that fitness work divorced from tactical intent produces technically flat or tactically incoherent teams, and when they want training load and match demands to be inseparable.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Define the Tactical Model

1. **State the team's core tactical identity in three dimensions:**
   - **Possession structure:** How does the team build (compact, wide, through center)? How does it progress (short passes, switches, direct)? Where does it attempt to score (overlaps, cutting inside, set plays)?
   - **Pressing trigger:** At what loss of possession does the team press? Which zones (front third only, middle third everywhere)? Which players lead the press?
   - **Defensive shape:** How many lines? Where is the defensive block positioned (high, medium, low)? How do fullbacks behave out of possession?
   Write these as observable, repeatable patterns, not aspirations.

2. **Map the next opponent's tactical model** using the same three dimensions. This is the constraint — your training must prepare players to execute your model *against* theirs.

3. **Identify the critical tactical moments** where your model meets the opponent's. Examples: your pressing trigger against their build; their counter against your possession; your fullback positioning against their wide threat. List 4–6 of these friction points.

### Phase 2 — Design the Microcycle (typically 7 days)

4. **Assign each critical moment to a specific session** within the microcycle, respecting player recovery windows:
   - **Day 1 (post-match):** Light technical work; low tactical load.
   - **Day 2–3:** Introduce one critical moment per session (e.g., your press vs. their build on Day 2; defending their counter on Day 3). Intensity rises.
   - **Day 4:** Full-model session. Execute your possession structure, pressing, and defensive shape together at moderate intensity.
   - **Day 5:** Targeted opposition-specific work on the highest-risk friction point.
   - **Day 6:** Full model at competitive intensity, adding randomization (opponent adapts tactics mid-drill).
   - **Day 7:** Pre-match activation or rest.

5. **For each session, specify:**
   - **The tactical focal point** (e.g., "Full-back press against their winger crossing sequence").
   - **The opposition structure** that will be simulated (e.g., 4 outfield players in a defensive unit, 3 in their attacking shape).
   - **The rule constraint** that forces execution of the tactical moment (e.g., "Ball starts from center-back; pressing unit must recover possession within 20 seconds or drill resets").
   - **The scaling of opposition resistance** (passive, then active, then reactive — do not jump to reactive without establishing shape first).
   - **Duration and repetitions** (e.g., 6 × 90 seconds with 30 seconds recovery, not fixed time).

### Phase 3 — Encode Tactical Principles into Physical Demands

6. **Do not prescribe generic fitness work.** Instead, extract the physical demand *from the tactical task.* Example: if the pressing trigger demands that the center-back sprints 30 meters to intercept, the session output is "6 × max-effort sprints of 25–35 meters within a defensive shape constraint," not "6 × 35m sprints on an empty pitch."

7. **Link every set and repetition to the opponent.** Document: "This pressing sequence mirrors Opponent's Build Pattern #2, in which their left center-back receives between the lines; we practice high pressing for 40 meters."

8. **Verify that players execute the tactical pattern *while* meeting the physical demand.** If a player completes the sprint but then makes a poor pass, the drill failed tactically (even if it succeeded physically). Reset and re-attempt.

### Phase 4 — Match-Day Activation and Monitoring

9. **On match day, run a 20–30 minute activation session** that rehearses only the highest-risk friction points (typically 2–3 specific moments, not the full model). This is not fitness; it is tactical and technical priming.

10. **Post-match, within 36 hours, conduct a video review** that maps what actually happened in the match back to the microcycle. Did the opponent deviate from their model? Did your team execute the trained moments? Use this to adjust the next microcycle immediately — do not wait for a full week to pass before incorporating match feedback.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Tactical model is stated in three dimensions (possession, pressing, shape) | Y/N |
| Opponent's model is mapped independently, not strawmanned | Y/N |
| Critical tactical moments (friction points) are identified and numbered | Y/N |
| Microcycle assigns one friction point per session with clear dates | Y/N |
| Each session specifies focal point, opposition structure, rule constraint, and scaling | Y/N |
| No generic fitness work appears; all demands are derived from tactical tasks | Y/N |
| Post-match review is scheduled within 36 hours and mapped to microcycle | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Microcycle sessions are titled "Speed & Agility," "Strength," "Endurance" — these are generic, not tactical. The framework is not being applied.
- Opponent's model is described in vague terms ("they press high") without concrete structure. You cannot prepare for a model you haven't defined.
- Opposition resistance scales wrong: the team is asked to press at game-speed against a reactive opponent on Day 2 before the defensive shape is stable. Players fail and the drill is abandoned.
- Players execute the physical demand (e.g., the sprint) but abandon the tactical shape. The session scored a fitness goal but missed the tactical goal — this is a red flag that the constraint is not tight enough.
- Match-day activation is a standard warm-up unrelated to the microcycle. The final reinforcement is disconnected from the training work.
- Post-match review is skipped or delayed beyond 36 hours. The feedback loop is broken, and the next microcycle is designed in a vacuum.

# FIFA 11+ / RAMP Protocol

## Overview

The FIFA 11+ protocol (and its successor RAMP — Running, Agility, Mobilisation, Plyometrics) is a structured, evidence-based warmup sequence designed to reduce the incidence of lower-limb injuries, particularly ACL, hamstring, and ankle injuries in team sports. The lens operates on a training session or warmup program and evaluates whether it addresses the biomechanical and neuromuscular risk factors that precede injury. Practitioners use it to retrofit existing warmups or to validate new ones against a research-backed injury-prevention standard.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Map the Risk Factors

1. **List the primary injury mechanisms in your sport.** Identify 3–5 injury types most common in your player population (e.g., ACL tears, hamstring strains, ankle inversions, groin pulls). For each, name the biomechanical or neuromuscular deficit that precedes it.
   - Example: ACL injury is preceded by poor knee alignment, weak hip abductors, and delayed hamstring-to-quad activation ratio.

2. **For each risk factor, note the warmup stimulus it requires.** Does it need strength, range of motion, proprioceptive challenge, or movement pattern retraining?

### Phase 2 — Audit the Existing Warmup

3. **Segment the warmup into phases:** running, dynamic stretching, agility drills, strengthening, and plyometrics. Time each phase.

4. **For each phase, record:**
   - Duration (minutes)
   - Exercises (name, reps/time, load if any)
   - Primary stimulus (what capacity is being trained: aerobic, flexibility, proprioception, eccentric strength, reactive power, etc.)

5. **Cross-reference each exercise against the risk factors from Phase 1.** Use a matrix:
   | Risk Factor | Addressed By | Exercise | Dose | Adequate? |
   |---|---|---|---|---|
   | Poor hip abductor strength (ACL) | Side plank, clamshells, lateral band walks | [name] | [reps/time] | Y/N |

### Phase 3 — Evaluate Against FIFA 11+ / RAMP Standards

6. **Check for the required components.** FIFA 11+ and RAMP both require:
   - **Running (5–10 min):** Begin with jogging, progress to directional changes, sprints, and lateral movements. Establishes work capacity and movement readiness.
   - **Dynamic stretching (5–10 min):** Hip flexors, hamstrings, groin, calf, adductors via walking lunges, leg swings, inchworms. Static stretching is not included.
   - **Strengthening (7–10 min):** Core stability (planks, side planks), hip abduction/external rotation (clamshells, lateral band walks, fire hydrants), eccentric knee control (single-leg squats, hamstring curls). Must include single-leg or unilateral variations.
   - **Plyometrics (3–5 min):** Double-leg hops, single-leg hops, lateral hops, bounding. Reactive power and landing mechanics are the targets.

7. **For each component, score its execution:**
   - **Present:** The warmup includes a recognizable version of this component (Y/N)
   - **Adequate dose:** The component is performed long enough to induce adaptation (Y/N)
     - Running: ≥5 min, includes directional and speed variation
     - Dynamic stretching: ≥5 distinct movement patterns, each ≥8 reps
     - Strengthening: ≥3 distinct hip/core exercises, single-leg variants included
     - Plyometrics: ≥2 distinct jump patterns, ≥10 total contacts per leg
   - **Progression path:** Later reps or sets are harder than early ones (Y/N)

8. **Check for contraindications or gaps:**
   - Are static stretches removing range of motion needed for dynamic work? (Common error)
   - Is strengthening load too heavy to permit controlled, explosive movement? (Common error)
   - Are plyometrics performed when the player is fatigued? (Should be when fresh)
   - Is there a progression from bilateral to unilateral movement?

### Phase 4 — Gap Analysis and Recommendations

9. **For each missing or inadequate component, estimate the injury risk that remains unaddressed.** Use the matrix from Phase 2 to flag which risk factors are not covered.

10. **Propose specific additions or modifications.** Be concrete: name the exercise, reps, duration, placement in the warmup sequence.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| All 3–5 primary injury mechanisms for the sport are named | Y/N |
| Each injury mechanism is linked to a biomechanical risk factor | Y/N |
| Warmup is segmented into running, stretching, strength, plyometrics | Y/N |
| Each segment is timed and exercises are listed with dose | Y/N |
| Risk factor × Exercise matrix is complete (no empty cells) | Y/N |
| At least 4 FIFA 11+ / RAMP components are present | Y/N |
| Dose for each component meets the standard listed in Step 6 | Y/N |
| Gaps and modifications are specific (exercise name, reps, placement) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Warmup includes long static stretching. This indicates the designer may not understand FIFA 11+ principles (dynamic range of motion is the target, not passive flexibility).
- No single-leg or unilateral exercises in the strengthening phase. Injury happens to one limb; bilateral exercises don't train the stabilizers needed to prevent it.
- Plyometrics appear at the end of a full training session (i.e., when players are tired). Landing mechanics degrade under fatigue, so this is the opposite of the intended stimulus.
- More than 50% of the warmup is jogging at steady pace with no directional or speed change. This trains conditioning, not injury prevention.
- Gap analysis is absent or vague ("more core work"). Without naming the missing exercise and dose, the recommendation is not actionable.
- No progression from bilateral to unilateral movement within the warmup sequence.

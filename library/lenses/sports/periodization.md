# Periodization (Matveyev)

## Overview

Periodization divides a training year (or preparation cycle) into nested time blocks — macrocycles (months to a year), mesocycles (weeks to months), and microcycles (days to weeks) — each with distinct physiological objectives and training intensity. The framework assumes that adaptation to training stress follows a predictable curve: stress triggers fatigue, fatigue dissipates during recovery, and the athlete emerges slightly stronger. Peak performance occurs when this cumulative adaptation peaks at a competition date. Practitioners use periodization to align training stress, recovery, and competition timing so the athlete's readiness crests exactly when it matters. Without it, training becomes reactive — endless volume without structure — and peaks become accidental.

## Analytical Procedure

### Step 1 — Define the Competition Calendar

1. **Identify the target competition date(s).** If multiple competitions exist, distinguish the primary goal (when absolute peak is needed) from secondary goals (when acceptable performance is sufficient).

2. **Work backward from the primary goal.** Calculate how many weeks you have until peak performance must be achieved. Matveyev's original model assumed a 52-week macrocycle, but modern adaptations use 16–52 weeks depending on sport and athlete level.

3. **Map all other competitions and training commitments into the timeline.** Mark them on a calendar with labels: Major (peak required), Minor (acceptable performance OK), Preparation (no peak needed, training priority).

### Step 2 — Identify the Macrocycle Structure

A macrocycle contains three distinct phases:

4. **Preparation Phase (40–50% of macrocycle duration).** Goal: build general physical capacity, technical foundation, and aerobic base. Training volume is high; competition intensity is low or absent. Example: an 8-week prep phase for a 16-week macrocycle.

5. **Competition Phase (20–30% of macrocycle duration).** Goal: apply preparation work to specific competitive demands. Training volume drops; competition frequency and intensity rise. The athlete is tested in real competition.

6. **Transition Phase (10–20% of macrocycle duration).** Goal: active recovery and psychological refresh. Volume and intensity both drop. This prevents burnout and allows the body to supercompensate (adapt beyond the stress level).

   If your timeline does not fit a 40–20–10 split, adjust proportionally but do not eliminate any phase.

### Step 3 — Subdivide the Macrocycle into Mesocycles

7. **Within the Preparation Phase, create 2–4 mesocycles.** Each mesocycle is 3–6 weeks and targets a specific adaptation (e.g., hypertrophy, strength, power, aerobic capacity). Order them from general to specific: general prep → strength → power/speed → sport-specific.

8. **Within the Competition Phase, create mesocycles aligned to competition schedule.** If competitions are monthly, one mesocycle per competition. If weekly, group 2–3 competitions into one mesocycle.

9. **The Transition Phase is typically one mesocycle** of 1–3 weeks with minimal structure.

### Step 4 — Subdivide Mesocycles into Microcycles

10. **Each mesocycle contains 1–6 microcycles** (typically 1 week each). A microcycle is a repeating sequence of training days with a consistent pattern:
    - **Monday–Wednesday:** Hard work (high volume, high intensity for the mesocycle's target adaptation).
    - **Thursday:** Moderate work or technique work.
    - **Friday:** Recovery session or technique.
    - **Saturday:** Optional second hard session (in high-volume sports).
    - **Sunday:** Full rest or very light activity.

11. **Within each microcycle, apply the Intensity-Volume Tradeoff:**
    - If intensity is high (near-maximal effort), volume is low (few reps, sets, or duration).
    - If intensity is low (submaximal), volume is high.
    - Do not combine high intensity + high volume in the same microcycle; that is overtraining.

### Step 5 — Define Training Stimulus by Mesocycle

12. **Assign a primary training objective to each mesocycle.** Use one of Matveyev's core categories:

    | Mesocycle type | Primary objective | Example intensity | Example volume |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | General Preparation | Aerobic base, work capacity, movement patterns | 60–75% max effort | High (8–12 reps, 3–5 sets) |
    | Specific Preparation | Sport-specific strength, power endurance | 75–85% max effort | Moderate (5–8 reps, 4–6 sets) |
    | Competitive/Pre-competitive | Peak power, technique under fatigue | 85–95% max effort | Low (3–5 reps, 3–4 sets) |
    | Transition | Recovery, variety, general fitness | 50–65% max effort | Low |

13. **For each mesocycle, define concrete training targets.** Not "get stronger" but:
    - "Increase back squat 1RM by 5kg without form breakdown."
    - "Run 5km at 4:00/km pace with HR ≤ 160 bpm."
    - "Complete 10 game-simulation sessions without fatigue-related errors."

### Step 6 — Build the Annual Plan Grid

14. **Create a table with months/weeks as rows and phases as columns:**

    ```
    | Week | Macrocycle | Mesocycle | Microcycle | Focus | Volume | Intensity | Competition |
    |---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
    | 1–4 | Year 1 | Gen Prep 1 | Week 1 | Aerobic base | High | 65% | None |
    | 4–8 | Year 1 | Gen Prep 2 | Week 2 | Movement patterns | High | 70% | None |
    | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
    ```

    Leave room to adjust as the season progresses and real performance data emerges.

### Step 7 — Apply the Supercompensation Principle

15. **After each mesocycle, plan a deload.** A deload is a 4–7 day microcycle (usually the last week of the mesocycle or first week of the next) where volume drops 40–50% and intensity drops to 50–70%. This allows the nervous system and connective tissue to recover and adapt upward.

16. **Expect a performance dip during the deload week.** Athletes often feel weaker. This is normal. The adaptation peak occurs 5–10 days after the deload, not during it.

### Step 8 — Validate Against the Competition Calendar

17. **Cross-check the final plan:** Does the competition phase line up with your target competition date? Will the athlete be in the Competition mesocycle (low volume, high intensity, frequent competition) 2–4 weeks before the peak goal? If no, rebuild the mesocycle boundaries.

18. **Check for overtraining red flags:** Do any two hard mesocycles run back-to-back without a deload? Do Specific Prep and Competitive phases overlap (which forces impossible intensity-volume combinations)? If yes, adjust phase boundaries.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Target competition date is explicit and single (primary goal identified) | Y/N |
| Macrocycle is divided into three distinct phases (Prep / Competition / Transition) | Y/N |
| Each phase duration is ≥10% of total macrocycle | Y/N |
| Mesocycles are ordered Preparation → Competition → Transition (no reordering) | Y/N |
| Every mesocycle has a single defined training objective (e.g., "aerobic base," "peak power") | Y/N |
| No mesocycle combines high volume + high intensity in the same week | Y/N |
| At least one deload/recovery mesocycle is planned after Preparation and before peak competition | Y/N |
| The plan is testable: each mesocycle has measurable targets (e.g., "5km at 4:00/km" not "get faster") | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Competition Phase is >40% of the macrocycle. The athlete is competing too early and too often; too little time remains for building the fitness needed to peak.
- Preparation Phase is <30% of the macrocycle. General and specific preparation are being rushed; adaptation will be shallow.
- All mesocycles have identical structure (same exercises, same rep ranges, same volume). Periodization is in name only; no real phase progression.
- No deload mesocycle exists. The plan assumes continuous linear progression; supercompensation cannot occur, and fatigue will accumulate.
- The target competition is in week 48 but the Competitive Phase starts in week 44. Four weeks is often insufficient for tapering and peaking; the athlete may still be fatigued at the goal event.
- Training objectives are vague ("increase strength," "build endurance"). Without concrete metrics, you cannot track whether the mesocycle is working.
- The plan shows mesocycles but no microcycles (week-level variation). Daily training decisions will be ad hoc, undoing the periodization structure.

# Cornell Note-Taking

## Overview
Divide each page into three zones — a narrow left margin (2–3 inches) for questions and cues, a wide right section for lecture notes, and a footer for summary — then fill them sequentially during and after class. The discipline is the *structured review*: you generate study material while recording, not after. Practitioners use this when they need notes that double as retrieval practice without a separate study-prep phase.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Page Setup

1. **Draw a vertical line 2–3 inches from the left edge of the page.** Use a ruler. The left zone is the *cue column*; the right zone is the *notes section*. Leave the bottom ½–¾ inch of the page blank for the summary.

2. **Label the three zones:** write "Cues" at the top of the left column, "Notes" at the top of the right section, and "Summary" above the footer area. This makes the structure visible.

### Phase 2 — During Capture (Lecture, Reading, or Source Material)

3. **Record in the Notes section only.** Write on the right side of the line. Do not use the cue column yet.

4. **Use telegraphic language — phrases, not sentences.** Write "metabolic pathway disrupted in diabetes" not "In diabetes, the metabolic pathway is disrupted." Abbreviate heavily. Skip connecting words. This forces compression and reduces review time later.

5. **Skip lines between ideas.** Leave space so you can add detail later without crowding. Crowd is a signal that review will fail.

6. **Mark significant or unclear points with a marker or asterisk.** Do not stop to resolve them — mark and move on.

### Phase 3 — Immediate Post-Processing (Within 24 Hours)

7. **Review the Notes section and fill the Cue column.** For each cluster of notes on the right, write a question or keyword on the left that would prompt recall of the notes.
   - If notes say "mitochondria generate ATP via oxidative phosphorylation," the cue is "How do mitochondria make ATP?"
   - If notes list three types of immune response, the cue is "Types of immune response?" or just "Immune: 3 types?"

8. **Use a different pen color for cues.** This makes review navigation fast — you can quickly see what you wrote during capture vs. what you added later.

9. **Resolve marked unclear points.** Look up definitions, fill in gaps. Write the clarification in the Notes section (use a highlighter or bracket to mark it as post-lecture addition).

10. **Write the Summary in the footer.** Compress the entire page into 3–4 sentences. Answer: "What is the point of this page? What should I remember?" Write only the summary, not the detail.

### Phase 4 — Review (Spaced Repetition)

11. **Fold or cover the Notes section, leaving only the Cue column visible.** Read each cue and try to recall the notes before looking.

12. **Check your recall against the Notes section.** Mark cues you got right. Revisit cues you missed or got partly wrong.

13. **Use the Summary to anchor the big picture.** After cue-based recall, skim the summary to confirm you've captured the page's core.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Three zones are visually distinct and labeled | Y/N |
| Notes section uses telegraphic language (phrases, not sentences) | Y/N |
| Cue column contains ≥1 question or keyword per note cluster | Y/N |
| Cues are phrased as retrieval prompts, not chapter headings | Y/N |
| Summary is ≤4 sentences and answers "What is the point?" | Y/N |
| No notes section written in the cue column during capture | Y/N |
| Post-processing cues are visually distinct from capture (different pen/color) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Notes section contains full sentences or paragraphs. This defeats compression and makes review slow.
- Cue column is empty or contains only chapter titles ("Mitochondria," "Immune System"). These are topics, not retrieval cues. A cue must be a question or prompt.
- Summary is longer than 4 sentences or repeats phrases from the notes verbatim. The summary should compress, not echo.
- Cues were written during capture instead of during post-processing. Cues written live often miss the actual difficulty points.
- Left margin contains notes written during class. The left side must stay blank during capture or the structure collapses.
- No visual distinction between during-capture and post-processing additions. This makes it hard to tell what was captured vs. what was researched later.

# SQ3R / PQ4R

## Overview
SQ3R (Survey, Question, Read, Recite, Review) and its extension PQ4R (Preview, Question, Read, Reflect, Recite, Review) are structured reading protocols that convert passive consumption into active knowledge construction. Instead of linear page-turning, readers interrogate the text before, during, and after engagement, forcing the brain to encode information as answers to self-generated questions rather than as isolated facts. Practitioners use this when absorbing technical material, textbooks, research papers, or any text where retention and conceptual understanding matter more than speed.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Preview (P in PQ4R) / Survey (S in SQ3R)

1. **Skim the entire text for structure without reading prose.** Spend 3–5 minutes per 10–15 pages. Look at:
   - Title, subtitle, and publication metadata
   - Chapter or section headings
   - Subheadings, bolded terms, bullet lists
   - Diagrams, tables, captions
   - First and last paragraph of each major section
   - Glossary or summary if present

2. **Write a single-sentence prediction:** What is this text about at the highest level? (Not "machine learning" but "how gradient descent minimizes loss in neural networks.")

3. **Note the intended audience.** Is this aimed at beginners, peers in your field, or specialists? This tells you whether unfamiliar terms are defined or assumed.

### Phase 2 — Question (Q in both)

4. **Convert each heading into a question.** For each section heading, subheading, or major paragraph topic, write a question that the section should answer. Do not read yet.
   - Heading: "The Role of Attention Mechanisms"
   - Question: "Why are attention mechanisms necessary, and what problem do they solve?"

5. **Add your own questions.** Before reading, write 2–3 questions you hope the text will answer. These come from your current confusion, skepticism, or curiosity.

6. **Collect all questions in a single list.** You will use this list to guide reading and to structure review.

### Phase 3 — Read (R in both)

7. **Read only to answer the questions from Phase 2.** Do not try to memorize. Do not read every word with equal weight. Skim sections that don't speak to your questions; slow down where answers appear.

8. **Underline or highlight sparingly.** Mark only the phrase that directly answers a question. Avoid marking more than one sentence per question. (Heavy highlighting is a sign that you're not yet clear on what the answer is — re-read that section.)

9. **Write the answer to each question in the margin or in a separate notebook.** Use your own words. If you cannot answer in your own words, you do not yet understand — re-read that section.

10. **Note connections.** If a section answers multiple questions or contradicts an earlier section, mark it. If new questions emerge, add them to your list.

### Phase 4 — Reflect (in PQ4R only; less critical in SQ3R)

11. **Pause every 2–3 questions or every 5–10 minutes of reading.** Ask:
    - What does this answer mean in my own context?
    - How does this connect to something I already know?
    - Do I agree? Where do I doubt?
    - What example or application comes to mind?

12. **Write a brief reflection (2–3 sentences) that anchors the material to your experience or prior knowledge.** This step is what separates memorization from understanding.

### Phase 5 — Recite (R in both)

13. **Close the text.** Without looking, recite aloud the answer to each question. Do this in your own words and with as much detail as you can recover.

14. **Check your answers against your notes.** Where you forgot or got wrong, mark those questions as "weak."

15. **For weak questions, re-read that section and re-answer.** Do not move on until you can recite the answer without the text in front of you.

### Phase 6 — Review (R in both)

16. **On the same day, re-read your list of questions and answers once.** Do not re-read the text. This takes 10–15 minutes and cements encoding.

17. **Schedule a second review 2–3 days later.** Use the same questions; recite answers from memory. If you falter, re-read only that section.

18. **Schedule a third review one week later.** Same process.

19. **Optional: Create a concept map or summary document.** Map how the answers relate to each other and to your prior knowledge. This is the final encoding step and produces durable, retrievable knowledge.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Preview completed in under 10% of total reading time | Y/N |
| At least one question generated per major heading | Y/N |
| Questions are answerable by the text (not too vague) | Y/N |
| Answers were written in own words during reading | Y/N |
| Reflection (PQ4R) connected material to prior knowledge | Y/N |
| Recitation produced ≥70% recall without text | Y/N |
| Review was scheduled for days 2–3 and day 7 | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Reader skipped Preview and jumped to reading. The protocol loses its power without front-loading — recall drops sharply.
- Questions are too general ("What is this about?") or too narrow ("What color was mentioned on page 4?"). Good questions sit between — they demand understanding, not trivia.
- Heavy underlining throughout (more than 10% of text). This signals confusion about what matters. The reader is trying to memorize rather than answer questions.
- Answers in margins are copied verbatim from text. Paraphrasing is non-negotiable — it forces encoding.
- Recitation produces <50% recall. The protocol was not followed correctly; likely skipped Reflection or did not re-answer weak questions.
- Review was done only once. Without spaced repetition (days 2–3, day 7), forgetting curves erase the benefit by the second week.
- No concept map or integration step. The reader extracted answers but never synthesized them into a coherent model.

# Participant Observation

## Overview
Participant Observation is systematic documentation of language as it occurs in natural settings, with the observer embedded enough to understand the social rules and constraints that shape speech. The observer is not a passive recorder but an active participant whose presence and questions inform what gets said and how. Practitioners use this when they need to account for context that formal interviews or corpora cannot capture — why a speaker code-switches at a specific moment, what counts as polite or transgressive, or how participants repair understanding when literal language fails.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Prepare and Establish Positionality

1. **Define the speech community.** Name the group (age range, occupation, geographic location, shared practice, or identity marker), its approximate size, and its primary physical or virtual spaces. Write 2–3 sentences on why language in this group is worth studying and what tensions or shifts you expect to find.

2. **Declare your positionality.** What is your relationship to the group — insider or outsider, native speaker or learner, same-gender or different, power differential (teacher/student, researcher/participant)? How will your presence shape what speakers do? Do not assume objectivity; state instead what biases you bring and what access you have.

3. **Negotiate entry and role clarity.** If the group is bounded (a workplace, classroom, family), secure explicit permission and clarify whether you are a participant, a "non-participating observer," or something in between. Clarify what you will and will not record, how data will be used, and who sees it. Document this agreement.

4. **Establish baseline routines and turn-taking norms.** Before targeted recording, spend 1–2 sessions observing without a recorder: How do people greet? Who speaks first? What interrupts turn-taking? How long are pauses? Are some voices raised, others soft? Write field notes on these baseline patterns so you can recognize deviations later.

### Phase 2 — Collect Observations in the Field

5. **Conduct weekly or bi-weekly sessions of 45–90 minutes.** Shorter sessions reduce fatigue and novelty effects; longer ones let you see how language evolves as self-consciousness fades. Attend at least 6 sessions before attempting analysis (origin uncertain, but convention in linguistic ethnography is n ≥ 6 for pattern stability).

6. **Record audio and/or video, or write real-time field notes, depending on your access and consent.** If recording, note the date, time, participants present (pseudonyms), activity (conversation, meeting, lesson), and anything unusual (absences, tension, visiting guests). If writing notes, use a structured format:
   - **Time stamps** for key utterances
   - **Speaker identities** (role, pseudonym, relationship to others present)
   - **Verbatim fragments** of speech, especially unusual constructions or code-switches
   - **Paralinguistic details** (tone, pace, gesture, facial expression if visible)
   - **Context notes** (what precipitated the speech, what reactions followed, what was interrupted)
   - **Analyst asides** (flagged clearly as your interpretation, not the data)

7. **Notice and record departures from baseline.** When someone's speech differs from the norms you documented in Phase 1 — a shift in tone, unexpected code-switching, a violation of turn-taking, avoidance of a topic — mark it with a flag (e.g., `[SHIFT]`, `[BREACH]`) and note what might have triggered it.

8. **Ask clarifying questions *in the moment* when linguistic meaning is unclear.** Do not wait for transcription. For example: "When you said 'that's not tight,' did you mean...?" or "I heard you switch to English just then — what made you do that?" Write down the speaker's answer verbatim. This is not leading; it is disambiguation.

9. **Maintain a separate reflective journal.** After each session, write 1–2 pages on:
   - Patterns you noticed and why they stand out
   - Moments when your presence seemed to matter (e.g., people spoke more formally, avoided a topic, explained something for your benefit)
   - Questions that emerged that you could not answer in the moment
   - Changes in your own speech or comfort level in the group

### Phase 3 — Analyze Patterns and Constraints

10. **Transcribe or review all recordings/notes for recurring phenomena.** Sort by category:
    - **Register and formality shifts** — moments when the same speaker uses noticeably different vocabulary, grammar, or accent depending on who is present or what is being discussed
    - **Code-switching and code-mixing** — shifts between languages, dialects, or styles within a single turn or conversation
    - **Repair sequences** — moments when understanding breaks down and speakers negotiate meaning ("What do you mean by X?" "Oh, I meant Y")
    - **Taboo or sensitive topics** — subjects that trigger euphemism, hedging, lowered volume, or topic avoidance
    - **Role-specific speech** — how authority, status, or relationships constrain what someone can say (e.g., younger people use "sir," elders use imperatives)
    - **Metalinguistic commentary** — moments when speakers explicitly discuss language ("That's not how we say it," "You're not pronouncing that right")

11. **For each category, extract 5–10 representative examples.** Include the full context (who, when, what precipitated it, what followed). Alongside each example, write: "This is noticeable because..." and "The likely constraint is..." (social rule, power relation, identity marker, genre expectation, cognitive limitation, etc.).

12. **Identify what participants *don't* say.** Interview a few participants separately (outside the group) and ask: "Is there anything about [topic] that people usually don't talk about here? What would happen if someone did?" Compare the gap between private interviews and group speech. This often reveals unstated rules.

13. **Document the exceptions.** For each pattern you identified, find moments where someone broke it. Ask that person (if possible) why. Did they not know the rule? Did they break it deliberately? Did circumstances override the rule?

### Phase 4 — Produce Findings

14. **Write up each major pattern as a finding.** Each finding has three components:
    - **The pattern:** Describe what recurs and how often (e.g., "Code-switches to English occur in 18 of 42 recorded turns, predominantly when discussing technical topics or expressing intensity").
    - **The constraint:** Name the social rule, relationship, or context that explains it (e.g., "English is the language of technical education and emotional emphasis in this community; Swahili is reserved for formal greetings and social navigation").
    - **The evidence:** Link to 2–3 examples from your data.

15. **Distinguish between observation and interpretation.** If you observed that speakers lower their volume when discussing a topic, that is observation. If you then claim that "they are embarrassed," that is interpretation and needs either explicit participant confirmation or careful hedging ("The lowered volume may indicate...").

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Positionality is explicit (relationship to group, biases, access) | Y/N |
| Baseline norms documented before targeted recording | Y/N |
| Minimum 6 sessions conducted, each 45+ minutes or equivalent depth | Y/N |
| Field notes include timestamps, speaker IDs, verbatim fragments, and context | Y/N |
| Departures from baseline are flagged in the moment | Y/N |
| At least 5 linguistic categories extracted with 5+ examples each | Y/N |
| Each pattern is attributed to a named constraint (rule, role, context) | Y/N |
| Observations distinguished from interpretations (no unhedged claims about internal states) | Y/N |
| Exceptions to patterns are documented and investigated | Y/N |
| Reflective journal shows iteration and surprise, not just confirmation | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All speech in the group sounds the same across all participants and contexts. Either homogeneity is real (rare; document carefully) or you are not hearing variation because your ear is not tuned yet. Record a session, play it back, and listen again.
- Departures from baseline are recorded but never explained. A code-switch without context or follow-up is noise, not finding. Go back and ask.
- The constraints you identify are universal ("people are more polite with authority figures") rather than group-specific. The lens requires discovering *this* community's rules, not confirming anthropological clichés.
- Your reflective journal shows no moments of surprise or revision. If every session confirms what you expected, you are likely observing selectively. Force yourself to note three things in each session that contradicted your prior assumptions.
- Participants are never asked for clarification or meta-commentary. Participant Observation is not passive. Without asking, you cannot distinguish between "the norm here is X" and "I think the norm is X."
- Interpretations about participants' emotions, intentions, or awareness are presented as fact. "She was annoyed" needs either explicit confirmation ("I asked her if she was annoyed, and she said yes") or hedging ("Her voice dropped and she looked away; this *may* indicate annoyance").

# Devil's Advocacy

## Overview

Devil's Advocacy assigns one person or subgroup the explicit role of constructing and defending the strongest case *against* a favored proposal, even if they privately agree with it. The discipline is the *construction* — a weak or strawmanned opposition is worse than none, because it creates the illusion of scrutiny without substance. Practitioners use this when a decision has begun to feel settled, dissent has gone quiet, or the cost of being wrong is high enough to warrant manufactured friction.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish the Proposal and Baseline

1. **State the proposal in writing.** One paragraph. Include the decision being made, the intended outcome, and the assumed constraints (budget, timeline, risk tolerance, stakeholder buy-in).

2. **Identify the core arguments in favor.** List the 4-6 strongest reasons the proposal is on the table. Do not caricature them. If someone disagreed with you on this list, you'd need to revise it. Write each as a complete sentence.

3. **Document the current consensus level.** On a scale of 1–10, how settled is the decision? Who are the key decision-makers? Who has dissented, and what happened to them (were they heard, dismissed, or silent)? This baseline matters — Devil's Advocacy is most valuable when consensus is high.

### Phase 2 — Construct the Opposition

4. **Assign the advocate role explicitly.** One person takes responsibility for building the case against the proposal. They are not voicing their personal opinion; they are constructing an argument. Make this distinction clear in the room.

5. **For each core argument in favor (from Step 2), generate a direct rebuttal.** Do not dismiss it — counter it. The template is:
   - Argument in favor: "<claim>"
   - Rebuttal: "<specific contradiction or weakness>"
   - Evidence or logic supporting the rebuttal: "<what fact, principle, or precedent supports the rebuttal>"

6. **Add three category-specific critiques,** even if not directly contradicting the stated arguments:
   - **Hidden cost**: What is the proposal NOT accounting for? (Time to integrate, organizational friction, technical debt, team morale, opportunity cost of not doing something else.)
   - **Assumption under pressure**: What assumption would collapse if one key fact changed? Test it. (E.g., "This plan assumes the timeline won't slip. What if it does?")
   - **Precedent or pattern**: Has something similar been tried? What happened? Does the proposal account for why it might fail again?

7. **Construct a "best case for rejection."** Write a 2-3 sentence summary of the strongest single reason NOT to do this. This is not a list of objections — it is the core case the advocate would make to a skeptic who asked, "Why should we not do this?"

### Phase 3 — Stress the Opposition

8. **The devil's advocate presents the case to decision-makers.** At least 5 minutes of uninterrupted statement. Do not hedge with "but I actually think..." or "this is just a thought experiment." Speak as though you believe it.

9. **Decision-makers respond with rebuttals to the opposition.** The advocate then defends their position. This is genuine debate, not a scripted exchange. The goal is not to convince anyone to change their vote — it is to expose gaps in reasoning, unexamined assumptions, or overconfidence.

10. **Document where the proposal was challenged.** Which arguments in favor held up under pressure? Which were hedged or abandoned? Where did decision-makers retreat into "but we have to do something" or "I just have a feeling this is right"? These concessions are the output of the lens — they are where the real risk lives.

### Phase 4 — Assess Revised Confidence

11. **Revisit the baseline consensus from Step 3.** Has it changed? By how much? If consensus is now *lower*, the devil's advocacy worked — you've surfaced doubt. If consensus is *unchanged*, ask why the opposition had no effect. Was the advocate insufficiently adversarial? Did decision-makers dismiss it without engaging?

12. **Identify any modifications to the proposal.** The goal of Devil's Advocacy is not to kill decisions — it is to make them more honest. If the debate forced a scope reduction, a contingency plan, or an exit criteria, document it.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Proposal is stated in one paragraph with intended outcome and constraints | Y/N |
| 4-6 strongest arguments in favor are documented (not caricatures) | Y/N |
| Baseline consensus level recorded before opposition is constructed | Y/N |
| Every argument in favor has a direct, specific rebuttal (not dismissal) | Y/N |
| Hidden cost, pressure assumption, and precedent are all addressed | Y/N |
| Devil's advocate speaks unhedged for ≥5 minutes | Y/N |
| Decision-makers genuinely respond (not preempt or ignore) | Y/N |
| Concessions or modifications to proposal are documented | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The advocate's case is weaker than the case in favor. This is not opposition — it is a controlled demolition designed to make the proposal look unassailable.
- Consensus is unchanged after the opposition. Either the advocate was insufficiently adversarial, the decision-makers have not actually engaged (they are listening to conclude, not to think), or the proposal is genuinely robust. Only the third is acceptable — the other two are failures.
- The advocate is someone already known to oppose the proposal. Devil's Advocacy loses force if performed by someone with pre-existing skin in the game. Skeptics are not devil's advocates.
- Decision-makers respond with "that's a good point, but..." and then do nothing. Acknowledgment without action is a way to neutralize dissent while preserving consensus. Any modifications to the proposal should be documented.
- The debate is scripted or performative. The value of Devil's Advocacy is in the friction — if it feels like a box to check, it is.
- The advocate argues against a strawmanned weak version of the proposal instead of the strongest version. The whole point is to test the best case, not the worst case.

# Blue Ocean ERRC Grid

## Overview
The ERRC Grid (Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create) is a structured tool for breaking free of the factors that define your industry's competitive landscape. Rather than competing harder on the dimensions the industry already fights over, the grid prompts you to systematically ask which factors to eliminate (drop entirely), reduce (move below industry standard), raise (push above industry standard), and create (introduce something the industry has never offered). Practitioners use this when their market feels saturated and customers show declining willingness to pay for marginal improvements to known features.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish Baseline

1. **List every factor the industry currently competes on.** These are the dimensions customers have learned to compare. Include pricing, quality metrics, features, service speed, brand associations, distribution channels — everything competitors advertise and customers evaluate. Aim for 8-15 factors; fewer suggests you've missed categories, more suggests you've over-granularized.

2. **For each factor, determine the current industry standard.** If the industry has consolidated around a feature (e.g., "all phones have touchscreens"), note it. If there is price-based segmentation (e.g., "budget vs. premium"), map both tiers.

3. **Rate each factor's emotional weight with end customers:** — Does it drive purchase decisions, or is it hygiene (expected but not differentiating)? Tag each as "hygiene," "differentiator," or "unknown." Validate against real customer research if possible; do not guess.

### Phase 2 — Eliminate

4. **For each factor, ask: "What does the industry take for granted that customers no longer need?"** Look especially for factors that:
   - Persist due to historical competition (rivals matched each other so both kept doing it)
   - Were once valuable but have been commoditized
   - Customers tolerate but don't actively want
   - Increase cost without increasing perceived value
   
5. **Shortlist 2-4 candidates for elimination.** Write a one-sentence rationale for each: why remove it, and what value does the customer actually lose (or gain via cost reduction)?

### Phase 3 — Reduce

6. **For each remaining factor, ask: "Below what level is this good enough?"** Industrial-standard is not always customer-desired. For factors currently at or above industry norm, identify which ones could drop 20-40% without materially harming customer perception or outcome.

7. **Mark each with a concrete reduction target.** Not "simplify," but "reduce manufacturing tolerances from ±0.1mm to ±0.5mm" or "cut customer service response time from 2 hours to 24 hours." Vagueness here signals the grid is being completed carelessly.

### Phase 4 — Raise

8. **For each factor where the industry underinvests relative to customer frustration, ask: "What would delight customers?"** Look for gaps between what customers say they want and what competitors offer. These are often obvious in the field (customer complaints, feature requests) but invisible in marketing materials.

9. **Shortlist 2-4 factors to raise and define the target level explicitly.** Not "better quality," but "reduce defect rate from 2% to 0.1%" or "cut lead time from 6 weeks to 2 weeks."

### Phase 5 — Create

10. **Brainstorm factors the industry has never competed on.** These may be:
    - Attributes entirely outside the category (e.g., fashion brands introducing sustainability into retail)
    - Emotional or experiential dimensions competitors ignore (e.g., "feeling of control" for a tool)
    - Business model or delivery innovations (e.g., subscription vs. ownership)
    - Customer segment expansions (who the industry has ignored)

11. **For each candidate creation, verify it is genuinely new to the competitive set, not already offered by some player.** Research competitor offerings; do not rely on memory. Then test customer demand: does this actually matter to your target segment, or is it novelty without stickiness?

12. **Shortlist 1-3 creations and define them concretely.** "Personalization" is not testable; "AI-generated daily plans tailored to fitness level and available time" is.

### Phase 6 — Integration and Coherence Check

13. **Map the four quadrants onto a single canvas: Eliminate | Reduce | Raise | Create.** Lay out the factors visually.

14. **Check coherence.** The factors you eliminate and reduce must support the factors you raise and create. If you eliminate "premium manufacturing" but raise "durability," you have a contradiction. A coherent grid tells a single story about what the new offering is *for*.

15. **Sense-check against price positioning.** If you eliminate and reduce heavily, you are targeting cost-conscious buyers — creations and raises must align with lower price. If you raise several high-value dimensions, you cannot simultaneously eliminate cost.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Industry baseline captures 8+ distinct competitive factors | Y/N |
| Each factor is rated for emotional weight (hygiene/differentiator/unknown) | Y/N |
| Eliminate list includes 2-4 factors with one-sentence rationale per | Y/N |
| Reduce list specifies concrete reduction targets (not vague) | Y/N |
| Raise list specifies concrete improvement targets (not vague) | Y/N |
| Create list includes 1-3 verified-new factors with customer demand signal | Y/N |
| ERRC grid is internally coherent (no contradictions) | Y/N |
| Price positioning is consistent with the elimination/reduction/raise/create mix | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Eliminate list is empty. Every industry has convention; refusing to challenge any of it signals insufficient rigor.
- Reduce and Raise lists are nearly identical to current competitors. No strategic repositioning has occurred.
- Create list borrows heavily from adjacent industries without testing customer fit. Novelty is not innovation.
- Factors on Raise list are described generically ("better," "faster") with no concrete target. This grid is incomplete.
- The four quadrants tell no coherent story. Eliminate and Create point in opposite directions, or Reduce and Raise seem arbitrary.
- No price positioning decision was made. A Blue Ocean move is meaningless without clarity on whether you are pursuing cost leadership or premium differentiation.
- Factors were rated by executive intuition only, never validated against customer research. The grid is speculation, not strategy.

# Getting to Yes

## Overview
Rather than trading offers and counter-offers on stated positions, identify each party's underlying interests, separate interpersonal friction from the problem itself, and expand the solution space before evaluating options against objective criteria. Practitioners reach for this when negotiation has stalled on incompatible demands or when they suspect positions are masking deeper, potentially compatible needs. The discipline is discipline: not evaluating options until the room is full of them, and not conflating the other party's person with their interests.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Separate People from Problem

1. **Map the parties and their stated positions.** List each side and what they are explicitly asking for or rejecting. Be precise — "We want fair terms" is not a position, "We want 30% discount on volume orders" is.

2. **For each party, conduct an interests interview.** Ask:
   - Why do they want what they stated? What need, fear, or constraint drives it?
   - What would happen to them if they don't get it?
   - What are they concerned will happen if they accept your proposal?
   - What do they value beyond money (reputation, time, control, precedent, relationship)?
   Write down at least 3 interests per party. If you cannot articulate their interests in your own words without them correcting you, keep interviewing.

3. **Identify emotional/relational blockers.** Are there:
   - Unaddressed slights or broken trust?
   - Communication breakdowns (one party believes the other won't listen)?
   - Identity threats (agreeing feels like losing face)?
   - Competing narratives about who is at fault?
   Do not try to solve these in Phase 1. Just name them. They are separate problems from the substantive deal.

4. **Distinguish the person from the position.** A person can be difficult without their interests being unreasonable. Mark:
   - Is the difficulty behavioral (tone, pace, stubbornness) or substantive (their interests truly conflict)?
   - Can you address the behavioral part independently of the deal? (e.g., "I hear you think we're acting in bad faith. Let's talk about how we rebuild confidence, separate from the pricing conversation.")

### Phase 2 — Focus on Interests, Not Positions

5. **Create an interests matrix.** Rows are each party (including yourself). Columns are interest categories: Financial, Operational, Relational, Legal/Risk, Time-bound, Precedent-setting.
   - Fill each cell with the party's real interests in that category, not their stated position.
   - Mark which interests are compatible (both parties benefit from the same outcome) and which are in tension.
   - Example: Party A values speed (wants decision in 30 days). Party B values deliberation (wants 90 days). The tension is real, but both value *predictability* — a shared interest in knowing the timeline now.

6. **Highlight shared and asymmetric interests.**
   - Shared: "Both of us need this deal to close within Q2 for budget purposes."
   - Asymmetric but reconcilable: "You need flexibility on payment terms; we need upfront cash. You value a long-term relationship; we value immediate certainty."
   - Directly opposed: "You want us to take all legal liability; we will not." — This is genuinely adversarial and must be addressed later.

7. **Prioritize interests within each party's set.** Ask each side: "If you could satisfy only three of your interests in this negotiation, which three?" This forces clarity on what actually matters. Positions often include noise; interests rarely do.

### Phase 3 — Generate Options Before Evaluating

8. **Expand the solution space.** Do NOT evaluate proposals yet. Goal: quantity, not quality. Techniques:
   - **Brainstorm freely.** Each side proposes solutions with zero commitment. Bad ideas are fine; they unblock good ones.
   - **Add variables beyond the obvious one.** If the core tension is price, add: payment schedule, volume commitments, warranties, exclusivity, training, ongoing support, performance bonuses, opt-out clauses, third-party verification.
   - **Ask "What if?"** — "What if we shared the risk instead of one of us bearing it?" "What if we started small and scaled?" "What if a third party arbitrated the disputed term?"
   - **Invert positions.** If Party A wants exclusivity and Party B wants freedom, ask what partial exclusivity looks like.
   Write every option down. Nothing is off the table yet.

9. **Group options into packages.** Not all options work together. Cluster them:
   - Package 1: Low price, long payment terms, limited warranty
   - Package 2: Higher price, immediate payment, full warranty and training
   - Package 3: Hybrid — price tied to performance metrics, payment by milestone
   Each package should satisfy different priority combinations from the interests matrix.

### Phase 4 — Use Objective Criteria

10. **Define standards for evaluation before comparing packages.** These are the yardsticks, not the outcomes. Examples:
    - Market rate for this service (check 3+ independent sources)
    - Industry precedent (what do similar deals look like?)
    - Cost-of-alternatives for each party (BATNA — Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement)
    - Fairness principle (equal percentage risk, equal profit margins, etc.)
    - Scientific or technical standard (engineer-determined safety minimum)
    Agree on the standard *with the other party* if possible. If not, state which standard each of you proposes and why.

11. **Apply the standard to each package.** Score each option against each criterion:
    - Does Package 1 beat market rate? By how much?
    - Does Package 2 create precedent risk? What precedent?
    - Does Package 3 align with both parties' cost-of-alternatives?
    Use objective data (contracts, market reports, engineering specs), not intuition.

12. **Identify legitimate disagreement.** Even with objective criteria, parties may weight them differently:
    - You prioritize market rate (suggests Package 2).
    - They prioritize precedent (suggests Package 1).
    This is acceptable. The disagreement is now transparent and bounded.

### Phase 5 — Negotiating Hard Cases

13. **For positions that remain incompatible after Phase 4:**
    - Reframe as a shared problem: "We both want this deal to work. The term in question is blocking it. What would need to change for you to agree?"
    - Test the walking-away point. Ask: "If we cannot resolve this, what is your next move?" This is their BATNA. If their BATNA is worse than your worst-case offer, room for agreement exists.
    - Propose criteria for deciding the term if direct agreement fails: coin flip, median of proposals, expert determination, phased implementation with review.

14. **Protect yourself against manipulation.** If the other party:
    - Claims artificial scarcity ("This offer expires tomorrow") — ask for it in writing; verify the deadline independently.
    - Demands you move without reciprocation — pause and ask what they are conceding in return.
    - Changes the negotiating team or narrative mid-process — restart clarification (Phase 1, steps 2-3).

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Stated positions for all parties are documented | Y/N |
| Each party's underlying interests (≥3 per side) are articulated | Y/N |
| Emotional/relational blockers identified separately from substantive issues | Y/N |
| Interests matrix completed (all parties, major categories) | Y/N |
| At least one asymmetric but reconcilable interest pair identified | Y/N |
| Options brainstorm produced ≥5 distinct proposals | Y/N |
| Options grouped into ≥2 packages combining multiple variables | Y/N |
| Objective criteria defined *before* package comparison | Y/N |
| Each package scored against each criterion | Y/N |
| Remaining incompatibilities are named and bounded | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Interests are restated positions ("They want a lower price" instead of "They need to reduce cost-of-goods to hit their margin target"). If you cannot tell someone's interest in a sentence without using their stated demand, you haven't found the interest yet.
- The brainstorm in Phase 3 produces fewer than 5 options, or all options cluster around a single variable. The solution space was not expanded.
- Objective criteria are chosen *after* packages are proposed. This invites cherry-picking standards that favor your preferred outcome. Criteria must come first.
- Emotional blockers were identified in Phase 1 but never addressed. They will resurface during evaluation and collapse the deal.
- A "final offer" appears before the options brainstorm. This kills the method — positions have reasserted themselves over interests.
- One party announces their BATNA and it is better than your best offer. You have a real impasse, not a negotiation problem. Acknowledge this explicitly.

# BATNA (Fisher/Ury)

## Overview
BATNA—Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement—is the course of action you will take if the negotiation fails. It is not a wish list or a bottom line; it is the concrete next step you will actually execute. A clear BATNA transforms negotiation dynamics: it anchors your decisions, prevents you from accepting a bad deal under pressure, and paradoxically increases your leverage by signaling that you have options. Practitioners use this lens before high-stakes negotiations to move from vague anxiety ("I hope I get a good deal") to grounded clarity ("I know exactly what happens if we don't reach terms").

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Identify Alternatives

1. **List every concrete action you could take if this negotiation fails.** Do not filter for desirability yet. Write down 5-8 alternatives, including:
   - Walking away and doing nothing (and what that costs you)
   - Going to a competing supplier, buyer, or partner
   - Fixing the problem yourself with internal resources
   - Accepting the status quo and living with the current problem
   - Escalating to a different decision-maker or venue
   - Pursuing a partial solution with a subset of your needs met

2. **For each alternative, estimate the tangible outcomes.** Do not use vague language. For each, write:
   - **Cost**: How much will it cost in time, money, reputation, or opportunity?
   - **Timeline**: How long until this alternative delivers value or closes the gap?
   - **Risk**: What could go wrong? Assign a rough probability (high/medium/low).
   - **Acceptability**: Is this genuinely acceptable to you, or are you pretending?

3. **Discard alternatives that fail the acceptability test.** If you would not actually execute an alternative when the moment comes, remove it from the list. A BATNA you won't use is not a BATNA; it is a fantasy.

4. **Rank the remaining alternatives by net value.** The formula is crude but useful:
   - Net Value = (Expected Outcome) − (Cost + Risk Discount)
   - Cost and Risk Discount are both dollar equivalents (or time/reputation equivalents translated to a common scale).
   - Do not obsess over precision. The ranking just needs to show which alternatives are clearly better and which are clearly worse.

### Phase 2 — Select and Test Your BATNA

5. **Identify your single BATNA.** It is the highest-ranked alternative from Phase 1. Write it down as a complete sentence describing the action you will take. Be specific:
   - Bad: "Find another vendor."
   - Good: "Call Supplier X on Tuesday morning, request a meeting by Friday, and negotiate a 3-month trial contract at 15% below their standard rate."

6. **Pressure-test your BATNA.** Ask yourself:
   - Am I genuinely willing to execute this if talks break down?
   - If the other party calls my bluff, will I follow through?
   - Does my BATNA account for the time lag before it delivers value? (e.g., a new vendor takes 6 weeks to onboard)
   - Have I validated that this alternative is actually available? (e.g., did I confirm the competitor can actually serve me?)
   - What does the other party know or suspect about my BATNA?

7. **Establish your reservation value.** This is the threshold: any deal better than your BATNA is worth taking; any deal worse is worth walking away from. Write it as a single measurable criterion:
   - Bad: "A reasonable price."
   - Good: "A price ≤ $50K/year with ≥99.5% uptime SLA and a 90-day exit clause."

### Phase 3 — Calibrate Your Negotiating Position

8. **Compare your BATNA to the other party's likely BATNA.** What happens if *they* walk away? If their alternative is much worse than yours, you have leverage. If their alternative is better, they have leverage. Write down:
   - What you believe their BATNA is (based on public information, market dynamics, or explicit hints)
   - How it compares to yours in terms of time, cost, and risk
   - What this asymmetry means for your negotiating room (e.g., "They can walk to Vendor Y in 2 weeks; I can walk to Vendor Z in 6 weeks — they have more time flexibility")

9. **Identify your ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement).** This is the range between:
   - Your reservation value (anything worse than your BATNA is unacceptable)
   - The other party's reservation value (anything worse than *their* BATNA is unacceptable)
   - If your reservation value is higher than their reservation value, there is no ZOPA. Walk away.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| At least 5 concrete alternatives were identified (no vague options) | Y/N |
| Each alternative has estimated Cost, Timeline, Risk, and Acceptability | Y/N |
| All unacceptable alternatives were removed from consideration | Y/N |
| BATNA is stated as a complete action plan, not a category | Y/N |
| Reservation value is measurable and reflects the BATNA outcome | Y/N |
| The interrogation in Phase 2, step 6 was answered honestly (not defensively) | Y/N |
| ZOPA was calculated or explicitly confirmed as nonexistent | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- No alternatives are truly acceptable, only "better than nothing." This signals that the negotiation itself is desperation, not choice. Proceed with caution or reconsider whether you should be negotiating.
- Your BATNA is vague ("find another partner") or hypothetical ("I could theoretically build this myself"). You have not cleared Phase 1. Do not enter the negotiation.
- Your reservation value is identical to what you hope to get from the negotiation. This is not a reservation value; it is wishful thinking. Lower it to what you'll actually accept if the other party refuses.
- You have not investigated the other party's BATNA. You are negotiating blind.
- The other party's BATNA is much better than yours, the ZOPA is tiny or nonexistent, but you're entering the negotiation anyway "hoping for the best." This is not negotiation; it is optimism under pressure. Either improve your BATNA first or reset your expectations.
- You discover mid-negotiation that your BATNA isn't actually available (the competitor declined, the internal fix is infeasible). You have lost your exit and your leverage. This failure happens because Phase 1 was not validated; alternatives were assumed, not confirmed.

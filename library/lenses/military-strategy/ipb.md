# Intelligence Preparation of the Battlefield

## Overview
IPB is a structured, four-step process for analyzing the operational environment — terrain, weather, adversary capabilities, and likely adversary intentions — before committing forces to a plan. It operates on raw intelligence (maps, reports, sensor data, historical records) and produces a coherent picture of what the enemy can do and what the terrain permits. Practitioners use it to identify which outcomes are physically possible, which are likely given adversary doctrine, and which gaps in intelligence matter most for the decision.

## Analytical Procedure

### Step 1 — Define the Battlefield

1. **Establish geographic boundaries.** Define the area of interest (AOI) and area of operations (AO) in specific coordinates or terrain features. Include depth: forward edge of own forces, rear area, flanks, and any sanctuaries or neutral zones.

2. **List the natural and built features.** For each terrain feature, record:
   - **Mobility corridors:** roads, valleys, open ground where vehicles/troops can move freely
   - **Obstacles:** ridgelines, swamps, urban terrain, rivers, minefields that channel or slow movement
   - **Key terrain:** hilltops, bridges, supply nodes, command posts — places whose control dominates adjacent areas
   - **Concealment and cover:** forests, buildings, ditches that hide forces from observation

3. **Document weather patterns.** For the season and time of year of the operation:
   - Temperature range, precipitation, frost/snow likelihood
   - Wind direction and speed (affects smoke, sound, drone/artillery operations)
   - Visibility (fog, dust, darkness duration)
   - Seasonal constraints (mud season, monsoon, etc.)

4. **Create a sketch or matrix.** Annotate a map or gridded table showing corridors, obstacles, key terrain, and concealment. This becomes the baseline for all subsequent analysis.

### Step 2 — Describe the Adversary

5. **Identify the adversary's organization.** Write down:
   - Command structure (who reports to whom)
   - Unit types and sizes (squads, platoons, companies, battalions)
   - Attached specialists (engineers, snipers, heavy weapons, air assets)
   - Reserve forces and reaction units

6. **Document adversary capabilities.** For each unit type, record:
   - **Firepower:** weapon types, effective ranges, accuracy, rate of fire
   - **Mobility:** vehicle types, road speed, cross-country speed, fuel range
   - **Protection:** armor thickness (for vehicles), body armor (for personnel), fortification quality
   - **Sustainability:** supply lines, logistics footprint, resupply timeline
   - **Special capabilities:** aviation, indirect fire (mortars/artillery), electronic warfare, engineer equipment

7. **Record adversary doctrine and SOP.** Based on training manuals, past operations, or interrogation reports:
   - How does the adversary defend? (linear, defense in depth, mobile defense, etc.)
   - How does the adversary attack? (frontal, indirect approach, infiltration, etc.)
   - How does the adversary react to specific triggers? (air strike, armor advance, encirclement, etc.)
   - What do they prioritize? (protect supply lines, secure key terrain, preserve reserves, etc.)

8. **Assess adversary command effectiveness.** Rate the quality of:
   - Officer/leader training and experience
   - Soldier discipline and cohesion
   - Speed of decision-making and communication
   - Adaptability to unexpected situations

### Step 3 — Evaluate Terrain Effects on Adversary Courses of Action

9. **List plausible adversary courses of action (COAs).** Given the adversary's capabilities and doctrine, what are 2-4 realistic operational options? For each, write:
   - The objective (what outcome does this COA achieve?)
   - The general approach (how will the adversary move and attack?)
   - The sequence of moves (step-by-step timeline)
   - Where and when the adversary is most vulnerable

10. **For each COA, trace the match between adversary capabilities and terrain.** Create a table:

| COA | Mobility corridors used | Obstacles that slow it | Key terrain that controls it | Concealment that protects it | Sustainability (supply, duration) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| <COA name> | <which corridors?> | <which obstacles block or slow?> | <which terrain features decide success?> | <where can they hide?> | <how long can they sustain?> |

11. **Identify adversary "named areas of interest" (NAIs).** These are locations where the adversary *must* appear to execute each COA. Example: "If the adversary intends to attack the northern approach, they must assemble forces in Grid XYZ valley before dawn." NAIs are the places you monitor and the places where you want to disrupt the adversary.

12. **Identify "decision points."** These are moments and locations where the adversary makes a choice that commits them to one COA over another. Example: "If the adversary turns east at the crossroads (Grid ABC), they are executing COA-North. If they turn west, COA-South." Decision points are where you fight — because interdicting the adversary at a decision point forces a new choice and slows their execution.

### Step 4 — Determine Likely Adversary Intentions

13. **Assess the strategic context.** What is the adversary trying to achieve overall? (territorial control, destruction of your force, delaying action, etc.)

14. **Weight each COA by likelihood.** For each COA, estimate a probability (high/medium/low) based on:
   - **Alignment with adversary strategy:** Does this COA help achieve the overall goal?
   - **Terrain fit:** Can the adversary execute this COA given the terrain?
   - **Force adequacy:** Does the adversary have enough troops and equipment?
   - **Doctrinal preference:** Does the adversary's doctrine favor this approach?
   - **Historical pattern:** Has the adversary used this approach before?
   - **Intelligence:** Any specific evidence the adversary is preparing for this?

15. **Identify the most likely COA.** This is the adversary course you should plan to defend against or counter first. It is NOT necessarily the most dangerous — that is a separate judgment.

16. **Identify the most dangerous COA.** This is the adversary course that, if executed successfully, causes the greatest harm to your force or mission. It may be less likely but still must be defended against.

17. **Document critical assumptions.** List what you are assuming about the adversary that, if false, would change your assessment. Example: "We assume the adversary has no air assets; if they do, our defensive position becomes untenable." These assumptions are intelligence collection priorities — you must monitor them.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| AOI/AO boundaries are defined by specific coordinates or recognizable terrain features | Y/N |
| Mobility corridors, obstacles, key terrain, and concealment are mapped or listed | Y/N |
| Relevant weather for the season is documented (temperature, precipitation, visibility, wind) | Y/N |
| Adversary organization includes at least 3 unit types with assigned roles | Y/N |
| Adversary capabilities are recorded by category (firepower, mobility, protection, sustainability) | Y/N |
| Adversary doctrine/SOP is sourced (manual, past operations, interrogation, or assessed) | Y/N |
| At least 2 plausible adversary COAs are described with objective, approach, and sequence | Y/N |
| NAIs are specific locations tied to specific COAs | Y/N |
| Decision points represent actual fork-in-the-road choices | Y/N |
| Each COA is weighted (high/medium/low) with justification | Y/N |
| Critical assumptions about adversary are stated explicitly | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Terrain analysis is generic ("hilly", "wooded") without specific locations or impact on movement. IPB terrain work must be linked to actual military tasks: can tracked vehicles cross here? Can dismounts move unobserved? Can artillery observers see this location?
- Adversary capabilities listed without numbers. "They have mortars" is not actionable. "They have six 82mm mortars with 10km range and 10-round magazines per position" is.
- COAs are indistinguishable from one another. If two COAs differ only in minor details, they are not separate courses — consolidate them.
- NAIs or decision points are vague. "Enemy activity in the valley" is not an NAI. "Enemy assembly in Grid XYZ-1234 between 0200 and 0400 hrs" is.
- Likelihood weighting has no justification. "COA-North is most likely" without explaining why (terrain fit, doctrinal preference, evidence) is a guess masquerading as analysis.
- No mention of critical intelligence gaps. Every assessment rests on assumptions; missing ones means you did not interrogate your own analysis.

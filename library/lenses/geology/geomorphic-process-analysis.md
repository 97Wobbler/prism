# Geomorphic Process Analysis

## Overview
Landscapes are shaped by four linked processes: weathering breaks rock into fragments, erosion removes material from source areas, transport carries it downstream or downslope, and deposition drops it when energy declines. This lens operationalizes the distinction by teaching you to read the physical evidence of each stage in the field — distinct grain sizes, sorting patterns, weathering textures, and landform geometry. Practitioners use it to decode landscape history, locate sediment bottlenecks, and predict erosion or flooding behavior in engineered systems and natural basins.

## Analytical Procedure

### Stage 1 — Observe and Document the Landform

1. **Map the study area at 1:5,000 or finer scale.** Mark slope steepness using a clinometer or GPS elevation data. Identify water flow paths (streams, seepage zones, runoff concentrations).

2. **Photograph each distinct surface.** Take wide shots showing overall form and close-ups (ruler or coin in frame) of grain texture, weathering, and color. Georeferenced photos are best.

3. **Collect a representative sediment sample from each surface or layer.** Mark location, depth, and context (e.g., "2 m above channel on left bank, in silt lens"). Collect at least 500 g.

### Stage 2 — Assess Weathering Signatures

4. **Examine rock and grain surfaces with hand lens (10×).** Look for:
   - **Fresh surfaces:** minimal color change, sharp edges, no oxidation staining.
   - **Moderately weathered:** slight color change (iron staining, patina), some edge rounding, micro-cracks visible.
   - **Intensely weathered:** deep discoloration, spalling or exfoliation, grains friable to finger pressure.

5. **Rate the weathering intensity on the Wentworth weathering scale or simplified 1–5 scale:**
   - 1 = Fresh (no oxidation, sharp edges)
   - 3 = Moderate (some color change, slight rounding)
   - 5 = Complete (grains separable by hand, grus)
   Record the rating for each sample and location.

6. **Note weathering patterns.** Is weathering uniform across the surface or concentrated along joints/fractures? Is oxidation deep (indicating long exposure) or superficial (recent exposure)? Uneven weathering suggests episodic exposure or mixing of different ages.

### Stage 3 — Measure Erosion Signatures

7. **Identify erosion indicators:**
   - **Gullies and rills:** linear scour marks showing direction of runoff and erodibility of surface material.
   - **Exposure depth:** measure vertical distance from undisturbed soil/rock to eroded surface. Mark on photographs.
   - **Mass movement scars:** rotational slides, debris flow tracks, and headscarp geometry indicate failure thresholds.
   - **Undercut banks and overhang:** in streams, measure overhang height to infer shear stress and competence.

8. **Measure scour depth and width.** Use a ruler, tape, or survey rod. Record 3–5 measurements per feature to establish average erosion rate (if timestamps are available: pre/post event, or if dating is possible).

9. **Assess material cohesion.** Push on exposed soil/sediment with a pocket knife or probe:
   - **Granular, no cohesion:** sand, gravel (high erodibility).
   - **Weakly cohesive:** silt with sparse roots (medium erodibility).
   - **Strongly cohesive:** clay, cemented material, dense vegetation (low erodibility).
   Record the type for each eroded surface.

### Stage 4 — Analyze Transport Signatures

10. **Measure grain size distribution in collected samples.** Dry samples at 105 °C. Use a sieve stack (2 mm, 1 mm, 500 µm, 250 µm, 125 µm, 63 µm, pan) or laser grain-size analyzer. Record the percentage in each bin.

11. **Calculate sorting and skewness:**
    - **Well-sorted:** 90% of grains within one Φ unit (e.g., mostly 250–500 µm); indicates transport over distance or selective deposition.
    - **Poorly sorted:** grains span 3+ Φ units; indicates mass failure, debris flow, or proximal colluvium.
    - **Positively skewed:** fine tail dominates; indicates suspension in flowing water.
    - **Negatively skewed:** coarse tail dominates; indicates saltation or rolling.
    Plot cumulative frequency curves; visual shape reveals transport regime.

12. **Examine grain rounding and surface texture with 10–20× magnification (hand lens or dissecting microscope):**
    - **Angular:** recently fractured by weathering or mass movement.
    - **Subrounded:** transported 10–100 m in high-energy flows.
    - **Well-rounded:** transported >100 m in rivers or >1 km on hillslopes; fluvial transport typical.
    - **Frosted/matte surface:** aeolian transport (wind-blown).
    - **Striations, polish:** glacial transport.
    Record the dominant texture for each sample.

13. **Measure clast imbrication in channels or coarse deposits.** Select 10–15 aligned clasts along a cross-section. Measure dip angle of long axis. Mean dip angle indicates flow direction; steep imbrication (>30°) suggests high-energy flow.

### Stage 5 — Identify Deposition Signatures

14. **Locate deposition zones by landform and stratigraphy:**
    - **Channel bars and point bars:** inside bends of rivers; fine to medium sand overlying coarser channel lag.
    - **Flood plains:** overbank silts and clays draping older channel deposits; well-sorted by settling.
    - **Alluvial fans:** coarse, poorly sorted fan-head deposits grading to fine distal facies.
    - **Debris cones:** poorly sorted mix of all sizes; angular clasts; steep slope.

15. **Measure and describe depositional stratigraphy.** Dig or use an auger to expose vertical sections at least 1 m deep. Record:
    - Layer thickness (cm).
    - Grain size (sand, silt, clay) and sorting (visual estimate: poor, moderate, good).
    - Color and oxidation state.
    - Presence of roots, shells, organic matter (age indicators).
    - Boundary characteristics (sharp, graded, scoured).

16. **Estimate deposition rate (if datable layers are present).** Thickness ÷ time interval = mm/year (or cm/decade). Cross-validate with sediment yield estimates from upstream erosion areas.

17. **Examine lateral facies changes.** Traverse perpendicular to the depositional feature (e.g., across a fan, along a flood plain). Describe how grain size and sorting change with distance from the source. Proximal coarse + distal fine indicates progressive energy decline — expected for transport-driven deposition. Uniform grain size across distance indicates selective transport or reworking.

### Stage 6 — Integrate Process Chain

18. **Construct a process narrative.** For each identified surface or layer, assign the dominant process:
    - **Weathering-dominated:** high weathering intensity, minimal sorting, mixed grain sizes, in situ location.
    - **Erosion-dominated:** scour features, cohesion low, no sorting signature, material absent (not deposited nearby).
    - **Transport-dominated:** well-sorted, rounded grains, clear imbrication or orientation, intermediate position in slope profile.
    - **Deposition-dominated:** stratified, size-graded, fines concentrated, landform-filling geometry.

19. **Map process zones.** Shade the study map:
    - Red = weathering and erosion zone (source area).
    - Yellow = transport zone (channel, incised slope).
    - Blue = deposition zone (bars, flood plain, fan toe).
    Overlapping zones indicate complex or recent activity.

20. **Identify process bottlenecks.** Where is sediment being produced faster than it can be transported? (Gullied hillslope with no active channel → weathering > erosion). Where is material accumulating instead of moving on? (Bar growth, fan thickening → transport < deposition). Bottlenecks are the sites of highest landscape change and ecosystem impact.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Landform map includes slope steepness and water pathways | Y/N |
| Weathering intensity rated for ≥3 distinct surfaces | Y/N |
| Erosion depth and width measured in 3+ locations | Y/N |
| Grain size distributions calculated (sieve or laser) for ≥3 samples | Y/N |
| Sorting and rounding qualitatively assigned to each sample | Y/N |
| Depositional stratigraphy exposed and described to ≥1 m depth | Y/N |
| Process narrative explains each surface (weathering/erosion/transport/deposition) | Y/N |
| Process zones mapped and bottlenecks identified on the field map | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Weathering ratings are all 1 or all 5. Homogeneous weathering suggests the sample is either very young (fresh bedrock) or very old (complete breakdown) — both are degenerate cases. Mixed ratings are normal.
- All samples show identical grain size and rounding. Either the study area has uniform transport regime (possible but rare) or sampling was too coarse — resample from different positions in the landscape.
- No deposition zone identified in a drainage system with active erosion. Sediment must go somewhere; missing deposition sites suggest the map is incomplete or the analysis stopped too far downslope.
- Stratigraphy is absent or uniform with depth. If every layer looks identical, the deposit may be recent (single event), reworked (no preservation), or excavated in the wrong location. Recheck field locations.
- Process zones overlap without explanation. Overlap is legitimate if the area is transitional or undergoing recent disturbance, but must be justified with evidence (e.g., "active gully cutting into fan: erosion zone expanding into old deposition zone").
- Imbrication measurements show random directions (no mean dip). Indicates either current scour (no preservation), highly turbulent flow (poor sorting), or measurement error. Repeat on a clearer section.

# ABCDE Approach

## Overview

The ABCDE Approach is a structured patient assessment framework used in emergency and critical care to quickly identify and prioritize life-threatening conditions. It progresses through five sequential categories—Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, and Exposure—ensuring that conditions that kill fastest are managed first. Practitioners use this when a patient presents with multiple complaints and time is limited, or when they need to communicate findings and priorities to a team. The discipline is the *sequencing*: stopping to treat a non-critical problem before assessing for airway obstruction can be fatal.

## Analytical Procedure

### Step 1 — Scene Safety and Initial Assessment

1. **Ensure scene safety.** Before approaching the patient, scan for hazards (fire, toxins, violence, unstable structures). If unsafe, do not enter. Call for backup or evacuation.

2. **Note the patient's general appearance and level of consciousness.** Are they alert, responsive to verbal stimuli, responsive to pain, or unresponsive? This is your alert-to-unresponsive (AVPU) scale, and it determines how aggressively you proceed. Unresponsive patients require immediate airway assessment.

3. **Determine mechanism of injury or chief complaint in 5-10 seconds.** Do not spend time on history yet. Trauma? Medical? This shapes which threats to prioritize.

### Step 2 — Airway (with cervical spine protection if trauma)

4. **Look, listen, feel for airway patency.** Is the patient speaking in full sentences (good sign), or unable to speak (red flag)? Listen for stridor (upper airway obstruction) or gurgling (secretions/blood in airway). Feel for air movement at the nose and mouth.

5. **If airway is patent (open), proceed to Breathing. If compromised, intervene immediately:**
   - Head-tilt/chin-lift (medical patients) or jaw thrust (suspected spinal injury)
   - Remove visible foreign bodies, blood, or vomit
   - Place airway adjunct (oropharyngeal airway) if patient is unresponsive and cannot protect airway
   - Consider bag-valve-mask ventilation or intubation if available and trained

6. **In trauma patients, protect the cervical spine** while securing the airway. Use manual in-line stabilization or a cervical collar if indicated.

### Step 3 — Breathing

7. **Count respiratory rate and observe work of breathing.** Normal is 12–20 breaths per minute at rest in adults. Note if breathing is shallow, labored, or using accessory muscles (intercostal retractions, nasal flaring, use of neck muscles).

8. **Assess for life-threatening breathing problems:**
   - **Tension pneumothorax:** unilateral absent breath sounds + hypotension + JVD + tracheal deviation = decompress immediately
   - **Massive hemothorax:** unilateral absent breath sounds + hypotension = prepare for fluid resuscitation
   - **Flail chest:** multiple rib fractures causing paradoxical breathing (inward motion during inhalation)
   - **Sucking chest wound:** open pneumothorax; seal three sides with tape or dressing

9. **If breathing is present but inadequate (severe hypoxia, CO₂ retention):** provide supplemental oxygen and consider assisted ventilation (bag-valve-mask or mechanical ventilation if trained).

### Step 4 — Circulation

10. **Check for pulse and external bleeding.** Palpate a central pulse (carotid or femoral) for ≤10 seconds. If absent, begin CPR. If present, assess quality (strength) and rate (normal ~60–100 bpm at rest).

11. **Identify and control external hemorrhage:**
    - **Minor bleeding:** direct pressure + elevation
    - **Severe bleeding:** direct pressure + tourniquet above the wound (for extremity bleeding) or packing with gauze and pressure for junctional hemorrhage (groin, axilla, neck)
    - Apply high-flow oxygen (target SpO₂ >94%)

12. **Assess perfusion status:**
    - **Skin:** warm and dry (good) vs. pale, cool, clammy (shock)
    - **Capillary refill:** press fingertip until white, release; normal is <2 seconds (in adults; <3 seconds in children)
    - **Blood pressure:** normal systolic >100 mmHg (adults). <90 mmHg suggests shock.
    - **Mental status:** alert and oriented suggests adequate cerebral perfusion; confusion suggests hypoperfusion

13. **If signs of shock present (hypotension, poor perfusion, tachycardia):** initiate fluid resuscitation (IV access, crystalloid bolus) and prepare for transport to facility with blood products or intervention capability.

### Step 5 — Disability (Neurological Status)

14. **Assess level of consciousness using AVPU or Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS).** AVPU is faster (Alert, Verbal, Pain, Unresponsive); GCS is more precise (scored 3–15, with 8 or below indicating severe impairment and need for airway protection).

15. **Test for focal neurological deficit:**
    - **Motor:** Can patient move all four limbs equally? Weakness on one side suggests stroke or spinal cord injury.
    - **Sensation:** Can patient feel touch on all extremities? Absent sensation in legs suggests spinal cord injury.
    - **Pupil response:** Unequal pupils suggest intracranial pathology (epidural hematoma, brainstem herniation).
    - **Speech:** Slurred speech, inability to speak, or expressive/receptive difficulty suggests stroke or intoxication.

16. **If GCS <8 or rapidly declining:** secure airway, hyperventilate slightly (target pCO₂ 30–35) to reduce intracranial pressure, and arrange immediate neurosurgical evaluation.

### Step 6 — Exposure and Environmental Control

17. **Remove enough clothing to perform a full secondary exam** while maintaining patient dignity and preventing hypothermia. In trauma, this includes inspection of back, buttocks, and perineum (log roll if spinal injury suspected).

18. **Perform a rapid head-to-toe examination:**
    - **Head:** lacerations, skull fractures, Battle sign (mastoid ecchymosis), raccoon eyes (orbital fractures)
    - **Neck:** JVD, tracheal deviation, neck veins
    - **Chest:** rib fractures, flail segments, breath sounds bilaterally, heart sounds
    - **Abdomen:** distension, rigidity, tenderness, bruising, evisceration
    - **Pelvis:** instability, crepitus
    - **Extremities:** deformity, swelling, compartment syndrome signs (pain out of proportion, paresthesia)
    - **Neurological:** repeat motor/sensory/reflexes

19. **Manage environmental exposure.** In hypothermia or cold environment, remove wet clothing but avoid rough movement (paradoxical afterdrop). Cover patient with blankets. In heat exhaustion, remove excess clothing and cool aggressively.

### Step 7 — Reassessment and Ongoing Monitoring

20. **Repeat the ABCDE assessment every 5–15 minutes** depending on acuity and response to treatment. Conditions change; a patient stable at the 5-minute check may deteriorate rapidly. Document all reassessments.

21. **Communicate findings to receiving facility.** Report ABCDE findings, interventions, and patient response to guide handoff and continued care.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Scene safety was assessed before patient contact | Y/N |
| Airway was evaluated and patency confirmed or obstructive cause identified | Y/N |
| Breathing rate and work of breathing were quantified; hypoxia addressed if present | Y/N |
| Pulse was palpated; external bleeding identified and controlled | Y/N |
| Perfusion status (skin, BP, capillary refill) was documented | Y/N |
| Level of consciousness was assessed (AVPU or GCS) and focal neurological deficits ruled out | Y/N |
| Full body exposure exam was performed; environmental control documented | Y/N |
| At least one life threat was identified and intervention initiated | Y/N |
| Reassessment was planned at appropriate interval | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Airway adequacy assumed without visualization. Patients who are talking clearly may still be at risk if upper airway edema is progressing.
- Breathing assessed by visual inspection alone, with no measurement of rate or auscultation for breath sounds. Tension pneumothorax can be silent on one side.
- Circulation step skipped because patient is conscious. Uncontrolled internal bleeding can cause sudden decompensation; don't assume stability.
- Pulse check takes >30 seconds. In cardiac arrest, delays in CPR initiation worsen outcome. Aim for ≤10 seconds; if unsure, start CPR.
- Disability assessment documents only "alert" with no further detail. A patient can be alert but not oriented; this distinction matters for intracranial pathology.
- Patient completely undressed and left exposed to cool environment, leading to iatrogenic hypothermia. Exposure is for examination, not indefinite.
- No reassessment performed. Initial exam is a snapshot; ongoing monitoring catches deterioration.
- Interventions attempted before airway security (e.g., aggressive fluid resuscitation in uncontrolled hemorrhage before tourniquet application). Sequence matters.

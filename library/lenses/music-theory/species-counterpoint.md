# Species Counterpoint (Fux)

## Overview
Species Counterpoint is a pedagogical method for training composers to write independent melodic lines that move in synchrony with harmonic constraint. Rather than composing all voices simultaneously, the method fixes a cantus firmus (plain chant) and adds countervoices one species at a time, each species introducing new rhythmic and harmonic demands. Practitioners use this when learning to balance melodic interest (smooth motion, singability) against harmonic function (avoiding parallel fifths, maintaining consonance). The discipline is constraint satisfaction under increasing complexity.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Species Selection and Setup

1. **Identify the species being evaluated.** Determine which of the five species the countervoice belongs to:
   - Species 1: One note of counterpoint against each note of the cantus firmus (1:1)
   - Species 2: Two notes of counterpoint against each note of the cantus firmus (2:1)
   - Species 3: Four notes of counterpoint against each note of the cantus firmus (4:1)
   - Species 4: Syncopated counterpoint (notes tied across the beat, creating suspension chains)
   - Species 5: Florid counterpoint (a mix of rhythmic values, typically combining species 2, 3, and 4)

2. **Extract the cantus firmus.** Write out the given bass or soprano line (the fixed melodic foundation) and verify it is complete and unaltered.

3. **Extract the countervoice(s).** Write out the voice(s) being added above or below the cantus. If there are multiple countervoices, evaluate each separately but in order (lowest voice first).

### Phase 2 — Melodic Independence Evaluation

4. **Check melodic contour and direction.**
   - Does each voice move primarily by step (seconds) or leap (thirds and larger)?
   - Are large leaps (fourths or larger) followed by motion in the opposite direction?
   - Does the voice have a clear climax or turning point, or does it meander?
   - **Pass:** Leaps are rare, stepwise motion predominates, large leaps are filled in or reversed. **Fail:** Frequent large leaps without compensation, or aimless wandering.

5. **Verify singability.**
   - Is the range of the voice appropriate for a human singer (typically 10–12 semitones, no wider than an octave plus a third)?
   - Does the voice avoid awkward register jumps that would be difficult to perform continuously?
   - Are there long stretches without a rest or cadence point where a performer could breathe?
   - **Pass:** Range is natural, register is consistent, breathing points exist. **Fail:** Extreme range, jarring register shifts, or no breaks for breath.

6. **Assess metric placement and phrasing.**
   - Do strong beats fall on strong scale degrees (1, 3, 5 of the prevailing key) and weak beats on weaker degrees?
   - Is there a sense of harmonic rhythm — do harmonic changes align with the metric structure?
   - Are cadences (half, full, plagal) placed at natural phrase boundaries?
   - **Pass:** Metric and harmonic accents align; phrasing is clear. **Fail:** Weak notes on strong beats; harmonic rhythm is erratic.

### Phase 3 — Harmonic Constraint Satisfaction

7. **Identify all sonorities (vertical intervals) between the countervoice and the cantus (or existing voices).**
   - On each beat, mark the interval(s): unison (1), second (2), third (3), fourth (4), fifth (5), sixth (6), seventh (7), octave (8).
   - For species 2–5 (where multiple notes may sound against one note of the cantus), mark all intervals that occur.

8. **Check consonance rules.**
   - Imperfect consonances (thirds, sixths) may occur freely on any beat.
   - Perfect consonances (unison, fourths, fifths, octaves) may occur only on strong beats (in species 1 and 2: beat 1 and, in duple meter, beat 3; in triple meter: beat 1 only). **Exception:** Fourth is dissonant unless it is a suspension or occurs above the bass.
   - Seconds and sevenths are dissonances and must be prepared (approached by step) and resolved (leave by step in the opposite direction). Mark each dissonance and check that both preparation and resolution are present.
   - **Pass:** All consonances are on strong beats; all dissonances are prepared and resolved. **Fail:** Perfect consonance on a weak beat; dissonance without preparation or resolution.

9. **Check parallel motion rules.**
   - Avoid direct fifths or octaves (two voices moving by the same interval to a perfect consonance). **Exception:** If the upper voice moves by step, direct fifth or octave is permitted.
   - Avoid parallel unisons.
   - Mark any instance of two voices moving in the same direction to a perfect consonance and check for the step exception.
   - **Pass:** No prohibited parallels. **Fail:** Direct fifths or octaves without step motion in the upper voice.

10. **Verify voice leading in passages of motion.**
    - When moving from one chord to the next, do voices move by step or stay on a common tone?
    - Is motion smooth (no voice crosses another; register is maintained)?
    - Are there awkward leaps or abrupt register shifts?
    - **Pass:** Smooth voice leading with stepwise motion or common tones; no crossing. **Fail:** Awkward leaps, voice crossing, or unclear harmonic progression.

### Phase 4 — Species-Specific Requirements

11. **For Species 1 (1:1):**
    - Every note of the countervoice must be a consonance with the corresponding note of the cantus.
    - The first and last notes must be unison, octave, or fifth with the cantus.
    - Check completion: no more, no fewer notes than the cantus.

12. **For Species 2 (2:1):**
    - The first note of each pair is on the beat; the second note is on the off-beat.
    - Only the on-beat notes need consonance with the cantus. Off-beat notes may be passing tones (approached and left by step in the same direction) or neighbor tones (approached and left by step in opposite directions).
    - Check that off-beat notes are truly passing or neighbor tones and not random motion.

13. **For Species 3 (4:1):**
    - The first of the four notes is on the beat. The remaining three are subdivisions of the beat.
    - Only the first note needs strict consonance with the cantus. The inner three may include passing tones, neighbor tones, and (rarely) suspension chains if the species allows.
    - Check rhythm: all four notes should fit the metric grid with consistent subdivisions (no irregular rhythms).

14. **For Species 4 (syncopation/suspension):**
    - Notes are tied across the metric beat, creating cross-rhythms.
    - Each note must be prepared (either consonant on the beat where it is tied from, or preceded by step); it may be dissonant on the strong beat (creating a suspension that must resolve downward by step).
    - Check: each long note either stays as a consonance or is a suspension with a step-down resolution.

15. **For Species 5 (florid/mixed):**
    - The countervoice may use a mixture of rhythmic values (whole, half, quarter notes, or slower subdivisions depending on the tempo).
    - All of the rules from Species 1–4 apply according to which rhythmic value and metrical placement is in use at any given moment.
    - Check systematically: for each note, determine what species constraint it falls under and verify compliance.

### Phase 5 — Synthesis and Reporting

16. **Tally violations.** Count the number of failures in melodic independence (Phase 2) and harmonic constraint (Phase 3 & 4). For each violation, note its measure number and type.

17. **Assess overall execution.** Determine the severity:
    - **Exemplary:** All checks pass; voice is independent, singable, and harmonically sound.
    - **Competent:** 1–2 minor violations (e.g., one awkward leap that is compensated, or a single minor voice-leading imprecision); learner is clearly applying the rules consistently.
    - **Developing:** 3–5 violations, or one major violation (e.g., unresolved dissonance, prohibited parallel); rules are partially internalized but application is inconsistent.
    - **Needs revision:** More than 5 violations, or multiple major violations; fundamental rules are not yet secure.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Species correctly identified and rules for that species stated | Y/N |
| Every note of the countervoice checked for consonance or dissonance | Y/N |
| All dissonances are prepared and resolved | Y/N |
| No prohibited parallel fifths or octaves without step exception | Y/N |
| Melodic contour is singable and has clear phrasing | Y/N |
| Voice range is within 1–1.5 octaves and natural for the voice type | Y/N |
| All species-specific constraints (rhythm, suspension, passing tone) are met | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Perfect consonance (fifth, octave) appears on a weak beat in Species 1 or 2 without justification.
- A dissonance is present but not prepared or not resolved by step.
- Two voices move in parallel to the same perfect consonance without the upper voice moving by step.
- The countervoice range exceeds an octave, or the voice is restricted to fewer than 6–8 different pitches (sign of laziness or misunderstanding of the range of the voice type).
- In Species 5, the rhythm is ambiguous or mixes meters — the evaluator cannot map every note to a beat.
- The countervoice does not move independently of the cantus — instead, it shadows it (similar motion and rhythm throughout).
- Off-beat notes in Species 2–3 are not step-wise motion relative to adjacent notes; they are leaps or static repetitions.

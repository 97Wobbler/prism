# Sonata Form Analysis

## Overview
Sonata form structures a large instrumental movement into three functional sections — Exposition (presents two contrasting themes), Development (fragmentation and tonal exploration), and Recapitulation (restatement with harmonic resolution). Practitioners analyze it to understand how Classical and Romantic composers built coherence across 5–15 minute spans, manage tonal tension, and resolve thematic conflict. The lens is less prescriptive than diagnostic: it helps you read what a composer actually did, not what the textbook says they should have done.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Score Preparation

1. **Obtain the complete score or a reliable recording with score sync.** Digital scores (IMSLP, MuseScore) are preferred; audio-only analysis is harder but possible. Mark measure numbers clearly.

2. **Listen to the entire movement without annotation.** Get a gestalt sense of its length, tempo, and emotional arc. Note where you hear the most dramatic contrast and where you hear return.

3. **Identify the key signature and primary tempo.** Record the tonal center (e.g., D major, G minor) and the time signature. This is your harmonic anchor.

### Phase 2 — Exposition Mapping

4. **Locate the first theme (P — Primary theme).** It begins at m.1 and continues until there is a clear harmonic shift (usually a modulation toward the dominant key or relative major). Mark its boundary. Document:
   - Measure range
   - Tonal center
   - Instrumentation and texture (is it a melody with accompaniment? tutti block? solo?)
   - Character (lyric, energetic, fragmentary?)
   - Melodic contour (does it rise, fall, oscillate?)

5. **Locate the transition (TR).** Between P and the second theme, there is a modulatory passage that shifts the tonal center. It may be short (2–4 measures) or long (8+ measures). The transition often rises in intensity and may fragment P or introduce new figuration. Mark its boundaries and note:
   - Does it use material from P, or is it new?
   - What key(s) does it visit en route to the secondary key?
   - Does intensity rise or fall?

6. **Locate the second theme (S — Secondary theme).** After the transition, a new theme arrives in a stable secondary key (usually V in major keys, III in minor keys; sometimes VI). It is typically lyric or contrasting in character to P. Mark its boundaries and document:
   - Measure range and secondary key
   - Instrumentation and texture
   - Character (cantabile, flowing, lyric, gentle?)
   - Melodic contour and phrase structure

7. **Locate the closing section (C).** After S ends, there is often a closing or concluding passage (sometimes called a codetta in the Exposition). It may restate fragments of P or S, or introduce new material. It confirms the secondary key and signals the end of the Exposition. Mark its boundaries.

8. **Verify the repeat structure.** Sonata form traditionally has a repeat of the Exposition (often marked with repeat signs). Note whether one is present.

9. **Document the Exposition summary:**
   - Total measure count
   - Key scheme (e.g., "D major → A major" for a major-key sonata)
   - Thematic divisions with measure ranges
   - Character contrasts (P vs. S)

### Phase 3 — Development Mapping

10. **Locate the Development section.** It begins immediately after the Exposition repeat (or after the closing section if no repeat). The Development is typically shorter than the Exposition and characterized by **tonal instability** and **thematic fragmentation**. Mark where it begins.

11. **Identify fragmentation and sequence.** The Development usually breaks P and/or S into motives and repeats them in sequence, often rising in pitch or intensity. Scan for:
    - Measure ranges where a motive repeats sequentially
    - Modulations: how many different keys are touched? (e.g., "touches F, Bb, Eb, C minor...")
    - Does the Development rise to a climax? Where?
    - Is there a sudden drop or surprise (a false recapitulation or sudden key shift)?

12. **Mark the retransition (RT).** Near the end of the Development, there is usually a passage that prepares the return to the home key and the first theme. It may use a dominant pedal (the dominant note held or repeated under changing harmonies above), a crescendo, or a harmonic progression that pulls back toward the tonic. Mark its start and the harmonic preparation.

13. **Document the Development summary:**
    - Total measure count
    - Keys visited
    - Primary motives used (from P, S, or new?)
    - Intensity arc (does it build, plateau, fall?)
    - Presence and character of retransition

### Phase 4 — Recapitulation Mapping

14. **Locate the Recapitulation.** It begins when the primary theme (P) returns in the home key. This is almost always unambiguous: the opening melody of the movement returns in the original tonal center. Mark the recapitulation start.

15. **Map P and TR in the Recapitulation.** P should return largely as it was in the Exposition (same melody, same texture, same key). The transition (TR) may be shortened, lengthened, or recomposed, but its function is the same: to modulate toward S. Document any changes from the Exposition version.

16. **Map S in the Recapitulation.** Crucially, S now appears in the home key (or closely related key), not the secondary key. This **harmonic resolution** is the structural point of the Recapitulation: the tonal conflict set up in the Exposition is resolved. Document:
    - Whether S is transposed, reorchestrated, or otherwise altered
    - The key it now inhabits

17. **Map the closing section (C) in the Recapitulation.** Often expanded or embellished compared to the Exposition. It confirms the home key and may introduce a coda (a final extended section). Mark its boundaries.

18. **Document the Recapitulation summary:**
    - Total measure count
    - Key scheme (e.g., "D major → D major")
    - Thematic divisions with measure ranges
    - Character and any significant changes from the Exposition

### Phase 5 — Cross-section Analysis

19. **Compare the Exposition and Recapitulation directly.** Create a side-by-side table:
    - P: same or changed?
    - TR: same, shortened, lengthened, recomposed?
    - S: same melody, new key?
    - C: expanded, contracted, or stable?

20. **Assess the Development's dramatic function.** Ask:
    - Does it destabilize or intensify?
    - Does it introduce new material or just fragment exposition themes?
    - Is there a clear climax?
    - Does the retransition feel inevitable or surprising?

21. **Evaluate overall coherence.** Does the form feel like a unified narrative, or do the sections feel stitched together? Is there a dramatic arc: setup (Exposition) → conflict (Development) → resolution (Recapitulation)?

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| P (primary theme) located and boundaries marked clearly | Y/N |
| Secondary key identified correctly (V in major, III in minor, or alternative justified) | Y/N |
| S (secondary theme) located in exposition secondary key | Y/N |
| Development shows tonal instability and/or thematic fragmentation | Y/N |
| Retransition identified and harmonic preparation noted | Y/N |
| Recapitulation begins unambiguously with P in home key | Y/N |
| S in Recapitulation confirmed in home key (or closely related) | Y/N |
| Exposition and Recapitulation compared directly | Y/N |
| All measure counts recorded | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The "secondary key" in the Exposition is not a stable key area but just a passing modulation. Sonata form demands a stable secondary tonal area.
- The Development is absent or entirely new (not derived from exposition material). Most classical developments are tightly crafted from exposition themes.
- The Recapitulation does not bring P back in the home key. This is the defining harmonic event of sonata form; if it doesn't happen, the form is not sonata.
- S appears in a different key in the Recapitulation than in the Exposition *but* that new key is not the home key or closely related. This breaks the harmonic logic.
- The measure counts are approximate or inconsistent; the Exposition is recorded as "roughly m.1–80" without section boundaries. Precision matters for teaching and analysis.
- No climax or tonal instability identified in the Development. Some developments are understated, but the section should have *some* harmonic or dynamic tension.

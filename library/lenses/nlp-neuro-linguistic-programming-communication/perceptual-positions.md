# Perceptual Positions

## Overview
Perceptual Positions is a systematic method for examining a conflict, decision, or interaction from three distinct psychological standpoints: first person (self), second person (the other), and third person (neutral observer). Rather than remaining locked in one perspective, the analyst shifts deliberately between positions to surface hidden assumptions, unspoken needs, and structural misalignments that a single viewpoint cannot see. Practitioners reach for this when communication feels stuck, when both parties feel unheard, or when a decision generates persistent resistance from people who seem reasonable.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish the Situation

1. **Name the interaction or conflict in one sentence.** What two or more parties are involved? What are they disagreeing about or failing to coordinate on? Write it neutrally (no blame).
   - Example: "The product team wants to ship the feature by Friday; the engineering team believes it needs two more weeks for stability testing."

2. **Identify the three positions you will occupy:**
   - **First Position (Self):** Your own perspective, needs, emotions, constraints, and goals in this situation.
   - **Second Position (Other):** The perspective of the other party (or each other party, if more than two). Their needs, emotions, constraints, and goals as they would articulate them.
   - **Third Position (Observer):** A neutral, external viewpoint that cares about the relationship, the system, and the long-term outcome. No allegiance to either side.

3. **Gather raw material for each position.** Collect statements, complaints, body language observations, or inferred motivations. These will anchor your shift into each position. Do not interpret yet — just gather.

### Phase 2 — Enter First Position

4. **Physically or mentally occupy First Position.** If working alone, actually sit in a designated chair or spot, or close your eyes and inhabit the perspective. If working with others, have each person sit or stand as "Self."

5. **Ask and answer as that person:**
   - What do I want in this situation?
   - What am I afraid of? What would happen if I don't get what I want?
   - What am I not saying because I assume the other person won't understand or care?
   - What assumptions am I making about the other person's intentions?
   - What do I need from the other person to move forward?
   - Write or speak these answers in first person, present tense ("I want," "I'm afraid," not "they want me to").

6. **Mark what you discover:** This is what the first-position person *actually* cares about — often not what they've been arguing for on the surface.

### Phase 3 — Enter Second Position

7. **Shift physically or mentally to Second Position.** Move to a different chair, close your eyes and re-inhabit, or have the other person (if present) speak.

8. **Ask and answer from the other's viewpoint:**
   - What does this person actually want? (Not what you think they should want — what they would say they want.)
   - What are they afraid of?
   - What do they think about you? What do they believe your intentions are?
   - What are they not saying?
   - What do they need from you to move forward?
   - Stay in second-person perspective throughout: "I want," "I'm afraid," not "they want."

9. **Do not defend against what you hear.** The goal is to model the other's world accurately, not to judge it. If you find yourself mentally arguing, pause and re-center in second position.

10. **Mark what you discover:** Often the other person's real concern is orthogonal to what you were defending against.

### Phase 4 — Enter Third Position

11. **Shift to Third Position.** This is the observer — calm, interested in both parties' wellbeing and the system's health. No investment in either side "winning."

12. **Ask and answer as the system:**
    - What do both of these people actually need that they aren't getting?
    - What is each person protecting (identity, safety, autonomy, fairness)?
    - Where are they talking past each other? (First says X, Second hears Y.)
    - If both got exactly what they said they want, would the relationship or system actually be healthy?
    - What would a solution look like that honors both people's underlying needs?
    - What is each person blind to?
    - Write in third-person: "They both fear," "The conflict is rooted in," "Neither has articulated."

13. **Document the structural insight:** Third position often reveals that the conflict isn't about the stated issue at all.

### Phase 5 — Reconcile and Decide

14. **Return to First Position with Third-Position insight.** Now that you understand the other person's world and the structural issue, what do you want to do? Has your goal changed? Your strategy?

15. **Identify commonalities across all three positions.**
    - What does each party actually care about?
    - Where do their underlying needs overlap (e.g., both want stability, both want to be heard, both want the product to succeed)?

16. **Co-create a path forward.** If working with multiple people, each returns to their position and, armed with the insights from the other positions, proposes what they'd be willing to do. The Third Position mediates.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Situation named neutrally in one sentence | Y/N |
| First Position answers collected without defense or rebuttal | Y/N |
| Second Position answers reflect the other's world, not your interpretation of it | Y/N |
| At least one assumption or unspoken need surfaced in First or Second Position | Y/N |
| Third Position reveals a structural mismatch or shared blind spot | Y/N |
| Commonalities between positions identified | Y/N |
| Proposed path forward acknowledges needs from all three positions | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- First Position and Second Position describe the same disagreement using different words. If they are just trading arguments, the positions haven't surfaced anything new.
- Third Position is ignored or treated as "the right answer." If one side is clearly correct and the other is simply wrong, you're not in a true conflict — you're in a judgment call. Third Position works on conflicts where both sides are defending something real.
- The practitioner stays in First Position and intellectually "predicts" what Second Position would say, rather than actually shifting perspective. The insight comes from genuine discomfort when inhabiting the other's stance.
- No new information emerges in any position. Either the situation is simpler than it appeared, or the positions weren't entered deeply enough.
- Proposed solution privileges one position's goal over another's. A third-position solution honors the underlying needs of all parties, not the stated demands.

# Rapport Building (Pacing & Leading)

## Overview

Rapport Building is a two-phase conversational technique: first establish synchrony with the other person's current state, worldview, or communication style (pacing), then gradually introduce new ideas, reframes, or directions (leading). The discipline is the *sequencing* — rushing to the lead before pacing is complete triggers defensiveness and dismissal. Practitioners use this when they need the other person to remain open to influence, especially under conditions of resistance, skepticism, or high emotional activation.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Pacing (Establish Synchrony)

1. **Identify the other person's current state across four dimensions:**
   - **Sensory preference** — Are they thinking in visual (images, metaphors), auditory (sound, rhythm, dialogue), or kinesthetic (feeling, movement, touch) terms? Listen for predicates: "I see," "that sounds," "I feel."
   - **Emotional state** — What emotion dominates right now? (Frustration, excitement, caution, grief, etc.) Not what they *should* feel, but what is present.
   - **Model of the world** — What beliefs, constraints, or values are they operating from? What do they assume is true about the situation?
   - **Pace of speech and thought** — Are they fast or slow? Logical or associative? Jumping between ideas or drilling deep?

2. **Match the dimension you identified.** Do not try to pace all four at once; start with the strongest signal.
   - If visual: Use visual language ("I see what you mean," "the picture is..."), describe scenarios, reference appearance or spatial layout.
   - If auditory: Use rhythm, tone matching, dialogue-based language ("I hear you," "that resonates").
   - If kinesthetic: Use touch-related language ("I feel you," "let's move toward..."), ground ideas in sensation or action.
   - If emotional: Use language that acknowledges the emotion without trying to fix it ("That's frustrating" not "Don't be frustrated").
   - If slow thinker: Slow your own delivery, pause, give space. If fast: Match their tempo.

3. **State back what you've heard in their own language.** This is not parroting; it is translating their concern into their sensory or emotional framework. Example: If they say "I'm worried about the timeline," pace by saying "You're concerned about whether we can move quickly enough" (kinesthetic/action frame) rather than "You think the deadline is unrealistic" (analytical frame).

4. **Continue pacing until you see three markers of synchrony:**
   - Their body language or tone noticeably relaxes
   - They agree with or expand on your restatement ("Exactly" or "and also...")
   - Their language begins to shift subtly toward yours (not fully, but enough to show movement)

5. **Verify pacing is sufficient before moving to leading.** If markers are weak, repeat steps 2–4 with a different dimension.

### Phase 2 — Leading (Introduce Change)

6. **Introduce a small shift.** Do not announce a new topic or ask them to abandon their frame. Instead, add a single idea, reframe, or question that extends their current state slightly. Examples:
   - Their state: "This deadline feels impossible." | Lead: "...and we've solved tighter timelines before by..."
   - Their state: "I don't see how this helps." | Lead: "...I'm curious what would make it visible to you..."
   - Their state: "I feel stuck." | Lead: "...that stuck feeling often comes just before movement..."

7. **Watch for acceptance.** If they nod, build on the shift. If they pull back (defensiveness, tone change, "but..."), return immediately to pacing. Do not proceed until pacing is re-established.

8. **Layer leads incrementally.** Each lead should be a step, not a leap. If the destination is a major reframe or proposal, break it into 4–6 micro-leads rather than trying to move them in one jump.

9. **End with explicit agreement on next action.** Once they are tracking your lead, propose the behavior or belief change you were working toward. If resistance resurfaces, pace again before attempting the final lead.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| All four dimensions of the other person's state were assessed (sensory, emotional, worldview, pace) | Y/N |
| At least one dimension was actively paced (language, tone, or content matched) | Y/N |
| Synchrony markers (relaxation, agreement, language shift) were observed before leading began | Y/N |
| At least one lead was introduced without returning to topic-switching | Y/N |
| If resistance occurred during leading, pacing was resumed | Y/N |
| The exchange ended with explicit agreement on a next step or reframe | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Pacing skipped or rushed. Leads introduced before the other person shows signs of synchrony almost always trigger pushback.
- "Pacing" amounts to only agreeing with everything they say, without actually matching their sensory or emotional framework. Agreement ≠ rapport.
- Leads are too large. Moving from "I'm worried about cost" directly to "we should triple the budget" is a leap, not a lead. Resistance means return to pacing, not pushing harder.
- The practitioner's own sensory preference dominates. ("They're visual, so I should use visual language" then never checks whether the other person actually responds to visuals.) Pacing is about *them*, not about best practice.
- No return to pacing when the lead is rejected. Pushing on after "but..." without re-establishing synchrony guarantees the conversation closes.
- The other person's emotional state was never named or addressed. "You seem frustrated" (pacing emotion) is more powerful than "that's just how the process works" (ignoring emotion).

# Milton Model

## Overview

The Milton Model is a collection of linguistic and hypnotic language patterns that create deliberate ambiguity, allowing listeners to project their own meanings onto the speaker's words while bypassing conscious critical filters. Rather than making explicit, testable claims, Milton patterns use vagueness, presupposition, and multiple-meanings to deliver suggestions that operate on an unconscious level. Practitioners use this lens to audit persuasive communication—both to craft it strategically and to recognize when it is being used on them—in contexts ranging from therapy and negotiation to advertising and political rhetoric.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Identify the Communication Sample

1. **Extract the target utterance(s).** Select a paragraph, sentence, or exchange you wish to analyze. Copy it verbatim. If analyzing a transcript or recording, transcribe the relevant section word-for-word, including pauses and intonation markers if available.

2. **Note the context.** Briefly document: who is speaking, to whom, what is the stated goal (e.g., "convince the client to sign"), and what is the apparent subject matter. This context will help you distinguish Milton patterns from ordinary speech.

### Phase 2 — Apply Milton Pattern Categories

For each pattern below, scan the utterance for matches. If a match is found, mark it and describe exactly where and how it appears. Do not force matches — if a pattern is absent, mark it as absent.

3. **Nominalization.** Look for abstract nouns formed from verbs or adjectives that hide agency and detail.
   - Examples: "growth" (from "grow"), "relationships" (from "relate"), "learning" (from "learn")
   - Find instances where the pattern obscures *how* something happens or *who* does it
   - Mark as: Present / Absent

4. **Deletion.** Identify places where essential information is removed, forcing the listener to fill in the blank.
   - Examples: "You can change" (change what? how?), "They understood" (understood what?)
   - Mark the deleted element(s)
   - Mark as: Present / Absent

5. **Unspecified referent.** Find pronouns or general nouns that do not clearly refer to a specific person or thing.
   - Examples: "It helps," "They know," "This can lead to..."
   - The listener must project their own meaning
   - Mark as: Present / Absent

6. **Presupposition.** Detect claims that are embedded as background assumptions, not directly asserted.
   - Examples: "As you relax..." (presupposes you are or will relax), "Before you decide..." (presupposes a decision is coming)
   - Mark the presupposed claim(s)
   - Mark as: Present / Absent

7. **Pacing and leading.** Identify statements that affirm something obvious or true about the listener's current experience (pacing), followed by a statement intended to guide them toward a desired state (leading).
   - Pacing: "You are sitting in this room" (obviously true)
   - Leading: "...and you can feel yourself becoming more resourceful"
   - Mark pairs of pacing + leading
   - Mark as: Present / Absent

8. **Analogical language.** Look for stories, metaphors, or extended comparisons that allow listeners to map the narrative onto their own situation without explicit instruction.
   - Example: A story about "someone I knew who overcame a similar challenge" without naming the listener's challenge
   - Mark as: Present / Absent

9. **Double bind.** Find choices or framings that present two or more alternatives, all of which lead to the speaker's desired outcome.
   - Example: "Would you prefer to start this change today or tomorrow?" (both options accept the premise of change)
   - Mark as: Present / Absent

10. **Ambiguous verb referents.** Identify sentences where it is unclear which noun a verb applies to, allowing multiple interpretations.
    - Example: "I know some people who felt better after they stopped worrying about it." (Who is "it"? What does "better" mean?)
    - Mark as: Present / Absent

### Phase 3 — Assess Impact and Intent

11. **Count total patterns present.** Tally the number of distinct patterns identified in the utterance. A single sentence may contain 0–6 patterns; longer passages may contain more.

12. **Map the direction of influence.** For each pattern, write one sentence describing what meaning or behavior the pattern appears designed to encourage the listener toward. Do not assume malice — note intent objectively.

13. **Check for internal coherence.** Do the patterns reinforce the same outcome, or do they work at cross-purposes? If they reinforce, the communication is tightly designed. If they conflict, the speaker may be uncertain or ambivalent.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Utterance extracted verbatim with minimal context | Y/N |
| All 10 pattern categories were checked (none skipped) | Y/N |
| Each pattern match includes a quoted example | Y/N |
| Presuppositions are explicitly stated, not inferred | Y/N |
| Impact assessment includes one sentence per pattern found | Y/N |
| Patterns are marked as Present or Absent (not "maybe") | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- No Milton patterns are found in a passage explicitly designed to persuade. Either the passage relies on direct argumentation (not Milton), or the analysis was too strict.
- All 10 patterns are marked Present in a single sentence. While possible, this is rare and suggests over-interpretation — genuine Milton sentences typically contain 2–4 patterns.
- Presuppositions are stated as inferences ("the speaker probably assumes...") rather than as logical entailments of the utterance.
- Pacing + leading is claimed but no pacing statement exists — the passage jumps directly to the desired state. This breaks the Milton pattern.
- Impact directions are vague ("makes the listener feel something") instead of concrete ("encourages acceptance of the premise that change is inevitable").
- Analogies are marked as present when only a generic illustration is given, not a true analogy that maps situation → listener.

# Reframing

## Overview

Reframing is not denial or positive spin. It is the rigorous identification of an alternative interpretation of the same fact or situation such that the new interpretation produces a different emotional response and opens new courses of action. Practitioners use it when a problem statement contains only one construal ("I'm a failure because X happened") and that construal is blocking progress. The discipline is systematic — you do not invent reframes by wishing; you interrogate the components of the original statement until you find a genuine alternative reading that the facts actually support.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Extract the Frame

1. **Identify the problem statement.** Write the exact words the person or team used to describe the situation. Example: "The product launch failed because we didn't have enough time."

2. **Parse the statement into four components:**
   - **Event** — what actually happened (observable facts, actions, outcomes)
   - **Interpretation** — the meaning attached to the event
   - **Feeling** — the emotional response to the interpretation
   - **Consequence** — what the person does or stops doing because of the feeling

   Example:
   - Event: "We released with 40% of planned features."
   - Interpretation: "We failed; lack of time meant we weren't good enough."
   - Feeling: Shame, self-doubt.
   - Consequence: Team avoids similar projects; no post-mortem; assumptions about timeline go unexamined.

3. **Mark the frame boundary.** The frame is the link between Event and Interpretation. Everything to the right of that arrow is a *choice*, not a fact. The Event is fixed; the interpretation is malleable.

### Phase 2 — Stress-Test the Current Interpretation

4. **Ask: "What else could this event mean?"** Generate at least 4 alternative interpretations of the same event, without judgment. Do not edit for politeness or optimism — just collect readings:
   - "We prioritized ruthlessly and shipped the core product on time."
   - "The constraint taught us what our users actually need vs. what we assumed they want."
   - "We discovered that our estimation process is off by 60%, and now we know it."
   - "We shipped faster than a competitor with unlimited time would have; speed is an asset we didn't know we had."

5. **Check each alternative against the Event.** Does this reading fit the observable facts? (Yes = valid; No = discard.) Do not accept "it's just optimism" as a reason to discard. If the facts support it, it's a genuine reading.

6. **For each valid alternative, trace its emotional and behavioral consequences:**
   - Alt 1: → Relief (we did what mattered) → Team examines what was actually essential.
   - Alt 2: → Curiosity → Post-mortem becomes data collection, not blame analysis.
   - Alt 3: → Pragmatism → Next project uses actual data to calibrate; confidence grows.
   - Alt 4: → Pride in constraint-solving → Team willing to attempt hard problems again.

### Phase 3 — Construct the Reframe

7. **Select the alternative that is:**
   - **Grounded in the same facts** (not denial)
   - **Emotionally accessible** (not a flat cliché; it must feel *true*)
   - **Generative** (it opens action, not closes it)

   Do not select based on positivity alone. A reframe that says "actually everything is fine" is worthless. A reframe that says "we have new information we didn't have before" is worth pursuing.

8. **State the reframe as a single sentence.** It should update the Interpretation without changing the Event. Example: "We shipped a tested core product faster than planned, and we now have data on what users will actually pay for."

9. **Verify the reframe by asking:** "If I believed this instead, what would I do differently?" If the answer is "nothing" or "the same thing," the reframe is cosmetic. Discard and select a different alternative.

### Phase 4 — Validate in Context

10. **Check for blindspots.** The original interpretation likely protected something (a belief, an identity, a fear). Ask:
    - What was the original frame protecting me from? (e.g., "If I failed because of lack of time, it's not my fault.")
    - Does the new frame ask me to own something I wasn't owning before?
    - Can I live with that ownership?

    If the answer to the last question is "no," return to Phase 2 and generate more alternatives. A reframe that demands sacrifice you're not ready for will not stick.

11. **State the new frame in the four-component format:**
    - Event: [same as original]
    - Interpretation: [reframed]
    - Feeling: [what the reframe produces]
    - Consequence: [what becomes possible]

12. **Test for durability.** Present the reframe to someone who wasn't invested in the original frame. Do they find it credible? If not, it may be a reframe only you can believe in — which is fine for personal change but weak for team contexts.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Original frame broken into Event, Interpretation, Feeling, Consequence | Y/N |
| At least 4 alternative interpretations generated | Y/N |
| Each alternative is checked against the actual Event (not just opinion) | Y/N |
| At least one alternative is marked as emotionally accessible and generative | Y/N |
| Reframe is stated as a single sentence that leaves Event unchanged | Y/N |
| Reframe passes the "what would I do differently" test | Y/N |
| Blindspot check completed (what was the original frame protecting?) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- All alternatives are variations on "think positive." Reframing is not motivation; it's honest re-interpretation.
- The reframe requires denying part of the Event. ("It didn't actually happen / wasn't as bad as it seemed.") That's gaslighting, not reframing.
- The original frame is not acknowledged as a valid protective mechanism. If the frame kept someone safe, dismissing it triggers defensiveness, not adoption of the new frame.
- The reframe is stated in vague language ("look on the bright side," "it was a learning experience"). Specificity is the test of whether the reframe is real.
- No one outside the reframing process finds the new interpretation credible. In that case, the reframe may be self-deceptive rather than genuinely alternative.
- The person agrees to the reframe but behavior doesn't change. Agreement is not adoption. The test is action, not belief-statement.

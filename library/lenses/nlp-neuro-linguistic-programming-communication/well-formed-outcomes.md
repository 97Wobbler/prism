# Well-Formed Outcomes

## Overview

A well-formed outcome is a goal statement that has been pressure-tested against five conditions: it is stated in positive terms (what you want, not what you don't), it is within the person's or team's sphere of control, it includes concrete sensory evidence of achievement, it preserves the benefits of the current state, and it fits into the broader context without creating unintended side effects. Practitioners use this when a stated goal sounds reasonable but cannot be acted on — the five conditions force specificity and reveal whether the goal is actually achievable or merely a wish.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Extract and State the Goal

1. **Write the goal exactly as stated.** Capture the original language verbatim. Do not paraphrase or interpret.

2. **Identify what the goal-setter wants to move away from.** Often the goal is framed negatively ("I don't want to be anxious," "Stop being late," "Eliminate technical debt"). Write this explicitly. The goal stated in negative terms is a red flag — it names the problem, not the outcome.

### Phase 2 — Apply the Five Conditions

3. **Condition 1: Stated in Positive Terms.** 
   - Rewrite the goal as what you *do want* rather than what you *don't want*.
   - Bad: "I don't want to procrastinate anymore."
   - Good: "I complete tasks within the planned timeline and deliver on schedule."
   - **Check:** Can you visualize achieving this without first visualizing the negative state? If you have to think about procrastination to understand the goal, it is not yet positive. Revise until the statement stands alone.

4. **Condition 2: Within Control (Self-Generated).**
   - Does achieving this goal depend on other people's choices, external events, or factors the goal-setter cannot directly influence?
   - Bad: "My manager appreciates my work" (depends on manager's judgment and standards).
   - Good: "I deliver clear, documented code reviews and solicit feedback on my contributions weekly" (fully within control).
   - **Check:** If the goal relies on someone else's permission, decision, or emotion, it is not well-formed. Reframe it as behavior the person can initiate and sustain independently. For team goals, all members must be able to influence the outcome through their own actions.

5. **Condition 3: Sensory Evidence of Achievement.**
   - Define what you will *see, hear, and feel* when the goal is accomplished. Be specific — not "feel good" but "feel calm when entering the meeting, speak at a steady pace, receive confirmation that the proposal was clear."
   - Bad: "Be more confident." (Confidence is internal; how do others see it?)
   - Good: "Speak without filler words, make eye contact for 70% of the presentation, answer questions directly without qualifying."
   - **Check:** An observer unfamiliar with the goal should be able to recognize when it has been achieved by watching or listening. If the evidence is vague or internal-only, make it observable and measurable.

6. **Condition 4: Preserves Positive Aspects of the Current State.**
   - Identify what the current unwanted behavior or state is giving the person (secondary gain). Procrastination might provide relief from perfectionism. Conflict avoidance might preserve relationships. Overwork might provide identity.
   - **Check:** What would the person *lose* by achieving this goal? List the benefits, however hidden, that the current state provides. The new outcome must preserve these benefits or the person will unconsciously sabotage progress toward the goal.
   - Example: "I want to be more direct in disagreeing with my team" — but disagreement avoidance keeps you liked. The well-formed outcome becomes: "I express disagreement respectfully, starting with what I agree with, and follow up privately to maintain relationships." This preserves the relational benefit.

7. **Condition 5: Ecological Fit (No Unintended Side Effects).**
   - Trace the consequences of achieving this goal across all domains: work, relationships, health, finances, identity.
   - Bad: "Work 70 hours a week to maximize output" — side effect: family relationships, health, burnout.
   - Good: "Increase deliverables by 15% through better prioritization during standard work hours" — higher output without trade-offs elsewhere.
   - **Check:** Ask: "If I achieve this goal perfectly, what else changes?" For each domain, identify one second-order consequence. If any are harmful, revise the goal or its scope to reduce unintended damage.

### Phase 3 — Finalize and Test

8. **Write the final well-formed outcome.** Incorporate all revisions from the five conditions into a single goal statement.

9. **Test it backwards.** Imagine the goal achieved. Now work backward: what is the immediate previous step? Then the step before that? If you cannot trace a causal chain back to where you are now, the goal lacks a path. Revise or accept that the path must be built.

10. **Assign a timeline and checkpoint.** Well-formed outcomes are incomplete without a date and intermediate measures. When will this be done? By what date should half the goal be visible?

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Original goal stated in negative terms is identified | Y/N |
| Goal rewritten in positive terms (what you want, not what you don't) | Y/N |
| Goal depends entirely on person's/team's direct actions | Y/N |
| Sensory evidence includes observable, measurable indicators | Y/N |
| Secondary gains of current state are named and preserved in new goal | Y/N |
| At least one second-order consequence across domains is traced | Y/N |
| Final goal has an explicit timeline or checkpoint | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Goal still uses negatives ("not fail," "avoid mistakes," "stop being") even after Condition 1. Rewrite required.
- Goal depends on someone else's approval, mood, or decision. It is not actionable as written.
- Sensory evidence is abstract ("feel good," "be better," "improve"). Observers cannot verify it.
- Secondary gains were not identified. The current unwanted state is meeting a need; ignoring this guarantees failure.
- Ecological fit was not checked. Achieving the goal will ripple into other domains and create backlash.
- No timeline exists. Without a date, the goal is aspirational, not a commitment.
- The backward chain from goal to current state breaks or requires external miracles. The path is illusory.

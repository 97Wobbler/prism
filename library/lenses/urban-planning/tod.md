# Transit-Oriented Development (Calthorpe)

## Overview
Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) is a planning framework that concentrates mixed-use, pedestrian-friendly development within a quarter-mile (walkable distance) of high-capacity transit nodes. Rather than dispersing development across a region and relying on cars, TOD clusters housing, retail, and employment around transit to reduce car dependency, support transit ridership, and create economically vital neighborhoods. Practitioners use this lens to assess whether a specific site or development proposal genuinely supports transit use or merely borrows the "transit-oriented" label while remaining car-dependent. The discipline is measuring the four load-bearing dimensions: density, mix, walkability, and transit access.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Establish the Transit Node

1. **Identify the primary transit stop or station.** Note its mode (rail, BRT, bus), current headway (minutes between vehicles), and projected future headway if known. Record the address and GPS coordinates.

2. **Define the quarter-mile radius (¼ mi ≈ 5-minute walk).** Draw a 1,320-foot radius circle around the transit stop on a map. This is your study area. Note: "¼ mi" assumes average walking speed of 3 mph; topography and pedestrian barriers (freeways, rivers, dead-end streets) may make some parts of the circle inaccessible even if they're within the radius.

3. **Check for actual pedestrian access, not just distance.** Identify barriers (highways, railroads, waterways, walls) that block direct walking routes. Trace the actual walking path from the site boundary to the transit entrance. If the walking path is >¼ mi because of a detour, flag it.

### Phase 2 — Measure Density

4. **Count residential units within the quarter-mile.** Use municipal assessor data, GIS parcel records, or on-site survey. Note: units under construction are counted; vacant land is not.

5. **Calculate gross residential density (units per acre).** Divide total units by the quarter-mile area (approximately 160 acres) or the actual developable acreage within the pedestrian-accessible radius. Record both gross and net (excluding parks, streets, non-developable land) if the data permits.

6. **Establish the density threshold.** Calthorpe's original TOD standard is ≥8 units/acre minimum for the quarter-mile (gross). Some markets accept ≥5 units/acre for lower-demand regions. Record which standard you are using and why.

7. **Cross-check with employment density.** Count jobs (office, retail, service, institutional) within the quarter-mile using business registries or employment surveys. Target: ≥5 jobs per acre. This ensures the "transit" isn't only a commute line but also a local destination.

### Phase 3 — Assess Mix (Land-Use Diversity)

8. **Inventory land uses within the quarter-mile.** Categorize as: residential, retail, office, institutional (schools, libraries, government), civic/recreation, industrial, surface parking, underused/vacant. Note the percent of each.

9. **Check for "vertical mix" — mixed-use buildings.** Count buildings with ≥2 uses (e.g., ground-floor retail + upper-floor housing). Record their proportion of the total. Pure single-use development (all residential towers, all office parks) fails this criterion even if multiple uses exist elsewhere in the quarter-mile.

10. **Verify essential services are present or planned.** Within the quarter-mile (or within a 10-minute walk from the residences), check for:
    - Grocery store or farmer's market
    - Primary care clinic or pharmacy
    - Public library or community center
    - Public school (elementary or secondary, depending on age mix)
    - Parks or open space

    If any are missing and no plans exist to add them, flag as a shortfall.

11. **Assess ground-floor activation.** Walk or review photos of the ground floor along the pedestrian spine (main street or corridor leading to transit). Count active uses (retail, restaurants, services with windows and foot traffic generators) vs. passive uses (parking, blank walls, loading docks, closed storefronts). Target: ≥60% of ground-floor street frontage is active use.

### Phase 4 — Measure Walkability

12. **Rate street connectivity (block size).** Measure the average block perimeter in the quarter-mile. Calthorpe standard: average block ≤300 feet (short blocks encourage foot traffic). Longer blocks (>400 feet) force walking detours and reduce walkability. Use GIS or satellite imagery to measure.

13. **Inventory pedestrian infrastructure.** For the main walking routes from the quarter-mile boundary to the transit stop:
    - Sidewalk width (target ≥8 feet)
    - Sidewalk continuity (gaps, missing segments = major deduction)
    - Curb cuts and crossing signals at intersections
    - Lighting (overhead, not just street lights)
    - Shade trees or canopies (target ≥40% of walking route under shade)
    - Crossing times that accommodate seniors and wheelchair users (signal timing, refuge islands)

    Score as: complete (all present), partial (most present with minor gaps), poor (fragments only).

14. **Check for major barriers to pedestrian movement.** Freeways, at-grade railroad tracks, walls, large dead-end cul-de-sacs, or multi-lane roads with few crossings. Document their width and frequency along the walking routes. Each barrier reduces walkability score proportionally.

15. **Assess hill slope if in hilly terrain.** Slopes >5% are noticeable; >8% deter many pedestrians. If the site or major walking routes exceed 8% sustained grade, note and adjust walkability scoring downward.

### Phase 5 — Evaluate Transit Access and Frequency

16. **Document transit service baseline.** Record:
    - Current weekday peak headway (minutes between vehicles)
    - Current weekday off-peak headway
    - Weekend headway (if applicable)
    - Service hours (e.g., 5am–midnight vs. 24-hour)
    - Reliability (on-time %; from agency data if available)

17. **Apply the frequency threshold.** Calthorpe and transit-oriented development best practices require:
    - Peak hour headway ≤15 minutes (allows spontaneous ridership without schedule-checking)
    - Off-peak headway ≤20–30 minutes (varies by region and mode)

    If current headway exceeds these, check for planned improvements and their timeline.

18. **Evaluate feeder connectivity.** Does the primary transit stop connect to other transit lines (bus-to-rail, rail-to-rail)? Are wait times at connections reasonable (<10 min typical wait)? A transit stop that is an endpoint or has poor connections reduces TOD viability despite proximity.

19. **Check fare integration.** Can a rider use one payment method across the primary transit + feeder system? Separate payment systems deter transfers and reduce the effective reach of TOD.

### Phase 6 — Synthesis and Viability

20. **Score each dimension on a 1–4 scale:**
    - **Density:** 1 = <3 units/acre; 2 = 3–5 units/acre; 3 = 5–8 units/acre; 4 = >8 units/acre (or local equivalent threshold).
    - **Mix:** 1 = single-use only; 2 = two uses, mostly segregated; 3 = mixed-use with some buildings housing ≥2 uses; 4 = vertical mix throughout, essential services present, >60% active ground floor.
    - **Walkability:** 1 = auto-dependent (large blocks, poor pedestrian infrastructure); 2 = car-oriented with some walkability; 3 = mixed (good in core, poor at edges); 4 = pedestrian-friendly throughout (small blocks, complete sidewalks, shade, safe crossings).
    - **Transit:** 1 = >30 min headway or unreliable; 2 = 20–30 min headway; 3 = 10–20 min headway; 4 = ≤15 min headway, frequent feeder connections, fare integration.

21. **Compute the TOD Viability Index:** (Density + Mix + Walkability + Transit) ÷ 4. Round to nearest 0.5.
    - 3.5–4.0 = Strong TOD (investment-ready, supports transit ridership)
    - 2.5–3.4 = Moderate TOD (viable with targeted improvements)
    - 1.5–2.4 = Weak TOD (transit-adjacent, car-dependent despite proximity)
    - <1.5 = Not TOD (label misapplied)

22. **Identify improvement priorities.** Which dimension scored lowest? That is the highest-leverage investment. For example, if Walkability = 1 but Transit = 4, installing complete pedestrian infrastructure will unlock the full value of the transit investment. If Density = 1 and Mix = 1 but Transit = 4, upzoning and incentivizing mixed-use development is the bottleneck.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Transit node identified with headway and mode documented | Y/N |
| Quarter-mile boundary established; pedestrian access checked (not just distance) | Y/N |
| Residential and employment density calculated (gross and/or net) | Y/N |
| Land-use inventory complete with % breakdown | Y/N |
| Mix includes check for vertical mixed-use, essential services, and ground-floor activation | Y/N |
| Walkability scored across block size, sidewalk quality, barriers, and slope | Y/N |
| Transit frequency scored against Calthorpe thresholds (≤15 min peak); feeder connectivity assessed | Y/N |
| Each dimension scored 1–4; TOD Viability Index calculated | Y/N |
| Lowest-scoring dimension identified with improvement priorities | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Transit stop identified by name only (e.g., "near the light rail") without confirmed headway or actual distance measurement. Proximity claims without verification are the most common TOD mislabeling.
- Density scored high but all units are in a single residential tower with no mixed-use. A single 500-unit building in an otherwise single-use environment is not TOD.
- Mix tally shows ≥3 land uses but they are completely segregated by geography (residential on one block, retail three blocks away). Vertical or tight geographic mix matters; scattered uses do not.
- Walkability scored as "good" but the walking route to transit requires crossing a 6-lane highway or passing through a parking lot. Major barriers negate block-size improvements.
- Transit headway exceeds 20 minutes off-peak and is described as "acceptable because this is a smaller market." Apply the framework's threshold anyway. If the market can't support the threshold, the site is not TOD — it's transit-adjacent.
- TOD Viability Index is 2.5–3.4 but no improvement priorities are listed. The index is meaningful only if paired with a roadmap for strengthening the weakest dimension.
- Ground-floor activation is not assessed; only counted. A storefront on every corner means nothing if half are vacant or defunct. Walk the streets or use Street View; survey photos if available.

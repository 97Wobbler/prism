# Moral Panic Analysis

## Overview

A moral panic is a process in which a group, behavior, or event is defined as a threat to social order; media amplifies the threat; the public and officials respond with disproportionate action; and institutional measures persist even when the original threat recedes. Cohen's framework identifies five discrete stages in this cycle. Practitioners use this lens to distinguish genuine social problems from manufactured crises, to audit whether policy responses are proportional, and to understand how fear narratives become self-reinforcing. The work product is a society or institution's response to a claimed threat (media coverage, policy, public debate, enforcement action).

## Analytical Procedure

### Stage 1 — Identification of the Deviant/Threatening Group or Behavior

1. **Name the alleged threat explicitly.** What group, behavior, or event is being framed as dangerous? Write it in neutral language first; note the inflammatory terms used in media and official discourse separately.

2. **Document the initial trigger event or claim.** What incident, study, statistic, or statement sparked concern? When did it appear publicly? Who made it?

3. **Identify who initially labeled this as a threat.** Was it a politician, journalist, academic, religious figure, activist, or law enforcement? Record their stated reasoning.

4. **List the negative stereotypes, labels, or "folk devil" characterizations applied.** These are often catchy, reductive, emotionally loaded names. Examples: "Teddy Boys," "Satanic cults," "super-predators." Write them verbatim.

### Stage 2 — Media Amplification and Distortion

5. **Collect media coverage across at least 3 outlets over a 6-month span after initial identification.** Capture headlines, key phrases, imagery, and tone.

6. **Measure distortion by comparing:**
   - **Frequency claim**: How often does the alleged threat appear in coverage? (e.g., "gang violence" mentioned 50 times per week vs. actual crime statistics)
   - **Severity claim**: What language escalates the threat? (e.g., "rampant epidemic" vs. "isolated incidents")
   - **Scope claim**: Is the threat presented as widespread or contained? Does coverage conflate isolated cases into a pattern?
   - **Causality claim**: Is the threat attributed to the group's inherent nature or to external circumstances? (e.g., "feral youth" vs. "youth living in high-poverty areas with underfunded schools")

7. **Note repetition and symbolism.** Do specific images, names, or incidents get recycled? Does the media use stock narratives (gang warfare, cult rituals, immigrant crime) that fit the threat regardless of evidence?

### Stage 3 — Public and Official Reaction

8. **Document public response indicators:**
   - Public opinion polls or surveys on the perceived threat (if available)
   - Public calls for action (letters, protests, social media campaigns)
   - Vigilante or retaliatory action (violence against the identified group)
   - Community organizing or moral crusades

9. **Document official response indicators:**
   - Statements by law enforcement, prosecutors, or politicians
   - Legislative proposals or new laws
   - Funding allocation increases
   - Task forces, commissions, or investigations launched
   - Enforcement intensity (arrests, raids, surveillance)

10. **Assess proportionality by comparing:**
    - **Official response scale** vs. **baseline threat incidence**. Example: If gang-related homicides are 2% of all homicides but trigger 40% increase in police budget, note the disproportionality.
    - **Official rhetoric** vs. **available data**. Does the stated urgency match crime statistics, epidemiological data, or other objective measures?

### Stage 4 — Social Control and Institutional Measures

11. **List all formal controls enacted or intensified:**
    - New criminal statutes or enhanced penalties
    - Surveillance infrastructure (databases, monitoring)
    - Treatment or remedial programs (rehabilitation, therapy, education)
    - Isolation measures (curfews, restrictions on movement or association)
    - Professional expansion (new police units, courts, agencies)

12. **Evaluate feedback loops.** Do the control measures increase detection of the alleged behavior, even if the behavior itself hasn't changed? Example: More police patrols in a neighborhood will find more violations, even if violation rates haven't risen. This creates an illusion of worsening threat.

13. **Track institutional interests.** Who benefits from the panic? (Police budgets, political re-election, media ratings, professional expansion, funding to nonprofits.) Document whether these institutions have incentives to sustain the panic.

### Stage 5 — Decline and Legacy

14. **Determine decline timeline.** When did public and official concern visibly diminish? What caused the decline — did the original threat materialize (rare), did evidence contra-indicate the threat, did media attention shift, or did the panic simply exhaust itself?

15. **Assess residual institutional legacy.** Even after panic subsides, some measures persist (laws remain on books, agencies survive, stigma attaches to the group). Document what remained and why.

16. **Measure harm to the identified group.** Did the panic result in wrongful convictions, violence, discrimination, or social exclusion? Was there any exoneration or apology?

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Alleged threat identified explicitly and neutrally; inflammatory terms noted separately | Y/N |
| Initial trigger event and originating actor(s) documented with dates | Y/N |
| Media coverage sampled across ≥3 outlets and ≥6 months; distortion measured on 4 dimensions (frequency, severity, scope, causality) | Y/N |
| Public and official reactions documented with quantified metrics (budget changes, law enforcement data, polling if available) | Y/N |
| Proportionality assessment compares response scale to baseline incidence and official rhetoric to available data | Y/N |
| Formal controls listed with feedback loops and institutional incentive analysis | Y/N |
| Decline timeline established; residual legacy and harm to identified group assessed | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Media coverage shows little escalation or distortion over time. If tone and frequency remain flat, this may not be a moral panic — it may be a genuine, accurately-reported threat.
- Official response is modest or proportional to the claimed threat. Moral panics are characterized by disproportionate institutional mobilization; proportionate responses suggest the threat is real.
- The identified group actively engages in the behavior claimed (e.g., a youth gang does commit robbery). This does not disqualify a moral panic — moral panics can contain a kernel of truth — but the analysis must show that the *scale* or *nature* of the threat was distorted.
- No institutional interests are identified. Every panic has beneficiaries; missing them suggests the analysis wasn't critical enough.
- Decline occurs because the threat was successfully eliminated. Moral panics usually decline due to attention fatigue, shifting media focus, or exhaustion of easy enforcement targets — not because the threat was addressed.
- The "harm to the group" section is empty. If no documented harm occurred, the panic may have been rhetorical rather than institutional.

# SOSTAC Framework

## Overview
SOSTAC structures a marketing plan into six sequential phases: Situation (current state), Objectives (measurable goals), Strategy (the broad approach), Tactics (specific activities), Action (execution plan), and Control (measurement and adjustment). Practitioners use this when building a campaign from scratch or stress-testing an existing plan for internal coherence. Each phase constrains the next — a weak Situation analysis will corrupt the Objectives, which will corrupt the Strategy. The discipline is not skipping phases and not letting each phase drift from what preceded it.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Situation

1. **Document the current state without interpretation.** Write factual statements only:
   - Market size (units/revenue), growth rate
   - Competitive landscape (named competitors, their positions)
   - Customer segments (size, buying power, pain points, data source)
   - Your brand's current position (market share %, awareness, perception, vs. competitors)
   - Internal assets (budget, team, capabilities, constraints)
   - External trends (regulatory, economic, technological, cultural)

2. **Identify a specific problem or opportunity the plan will address.** Write it as a gap: "We have X% awareness but our target segment requires Y% to consider us" or "Competitor Z is growing at 30% and we are at 5%." Do not propose a solution yet.

3. **Tag each fact with its source.** (proprietary data, third-party research, assumption). If a critical fact is an assumption, flag it for validation during Action.

### Phase 2 — Objectives

4. **Extract 3–5 measurable business objectives from the Situation.** Each must:
   - Be quantified with a start value, target value, and timeline (e.g., "increase brand awareness from 12% to 28% in 18 months")
   - Address a specific audience segment (not "all customers")
   - Be achievable given the budget and internal constraints identified in Situation
   - Have a clear success metric (brand lift, conversion rate, market share, revenue, customer acquisition cost, etc.)

5. **Check each objective against the Situation.** If the Situation does not justify the objective, either revise the objective or add missing Situation data. Do not write an objective that contradicts or ignores the Situation.

6. **Rank the objectives by business impact.** Primary, secondary, tertiary. Tactics and Action will be built to hit the primary objective first.

### Phase 3 — Strategy

7. **For each primary objective, write the broad strategic approach in 2–3 sentences.** Strategy answers "how will we close the gap identified in Situation?" at a high level. Examples:
   - "Become the trusted authority in X by publishing thought leadership and earning third-party credibility."
   - "Dominate the price-sensitive segment by building a direct-to-consumer channel and undercutting distribution costs."
   - "Shift perception from legacy to innovative by associating the brand with new customer archetypes."

8. **Ensure the strategy is grounded in the Situation.** Does it exploit our strengths, avoid our weaknesses, address the competitive threat, or serve the target segment's stated pain? If not, revise.

9. **Check for internal coherence.** If you have multiple objectives, do their strategies reinforce or conflict? (Example conflict: "grow luxury perception" + "maximize volume with low-price offer" are at odds.) Flag conflicts; do not ignore them.

### Phase 4 — Tactics

10. **List the 4–8 specific activities that execute the strategy.** Tactics are concrete, not abstract:
    - Bad: "Build brand awareness."
    - Good: "Launch a 3-month paid social campaign targeting 18–35 year-olds interested in fitness, with 15M impressions budgeted at $0.80 CPM, running on Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube."

11. **For each tactic, document:**
    - The channel or medium (social, email, events, PR, partnerships, content, paid search, etc.)
    - The audience (segment, size, access method)
    - The message or creative direction (one sentence describing what is being communicated)
    - The duration and frequency
    - The budget or resource allocation

12. **Cross-reference each tactic back to the strategy and objective it serves.** If a tactic doesn't ladder up to a stated objective, either cut it or add the objective. Do not carry tactics that are orphaned.

### Phase 5 — Action

13. **For each tactic, create an execution plan:**
    - Responsible owner (name/role)
    - Key milestones and deadlines (launch date, review dates)
    - Dependencies (creative approvals, budget release, data access, vendor contracts)
    - Success criteria (the specific metric that tactic is measured against)
    - Contingencies (what if the channel performs below target, above target, or not at all?)

14. **Identify resource gaps and risks.** Review all Action items:
    - Do we have budget for all tactics? If not, which are optional?
    - Do we have skilled personnel? If not, are we hiring or contracting?
    - Are there external dependencies (vendors, platform policies, legal approvals)? Document lead times.
    - What is the single point of failure? (Often: one person knows the vendor, one data pipeline is fragile, one approval is slow.) Flag these.

15. **Create a timeline.** Tactics do not launch simultaneously. Write a phased launch plan that respects dependencies and allows measurement of early tactics before committing full budget to late tactics.

### Phase 6 — Control

16. **For each objective and tactic, define the measurement plan:**
    - Metric (the number you will track)
    - Data source (where the number comes from — web analytics, CRM, third-party survey, etc.)
    - Collection frequency (daily, weekly, monthly, post-campaign)
    - Baseline (the number before the campaign)
    - Target (the number the objective specifies)
    - Alert threshold (the number at which you trigger a course correction)

17. **Schedule reviews.** At minimum:
    - Weekly operational review (Are tactics launching on schedule? Are vendors performing?)
    - Monthly performance review (Are metrics hitting targets? Which tactics are outperforming? Which are underperforming?)
    - Quarterly strategy review (Has the Situation changed? Do objectives still make sense? Should tactics be reallocated?)

18. **Write the decision rules for course correction.** Example: "If paid social CAC exceeds $45 in month 1, we pause creative Z and reallocate budget to creative X." Do not leave control as a vague "we will watch and decide."

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Situation includes market size, competitors, customer segments, internal constraints, and external trends | Y/N |
| Each objective is quantified with start value, target value, timeline, and tied to a specific segment | Y/N |
| Strategy statements are grounded in the Situation and do not assume facts not in evidence | Y/N |
| All tactics ladder up to at least one objective or strategy | Y/N |
| No tactic is described at the "awareness building" level — all are concrete with channels, audiences, and budgets | Y/N |
| Each tactic has a named owner, timeline, and success metric in Action | Y/N |
| Control section includes data sources, collection frequency, and specific decision rules for adjustment | Y/N |
| No phase contradicts or ignores the previous phase | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Situation is mostly internal opinion ("our product is best") with few external facts (market data, competitive moves, customer research).
- Objectives are vague ("increase sales," "improve engagement") or unmeasurable ("become the market leader in perceptions").
- Strategy reads like a tactic ("run a TikTok campaign") instead of a broad approach.
- Tactics do not reference the strategy or objective — they look like a feature request list.
- Action section has no named owners or only lists "TBD."
- Control section is absent or says "we will track metrics" without specifying which metrics, where data lives, or how often.
- Multiple objectives have contradictory strategies (e.g., premium positioning + lowest-price positioning).
- Timeline has all tactics launching simultaneously, making it impossible to learn from early results.

**Market context:**

<size, profile, pain points>

<market share, awareness, perception>

**Internal assets & constraints:**
<budget, team, capabilities, limitations>

**Problem/opportunity statement:**
<specific gap or trend being addressed>

### Objectives
1. <Quantified objective with timeline and audience> [Primary/Secondary/Tertiary]
2. ...

### Strategy
**For [Primary Objective]:**
<2–3 sentence strategic approach, grounded in Situation>

**Strategic coherence check:** [No conflicts noted / Conflicts: <list>]

### Tactics
| Tactic | Channel | Audience | Message | Duration | Budget |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| <name> | <medium> | <segment, size> | <one sentence> | <dates> | $<amount> |

### Action
| Tactic | Owner | Key Milestones | Dependencies | Success Metric | Contingency |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| <name> | <name> | Launch <date>, Review <date> | <approvals, data, vendor> | <target number> | <if underperforms: X> |

**Resource gaps:** <budget, headcount, vendor, other>

**Launch timeline:** [Phased by <dependency or sequence>]

### Control
| Objective / Tactic | Metric | Data Source | Frequency | Baseline | Target | Alert Threshold |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| <objective> | <KPI> | <system> | <cadence> | <start #> | <end #> | <trigger #> |

**Review cadence:** Weekly ops, Monthly performance, Quarterly strategy

**Decision rules for adjustment:** <If <metric> <condition>, then <action>>

### Confidence
<high/medium/low> — <justification: data quality, assumption risk, team alignment, market volatility>

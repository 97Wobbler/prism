# Problem/Agitate/Solution

## Overview
PAS is a three-stage framework for constructing persuasive narratives. It moves an audience from problem recognition (Problem), through emotional engagement and stakes amplification (Agitate), to a single remedy (Solution). Practitioners use it to write marketing copy, sales pitches, and fundraising narratives where the goal is to shift a passive reader into a committed actor. The discipline is the *agitation phase* — most people compress it or skip it, leading to weak conversion.

## Analytical Procedure

### Stage 1 — Problem Identification

1. **Name the specific, concrete pain the target audience experiences.** Not "businesses struggle with data," but "a sales director loses 3 hours daily to spreadsheet reconciliation." Make it particular enough that a person in that role nods immediately. Write it as a single short sentence.

2. **Verify the problem is felt, not theoretical.** Ask: Does the audience already *know* they have this problem, or are you announcing it to them? If you're announcing it, the problem is too abstract for PAS. Go deeper into what blocks them daily.

3. **Identify the cost of the problem.** Estimate in concrete units: time lost, money wasted, opportunity foregone, or stress endured. Do not be vague ("costs a lot"). Specificity here powers the agitation phase.

### Stage 2 — Agitation

4. **Enlarge the problem.** For each cost identified in Stage 1, trace the second-order consequences. If a sales director loses 3 hours daily to reconciliation, they also:
   - Miss coaching their team
   - Submit late reports to leadership
   - Make forecast errors
   - Miss upsell opportunities
   
   Write each consequence as a separate bullet. Do not combine them.

5. **Connect consequences to the audience's values.** For each consequence, ask: What does this person care about that this consequence threatens? (Career advancement, team morale, revenue, credibility, peace of mind, etc.) Write the threat in their language, not yours.

6. **Quantify or emotionalize the total burden.** Sum the consequences into a single statement of what's at stake. This should feel significant. Example: "You're leaving $200k in annual revenue on the table while your team resents the busywork." Not "this is inconvenient."

### Stage 3 — Solution Introduction

7. **Present the remedy as the only logical exit from the agitated state.** Do not claim it's the best solution or the most innovative. Claim it *solves the specific problem you agitated.* If the problem is "3 hours lost to reconciliation," the solution is "reconciliation happens in 15 minutes."

8. **Match the remedy to the agitation, not to adjacent benefits.** If you agitated around "losing revenue," do not pivot to "and it also has a beautiful dashboard." The solution should feel inevitable, not clever.

9. **Establish why this remedy works for this audience specifically.** Not "our software is powerful," but "our software integrates with Salesforce directly, so you don't retrain anyone." Tailor to the person in the problem statement.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Problem is named with a specific, concrete pain (not abstract) | Y/N |
| Problem cost is quantified in units the audience recognizes | Y/N |
| Agitation contains at least 3 distinct second-order consequences | Y/N |
| Each consequence is connected to an audience value or goal | Y/N |
| Total burden (Stage 2 conclusion) feels significant and urgent | Y/N |
| Solution directly solves the problem stated in Stage 1 | Y/N |
| Solution has no features unrelated to the agitated problem | Y/N |
| Remedy is tailored to the specific audience (not generic) | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The Problem statement is a category ("SMBs struggle with X") rather than a scene (a CFO loses Friday nights to month-end close). Abstraction kills PAS.
- The Agitate section is shorter than the Problem section. If you're not spending at least 2x the words amplifying consequences, you're underestimating how much resistance you need to overcome.
- The Solution section introduces a new benefit not mentioned in agitation. This signals the remedy was chosen before the problem was fully understood.
- All consequences in Agitate are financial. The audience is human, not a spreadsheet. Weave in at least one emotional or interpersonal consequence (stress, reputation, team morale).
- The audience is treated as rational. PAS works because it *isn't* rational — it moves through feeling. If your agitation is purely logical, it's not working.
- Transitions between stages are jarring ("Well, here's the good news…"). The solution should feel like the inevitable exit from the agitated state, not a hard sell pivot.

# Stakeholder Mapping (Mendelow)

## Overview

Stakeholder Mapping plots each actor involved in or affected by a policy, initiative, or decision on two dimensions: their *power* (ability to influence or block outcome) and their *interest* (stake in the outcome or motivation to engage). The resulting four quadrants—High Power/High Interest, High Power/Low Interest, Low Power/High Interest, Low Power/Low Interest—each demand a distinct engagement strategy. Practitioners use this lens when a policy or project involves multiple actors with competing priorities and unclear influence paths, or when communication effort is scarce and must be allocated strategically.

## Analytical Procedure

### Step 1 — Identify all stakeholders

1. **List every actor or group with a material stake in the outcome.** Include internal actors (agency staff, elected officials, senior leadership), external actors (affected populations, advocacy groups, other government bodies, private sector), and latent actors (taxpayers, future beneficiaries, those harmed if the initiative fails).

2. **For each stakeholder, write one sentence describing their primary interest or concern.** Do not assume neutral intent; note whether they benefit, bear risk, or have competing goals.

3. **Group stakeholders by shared interests only if they will act as a unit.** If two groups have aligned interests but operate independently, list them separately.

### Step 2 — Score power

4. **Define what "power" means for this initiative.** Examples:
   - Ability to block approval or delay implementation (veto power).
   - Control over budget, resources, or personnel needed to execute.
   - Legal or regulatory authority over the domain.
   - Ability to mobilize public opinion, media, or allied institutions.
   - Control over access to beneficiaries or implementation sites.

5. **For each stakeholder, score power on a 3-point scale: Low, Medium, High.**
   - **Low:** Can advocate or complain but cannot unilaterally stop or significantly delay the initiative.
   - **Medium:** Can slow, modify, or redirect the initiative; requires negotiation to move forward.
   - **High:** Can veto, defund, or halt the initiative unilaterally or can fast-track it.

6. **If power is highly uncertain, interview or research the stakeholder to clarify.** Do not guess. If power varies over time (e.g., a stakeholder gains power after a decision point), note the timeline separately.

### Step 3 — Score interest

7. **Define what "interest" means.** Interest is the stakeholder's motivation to engage, measured by:
   - Perceived stake in the outcome (personal, organizational, financial, ideological).
   - Likelihood they will pay attention to the initiative or seek involvement.
   - Magnitude of impact on them if the initiative succeeds or fails.

8. **For each stakeholder, score interest on a 3-point scale: Low, Medium, High.**
   - **Low:** Initiative has minimal impact on them or they have competing priorities that dominate their attention.
   - **Medium:** Initiative affects them materially; they will engage if activated or if outcomes change.
   - **High:** Initiative is central to their mandate, mission, or survival; they will seek involvement proactively.

9. **Distinguish between *stated* interest (what they say) and *revealed* interest (what they do).** If a stakeholder says they care but has never shown up to meetings or submitted input, their revealed interest is low. Note the gap.

### Step 4 — Plot and assign quadrants

10. **Create a 2×2 matrix with Power (vertical) and Interest (horizontal), each with Low/Medium/High.**

11. **Plot each stakeholder at the intersection of their power and interest scores.** If a stakeholder straddles two cells (e.g., High power but interest ranges from Low to Medium), assign them to the dominant quadrant and note the variance.

12. **Assign each stakeholder to one of four quadrants:**

| Quadrant | Power | Interest | Label | Engagement Need |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | High | High | **Key Players** | Manage closely; keep satisfied; consult on major decisions; build coalition with them. |
| II | High | Low | **Keep Satisfied** | Monitor; inform proactively; accommodate concerns to prevent them from blocking; avoid surprises. |
| III | Low | High | **Keep Informed** | Listen to their concerns; explain decisions; involve in design if feasible; manage expectations. |
| IV | Low | Low | **Monitor** | Scan for shifts in power or interest; minimal active engagement unless they organize. |

### Step 5 — Identify misalignments and risks

13. **Check for dangerous gaps:**
   - **High Power / Low Interest + Low Power / High Interest gap:** Key stakeholders (Quadrant I) are few or absent. High-interest groups lack power to influence outcomes. Risk: implementation faces unexpected blockage or low adoption.
   - **Quadrant II overweight:** Many powerful actors are not invested. Risk: they may block from inertia or self-interest if not actively kept satisfied.
   - **Quadrant III overweight:** Many affected populations lack power. Risk: unmet expectations, grievances, or post-implementation resistance if their concerns are not addressed.
   - **All in Quadrant IV:** Initiative has no champions and no organized opposition. Risk: drift, low priority, or silent failure.

14. **For each gap, write a sentence describing the strategic risk and who needs to shift quadrant (and how).**

### Step 6 — Derive engagement strategy per quadrant

15. **For each quadrant represented in your map, define a concrete engagement approach:**

| Quadrant | Strategy | Tactics |
|---|---|---|
| **I: Key Players** | Co-design and alignment. | Monthly steering meetings; early access to drafts; explicit decision-making role; escalation path for disputes. |
| **II: Keep Satisfied** | Anticipatory accommodation and transparency. | Regular briefings; explicit acknowledgment of their concerns; adjustments to design/messaging that don't compromise core goals; clear "no surprises" channel. |
| **III: Keep Informed** | Listen and explain. | Public comment periods; advisory councils or listening sessions; published rationale for decisions; channels to escalate feedback. |
| **IV: Monitor** | Scan for activity. | Quarterly check-ins on whether conditions have shifted; trigger reassessment if media, political, or organizational shifts elevate interest or power. |

16. **For each stakeholder in Quadrants I and II, define who is the primary relationship owner (a named person, not "the team").**

17. **Define success metrics for each quadrant.** Examples:
   - **Quadrant I:** Agreement on at least 80% of design trade-offs; signed memoranda of understanding.
   - **Quadrant II:** No public opposition; baseline satisfaction survey ≥ 50%.
   - **Quadrant III:** >70% aware of key decisions; <20% surprised by outcomes.
   - **Quadrant IV:** No emergence into higher quadrants during implementation window.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| All material stakeholders (internal and external) are listed | Y/N |
| Power definition is explicit and consistent across stakeholders | Y/N |
| Interest definition is explicit; stated vs. revealed interest noted where they diverge | Y/N |
| Each stakeholder is scored Low/Medium/High on both dimensions | Y/N |
| All four quadrants are populated or explicitly explained as empty | Y/N |
| At least one strategic misalignment or gap is identified with risk articulated | Y/N |
| Each occupied quadrant has a named engagement strategy with concrete tactics | Y/N |
| Relationship owners are named (person, not title) for Quadrants I and II | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- Stakeholder list is homogeneous (all internal or all political allies). Adversaries, skeptics, and affected populations are missing.
- Power scores are all "Medium." Differentiation failure — the analyst either doesn't understand the decision structure or is avoiding uncomfortable truths about who actually has power.
- Interest scores are all based on stated intent with no revealed-behavior check. A stakeholder who claims investment but never attends meetings is Low Interest, not Medium.
- All stakeholders end up in Quadrant I (Key Players). Either every actor is equally important (unlikely) or the scoring conflates "should engage" with actual power/interest.
- No gaps or misalignments are noted. Every map has tensions. If none appear, the analysis was not adversarial enough.
- Engagement strategies are generic ("communicate," "involve stakeholders"). Tactics must be specific: name the meeting cadence, decision-making role, and escalation path.
- Relationship owners are titles ("the project manager," "communications team") instead of individuals. Accountability evaporates.

# Microhistory (Ginzburg)

## Overview

Microhistory inverts the usual direction of historical inquiry: instead of starting with large structures and filtering down to individuals, begin with a single person, document, or local event and reconstruct the entire cultural system from which it emerged. Ginzburg's method treats anomalies—heresy, crime, gossip, marginalia—as windows into the mental and social architecture of an era. Practitioners use this lens when conventional macro-level sources (laws, chronicles, institutional records) are silent or deceptive, and when the case study is precise and well-documented enough to support inference.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Select and Establish the Microhistorical Subject

1. **Identify a historical subject with rich documentation but marginal visibility.** The ideal candidate is:
   - A person of low or ambiguous status whose words or actions were recorded (trial testimony, letters, confessions, autobiographies)
   - A local event (crime, miracle, heresy trial, village dispute) that generated administrative or legal records
   - A cultural artifact of limited circulation (a book, recipe, ritual, tool, artwork) that survived by accident
   - NOT a major figure or event already saturated by historical interpretation
   
   *Why marginal?* Because the historical mainstream leaves it unexamined, forcing you to read against the grain and invent methods to extract meaning.

2. **Establish the documentary base.** List all primary sources that mention or involve the subject:
   - Trial records, depositions, interrogation protocols
   - Personal correspondence, notebooks, marginalia
   - Administrative documents (tax rolls, church records, guild membership)
   - Visual or material artifacts (paintings, household inventories, tools)
   - Oral traditions recorded by others
   
   Assess the reliability of each source. Note who created it, when, for what audience, and what they had to gain or lose by what they wrote.

3. **Map the subject's social world.** Create a network of relationships, affiliations, and conflicts:
   - Family, patrons, rivals, associates
   - Institutional memberships (church, guild, military, fraternity)
   - Economic relationships (debts, transactions, dependencies)
   - Intellectual or spiritual influences (books read, teachers, peers)
   
   This map becomes the context from which you infer meaning.

### Phase 2 — Read for Strangeness and Rupture

4. **Identify what doesn't fit.** Scan your documents for:
   - Contradictions between the subject's own account and others' accounts
   - Anachronisms (language, concepts, or behaviors inconsistent with the era)
   - Silences (things the subject refuses to answer, despite pressure)
   - Excess (unusual detail, emotion, repetition in testimony)
   - Reiterations (the same claim repeated verbatim in multiple sources — often a sign of coaching or formula)
   
   Do not iron these out. They are your material.

5. **Formulate a tension or puzzle.** Ask: What would cause a rational, ordinary person in this time and place to act/speak/believe in this anomalous way? Do not assume stupidity, delusion, or unique idiosyncrasy. Assume the person was intelligible within their own cultural system and that the anomaly reveals something about that system.

### Phase 3 — Reconstruct the Mental Framework

6. **Examine what the subject knew and believed.** From the documents, infer:
   - What books, stories, or teachings did the subject encounter?
   - What folk traditions, superstitions, or practical wisdom guided their choices?
   - What authority did they acknowledge (religious, legal, familial, natural)?
   - How did they understand concepts central to their era (sin, honor, property, nature, causation)?
   
   Use anachronism detection: when the subject uses a term or logic that seems alien, map it onto the intellectual currents of their time, not ours.

7. **Identify the cultural code the subject was operating within.** This is the crux. The subject was not inventing meaning alone. They were drawing from:
   - Religious or philosophical traditions (theology, scholasticism, folk belief)
   - Legal or customary norms (precedent, unwritten rules)
   - Trade or craft knowledge (guild secrets, technical know-how)
   - Narrative or linguistic conventions (story shapes, metaphors, speech patterns)
   
   The subject's anomalous behavior makes sense if you can identify which code(s) they were following and how they understood the code's application.

### Phase 4 — Test the Inference Against Alternative Readings

8. **Generate competing interpretations.** For the same documents, write at least two alternative explanations of the subject's behavior that are internally consistent and grounded in the era:
   - One reading that emphasizes religious or ideological motivation
   - One that emphasizes material or social motive
   - One that treats the subject as more passive/conformist than your primary reading
   
   Do not strawman these alternatives. State them at their strongest.

9. **Apply evidential discrimination.** For each interpretation, list what evidence it predicts and what it must ignore or explain away. Which reading makes better use of the full documentary record? Which requires the fewest ad-hoc assumptions?
   
   Mark each interpretation as *strong* (fits most evidence, minor lacunae), *plausible* (fits significant evidence but has notable gaps), or *weak* (requires ignoring major contradictions).

### Phase 5 — Trace the Inference to Macrohistorical Insight

10. **Generalize carefully from the case.** Ask: What does this one person's mental framework reveal about the culture, religion, economy, or power structure of their era? Do not claim direct universality. Instead, ask:
    - Is there evidence this mental framework was shared beyond this subject? (other trials, documents, behaviors in the same time/place)
    - What broader tension or contradiction in the era does this case exemplify?
    - What was invisible or unspoken in the official record that this marginal case makes visible?
    - How would the standard historical narrative need to shift if this case is representative?
    
    This is the bridge from micro to macro.

11. **Document the limits of the inference.** Specify:
    - What the documents *cannot* tell us (inner states, unrecorded communications, counterfactuals)
    - What social categories the subject belonged to and which remain opaque (gender, age, literacy)
    - Whether the case is truly representative or genuinely idiosyncratic (in which case its value is exemplary, not statistical)

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Subject is documented but marginal; not a major historical figure | Y/N |
| Documentary base is identified and reliability of each source assessed | Y/N |
| Social/institutional network of subject is mapped | Y/N |
| At least three anomalies or unexplained details in the documents are identified | Y/N |
| An original puzzle is formulated that assumes the subject was intelligible | Y/N |
| Mental framework or cultural code is reconstructed with specific citations | Y/N |
| At least two alternative interpretations are stated at full strength | Y/N |
| Evidence discriminating among interpretations is explicitly listed | Y/N |
| Macro-level insight is traced to the micro case; not merely asserted | Y/N |
| Limits of inference (what cannot be known) are acknowledged | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The subject is famous or well-worn historiographically. Microhistory works when the subject has been neglected.
- Documents are treated as transparent reports of fact. No attention to who created the document, for what purpose, or what they had to hide or prove.
- The anomaly is explained away as individual eccentricity or error rather than as a window onto cultural patterns.
- The "mental framework" reconstructed is generic (e.g., "medieval people believed in God") rather than specific to the subject's intellectual milieu.
- Only one interpretation is entertained; others are dismissed with a phrase rather than stated and systematically compared.
- The leap from case to macro-structure is unjustified. The case is treated as proof rather than as exemplary evidence that raises new questions.
- Silences in the documents are not acknowledged. The analyst fills gaps with assumption rather than admitting what the record does not say.
- The analysis treats the subject anachronistically, imposing modern categories (psychology, nationalism, rights) rather than translating their concepts into the era's own conceptual vocabulary.

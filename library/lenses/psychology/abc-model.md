# ABC Model (Ellis REBT)

## Overview

The ABC Model traces emotional distress to a belief—not to the event itself. An **Activating event** (A) triggers a **Belief** (B) about that event, which produces a **Consequence** (C)—the emotional or behavioral outcome. The discipline is isolating B from A and C, because most people attribute C directly to A ("She rejected me, *therefore* I'm worthless") when the actual chain is ("She rejected me; I *believe* this means I'm fundamentally unlovable; therefore I feel despair"). Practitioners use this when a client, colleague, or team member is stuck in an emotional loop and conventional advice ("just think positive") hasn't worked. The model is diagnostic, not prescriptive—it exposes where the lever actually is.

## Analytical Procedure

### Phase 1 — Capture the Event and Consequence

1. **Identify the Activating Event (A).** Ask the person: "What happened?" Write down the observable fact, stripped of interpretation. Not "My boss humiliated me in the meeting" but "My boss corrected my proposal in front of the team." The event should be checkable by a camera or a neutral observer.

2. **Identify the Consequence (C).** Ask: "How did you feel and act afterward?" Write down the emotional state (anxiety, shame, rage, numbness) and the behavioral response (avoidance, lashing out, shutdown, rumination). Be specific: "I felt humiliated and didn't contribute for the rest of the meeting" is better than "I was upset."

3. **Verify that A and C are not directly causal.** The same event happens to different people with different consequences. The same person experiences different consequences on different days despite similar events. This gap is where B lives.

### Phase 2 — Excavate the Belief

4. **Ask the belief-surfacing questions.** The person is experiencing C. Go backward to find B:
   - "What did that [event] mean to you?"
   - "What did you tell yourself about what happened?"
   - "What are you afraid would be true if [event] is real?"
   - "What does this say about you as a person?"
   - "What's the worst part of what happened?"

5. **Listen for core beliefs, not situational thoughts.** Situational thoughts are about the event ("I made a mistake"). Core beliefs are about the self, others, or the world ("I am incompetent," "People will judge me," "The world is unsafe"). Core beliefs are usually stated in absolute terms—always, never, must, should, can't, am/is/are without qualification.

6. **Write the belief as a complete sentence.** Not fragments like "not good enough" but "I am fundamentally not good enough, and if people see my true self, they will reject me." The fuller the belief, the more you can interrogate it in Phase 3.

7. **Check for should/must statements.** Ellis called these "musts" and identified them as the emotional fuel. Scan for prescriptive language: "I *must* be perfect," "They *should* have known better," "Life *should not* be this hard." These are often the real drivers.

### Phase 3 — Verify the Chain

8. **Trace A → B → C explicitly.** Write the chain as: "When [A], I believed [B], which led to [C]." Example: "When my boss corrected my proposal, I believed *I am incompetent and will be fired because of this failure*, which led to *shame and withdrawal from the meeting*."

9. **Ask: "Does this belief explain why C happened?"** If the person says yes, you have the chain. If not, B is still incomplete. Return to Phase 2.

10. **Check for alternative beliefs.** Ask: "Could you have had a different thought about [A] that would have led to a different feeling?" If yes, write the alternative. This confirms that B, not A, is the driver. Example: "I could have thought *this is feedback that will help me improve*, which might have led to *determination to revise*."

### Phase 4 — Assess Belief Validity (Optional, for deeper work)

11. **Question the belief using evidence.** Ask:
    - "What evidence do you have that [belief] is true?"
    - "Is there evidence against it?"
    - "Has this belief always been true, or did it develop at a particular time?"
    - "Is this belief helpful, even if parts are true?" (A belief can be partially accurate and still be emotionally toxic.)

12. **Do not argue the person out of the belief.** The goal is to create space for them to examine it, not to convince them. Defensiveness usually shuts down the work.

## Evaluation Criteria

| Check | Pass |
|---|---|
| Activating Event is observable (camera-checkable), not interpreted | Y/N |
| Consequence includes both emotion and behavior specifically | Y/N |
| Belief is stated as a complete sentence in the person's own words | Y/N |
| Belief contains absolutist language (must, always, never, am/is) | Y/N |
| Chain A → B → C is traceable and the person confirms it | Y/N |
| At least one alternative belief was generated for comparison | Y/N |

## Red Flags

- The "event" is actually an interpretation ("I was disrespected"). Return to Phase 1 and ask what actually happened.
- The belief is vague ("I feel bad about myself"). Push for the specific fear, judgment, or "must"—the real belief is more concrete.
- The person says C would happen *no matter what belief* they had ("I'd feel bad anyway"). This usually means the belief hasn't been fully surfaced yet, or A was actually traumatic. Do not force the model onto trauma; consider whether Phase 2 questions are hitting emotional walls.
- The chain sounds logical but the person's actual feeling seems disconnected. The belief may be intellectual rather than felt. True beliefs are embodied—ask "Do you *feel* this is true, or do you think it *should* be true?"
- Multiple competing beliefs emerge without a coherent core. The person may have a belief system with internal conflicts. Name the conflict; it's data.

## Output Format

```
## ABC Analysis

**Activating Event (A):**
<observable fact; what a camera would record>

**Consequence (C):**
Emotion: <specific emotion>
Behavior: <specific behavior or avoidance>

**Belief (B):**
<complete sentence, in person's own words, including "must," "should," or absolutist terms if present>

**Chain Verification:**
"When [A], I believed [B], which led to [C]."
<verbatim chain>

Does this chain match your experience? [Y/N and why]

**Alternative Belief(s):**
<alternative belief that could lead to different C>

**Evidence for Belief:**
Supporting: <what evidence or logic supports B>
Contradicting: <what evidence or logic contradicts B>
Utility: <Is the belief helpful, even if partially true?>

**Confidence**
<high/medium/low> — <justification: e.g., "high — the person strongly confirmed the A→B→C chain and generated alternative belief independently" or "medium — the belief is plausible but the person seemed defended when questioned">
```

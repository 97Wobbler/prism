# Rites of Passage (Van Gennep)

## Overview

Rites of passage are the ritual structures by which societies mark and manage transitions between social statuses — birth to personhood, youth to adulthood, civilian to soldier, life to death. Van Gennep's core claim is that all such transitions follow a **universal three-phase structure**, and that understanding which phase an individual or group is in **predicts which ritual obligations, social restrictions, and behavioral norms apply**. The frame is not merely descriptive; it explains why certain behaviors are expected, why certain people are excluded from everyday spaces, and why the ritual process cannot be skipped without social cost. A transition that is not properly marked is incomplete in the eyes of the community.

## Categories

1. **Separation (Détachement)**
   - The individual is **ritually and socially removed from their prior status**.
   - Secular life and ordinary identity are suspended; the person enters a liminal space.
   - Discriminating criterion: the individual has undergone an initial rite (naming ceremony, initiation wound, shaving of the head, taking of vows) that signals to the community that they are no longer in their old role and not yet in their new one.
   - Example: a bride leaving her father's house on wedding day; a conscript having their hair cut and civilian clothes removed; a novice monk receiving new robes and a new name.

2. **Liminal (Transition, Liminality)**
   - The individual is **betwixt and between** statuses — no longer the old, not yet the new.
   - They occupy an ambiguous social space with its own rules, often marked by ambiguous dress, restricted movement, and separation from ordinary society.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is under ritual instruction, taboo, or seclusion; they have specific restrictions (no contact with opposite sex, ritual silence, no interaction with the general public) and are sometimes considered spiritually dangerous or ritually unclean.
   - Example: the period of seclusion during an initiation ritual; the forty days of Lenten preparation; the engaged couple during the betrothal period; a menstruating woman in societies with menstrual taboos.

3. **Incorporation (Agrégation)**
   - The individual is **formally readmitted to society in their new status**.
   - A final rite (feast, coronation, unveiling, proclamation) publicly announces and celebrates the new status; restrictions are lifted and ordinary social roles resume.
   - Discriminating criterion: a public rite has occurred, the person wears the regalia or insignia of the new status, and they are reintegrated into everyday social life with new rights and obligations attached to the new role.
   - Example: a bride and groom walking down the aisle as a married couple; a soldier's swearing-in ceremony; a newly crowned monarch; a graduate's conferral of degree.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Identify the transition.** Name the status change (e.g., youth→adult, layperson→priest, unmarried→married, life→death).
2. **Has a formal rite of removal occurred?** (naming ceremony, initiation wound, ritual cutting, taking of vows, departure from the home)
   - If **yes**, the individual has entered Separation or is moving beyond it. Continue to step 3.
   - If **no**, the transition has not yet been ritually marked. The individual is still in their prior status; classification may not apply yet.
3. **Are they under active ritual instruction, seclusion, or taboo?** (restricted movement, special dress or lack thereof, ritual instruction, isolation from general society, behavioral rules specific to this phase)
   - If **yes** → **Liminal**. They are in the ritual passage itself.
   - If **no**, go to step 4.
4. **Has a final public rite of incorporation occurred?** (coronation, feast, public proclamation, unveiling, conferral of insignia or new name)
   - If **yes** → **Incorporation**. The transition is complete; they hold the new status.
   - If **no** → **Separation**. The removal has been marked, but incorporation has not yet occurred.

## Implications per Category

| Phase | Social status | What is true | Ritual and social obligations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Separation** | Transitional (old status formally severed) | The person is publicly marked as departing their old role but has not yet assumed the new one. Their old relationships and obligations are suspended. | Must undergo specified ritual actions; may be subject to temporary restrictions; must not resume old status obligations; family or community often has active role in guiding the process. |
| **Liminal** | Ambiguous; neither old nor new | The person is outside ordinary society, under ritual authority, often in seclusion or special quarters. They may be considered ritually unclean, spiritually potent, or dangerous to ordinary people. | Subject to strict taboos and ritual prescriptions; no ordinary work or social roles; instruction in the knowledge and behavior required for the new status; interaction with fellow liminars but isolation from general society. |
| **Incorporation** | New status formally acquired | The person is reintegrated into society, holds new social roles and relationships, and has new rights and obligations. They are no longer ambiguous; the community recognizes them in the new role. | Begin the duties of the new status; may have post-incorporation restrictions or observances; normal social intercourse resumes; new social and legal privileges come into force. |

For a social analyst, the practical implication is that **rituals cannot be skipped or abbreviated without social consequence**. A transition marked only by Separation but never reaching Incorporation leaves the person in permanent liminality — socially ambiguous and without clear standing. Conversely, attempting Incorporation without first passing through Liminal instruction often results in the person being deemed unfit for the new role.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Liminal mistaken for Incorporation.** Assuming that the end of seclusion or the removal of obvious restrictions (e.g., "she left the initiation house") means the person has been incorporated, when in fact a final public rite is still required. The tell is that the person still wears transitional dress or remains in a specially marked space, and the community has not yet publicly acknowledged the new status.
- **Separation mistaken for incomplete transition.** Assuming that because no seclusion is yet underway, the transition has not really begun. Some rites of separation (taking a new name, cutting of the hair) happen in a moment; the Liminal phase may begin immediately. The tell is whether the person is still in ordinary social life and ordinary dress.
- **Treating the three phases as overlapping or interchangeable.** Each phase has distinct ritual work; skipping or collapsing phases leaves the transition incomplete. A society that omits the Liminal phase (instruction, seclusion, ordeal) may still mark Separation and Incorporation, but the new status holder may lack the knowledge and commitment expected of the role.
- **Classifying on behavior alone rather than ritual marking.** A person may behave as if they are in the new status before Incorporation is complete (e.g., a young person acting "adult"); the classification depends on whether a formal rite has publicly declared the status, not on their behavior or self-perception.
- **Failing to recognize Liminality in non-obvious settings.** Western secular transitions (pregnancy, engagement, apprenticeship, incarceration) often have unmarked or informal Liminal phases that function ritually even without explicit naming. The tell is seclusion, instruction, restricted behavior, and clear prerequisites for moving forward.

# Polanyi's Three Forms of Economic Integration

## Overview

Polanyi's frame sorts the mechanisms by which goods, labor, and resources move through a society into three fundamentally different organizing principles. Each form relies on a distinct social structure and decision logic — and conflating them is a source of both analytical error and institutional failure. The core claim is that no economy is pure; all real economies mix the three forms, but their relative weight and which form governs which sectors determines the society's resilience, equality, and capacity for change. Understanding which form is in play for a given transaction or sector is essential to predicting how it will behave under stress.

## Categories

1. **Reciprocity**
   - Goods and labor flow symmetrically between parties, typically embedded in kinship, ritual, or peer networks.
   - Movement of resources is **symmetric, delayed, and obligatory** — a gift creates an obligation to return, but not immediately or in exact measure.
   - Discriminating criterion: the transaction assumes ongoing relationship and mutual obligation; the value is not set by external price but by social custom and status. Refusal to reciprocate is a rupture of relationship, not a failed trade.
   - Example: bride price in a marriage alliance; sharing of harvest in a village; mutual aid in a kinship network; potlatch ceremonies.

2. **Redistribution**
   - Goods and labor flow toward a **central authority** (chief, state, temple), which allocates them downward according to rank, need, or decree.
   - All flows are **radial**: inward to the center, then outward from it. The center holds the right to decide allocation.
   - Discriminating criterion: presence of a central authority with legitimate power to collect and redistribute; individuals have no say in final allocation, only in contribution. Fairness is measured by the authority's judgment, not by market equivalence.
   - Example: taxation and public works; temple granaries in agrarian states; corvée labor; welfare distribution; a centrally planned economy.

3. **Market**
   - Goods and labor are exchanged through **price-setting negotiation** between independent parties with no assumed ongoing relationship.
   - Value is determined by supply and demand (or haggling), not by custom or authority. Exchange is **symmetric in price, instantaneous, and voluntary** — both parties can walk away.
   - Discriminating criterion: presence of a price mechanism and the possibility of refusal without social penalty. The exchange is complete upon settlement; no further obligation.
   - Example: buying bread from a baker you've never met; labor hired by the hour; stock trading; a competitive auction.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the **resource or labor in motion**: goods, land, labor time, prestige, food, etc.
2. Ask: **Who is the moving agent — a kinship/peer group, a central authority, or independent buyers and sellers?**
   - If **kinship or peer network** (no central coordinator, symmetric relationship expected) → Reciprocity.
   - If **a single central authority** (inflow to a center, then allocation downward) → Redistribution.
   - If **independent parties negotiating price** → Market.
3. Ask: **Is the value fixed by custom, by decree, or by negotiation?**
   - Custom (unwritten, mutual obligation) → Reciprocity.
   - Decree (authority sets rules) → Redistribution.
   - Negotiation / price mechanism → Market.
4. If the transaction mixes elements (e.g., a wage is set by negotiation but paid by a state employer), identify which form is **dominant** — which organizing principle would reassert itself if the other were removed?
5. State the classification and note that most real economies are **mixed** — different sectors (food, land, labor) may operate under different forms.

## Implications per Category

| Form | Social structure | Decision logic | Resilience & Limits |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reciprocity** | Kinship, peer networks, ritual groups | Mutual obligation, custom, status | Scales poorly beyond the group; depends on face-to-face trust and common identity. Highly resilient within group, vulnerable to scale. |
| **Redistribution** | Hierarchical, state or temple authority | Central authority allocates by rank, need, or decree | Efficient at mobilizing large resources for collective projects; vulnerable to corruption and loss of authority legitimacy. |
| **Market** | Independent parties, price negotiation | Supply, demand, self-interest | Highly scalable and efficient for information aggregation; vulnerable to monopoly, externalities, and inequality; requires rule of law and trust in price signals. |

A real economy uses all three: families reciprocate (Reciprocity), states tax and provide services (Redistribution), and firms and individuals trade (Market). The **problem** arises when the wrong form is imposed on a domain that historically used another — e.g., trying to commodify kinship or to centrally plan a market sector.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Market mistaken for Redistribution.** A monopolist or dominant state-owned firm may set prices unilaterally. The tell: is there an opportunity to negotiate or exit (→ Market) or is the price a decree? Ambiguity arises when the state subsidizes or regulates prices.
- **Reciprocity mistaken for Redistribution.** A chief who collects tribute may appear to be a central authority, but if the chief's legitimacy depends on **generosity and symmetry** (the chief redistributes more than collected), it is closer to Reciprocity. Tell: is refusal to contribute socially ostracizing (Reciprocity) or legally penalized (Redistribution)?
- **Market mistaken for Reciprocity.** A long-term supplier relationship may feel reciprocal, but if either party can exit without social penalty and prices are negotiated, it is Market. Tell: would the relationship survive if one party no longer had economic interest?
- **Imposing Market logic on Reciprocal systems.** Commodifying a gift economy (e.g., paying for care work that was once reciprocal kin labor) may accelerate the system's collapse, because the **obligation structure** is destroyed. The tell is a sharp drop in provision of the service after market entry.
- **Conflating formal with actual.** A state may decree that labor is bought and sold (Market form), but if wages are set by authority and mobility is restricted, it is closer to Redistribution (or worse). Formal rules ≠ actual organizing principle.

# Phase Operations (0-V)

## Overview

Phase Operations is a sense-making frame that sorts a military campaign or strategic operation into one of six phases based on the primary objective and the nature of the actor's engagement with the adversary and environment. Its core claim is that each phase calls for **structurally different force postures, targeting priorities, and rules of engagement** — and confusing the objectives of one phase with another (e.g., attempting stabilization tactics during active seizure operations) is a systematic source of resource waste and mission failure. The act of classifying an operation into a phase makes explicit what the campaign is *for* at this moment, and thus which playbooks and constraints are appropriate.

## Categories

1. **Phase 0: Shape**
   - Primary objective is to **establish favorable conditions** before active conflict, through military presence, training, partnerships, and information operations.
   - The adversary is not yet directly engaged; the goal is deterrence, relationship-building, and early warning.
   - Discriminating criterion: no sustained kinetic operations; force deployment is visible and intended to influence perception and build capability in partner nations.
   - Example: forward military presence in allied territory, military-to-military training with partner forces, freedom-of-navigation operations.

2. **Phase I: Deter**
   - Primary objective is to **prevent or delay adversary action** through credible threat of force, positioning, and strategic messaging.
   - Kinetic engagement is limited and responsive; the aim is to raise the cost of aggression without triggering major conflict.
   - Discriminating criterion: force posture is defensive or demonstrative; rules of engagement permit response but not initiative; messaging is explicit about red lines.
   - Example: air and naval presence along a contested border; staged military exercises signaling capability and resolve.

3. **Phase II: Seize**
   - Primary objective is to **gain initiative and control** through rapid, concentrated offensive operations against the adversary's military capability and key terrain.
   - Kinetic operations are sustained and decisive; the focus is on speed and mass to prevent adversary counter-response.
   - Discriminating criterion: rules of engagement permit and require offensive action; targeting prioritizes high-value military assets and command nodes; maneuver is deep and rapid.
   - Example: opening days of a major theater war, airpower campaign against air defenses and command centers, amphibious assault on defended coastline.

4. **Phase III: Dominate**
   - Primary objective is to **consolidate control** over the adversary's territory and neutralize residual military resistance.
   - Kinetic operations continue but shift from rapid maneuver to sustained presence and area control; the focus is on preventing regrouping and enforcing compliance.
   - Discriminating criterion: adversary conventional military capability is largely destroyed or dispersed; operations become distributed and lower-intensity; supply lines and occupation logistics dominate planning.
   - Example: occupation of major cities and infrastructure, counter-insurgency operations against remaining organized resistance, establishment of checkpoints and perimeter control.

5. **Phase IV: Stabilize**
   - Primary objective is to **restore order and prepare for transition** away from military primacy toward civilian and host-nation authority.
   - Kinetic operations are episodic and defensive; the focus shifts to civil-military cooperation, reconstruction, and restoration of governance.
   - Discriminating criterion: major combat operations are concluded; adversary is no longer in organized military form; military acts in support of civil administration, humanitarian assistance, and institutional rebuilding.
   - Example: peacekeeping patrols, protection of reconstruction teams, training and arming of host-nation security forces, enforcement of curfews and civil order.

6. **Phase V: Enable**
   - Primary objective is to **support host-nation self-sufficiency and regional stability** through training, equipment provision, and eventual force withdrawal.
   - Military engagement is training-focused and advisory; kinetic operations are limited to self-defense or in direct support of host-nation requests.
   - Discriminating criterion: host-nation institutions are functioning; foreign military presence is transitional; exit strategy and drawdown timeline are explicit.
   - Example: embedded military advisors with host-nation units, equipment handover and training pipelines, gradual reduction of forward-deployed forces, intelligence-sharing agreements.

## Classification Procedure

1. Write a one-paragraph statement of the current military situation: what is the adversary's capability and posture, what is the status of host-nation institutions, and what is the primary theater objective *right now*.
2. Ask **"Is the adversary currently engaged in organized military operations against our forces?"**
   - If **no**, go to step 3.
   - If **yes**, go to step 4.
3. Ask **"Do partner-nation institutions and security forces exist and function?"**
   - If **no or minimally** → Phase 0 (Shape) or Phase I (Deter), depending on whether conflict is imminent.
   - If **yes and strengthening** → Phase IV (Stabilize) or Phase V (Enable), depending on whether military presence is still primary.
4. Ask **"Does the adversary control significant territory or maintain centralized command?"**
   - If **yes** → Phase II (Seize) if we are initiating offensive operations, or Phase III (Dominate) if we are consolidating control after initial offensive.
   - If **no** → Phase III (Dominate) if organized resistance remains, or Phase IV (Stabilize) if resistance is scattered and decentralized.
5. State the phase classification in writing and name the **primary resource constraint and operational focus** implied by the phase (see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Phase | Primary Objective | Force Posture | Targeting Priority | Logistics Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **0: Shape** | Establish favorable conditions; deter conflict initiation | Visible, non-threatening, partnership-oriented | Training, information operations, infrastructure | Sustainment of forward presence; training pipeline |
| **I: Deter** | Prevent or delay adversary action | Defensive, positioned on or near red lines | Command nodes, power projection capability, demonstration targets | Rapid response munitions; crisis resupply |
| **II: Seize** | Gain initiative and destroy adversary military | Concentrated, offensive, rapid maneuver | Air defense, command-and-control, early-warning systems, armor concentrations | Mass logistics for sustained campaign pace |
| **III: Dominate** | Consolidate control, prevent adversary regrouping | Distributed, area-denial, occupational | Residual military units, leaders, caches, resistance cells | Occupation infrastructure, civil-military operations |
| **IV: Stabilize** | Restore order, transition to civil authority | Mixed defensive and police-like, advisory-heavy | Spoiler organizations, weapons caches, governance threats | Reconstruction, humanitarian assistance, institutional capacity-building |
| **V: Enable** | Support host-nation self-sufficiency | Advisory, training, episodic presence | Threats to host-nation stability, external interference | Sustainment of host-nation forces, training logistics |

For a strategic planning agent, the practical implication is which **operational design template and constraint set** to load:
- Phase 0 → partnership-building template; presence and messaging are primary levers.
- Phase I → deterrence signaling template; credibility and cost imposition dominate.
- Phase II → rapid-maneuver template; speed and concentration of force are critical; supply risk is high.
- Phase III → area-control template; distributed operations and civil-military coordination are primary.
- Phase IV → transition template; civil authority and security-force training are the measure of success.
- Phase V → advisory template; host-nation leadership and force autonomy are the measure of success.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Seize mistaken for Dominate.** Continuing to use rapid-maneuver force posture and aggressive targeting after the adversary's conventional military is destroyed. The tell is that supply lines are stretched, casualties from attrition rise, and local resistance hardens rather than disperses. Remedy: shift to area-denial and occupation posture.
- **Dominate mistaken for Stabilize.** Applying occupation-force tactics when host-nation institutions have already begun to function. The tell is that the military is substituting for civil authority and creating dependency rather than enabling transition. Remedy: accelerate handoff to civil administration and host-nation security forces.
- **Stabilize mistaken for Enable.** Remaining in a primary military role long after the requirement has become advisory. The tell is that withdrawal timelines are constantly extended and host-nation forces remain unable to operate independently. Remedy: explicitly set exit criteria and drawdown timeline.
- **Deter mistaken for Shape.** Deploying forces as if only presence is required, when an adversary is actively preparing to attack. The tell is that the adversary's military capabilities continue to concentrate unopposed. Remedy: shift messaging and rules of engagement to make the threat of force credible and costly.
- **Classifying the whole operation when different regions or objectives belong to different phases.** A theater-level operation often contains multiple simultaneous phases — seize in one sector, stabilize in another, enable in a third. Misclassifying the whole prevents the granularity needed for resource allocation and rules of engagement.

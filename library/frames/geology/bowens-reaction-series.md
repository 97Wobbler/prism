# Bowen's Reaction Series

## Overview

Bowen's Reaction Series sorts minerals into two interdependent crystallization sequences — **discontinuous** and **continuous** — based on temperature and composition. Its core claim is that as a melt cools, minerals do not crystallize randomly or all at once; instead, they precipitate in a predictable order, with earlier minerals partially or completely reacting with the remaining liquid to form new minerals. Understanding which sequence applies to a cooling magma and where in that sequence crystallization has progressed allows a geologist to infer the thermal history, composition, and likely mineral assemblage of an igneous rock.

## Categories

1. **Discontinuous Series (Ferromagnesian / Mafic Sequence)**
   - Olivine → Pyroxene → Amphibole → Biotite (and related silicates rich in iron and magnesium).
   - Definition: a temperature-dependent sequence in which each new mineral crystallizes at a lower temperature and the previous mineral reacts with the remaining liquid to transform into the next mineral.
   - Discriminating criterion: the mineral is iron-magnesium rich, crystallizes in isolation (not in solid solution with others), and is replaced by a chemically and structurally distinct successor as temperature drops.
   - Example: olivine is the first ferromagnesian mineral to crystallize from a basaltic melt; as temperature drops, olivine crystals react with the melt to form pyroxene, which later transforms to amphibole.

2. **Continuous Series (Plagioclase Feldspar Sequence)**
   - Anorthite (Ca-rich) → Oligoclase → Albite (Na-rich).
   - Definition: a temperature-dependent sequence in which composition changes continuously rather than discretely; earlier crystals remain stable but their internal composition shifts as temperature drops.
   - Discriminating criterion: the mineral is a plagioclase feldspar, forms a solid solution, and earlier crystals partially equilibrate with the liquid by diffusion, shifting from Ca-rich to Na-rich composition.
   - Example: an anorthite crystal that crystallizes early at high temperature will have Ca-rich cores that gradually become more Na-rich toward the rims as the melt cools.

3. **Orthoclase / Potassium Feldspar (Late-Stage / Final Crystallize)**
   - Definition: a silicate mineral rich in potassium that crystallizes at lower temperatures, after much of the olivine–pyroxene–amphibole series and plagioclase series have already crystallized.
   - Discriminating criterion: the mineral is potassium-rich, does not participate in solid solution with plagioclase, and appears only after significant cooling and fractional crystallization.
   - Example: orthoclase phenocrysts in granitic rocks, typically appearing in the final stages of cooling alongside quartz.

4. **Quartz (Latest-Stage Crystallize)**
   - Definition: the last major silicate mineral to crystallize from a cooling magma, present only in silica-rich (evolved / differentiated) melts.
   - Discriminating criterion: the mineral is pure silicon dioxide, crystallizes only after most other phases are solid, and indicates the magma was initially rich enough in silica to exhaust it only near the solidus.
   - Example: quartz is absent from basalts (silica-poor) but abundant in granites (silica-rich), and always appears as the final major crystalline phase.

5. **Immiscible Liquids / Pegmatitic Phase**
   - Definition: a late-stage, low-temperature assemblage (sometimes chemically distinct from the main crystal sequence) that includes rare minerals, volatiles, and elements incompatible with earlier phases.
   - Discriminating criterion: the phase crystallizes at very low temperature, often in large crystals (pegmatitic texture), may be immiscible from the main melt, and contains elements (rare earths, volatiles, incompatible trace elements) that are excluded from earlier minerals.
   - Example: pegmatite dikes containing tourmaline, topaz, and beryl; late-stage fluorite or apatite in a granitic system.

## Classification Procedure

1. Obtain a hand sample or thin section of the igneous rock and identify all visible minerals.
2. For each mineral, determine whether it is:
   - **Iron-magnesium rich** (olivine, pyroxene, amphibole, biotite) → belongs to Discontinuous Series.
   - **Plagioclase feldspar** with visible compositional zonation (Ca-rich core, Na-rich rim) or a range of compositions → belongs to Continuous Series.
   - **Potassium feldspar** (orthoclase, microcline) without plagioclase zonation → likely late-stage orthoclase.
   - **Quartz** → latest-stage crystallize (only in differentiated / felsic melts).
   - **Rare or volatile-bearing** (tourmaline, topaz, fluorite, apatite) → pegmatitic phase.
3. If the rock contains both olivine and pyroxene, with olivine showing reaction texture (embayed or resorbed edges), the olivine crystallized first (higher temperature) and reacted to form pyroxene as temperature dropped; both belong to Discontinuous Series.
4. If plagioclase shows compositional zoning, measure or estimate the composition range to confirm the continuous change from anorthite-rich to albite-rich.
5. Reconstruct the **crystallization order** by placing minerals along both sequences in temperature rank. The full sequence (from high to low temperature) is:
   - Olivine, Pyroxene, Amphibole, Biotite (Discontinuous)
   - Anorthite → Oligoclase → Albite (Continuous, partly overlapping with Discontinuous)
   - Orthoclase (late)
   - Quartz (latest, if present)
   - Pegmatitic phases (latest)

## Implications per Category

| Category | What it tells you | Inferences |
|---|---|---|
| **Discontinuous Series** | These minerals crystallized during early cooling and reacted as temperature dropped. | The magma was mafic (iron-magnesium rich); the presence of biotite instead of amphibole indicates slightly lower final temperature; reaction textures show disequilibrium with later cooling. |
| **Continuous Series** | Plagioclase crystallized progressively, with composition tracking the melt evolution. | Zoning in plagioclase records the thermal and compositional path; Ca-rich cores indicate early, high-temperature crystallization; Na-rich rims indicate later, lower-temperature growth. |
| **Orthoclase** | Crystallized after plagioclase saturation had dropped. | The magma had cooled significantly and differentiated; potassium had accumulated in the remaining liquid. |
| **Quartz** | Crystallized only after most silicates were solid. | The magma was silica-saturated and evolved; quartz presence confirms a granitic or similar felsic parentage. |
| **Pegmatitic Phase** | Crystallized at very low temperature from a chemically distinct, often volatile-rich melt. | Late-stage fractional crystallization or immiscible liquid separation; large crystal size indicates slow cooling and low viscosity. |

For a geologist, the practical implication is reconstruction of the **cooling path** and **degree of fractional crystallization**: the further along the series a rock has progressed (olivine present vs. olivine absent vs. quartz present), the more differentiated the magma was and the longer it cooled at accessible shallow depths.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Pyroxene mistaken for Amphibole without texture context.** Both are silicates with similar compositions, but pyroxene crystallizes *before* amphibole. The tell is cleavage angle (pyroxene ≈90°, amphibole ≈120°) and reaction texture on pyroxene rims. The consequence is incorrectly estimated crystallization temperature.
- **Plagioclase composition mistaken as two distinct minerals.** A single plagioclase feldspar ranges from anorthite (Ca-rich, high-temperature core) to albite (Na-rich, low-temperature rim). The tell is optical continuous zoning under the microscope; treating them as separate minerals overstates mineralogical diversity.
- **Orthoclase in the Continuous Series.** Orthoclase does not form a solid solution with plagioclase at equilibrium; it crystallizes *after* the continuous series has largely finished. The tell is the absence of intermediate compositions bridging plagioclase and orthoclase.
- **Quartz absence mistaken for Undersaturation.** Not all igneous rocks contain quartz; basalts and other mafic rocks are silica-undersaturated by design. The error is concluding a rock is quartz-poor because crystallization was incomplete, when in fact the parent magma composition never allowed quartz. Context: does the rock contain olivine? If yes, quartz is impossible (olivine + quartz are incompatible).
- **Reaction textures ignored as evidence of order.** A corroded or resorbed olivine surrounded by pyroxene is direct evidence that olivine crystallized first and reacted with the cooling melt. Mistaking the texture for disequilibrium or exsolution leads to backwards reconstruction of the cooling path.

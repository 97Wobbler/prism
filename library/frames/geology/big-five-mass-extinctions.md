# Big Five Mass Extinctions

## Overview

The Big Five Mass Extinctions frame sorts major biodiversity crises of the
Phanerozoic Eon (the last ~541 million years) into five distinct events,
each characterized by a unique **causal driver, taxonomic severity pattern,
and recovery timescale**. Its core claim is that each extinction event
reflects a different planetary stress mechanism — from ocean anoxia to
impact — and that the causal signature determines which taxa are vulnerable
and how long recovery takes. Sorting an extinction into its category reveals
what kind of planetary condition produced it and what lessons it holds for
understanding modern biodiversity loss.

## Categories

1. **Ordovician-Silurian Extinction** (~445 million years ago)
   - A two-pulse extinction killing roughly 85% of marine species, driven
     primarily by **rapid glaciation and sea-level drop**.
   - Discriminating criterion: glaciogenic (ice-sheet driven) with sharp
     habitat loss in shallow marine ecosystems; rapid recovery once
     climate stabilized.
   - Example: trilobites and brachiopods decimated; shallow-shelf
     communities restructured.

2. **Late Devonian Extinction** (~375–360 million years ago)
   - A protracted, multi-pulse extinction killing ~75% of marine species,
     driven by **ocean anoxia, sea-level fluctuations, and possible impact
     events**.
   - Discriminating criterion: prolonged oxygen depletion in oceans;
     affects reef ecosystems disproportionately; recovery over millions
     of years.
   - Example: reef collapse, loss of armored fish (placoderms), major
     turnover in ammonoid lineages.

3. **Permian-Triassic Extinction** (~252 million years ago)
   - The most severe extinction in Earth's history, killing ~96% of
     marine species and ~70% of terrestrial vertebrate species, driven by
     **massive volcanism (Siberian Traps), ocean anoxia, and extreme
     climate change**.
   - Discriminating criterion: global oxygen depletion, acidification,
     and temperature extremes; extends across all environments; recovery
     requires 5–10 million years.
   - Example: nearly total loss of reef ecosystems, ammonoid near-extinction,
     synapsid dominance in terrestrial realms interrupted.

4. **Triassic-Jurassic Extinction** (~201 million years ago)
   - An extinction killing ~76% of marine species and ~80% of terrestrial
     species, driven by **prolonged volcanism (Central Atlantic Magmatic
     Province), rapid climate change, and sea-level rise**.
   - Discriminating criterion: warm-water tropical ecosystems hit hardest;
     coastal and reef-dependent lineages decimated; dinosaurs become
     dominant during recovery.
   - Example: loss of large amphibians and most marine reptiles; opening
     for dinosaurian radiation.

5. **Cretaceous-Paleogene Extinction** (~66 million years ago)
   - A sudden, global extinction killing ~75% of species including all
     non-avian dinosaurs, driven by **bolide (asteroid) impact near the
     Yucatán Peninsula, combined with Deccan Traps volcanism**.
   - Discriminating criterion: impact signature (iridium layer, shocked
     quartz, crater); rapid darkness and cold; ecological collapse within
     years to decades; immediate post-impact recovery over millennia.
   - Example: end of the Mesozoic Era, loss of large vertebrates,
     emergence of mammalian and avian radiation.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the age (geological period) and radiometric dating of the
   extinction event in question.
2. Determine the **primary causal driver**: glaciation → Ordovician;
   sustained anoxia → Late Devonian; massive volcanism + anoxia → Permian;
   volcanism + climate → Triassic; impact → Cretaceous.
3. Examine the **severity pattern** (percentage of species lost) and
   which ecological niches are hit hardest. Reef ecosystems are sensitive
   to Late Devonian and Triassic events; tropical and coastal organisms
   are most vulnerable to Triassic and Cretaceous impacts.
4. Assess the **recovery timescale**: rapid (millions of years) for
   impact-driven (Cretaceous); protracted (5–10+ million years) for
   anoxic or volcanism-driven events.
5. Confirm by checking the **taxonomic signature** — which major clades
   went extinct or restructured — against the known event record for that
   time interval.
6. State the classification and note its causal implication (see
   Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Event | Primary Driver | Key Ecological Impact | Recovery Duration | Modern Analogue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ordovician-Silurian** | Glaciation & sea-level drop | Shallow marine habitat loss | ~1–2 million years | Rapid climate-driven habitat shrinkage |
| **Late Devonian** | Ocean anoxia & impacts | Reef ecosystem collapse | ~5–10 million years | Sustained hypoxia (dead zones) |
| **Permian-Triassic** | Volcanism + anoxia + warming | Global ecosystem near-collapse | ~5–10 million years | Combined climate & chemical stress |
| **Triassic-Jurassic** | Volcanism + climate change | Tropical ecosystem disruption | ~2–3 million years | Rapid warming + CO₂ rise |
| **Cretaceous-Paleogene** | Bolide impact + volcanism | Acute light/temperature collapse | ~1–2 million years (initial); ~10 my (full recovery) | Sudden global shock followed by recovery cascade |

For a paleontological agent, the practical implication is which **analytical
lens** to load:
- Glaciation-driven → examine sea-level curves and ice-core proxies.
- Anoxia-driven → analyze ocean redox chemistry and sediment trace metals.
- Impact-driven → verify impact markers (iridium, shocked quartz, crater
  geometry).
- Volcanism-driven → measure large igneous province (LIP) flux and
  carbon-cycle disruption.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Cretaceous-Paleogene mistaken for volcanism-driven.** The event is
  impact-primary with secondary volcanism; the tell is the global
  iridium layer and rapid ecological collapse over days to years, not
  gradual stress over millennia. Treating it as purely volcanic overstates
  the role of the Deccan Traps and misses the impact's causal weight.

- **Late Devonian mistaken for a single pulse.** It is a series of
  staggered pulses over ~15 million years, not a point event. The tell is
  multiple extinction horizons in stratigraphic sections; misclassifying it
  as a single pulse obscures the prolonged stress (anoxia) driving it.

- **Permian-Triassic mistaken for impact-driven.** There is no credible
  impact marker; the tell is massive Siberian volcanism, global anoxia,
  and extreme warming over millions of years. No iridium layer exists; the
  event is volcanism + ocean chemistry.

- **Ordovician-Silurian mistaken for anoxia-driven.** It is glaciation +
  sea-level drop. The tell is rapid ice-sheet expansion and shallow-shelf
  habitat loss, not global anoxia. Anoxia does occur but is secondary.

- **Triassic-Jurassic mistaken for impact-driven.** No credible impact
  marker exists; massive volcanism (CAMP) is the primary driver. The tell
  is a long-duration LIP eruption, not a sudden crater or iridium layer.

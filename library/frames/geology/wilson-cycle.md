# Wilson Cycle

## Overview

The Wilson Cycle sorts the geometric and kinematic state of continents and ocean basins into six successive stages that describe how supercontinents break apart and reassemble over geological time. Its core claim is that **continental configurations are not random but follow a predictable sequence**, and identifying which stage is present in a given region constrains plate motion, subsidence patterns, and resource-formation potential. Different stages exhibit structurally distinct tectonic signatures — rift basins do not behave like passive margins, which do not behave like collision zones — so proper stage identification determines which geological and geodynamic models are appropriate.

## Categories

1. **Cratons (Stable Continents)**
   - Large, old continental blocks characterized by **thick, cold lithosphere** with minimal internal deformation.
   - Interior is relatively quiescent; most tectonic activity is at the margins.
   - Discriminating criterion: absence of rifting, volcanism, or seismic activity in the continental interior; high thermal age and seismic velocity anomalies in the mantle.
   - Example: the Canadian Shield, the Kalahari Craton.

2. **Rifting (Continental Breakup)**
   - Continents are **actively stretching and thinning** in response to mantle upwelling or lithospheric instability.
   - Characterized by normal faults, rift basins, and incipient oceanic crust formation.
   - Discriminating criterion: presence of fault-bounded sedimentary basins, elevated heat flow, mafic magmatism, and mechanically coherent extensional structures.
   - Example: the modern East African Rift, the North Atlantic in the Triassic–Jurassic.

3. **Passive Margin (Drifting)**
   - Continental margins have **become passively thermally cooled** following successful continental separation; new oceanic crust forms at a spreading center between diverging continents.
   - Margins are characterized by thick terrigenous sediment wedges and thermal subsidence.
   - Discriminating criterion: asymmetric sedimentary architecture, absence of compressive structures, preservation of relict rift geometry, and age-distance relationships consistent with seafloor spreading.
   - Example: the Atlantic margins of North America and Europe today; the Indo-Pacific margins during much of the Mesozoic.

4. **Continental Collision (Orogeny)**
   - **Oceanic basin closure** and collision of two continental blocks initiates crustal shortening, thickening, and large-scale uplifting.
   - Characterized by fold-and-thrust belts, suturing, and metamorphic complexes.
   - Discriminating criterion: presence of compressive stress regimes, ophiolite obduction, syn-collisional magmatism, and double-thickness continental crust in seismic profiles.
   - Example: the modern Himalayas (India–Asia collision); the Paleozoic Caledonian Orogeny (Baltica–Laurentia).

5. **Obduction and Suturing**
   - Oceanic lithosphere is **thrust onto continental margins** as a collisional byproduct; suture zones form where oceanic crust is consumed and continental margins are welded together.
   - Characterized by obducted ophiolites, high-pressure metamorphic rocks, and crustal-scale faults.
   - Discriminating criterion: presence of exotic oceanic assemblages in continental settings, high-P/low-T or high-T/high-P metamorphic mineral assemblages, and discontinuous crustal structure.
   - Example: the Oman ophiolite on the Arabian margin; the Pan-African sutures in North Africa.

6. **Terminal Collision and Plate Integration**
   - Continental blocks are **fully integrated into a single plate**, with collision-related deformation localized at the new suture zone.
   - Post-collisional adjustment may include strike-slip faulting, extension, and epigenetic magmatism.
   - Discriminating criterion: cessation of shortening across the main collision zone, transition to strike-slip or oblique motion along suture, and spatial migration of magmatism away from the collision axis.
   - Example: the Appalachian orogen after the late Paleozoic; East Africa today (incorporating newly sutured blocks along rifts).

## Classification Procedure

1. **Gather the following data** for the region or basin in question:
   - Present-day plate motion vectors (GPS or GNSS data, or paleomagnetic apparent polar wander).
   - Subsurface architecture: seismic reflection and refraction profiles, gravity and magnetic anomalies, heat-flow measurements.
   - Surface deformation pattern: fault geometry, fold geometry, topographic relief.
   - Lithological associations: rift-fill sediments, ophiolites, metamorphic grade, volcanics.
   - Age constraints: radiometric dating, biostratigraphy, paleomagnetic chron assignment.

2. **Ask: "Is oceanic crust present between the continents?"**
   - If **no oceanic crust and margins are diverging** → Rifting (stage 2).
   - If **no oceanic crust and margins are converging** → check for crustal thickening and metamorphism (go to step 3).
   - If **oceanic crust is widening** → Passive Margin (stage 3).
   - If **oceanic crust is closing or consumed** → go to step 3.

3. **Ask: "Is evidence of collision (crustal thickening, suturing) present?"**
   - If **no collision signals and continents are stable** → Cratons (stage 1).
   - If **collision structures present but oceanic relics visible** → Obduction and Suturing (stage 5).
   - If **collision is ongoing with significant topography and metamorphism** → Continental Collision (stage 4).
   - If **collision structures are stabilized and post-collisional adjustment is underway** → Terminal Collision (stage 6).

4. **Integrate temporal information.** Assign a stage based on both present-day geometry and the age and kinematic history of the structures (e.g., a "young" rift in a 1–5 Ma history differs from an ancient inactive rift).

5. **State the stage in writing and name the expected tectonic regime and subsidence pattern** (see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Category | Tectonic Regime | Basin Evolution | Resource Potential |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cratons** | Intracontinental stability, slow subsidence or uplift. | Passive thermal sag; thin sediment accumulation if any. | Stable platform for mineral exploration; limited petroleum. |
| **Rifting** | Extension, elevated heat flow, normal faulting. | Fault-bounded grabens and half-grabens; rapid syn-rift sediment accumulation. | High hydrocarbon potential (source rocks, traps); mineral deposits in syn-rift volcanism. |
| **Passive Margin** | Thermal subsidence, gentle seafloor spreading. | Prograding sediment wedges; thick clastic and carbonate sequences. | World-class petroleum systems; salt diapirism common. |
| **Continental Collision** | Shortening, crustal thickening, high heat and pressure. | Foreland basin development; flysch-molasse sequences. | Metamorphic ore deposits; fold-and-thrust-hosted petroleum. |
| **Obduction and Suturing** | Transcurrent motion; localized compression. | Ophiolite emplacement; melanges and high-P assemblages. | Base-metal deposits in ophiolites; platinum-group elements. |
| **Terminal Collision** | Mixed (shortening + strike-slip + local extension). | Post-orogenic magmatism; strike-slip basins. | Epithermal and porphyry deposits; secondary petroleum generation. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Passive Margin mistaken for Rifting.** A thinned continental margin with preserved rift geometry can appear "rifted" in seismic sections, but the key tell is whether the margin is thermally cooling (passive, no extension now) or actively extending (rifting, ongoing extension). Heat-flow and strain-rate data distinguish them.

- **Continental Collision mistaken for Obduction.** A thrust-faulted margin with ophiolite slivers can appear to be full collision. The tell: in true collision, crustal thickening beneath the continents is massive and symmetric in high-pressure metamorphics; in obduction, oceanic thicknesses are modest and the continental basement is largely unmetamorphosed.

- **Cratons mistaken for Passive Margin.** Old, stable continental crust can have a blanket of thin sediments that resembles passive margin architecture. The tell: Cratons lack the systematic age-distance relationship of oceanic basement and show no thermal subsidence trend; passive margins have both.

- **Terminal Collision mistaken for Cratons.** A sutured and now-stable collisional belt can appear cratonized. The tell: the suture zone itself retains kinematic markers (pseudotachylytes, S-C fabrics, metamorphic grade contrasts); true Cratons are internally homogeneous.

- **Classifying based on topography alone.** High mountains can indicate ongoing collision or recent uplift in a rift or passive margin; low topography can mask active rifting in a region of thick lithosphere. Seismic velocity structure and heat flow are better discriminators than relief.

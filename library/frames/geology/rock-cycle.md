# Rock Cycle

## Overview

The Rock Cycle sorts rocks into three primary categories based on their **origin and the dominant geological process** that created or transformed them. Its core claim is that understanding which category a rock belongs to explains **which physical and chemical processes shaped it** — and predicts which processes it will undergo next. Rocks are not static: they are continuously transformed by heat, pressure, erosion, and chemical weathering. Sorting a specimen into its category reveals both its past and constrains its future behavior, making it essential for stratigraphic interpretation, resource exploration, and understanding landscape evolution.

## Categories

1. **Igneous Rock**
   - Formed by **solidification of molten magma or lava**, either beneath the surface (intrusive) or at it (extrusive).
   - Composition is determined by magma chemistry and cooling rate; faster cooling produces smaller crystals or glass.
   - Discriminating criterion: crystalline or glassy texture; no layering or fossils; minerals are interlocking and formed simultaneously.
   - Example: granite (intrusive), basalt (extrusive), obsidian (volcanic glass).

2. **Sedimentary Rock**
   - Formed by **lithification of sediment** — particles broken down by weathering and erosion, transported by water or wind, then compacted and cemented together.
   - Contains evidence of transport and deposition: layering, sorting, rounding of grains, fossils, cross-bedding.
   - Discriminating criterion: visible clastic texture (sand, silt, clay grains bound together); may contain fossils or chemical precipitates (salt, limestone); usually lighter in color than igneous rocks.
   - Example: sandstone, shale, limestone, conglomerate.

3. **Metamorphic Rock**
   - Formed by **solid-state transformation** of existing rock under conditions of high temperature and/or pressure, deep within the crust, without melting.
   - Original minerals recrystallize into new minerals stable at the new conditions; may show banding, foliation, or aligned crystals.
   - Discriminating criterion: banded or foliated texture (layers of different minerals); original rock texture is obscured or replaced; no visible magma origin; no fossils (heat destroys them).
   - Example: slate (from shale), marble (from limestone), gneiss (from granite or sedimentary protolith), schist.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Examine the texture and structure.** Is the rock composed of interlocking crystals (possibly of different sizes) with no layering? → likely Igneous. Are there visible layers, sand/silt grains, or fossils? → likely Sedimentary. Are there bands of different minerals, foliation, or evidence that the original texture has been destroyed? → likely Metamorphic.

2. **Look for visible clues to origin.**
   - Presence of **fossils** strongly suggests Sedimentary (metamorphism destroys fossils).
   - Presence of **large crystals (phenocrysts) in a fine-grained matrix** suggests Igneous (rapid cooling of magma).
   - Presence of **banding or foliation** — alternating light and dark mineral layers, or visible alignment of crystals — suggests Metamorphic.

3. **Test the grain size and uniformity.**
   - Uniform, interlocking crystals of similar or graduated size → Igneous.
   - Visible discrete grains (sand, pebbles) loosely bound → Sedimentary.
   - Crystals organized into visible layers or bands → Metamorphic.

4. **If still ambiguous, test hardness and weathering pattern.** Sedimentary rocks often weather more readily and may break along bedding planes. Metamorphic rocks are typically hard and break across foliation. Igneous rocks are hard and break without a preferred direction (unless jointed).

5. **State the rock type and the implied transformation pathway** (see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Category | Formation process | What happens next |
|---|---|---|
| **Igneous** | Cooling of magma at depth or surface. | Exposed to surface → weathered and eroded into sediment. Or buried deep → heated and pressured → metamorphic. |
| **Sedimentary** | Compaction and cementation of weathered particles. | Buried deep → heated and pressured → metamorphic. Or exposed → eroded. Or subducted into mantle → melted → magma → igneous. |
| **Metamorphic** | Solid-state transformation under heat/pressure. | Exposed to surface → weathered and eroded into sediment. Or heated further → partial melting → magma → igneous. |

For a lenslab agent, the practical implication is which **analytical lens** applies:
- **Igneous** → mineralogy lenses (identify minerals, infer cooling rate, estimate depth and pressure of formation).
- **Sedimentary** → depositional environment lenses (interpret grain size, sorting, cross-bedding, and fossils to reconstruct paleoenvironment).
- **Metamorphic** → pressure-temperature lenses (estimate the depth and temperature of metamorphism; identify the protolith).

## Common Misclassifications

- **Metamorphic mistaken for Igneous.** A banded gneiss (metamorphic) can resemble coarse granite (igneous) if foliation is not visible under low magnification. The tell is foliation or banding; true granite shows random crystal orientation. Consequence: misinterpreting the thermal history and depth of burial.

- **Sedimentary mistaken for Metamorphic.** A hard, indurated (well-compacted) sandstone or slate can resemble metamorphic rock. The tell is the presence of individual grains visible under magnification, or occasional fossils, neither of which occurs in metamorphic rock. Consequence: underestimating pressure-temperature history.

- **Igneous mistaken for Metamorphic.** A fine-grained porphyritic basalt with visible phenocrysts can be mistaken for a metamorphic rock with layering. The tell is that the phenocrysts are different sizes (not same-sized layers) and magmatic (not recrystallized). Consequence: misidentifying the cooling history and magma source.

- **Classifying a transitional or hybrid specimen.** Some rocks form at boundaries: a migmatite contains both igneous and metamorphic components (partial melting); some pyroclastic rocks (volcanic ash) are lithified into sedimentary-looking deposits. The tell is mixed texture (both crystalline and cemented grains). Consequence: picking the wrong single category; the correct move is to decompose the specimen into its components.

- **Confusing cooling rate with rock type.** A large, slow-cooled crystal is not necessarily metamorphic; it is likely Igneous (e.g., pegmatite). Metamorphic rocks are identified by banding and foliation, not by crystal size alone.

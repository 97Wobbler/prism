# Freytag's Pyramid

## Overview

Freytag's Pyramid is a structural frame that sorts a narrative into five sequential phases based on the **emotional and dramatic tension** at each point. Its core claim is that stories follow a recognizable arc — and that different phases of the arc have **fundamentally different narrative jobs** and require different kinds of dramatic work. A misplaced climax, undercooked rising action, or rushed denouement are often symptomatic of mis-sorting the boundaries between phases. The frame does not prescribe how to execute each phase; it simply makes the phases themselves visible and actionable.

## Categories

1. **Exposition**
   - The narrative **establishes the world, characters, and initial conditions** before significant tension arrives.
   - Function: introduce stakes, constraints, and the protagonist's ordinary state.
   - Discriminating criterion: the audience does not yet know what problem or goal drives the story forward.
   - Example: *The Hobbit* opening with Bilbo's comfortable life in the Shire before the dwarves arrive.

2. **Rising Action**
   - The narrative **introduces and escalates the central conflict**, raising stakes and deepening the protagonist's commitment or crisis.
   - Function: build toward the peak of tension by accumulating obstacles, revelations, or complications.
   - Discriminating criterion: each scene or beat compounds the problem or raises the cost of inaction; the protagonist moves steadily toward a point of no return.
   - Example: *Pride and Prejudice* from Elizabeth and Darcy's first meetings through misunderstandings that deepen mutual prejudice.

3. **Climax**
   - The narrative **reaches its point of maximum tension**, where the protagonist's central conflict is directly confronted and a decisive outcome becomes inevitable.
   - Function: deliver the irreversible moment where the protagonist acts (or fails to act) in a way that determines the story's ending.
   - Discriminating criterion: this is the moment of highest emotional intensity; after it, the outcome is no longer in doubt, only its elaboration.
   - Example: Luke Skywalker confronting Vader in *The Empire Strikes Back*, or Elizabeth Bennet refusing Mr. Darcy's first proposal.

4. **Falling Action**
   - The narrative **works out the consequences** of the climax, showing how the protagonist's decisive moment ripples through the world.
   - Function: release tension in a controlled way; tie up secondary threads and confirm the inevitability of the ending.
   - Discriminating criterion: the central conflict is resolved, but loose ends remain; forward momentum is present but the major question is answered.
   - Example: *The Lord of the Rings* after Frodo destroys the ring, as characters return home and the scope of victory becomes clear.

5. **Denouement** (also "Resolution" or "Conclusion")
   - The narrative **returns to equilibrium**, showing the new stable state after all consequences have settled.
   - Function: answer the final question "what does the world look like now?" and earn emotional closure.
   - Discriminating criterion: no new obstacles arise; this is the final image or reflection that the audience carries away.
   - Example: the epilogue of *Harry Potter* showing the characters years later, or the final scenes of *Casablanca*.

## Classification Procedure

1. Outline the story beat-by-beat in a single paragraph, naming the central conflict and the moment it is resolved.
2. Identify the **climactic moment** — the instant where the protagonist's choice or action determines the ending. Mark it explicitly.
3. Working backward from the climax, ask **"Does each earlier beat raise the stakes or add a necessary obstacle?"** If yes, it is Rising Action. If no, it is Exposition.
4. Working forward from the climax, ask **"Are we still resolving consequences of the climax?"** If yes, it is Falling Action. If not, it is Denouement.
5. Check the opening: is the protagonist's world, constraint, or goal already introduced? If not, you are still in Exposition. Once the reader understands what the protagonist must face, Exposition is complete.
6. State the boundaries between phases in terms of scenes, chapters, or time markers.

## Implications per Category

| Phase | Primary narrative task | What the writer should focus on |
|---|---|---|
| **Exposition** | Establish world, character, stakes, and the **question** the story will answer. | Make the ordinary state vivid and the initial constraint or desire clear; plant information the climax will depend on. |
| **Rising Action** | Escalate conflict and raise the cost of failure with each obstacle or complication. | Layer stakes; ensure obstacles feel earned, not arbitrary; show the protagonist's commitment deepening. |
| **Climax** | Deliver the irreversible moment where the protagonist faces the central conflict directly. | Make the choice or action **unmistakable and consequential**; this is the story's pivot point. |
| **Falling Action** | Show the ripple effects of the climax and confirm the inevitability of the ending. | Tie secondary threads; let the reader see the scope of what the protagonist's choice has set in motion. |
| **Denouement** | Return to equilibrium and deliver the final emotional beat. | Earn closure; answer lingering questions; show the protagonist in the new world their choice has created. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Exposition mistaken for Rising Action.** A long worldbuilding or character setup that delays introducing the central conflict. The tell is that fifty pages in, the reader still does not know what the protagonist *wants* or what stands in the way. Consequence: the reader loses urgency and pacing feels slack.

- **Rising Action mistaken for Climax.** A high-stakes obstacle or reversal that feels like the turning point but is followed by more major complications. The tell is that the outcome is still in doubt; the protagonist must act again. Consequence: the climax, when it comes, feels anticlimactic because tension was already released.

- **Climax mistaken for Falling Action.** A scene that resolves secondary conflicts but leaves the central question unanswered. The tell is that the protagonist still has a major choice or confrontation ahead. Consequence: the true climax lands after a release of tension, flattening the emotional arc.

- **Falling Action mistaken for Denouement.** Introducing new obstacles after the climax. The tell is that new consequences or reversals arise; the outcome is not yet settled. Consequence: the reader is re-engaged in conflict when the narrative should be in cooldown; pacing lurches.

- **Collapsing phases through compression.** In a very short story, all five phases may exist but be drastically shortened. The frame still applies — the question is whether each phase is *present*, not whether it occupies a proportional amount of page count. Misclassification happens when a compressed phase is invisible rather than brief.

# Three-Act Structure

## Overview

The Three-Act Structure sorts a narrative into three sequential phases based on the **functional role each section plays in the overall story arc**. Its core claim is that different acts call for **structurally different work** — Act One establishes stakes and asks a question; Act Two explores that question under pressure; Act Three answers it. A story that is structurally sound in all three acts holds attention; a story imbalanced in any act (too long an exposition, a sagging middle, a rushed resolution) creates predictable failure modes. Sorting a draft into acts makes visible which act is weak and what kind of revision it needs.

## Categories

1. **Act One: Setup**
   - Establishes the world, introduces the protagonist(s), and poses the central **question or problem** that will drive the narrative.
   - The act ends with an **inciting incident** — an event or choice that forces the protagonist into the central conflict.
   - Discriminating criterion: the protagonist has not yet committed fully to pursuing the central goal; exposition still dominates.
   - Example: in *The Wizard of Oz*, Dorothy is swept into Oz and meets the good witch, who tells her she must reach the Emerald City. In a detective story, the protagonist meets their client and takes the case.

2. **Act Two: Confrontation**
   - The protagonist actively pursues the goal posed in Act One, encountering **escalating obstacles and complications**.
   - This act typically contains rising action, subplot deepening, and mounting pressure. The protagonist's initial approach fails or proves inadequate.
   - Discriminating criterion: the stakes are clear, the protagonist is committed, and the question is no longer "Will they try?" but "Will they succeed?" and "At what cost?"
   - The act culminates in a **midpoint reversal or point of no return** — a moment that raises the stakes or changes the game fundamentally.
   - Example: Dorothy befriends the Scarecrow, Tin Man, and Lion while facing the Wicked Witch's obstacles. A detective faces dead ends, false leads, and pressure from external forces.

3. **Act Three: Resolution**
   - The protagonist faces the **final confrontation** or test, the central question is answered, and consequences are revealed.
   - This act contains the climax (the highest-stakes moment) and the denouement (the aftermath, which restores equilibrium and shows what has changed).
   - Discriminating criterion: the protagonist's fate is determined; the story world returns to a new stable state (even if that state is tragic or ironic).
   - Example: Dorothy defeats the Witch and returns home. A detective confronts the killer and either solves or closes the case.

## Classification Procedure

1. Write a one-paragraph summary of the narrative from beginning to end.
2. Identify the **inciting incident** — the event that forces the protagonist to commit to a goal or enter a conflict. Mark where in the narrative it occurs.
3. Identify the **climax** — the moment of highest stakes where the central question is answered or the protagonist's fate is sealed. Mark where it occurs.
4. Divide the narrative into three parts at these hinges:
   - Everything **before** the inciting incident is Act One.
   - Everything **between** the inciting incident and the climax is Act Two.
   - Everything **from** the climax onward is Act Three.
5. Verify the breakdown:
   - Act One: Does it establish the world and pose a clear question?
   - Act Two: Does it show sustained pursuit of the goal, with obstacles and escalation?
   - Act Three: Does it answer the question and show the new state of the world?
6. If any act is missing or extremely short (less than ~15% of total runtime/page count), flag it as structurally weak.

## Implications per Category

| Act | Primary work | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Act One (Setup)** | Establish world, protagonist, and stakes. Pose a clear question. End with inciting incident. | Invest enough time to make the world and protagonist credible; cut exposition that does not serve the question; ensure the inciting incident is *forced*, not chosen casually. |
| **Act Two (Confrontation)** | Escalate obstacles, deepen subplots, raise stakes. Sustain the protagonist's pursuit under mounting pressure. | Pace carefully — sagging middle is the most common failure; ensure obstacles are not repetitive; use a midpoint reversal to reset energy; do not resolve the central question yet. |
| **Act Three (Resolution)** | Execute the climax and show its consequences. Answer the question posed in Act One. | Make the climax earn all that came before it; avoid easy wins or deus ex machina; spend enough time on aftermath to show what the victory or defeat *cost*; end when the world is stable, not mid-action. |

For narrative diagnosis: Act One too long → exposition overload, reader loses patience before stakes are clear. Act Two too short → insufficient pressure, climax feels unearned. Act Three too long → the story ended; epilogue is redundant. Act missing → reader asks "why did this happen?" with no answer.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Act One mistaken for Act Two because the inciting incident is subtle.** The tell is that the protagonist is still choosing to pursue the goal (Act One behavior) rather than being forced to (Act Two commitment). Consequence: the reader is confused about what the story is actually about until well into Act Two.
- **The climax confused with a subplot peak.** A secondary character's problem is resolved, or a subplot reversal occurs, but the central question remains open. The tell is that the protagonist's fate is not yet determined. Consequence: Act Three begins too early; the real climax is mistaken for epilogue and rushed.
- **Act Two mistaken for two acts because a reversal is treated as an ending.** A protagonist's plan fails (a setback), but they immediately pursue a new plan (Act Two continues). The tell is that the central question is still unresolved. Consequence: false climax, sagging middle misdiagnosed as a structure problem when the real issue is pacing.
- **Act Three cut short or omitted entirely.** The climax is executed but the consequences are skipped; the story ends the moment the question is answered. The tell is "I wanted to know what happened to them next" or "I didn't feel satisfied." Consequence: reader feels robbed of the earned resolution.
- **Classifying a subplot as a separate three-act story when it should be integrated into the primary act structure.** A love story, a heist, or a revenge plot can each have its own shape, but if it runs parallel to the central story, its turning points must *reinforce* the central acts, not divide the narrative into false thirds.

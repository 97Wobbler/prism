# Hero's Journey (Campbell)

## Overview

The Hero's Journey is a narrative structure that sorts a story or personal experience into one of twelve archetypal stages, each representing a distinct psychological and dramatic transition. Campbell's core claim is that myths, legends, films, and individual life arcs follow a recognizable pattern — not because all storytellers copied it, but because the structure reflects how humans process transformation, risk, and growth. Different stages call for **different dramatic and emotional work** — the call to adventure plays a different role than the ordeal, which plays a different role than the return — and recognizing which stage an instance occupies constrains which narrative devices, character choices, and thematic explorations are appropriate.

## Categories

1. **The Ordinary World**
   - The hero's normal life before the story begins; the baseline against which all change will be measured.
   - Discriminating criterion: the hero is unaware of the adventure to come and operates within familiar social and physical constraints.
   - Example: Luke Skywalker on Tatooine; Frodo in the Shire; a person in their routine job before a crisis.

2. **The Call to Adventure**
   - An event, message, or opportunity that disrupts the ordinary world and invites the hero into a larger story.
   - Discriminating criterion: the hero becomes aware of a challenge, mystery, or possibility that lies outside their current world.
   - Example: Obi-Wan's message to Luke; Gandalf's arrival at Bilbo's door; a sudden diagnosis or job offer.

3. **Refusal of the Call**
   - The hero hesitates, fears, or actively resists the adventure; doubt and attachment to the ordinary world hold them back.
   - Discriminating criterion: the hero has heard the call but has not yet committed; internal or external obstacles create resistance.
   - Example: Luke's initial reluctance to leave the farm; Frodo's fear of leaving the Shire; an employee turning down a risky opportunity.

4. **Meeting with the Mentor**
   - A wise figure, guide, or internal realization appears that emboldens the hero and provides knowledge, permission, or tools to proceed.
   - Discriminating criterion: the hero receives encouragement, instruction, or a symbolic gift that shifts their confidence or capability.
   - Example: Obi-Wan giving Luke his father's lightsaber; Gandalf's counsel to Frodo; a therapist or coach helping someone commit to change.

5. **Crossing the Threshold**
   - The hero commits and enters the special world; the point of no return where the ordinary world is left behind.
   - Discriminating criterion: the hero has taken an irreversible action or crossed a boundary — they cannot easily go back.
   - Example: Luke leaving Tatooine; Frodo leaving the Shire with the ring; boarding a flight to a new city.

6. **Tests, Allies, and Enemies**
   - The hero encounters a series of smaller challenges, forms relationships, and learns the rules of the special world.
   - Discriminating criterion: the hero is gathering resources, friendships, and knowledge through repeated trials rather than one major ordeal.
   - Example: Fellowship forming and traveling together; Luke training with droids and meeting Han; a person's first weeks in a new job.

7. **Approach to the Inmost Cave**
   - The hero (and their companions) prepare for the major test; stakes and tension rise as they move closer to the central conflict.
   - Discriminating criterion: a sense of anticipation and gathering force — the hero knows the big challenge is imminent.
   - Example: the Fellowship's march toward Mordor; Luke's approach to the Death Star; the buildup before a crucial confrontation.

8. **The Ordeal**
   - The hero faces the central life-or-death crisis; the greatest test of courage, skill, or character.
   - Discriminating criterion: the hero confronts what they fear most, and the outcome is genuinely uncertain.
   - Example: Luke and Vader's duel; the Battle of Helm's Deep; a person's moment of maximum vulnerability or choice.

9. **The Reward (Seizing the Sword)**
   - The hero survives the ordeal and claims the prize, knowledge, or insight gained from enduring it.
   - Discriminating criterion: the hero has achieved what they came for, or learned something essential about themselves.
   - Example: Luke's acceptance as a Jedi; Frodo carrying the ring into Mount Doom; a person surviving illness or finally achieving a long-sought goal.

10. **The Road Back**
    - The hero prepares to return to the ordinary world, but often faces a new chase or complication that raises stakes again.
    - Discriminating criterion: the return journey is not automatic; there is a final push or obstacle that demands one more effort.
    - Example: the flight from Mordor; Luke escaping the Death Star; a person beginning the long recovery or integration phase.

11. **The Resurrection**
    - A final test or dark moment that forces the hero to use everything learned; the hero is reborn, transformed.
    - Discriminating criterion: the hero faces a last echo or magnification of the ordeal; this is the final proof of transformation.
    - Example: Luke's final confrontation with the Emperor; the last moments before destruction or escape; a person's final breakthrough into a new identity.

12. **Return with the Elixir**
    - The hero comes home changed and brings wisdom, healing, or a gift that enriches the ordinary world.
    - Discriminating criterion: the hero has integrated the journey and shares its fruits; the story closes with a new equilibrium.
    - Example: Luke as a Jedi helping others; Frodo returning to the Shire (forever changed); a person sharing what they learned with their community.

## Classification Procedure

1. Write a one-paragraph summary of the story, scene, or life event in question, including the protagonist, the world they inhabit, and the central tension or change.

2. Ask: **"Is the protagonist in a stable, known state, or have they been disrupted?"**
   - If stable and unaware of larger change → **Ordinary World**.
   - If disrupted but not yet committed → go to step 3.
   - If committed and actively in the adventure → go to step 4.
   - If returning home → go to step 9.

3. Ask: **"Has the protagonist committed and crossed a boundary, or are they still hesitating?"**
   - If hesitating, conflicted, or refusing → **Refusal of the Call** (or **Meeting with the Mentor** if a guide has just appeared).
   - If a guide or mentor figure has just appeared → **Meeting with the Mentor**.
   - If the protagonist has just committed and entered the new world → **Crossing the Threshold**.

4. Ask: **"Is the protagonist facing the central, life-or-death challenge, or are they still gathering knowledge and allies?"**
   - If gathering knowledge, forming relationships, facing smaller tests → **Tests, Allies, and Enemies** (or **Approach to the Inmost Cave** if building toward the major ordeal).
   - If the tension is clearly rising and the major challenge is imminent → **Approach to the Inmost Cave**.
   - If facing the central ordeal itself → **The Ordeal**.
   - If just past the ordeal, claiming the prize or insight → **The Reward**.

5. Ask: **"Has the protagonist achieved the goal or endured the ordeal, and are they now moving toward home?"**
   - If yes, and the return is straightforward → **The Road Back**.
   - If yes, but a final test or resurrection moment occurs → **The Resurrection**.
   - If the protagonist is home and changed → **Return with the Elixir**.

6. State the classification in writing and note what **narrative work** is appropriate for that stage (see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Stage | Narrative Work | What Should Happen |
|---|---|---|
| **Ordinary World** | Establish baseline | Show the hero's normal life, stakes, and what they have to lose. |
| **Call to Adventure** | Disrupt equilibrium | Introduce the challenge, opportunity, or mystery that demands response. |
| **Refusal of the Call** | Deepen character | Reveal fears, doubts, attachments; show why the hero resists. |
| **Meeting with the Mentor** | Grant permission & tools | Introduce wisdom, encouragement, or a symbolic gift; shift belief. |
| **Crossing the Threshold** | Commit irreversibly | Mark the point of no return; close the door on the old world. |
| **Tests, Allies, and Enemies** | Learn the rules | Introduce the special world's logic; form key relationships; gain small victories. |
| **Approach to the Inmost Cave** | Build tension | Raise stakes; move closer to the central challenge; gather final resources. |
| **The Ordeal** | Force transformation | Confront the greatest fear or challenge; outcome is uncertain. |
| **The Reward** | Claim insight or object | Acknowledge the victory; show what was learned or gained. |
| **The Road Back** | Complicate the return | Introduce a final obstacle or chase; raise stakes one more time. |
| **The Resurrection** | Final proof of change | A last echo of the ordeal that tests whether the hero is truly transformed. |
| **Return with the Elixir** | Integrate and share | Show the hero home, changed, and able to help or heal others. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Ordinary World mistaken for Refusal of the Call.** A hero in a stable world without a call is the Ordinary World; if they've heard the call but are resisting it, they're in Refusal. The tell is whether the hero is aware of an opportunity to change.

- **Meeting with the Mentor mistaken for Crossing the Threshold.** A mentor appears and advises (Mentor), but the hero hasn't yet acted on that advice or entered the new world. Crossing the Threshold is when the hero **commits and acts**, not when they are advised.

- **Tests, Allies, and Enemies mistaken for Approach to the Inmost Cave.** Tests are exploratory and varied; Approach is when all small tests stop and tension focuses on the **one imminent ordeal**. The tell is whether the hero is still learning or is now clearly moving toward a singular final challenge.

- **The Ordeal mistaken for The Reward.** The ordeal is the **crisis itself** (uncertain outcome); the reward is **after** the hero has survived it. If the hero is still in the ordeal (outcome unknown), do not classify as Reward yet.

- **The Road Back mistaken for The Resurrection.** The Road Back is the return journey with external obstacles; the Resurrection is a **final internal test** that proves the hero's transformation. If the hero is fleeing or returning home without a final dark moment, it's Road Back.

- **Classifying a stage by length rather than function.** A stage that occupies only one scene can be just as crucial as a stage that occupies many chapters. Classify by what **work** is being done (call, refusal, ordeal, return), not by page count.

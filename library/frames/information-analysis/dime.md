# DIME Framework

## Overview

DIME sorts the instruments through which a state (or strategic actor) exercises power and influence into four categories based on the **domain and mechanism of effect**. Its core claim is that different strategic objectives require different combinations of instruments, and that **misalignment between objective and instrument choice is a systematic source of policy failure**. DIME is not merely a taxonomy: it forces explicit classification of whether a given action or response belongs to diplomacy, intelligence, military, or economic leverage — and recognizing which domain you are operating in constrains which other instruments can reinforce it.

## Categories

1. **Diplomatic (D)**
   - Exercise of power through **negotiation, persuasion, and formal agreement**.
   - The mechanism is bilateral or multilateral dialogue, treaty-making, and soft power (cultural influence, legitimacy).
   - Discriminating criterion: the objective is achieved through consent or willingness of the other party, not through coercion or material incentive.
   - Example: a trade treaty negotiated through formal channels; mediation of a conflict; appeal to international law or norms.

2. **Informational (I)**
   - Exercise of power through **control, manipulation, or asymmetry of information**.
   - The mechanism includes intelligence collection, counterintelligence, surveillance, public messaging, propaganda, and denial of information to adversaries.
   - Discriminating criterion: the objective is achieved by shaping what an adversary (or ally) knows, believes, or can act upon.
   - Example: espionage to uncover an adversary's intentions; a disinformation campaign to erode confidence in an institution; signals intelligence collection.

3. **Military (M)**
   - Exercise of power through **force, threat of force, or coercive control of territory and movement**.
   - The mechanism is kinetic action (combat), armed presence, deterrence, blockade, or the threat of these.
   - Discriminating criterion: the objective is achieved through physical coercion or the credible threat of it, independent of the target's willingness.
   - Example: naval presence to deter aggression; a military strike on a target; occupation; armed humanitarian intervention.

4. **Economic (E)**
   - Exercise of power through **financial incentives, trade flows, sanctions, or control of resources**.
   - The mechanism is tariffs, aid, investment, loans, trade agreements, embargoes, currency manipulation, or restriction of market access.
   - Discriminating criterion: the objective is achieved through material incentive or penalty applied to the target's economic interest.
   - Example: economic sanctions to coerce behavior change; provision of development aid to build influence; control of critical supply chains.

## Classification Procedure

1. Write a brief statement of the strategic objective or action in question (e.g., "prevent an adversary from acquiring nuclear technology" or "build influence in a region").
2. Ask: **"Is the effect achieved primarily through the other party's consent or agreement?"**
   - If **yes** → Diplomatic (D).
   - If **no**, go to step 3.
3. Ask: **"Is the effect achieved through control or manipulation of what the target knows, believes, or can access?"**
   - If **yes** → Informational (I).
   - If **no**, go to step 4.
4. Ask: **"Is the effect achieved through physical coercion, force, or control of territory and movement?"**
   - If **yes** → Military (M).
   - If **no**, go to step 5.
5. Ask: **"Is the effect achieved through material incentive, trade flows, or financial penalty?"**
   - If **yes** → Economic (E).
   - If ambiguous or multiple instruments are in play simultaneously, name the **primary instrument** (the one without which the objective would not be achievable) and note any secondary instruments in play.
6. State the classification in writing and identify which **other DIME instruments** would reinforce it (see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Instrument | Primary Effect | What agents should recognize |
|---|---|---|
| **Diplomatic** | Builds legitimacy, consent, and durable agreements. | Works best when the target has incentive to cooperate. Fails when applied to an actor with misaligned interests. Requires credibility and trust accumulated over time. |
| **Informational** | Shapes perception, decision-making, and belief. | Fastest to deploy, hardest to sustain; erodes when adversary recognizes it. Effective at asymmetric cost. Can create windows of opportunity for other instruments. |
| **Military** | Imposes immediate physical constraint or coercion. | Slowest to build, fastest to execute. Highly visible; creates friction with diplomatic objectives. Effective at denial but poor at building lasting positive influence. |
| **Economic** | Incentivizes behavior through material cost-benefit. | Works at scale; reinforces over time as interdependence grows. Requires credible enforcement (willingness to pay the cost of sanction). Can be circumvented by alternative trade routes. |

Implicit pairing rule: Diplomatic objectives are **reinforced by Information** (narrative support) and **undermined by Military** (coercion contradicts consent-based framing). Military objectives are reinforced by **Economic** (imposing opportunity cost) and **Information** (shaping the target's perception of the military threat). Economic leverage is reinforced by **Diplomatic** frameworks (multilateral agreement on sanctions) and **Information** (shaping market confidence).

## Common Misclassifications

- **Economic mistaken for Diplomatic.** Offering aid or trade as if it builds consent, when the target perceives it as a coercive incentive (e.g., implicit threat of withdrawal). The tell is that the target does not feel "agreed" but rather "pressed." Consequence: allies resent the approach and coordination fails.
- **Military mistaken for Informational.** Believing that a show of force will change an adversary's belief, when it only imposes a constraint they work around. The tell is that kinetic action does not change the target's intent, only their **tactics**. Consequence: escalation spiral.
- **Informational mistaken for Diplomatic.** Running a messaging or soft-power campaign as if it constitutes an actual agreement. The tell is that the target may *appear* swayed but has made no formal commitment and reverses course once attention wanes. Consequence: false sense of alignment.
- **Classifying a **blended** action into a single instrument.** Most effective campaigns use multiple instruments in concert (e.g., military pressure + economic sanctions + diplomatic negotiations). The tell is that one instrument alone fails to achieve the objective. Solution: identify the **primary** instrument (the one without which the objective would fail) and name all others in play.
- **Confusing **threat** of an instrument with **exercise** of it.** A military blockade is Military; the *threat* of one is Informational (it works through belief). The tell is whether physical constraint is actually imposed. Consequence: misestimating credibility and cost.

# PMESII-PT Framework

## Overview

PMESII-PT is a decomposition frame that sorts the elements of a geopolitical, operational, or strategic situation into eight semi-independent analytical dimensions. The core claim is that **comprehensive situational analysis requires systematic coverage of all eight dimensions**, and that omitting any dimension creates a blind spot — often in precisely the area where the most consequential forces operate. Unlike sense-making frames that tell you *how to respond*, PMESII-PT tells you *what to examine*, ensuring that subsequent analysis and decision-making are built on complete reconnaissance rather than intuitive guessing.

## Categories

1. **Political (P)**
   - Governance structures, power distribution, political legitimacy, regime stability, and interstate relations.
   - Discriminating criterion: organizational entities (states, parties, tribal councils, militias) and the authority relationships between them.
   - Example: the distribution of veto power in a national parliament, the competing claims of warlords in a fragmented region, diplomatic alignment shifts.

2. **Military (M)**
   - Armed forces, military capability, doctrine, operational posture, and the balance of coercive power.
   - Discriminating criterion: kinetic capacity, force structure, readiness, and the stated or implied willingness to use force.
   - Example: armor concentration near a border, naval presence in a strait, the condition of supply lines.

3. **Economic (E)**
   - Resource flows, trade patterns, sanctions, currency stability, infrastructure, supply chains, and wealth distribution.
   - Discriminating criterion: material and financial dependencies, scarcities, and the economic incentives driving actors.
   - Example: oil dependency on a single exporter, a critical port's share of trade, the solvency of a banking system.

4. **Social (S)**
   - Demographics, ethnic and religious composition, class divisions, education levels, public morale, and social cohesion.
   - Discriminating criterion: population composition, grievance distribution, and the likelihood of mass mobilization or fracture.
   - Example: age pyramid (youth bulge), regional ethnolinguistic distribution, literacy rates, unemployment among a key demographic.

5. **Information (I)**
   - Media landscape, information access, propaganda, narrative control, cyber presence, and the credibility of institutions.
   - Discriminating criterion: who controls the dominant narrative, what information asymmetries exist, and what populations can see or believe.
   - Example: state censorship of social media, dominance of a single news outlet, disinformation campaigns, digital divide.

6. **Infrastructure (I — second I)**
   - Transportation, energy, water, communications networks, and critical facilities.
   - Discriminating criterion: chokepoints, redundancy, and the interdependencies between systems (e.g., power grid → water treatment → hospital function).
   - Example: a single bridge crossing a river; satellite vs. terrestrial communication; power plant capacity vs. demand.

7. **Physical Environment (P — second P)**
   - Terrain, climate, geography, natural resources, and environmental degradation.
   - Discriminating criterion: constraints on movement, habitability, resource availability, and seasonal variation.
   - Example: desert vs. mountain vs. riverine terrain; drought's effect on agriculture; the navigability of a waterway.

8. **Time (T)**
   - Temporal horizon, historical context, seasonal cycles, leadership tenure cycles, and the urgency of decisions.
   - Discriminating criterion: which timeframe matters most to the question (days for a military action, years for policy impact, decades for demographic change).
   - Example: the window before monsoon season; the end of a leader's term; the historical memory of an ethnic conflict.

## Classification Procedure

1. **State the situation or question** in a single sentence. Example: "What is the stability outlook for Country X over the next 18 months?"

2. **Create an eight-cell table** with one row per PMESII-PT dimension.

3. **For each dimension, ask:** "What are the key facts and drivers in this dimension that bear on the question?" Write 2–4 bullet points per dimension. Do not skip any dimension — a blank cell is data.

4. **For each dimension, identify the critical assumption or unknown** that would change your assessment if resolved.

5. **Cross-check for dependencies.** Do any dimensions interact in ways that could amplify or cancel effects? Flag these (e.g., Economic collapse → Social unrest → Political instability).

6. **Identify the dimension(s) most sensitive to the question.** For a military readiness assessment, Military and Infrastructure may dominate; for stability, Social and Political.

7. **Record the classification** as a completed PMESII-PT matrix and use it to scope subsequent analysis (lenses, wargames, or expert review).

## Implications per Category

| Dimension | Typical analytical focus | What to investigate |
|---|---|---|
| **Political** | Regime survival, alliance patterns, veto players | Who holds power, how is it contested, what are the formal and informal rules? |
| **Military** | Force balance, doctrine, threshold for escalation | Who has what weapons, what is the stated doctrine, what are the red lines? |
| **Economic** | Resource dependency, supply chains, fiscal health | What does the actor need to import, who controls it, what is the debt burden? |
| **Social** | Cohesion, grievance, mobilization potential | Are populations fractured or unified, what are the major grievances, who can mobilize at scale? |
| **Information** | Narrative control, information asymmetries, credibility | Who controls what story, what populations can see alternative narratives, who is trusted? |
| **Infrastructure** | Resilience, chokepoints, redundancy | What systems are single-points-of-failure, how interconnected are they, how recoverable are they? |
| **Physical Environment** | Constraints, resources, seasonal drivers | What does geography enable or forbid, what resources are contested, what changes seasonally? |
| **Time** | Horizon relevance, urgency, window closure | What is the relevant timeframe for the question, when do key events occur, how fast do things change? |

For a lenslab agent, the practical implication is that **each dimension often requires a different lens or expert**: Political analysis may use power transition theory; Military may use force-ratio models; Economic may use supply-chain mapping; Social may use demographic analysis; Information may use narrative mapping; Infrastructure may use dependency analysis; Physical may use terrain analysis; Time may use scenario timelines. Running a PMESII-PT frame first tells you which lens bundles to load.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Collapsing multiple dimensions into one.** Treating Political + Military as "Security" and skipping the distinction between power distribution and military posture — they move independently and require separate analysis. The consequence is missing early-warning signals of a shift in coercive balance.

- **Over-weighting one dimension because it is familiar.** An analyst with military expertise may dwell on Military dynamics and treat Political, Social, and Information as secondary — but Political legitimacy may shift in weeks while military capability takes months to reposition.

- **Treating Time as optional.** Skipping the Time dimension because "we always forecast 5 years" — but the question may actually turn on a 6-month window (e.g., before an election, before a monsoon, before a loan matures). The consequence is a forecast that answers the wrong question.

- **Assuming dimensions are independent.** They are not. Infrastructure failure → Economic disruption → Social unrest → Political crisis — but treating each in isolation misses the cascade. Always check for second-order effects.

- **Leaving a dimension blank because "we don't have data."** A blank is data; it signals a gap in your reconnaissance. Name it explicitly rather than assuming the dimension is irrelevant — often the most consequential blind spot is the one you don't realize you have.

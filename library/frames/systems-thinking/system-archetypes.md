# System Archetypes (Senge)

## Overview

System archetypes are recurring causal structures — feedback loop patterns — that appear across organizations, markets, and societies despite superficially different contexts. Senge's core claim is that **the same archetypal pattern produces the same unintended consequences regardless of domain**, and that recognizing the archetype *before* acting allows you to design interventions that work *with* the system structure instead of against it. Applying a standard intervention to an archetypal problem without recognizing its structure is a systematic source of failure: the system will route around your solution and restore the problem in a different guise.

## Categories

1. **Limits to Growth** (also: *Success to Failure*)
   - A reinforcing loop drives growth or improvement until a balancing loop constrains it; the constraint is often invisible until growth suddenly stops.
   - The discriminating criterion is the presence of two distinct phases: an initial period of rapid improvement, followed by a ceiling or reversal that surprises the actors.
   - Example: a startup scales sales rapidly until it runs out of operational capacity; throughput plateaus despite continued effort.

2. **Shifting the Burden** (also: *Problem Symptom, Fundamental Solution*)
   - An intervention addresses the symptom of a problem (relief loop) and temporarily improves the situation, but weakens the system's ability to solve the root cause (fundamental loop), making long-term problems worse.
   - Discriminating criterion: the situation improves when the intervention is applied, then deteriorates faster when it is removed; repeated applications are required to maintain the same effect.
   - Example: using antibiotics to suppress a chronic infection without addressing underlying immune dysfunction; the infection returns harder each time.

3. **Tragedy of the Commons**
   - Multiple independent actors each rationally maximize their own benefit from a shared resource, collectively depleting or degrading the resource; each actor's incentive to restrain individual use decreases as the resource degrades.
   - Discriminating criterion: individual rationality produces collective irrationality; the tragedy is inevitable without external enforcement or a shift in incentive structure.
   - Example: overfishing in international waters; a team hoarding tools and knowledge because "what's in it for me to share?"

4. **Fixes That Fail** (also: *Unintended Consequences*)
   - An intervention produces a desired effect in the short term but triggers a delayed side effect that negates or reverses the benefit over a longer time horizon.
   - Discriminating criterion: the symptom alleviates immediately, but a new problem (often worse) appears weeks or months later; the two are causally linked through a delayed feedback loop.
   - Example: pesticides reduce crop-damaging insects, but kill pollinators and degrade soil, reducing yields years later.

5. **Escalation** (also: *Conflict Spiral*)
   - Two parties (or teams) each take action to protect their interests, which the other party perceives as a threat, triggering defensive escalation; each side's defensive move triggers the other's defensive move, producing a self-reinforcing spiral.
   - Discriminating criterion: the intensity or stakes of the conflict increase over time despite neither side intending to provoke escalation; each action is framed as defensive.
   - Example: two rival business units each hoard resources to protect against the other's anticipated grab; the hoarding itself triggers the grab they feared.

6. **Success to Failure** (also: *Eroding Goals*)
   - Success in achieving a goal leads to relaxation of effort or standards, which gradually erodes the achievement; the erosion is not visible until the original problem returns.
   - Discriminating criterion: goals or standards are loosened *because* the situation has improved, not because conditions have fundamentally changed; the system slides backward invisibly.
   - Example: a safety program achieves zero accidents for a year, so training is scaled back; accidents slowly rise again as new hires are under-trained.

7. **Accidental Adversaries**
   - Two parties have genuinely aligned goals but pursue them through actions that inadvertently undermine each other; each party is unaware that the other exists or that their actions conflict.
   - Discriminating criterion: neither party is acting in bad faith; misalignment is structural or due to lack of communication, not intention.
   - Example: a product team optimizes for user engagement (more clicks, more session time) while a retention team optimizes for user value (less busy work, faster task completion); the two goals pull in opposite directions.

8. **Balancing Process with Delay** (also: *Corrective Actions That Overshoot*)
   - A balancing loop exists (the system does try to self-correct), but the delay between sensing the problem and the correction taking effect is long; the correction overshoots because the actor cannot perceive in real time that the action is already working.
   - Discriminating criterion: the system oscillates around the target instead of converging on it; early corrections trigger late-arriving counter-corrections.
   - Example: a thermostat with a 30-minute delay; the furnace runs until the house is too hot, then shuts off, but by the time the temperature response arrives, the house is already cooling.

## Classification Procedure

1. Describe the recurring problem or pattern in one or two sentences: what keeps happening despite efforts to fix it?
2. Ask **"Does the situation improve, then hit a ceiling or reverse?"** If yes → *Limits to Growth*. If no, go to step 3.
3. Ask **"Does the short-term fix work, but long-term gets worse?"** If yes → *Shifting the Burden* or *Fixes That Fail*. Ask whether the fundamental problem is being neglected (*Shifting the Burden*) or a side effect is appearing (*Fixes That Fail*). If no, go to step 4.
4. Ask **"Do multiple independent actors produce a collectively bad outcome?"** If yes → *Tragedy of the Commons*. If no, go to step 5.
5. Ask **"Are two parties locked in a defensive escalation spiral?"** If yes → *Escalation*. If no, go to step 6.
6. Ask **"Did relaxation of effort after success erode the original gain?"** If yes → *Success to Failure*. If no, go to step 7.
7. Ask **"Are two parties with aligned goals inadvertently working against each other?"** If yes → *Accidental Adversaries*. If no, go to step 8.
8. Ask **"Does the system oscillate around the target instead of settling?"** If yes → *Balancing Process with Delay*. If no, re-examine the problem description; you may have misidentified the pattern.

## Implications per Category

| Archetype | What it means | What not to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Limits to Growth** | You can grow until you hit a constraint. The constraint is load-bearing; pushing harder will break something. | Don't mistake the ceiling for a market limit; often it's a capacity or capability limit that can be rebuilt. |
| **Shifting the Burden** | The symptom relief is masking the root cause. Each fix weakens the system's ability to self-heal. | Don't keep applying the same bandage; you are making the patient's immune system worse. |
| **Tragedy of the Commons** | Individual incentives are misaligned with collective good. Uncoordinated behavior produces collapse. | Don't shame actors for rational self-interest; change the incentive structure or enforce a quota. |
| **Fixes That Fail** | The intervention works in the short term but has a delayed side effect. The system is fighting back. | Don't declare victory after 3 months; model the full causal chain and wait to see the delayed feedback. |
| **Escalation** | Both sides feel defensive and justified. The spiral is self-reinforcing. | Don't assume one side is irrational; each side's move is rational *given* its perception of the other's intent. |
| **Success to Failure** | Vigilance and standards eroded because the problem seemed solved. The system is backsliding. | Don't relax standards; institutionalize them so success doesn't breed complacency. |
| **Accidental Adversaries** | Misaligned actions, not misaligned goals. The parties don't see each other. | Don't assume bad faith; surface the structural conflict and realign metrics or communication. |
| **Balancing Process with Delay** | The system is self-correcting, but the delays cause overshoot. Oscillation is baked in. | Don't keep tightening the control loop; reduce the delay or add predictive damping. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Limits to Growth mistaken for Shifting the Burden.** The difference: In Limits to Growth, the ceiling is real (e.g., team capacity, market saturation). In Shifting the Burden, you *could* remove the ceiling but you're treating the symptom instead. The tell is whether removing the constraint would actually solve the problem.
- **Fixes That Fail mistaken for Shifting the Burden.** In Fixes That Fail, a side effect appears. In Shifting the Burden, the root cause is being ignored. Ask: is there a hidden consequence, or is the real problem untouched?
- **Success to Failure mistaken for Limits to Growth.** In Success to Failure, the erosion is *chosen* (standards relaxed). In Limits to Growth, the ceiling is *forced* (capacity genuinely exhausted). The tell is whether the goal or standard changed.
- **Escalation mistaken for Accidental Adversaries.** In Escalation, each side is reacting to the other's perceived threat. In Accidental Adversaries, the parties don't know the other exists. Ask: does each side see the other as an adversary?
- **Balancing Process with Delay mistaken for Escalation.** In Balancing Process with Delay, the oscillation is mechanical (lag in feedback). In Escalation, it's psychological (perceived threat). The tell is whether adding information or removing delay would help.

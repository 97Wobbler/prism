# Mode Analysis

## Overview

Mode Analysis sorts melodic and harmonic material into one of seven church modes (and their modern extensions) based on the root note, characteristic interval pattern, and tonal gravity. Its core claim is that different modes have **structurally distinct listening profiles** — and recognizing which mode is in play changes which analytical and compositional tools are appropriate. Each mode is built on the same twelve-tone chromatic set but privileges a different step as the tonal center, producing different interval relationships and emotional or color associations. Sorting material into its correct mode clarifies which intervals are structural (consonant, stable) and which are ornamental or leading.

## Categories

1. **Ionian (Major)**
   - Built on scale steps 1–8 of a major scale starting on the root.
   - Interval pattern (from root): W-W-H-W-W-W-H (whole and half steps).
   - Discriminating criterion: contains a major third above the root and a major seventh; the perfect fifth and third form the stable harmonic center.
   - Tonal character: bright, stable, resolved, major-key.
   - Example: C–D–E–F–G–A–B–C; the opening of Beethoven's "Ode to Joy."

2. **Dorian**
   - Built on scale steps 2–9 of a major scale (relative to the parent major), or equivalently, a major scale with a flatted 3rd and 7th.
   - Interval pattern (from root): W-H-W-W-W-H-W.
   - Discriminating criterion: contains a minor third (dark) but a major sixth (bright), creating a characteristic minor-with-a-raised-sixth color.
   - Tonal character: minor but not fully dark; softer, more introspective than Aeolian.
   - Example: D–E–F–G–A–B–C–D; "So What" by Miles Davis.

3. **Phrygian**
   - Built on scale steps 3–10 of a major scale, or equivalently, a major scale with flatted 2nd, 3rd, 6th, and 7th.
   - Interval pattern (from root): H-W-W-W-H-W-W.
   - Discriminating criterion: half step immediately above the root; dark, exotic, Spanish or Middle Eastern color.
   - Tonal character: darkest of the minor modes; Spanish flamenco or folk associations.
   - Example: E–F–G–A–B–C–D–E; opening of Rimsky-Korsakov's "Scheherazade."

4. **Lydian**
   - Built on scale steps 4–11 of a major scale, or equivalently, a major scale with a raised 4th.
   - Interval pattern (from root): W-W-W-H-W-W-H.
   - Discriminating criterion: raised (sharp) fourth above the root; major and bright but with a subtle lifted quality, almost unresolved at the 4th.
   - Tonal character: major but dreamy, floating, slightly dissonant relative to Ionian.
   - Example: F–G–A–B–C–D–E–F; "What a Wonderful World" contains Lydian passages.

5. **Mixolydian**
   - Built on scale steps 5–12 of a major scale, or equivalently, a major scale with a flatted 7th.
   - Interval pattern (from root): W-W-H-W-W-H-W.
   - Discriminating criterion: major third and major sixth but minor seventh; bluesy, bluegrass, or dominant character.
   - Tonal character: major with a flat 7 creates a "dominant without resolution" feeling; blues and rock-adjacent.
   - Example: G–A–B–C–D–E–F–G; "Superstition" by Stevie Wonder.

6. **Aeolian (Natural Minor)**
   - Built on scale steps 6–13 of a major scale, or equivalently, a major scale with flatted 3rd, 6th, and 7th.
   - Interval pattern (from root): W-H-W-W-H-W-W.
   - Discriminating criterion: minor third, minor sixth, and minor seventh; the relative minor of Ionian.
   - Tonal character: dark, minor, fully resolved to minor function without the brightness of Dorian.
   - Example: A–B–C–D–E–F–G–A; "The Lemon Song" by Led Zeppelin (A minor context).

7. **Locrian**
   - Built on scale steps 7–14 of a major scale, or equivalently, a major scale with flatted 2nd, 3rd, 5th, 6th, and 7th.
   - Interval pattern (from root): H-W-W-H-W-W-W.
   - Discriminating criterion: diminished fifth (tritone) above the root, half step above the root, and no major interval except the octave; darkest and most unstable.
   - Tonal character: dissonant, unstable, leading-tone quality; rarely used as a home mode; suggests motion.
   - Example: B–C–D–E–F–G–A–B; used in progressive metal and atonal contexts.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Identify the tonal center (root).** Listen for or analyze which pitch feels like "home" — the one the melody or harmony gravitates toward and rests on. This may be the lowest note, the first note, or the pitch that resolves longest phrase endings.

2. **Write out the seven consecutive pitches starting from the root.** Do not worry about octave doubling; use the natural note names (no enharmonic respelling yet).

3. **Compare the interval pattern to the characteristic patterns above.** Specifically check:
   - The 3rd above the root: is it major (4 semitones) or minor (3 semitones)?
   - The 7th above the root: is it major (11 semitones) or minor (10 semitones)?
   - The 2nd above the root: is it a whole step (2 semitones) or half step (1 semitone)?
   - The 5th above the root: is it perfect (7 semitones) or diminished (6 semitones)?

4. **Use the decision tree below:**
   - If 3rd is **major** and 7th is **major** → **Ionian**.
   - If 3rd is **major** and 7th is **minor**:
     - If 4th is **perfect** → **Mixolydian**.
     - If 4th is **raised (sharp)** → **Lydian**.
   - If 3rd is **minor** and 7th is **major** → **Dorian**.
   - If 3rd is **minor** and 7th is **minor**:
     - If 2nd is **whole step** → **Aeolian**.
     - If 2nd is **half step** → **Phrygian**.
   - If 5th is **diminished** (tritone) → **Locrian**.

5. **Record the mode, the root pitch, and one concrete example** (a melody, a chord progression, or a passage from a known work) that exemplifies it.

## Implications per Category

| Mode | Harmonic/Melodic character | Compositional use | Listener impression |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ionian** | Stable; major; consonant 3rd and 7th. | Default in Western tonal music; major keys. | Resolved, bright, settled, triumphant. |
| **Dorian** | Minor with raised 6th; softer minor. | Jazz, soul, folk; creates minor without darkness. | Introspective, soulful, floating minor. |
| **Phrygian** | Dark; Spanish/exotic; half-step gravity. | Flamenco, world music, prog-metal. | Exotic, dark, sensual, foreign. |
| **Lydian** | Major with raised 4th; floating. | Film scores, shoegaze, prog; dreamlike. | Ethereal, unresolved-but-major, floating. |
| **Mixolydian** | Major with minor 7th; bluesy. | Blues, rock, funk, reggae. | Bluesy, funky, dominant-without-closure. |
| **Aeolian** | Natural minor; fully dark; stable in minor. | Default minor key; classical and rock minor. | Dark, resolved-to-minor, melancholic. |
| **Locrian** | Diminished 5th; unstable; leading quality. | Atonal, metal, film horror cues. | Dissonant, unstable, demanding resolution. |

For harmonic analysis: modes guide which chords and extensions are structural (diatonic) versus chromatic (ornamental or borrowed). For melody composition: modes shape which intervals feel like "home" and which feel like "leading."

## Common Misclassifications

- **Mixolydian mistaken for Ionian.** The discriminating tell is the flatted 7th: in Mixolydian it creates a dominant-like pull; in Ionian the major 7th creates resolution. Compare the 7th scale degree carefully — is it natural or flat relative to the major scale on the same root?

- **Dorian mistaken for Aeolian.** Both are minor, but Dorian has a raised 6th while Aeolian is naturally minor throughout. The tell: sing or play the 6th above the root — Dorian's is bright (whole step below octave); Aeolian's is dark (three semitones below octave).

- **Phrygian mistaken for Aeolian.** Both are dark, but Phrygian has a half step above the root (very exotic gravity) while Aeolian has a whole step. The tell: the opening interval from the root — Phrygian's immediate half step is unmistakable; Aeolian's whole step feels more conventional minor.

- **Lydian mistaken for Ionian.** Both are major, but Lydian's raised 4th creates a lifting, unresolved quality. The tell: the 4th scale degree — Ionian has a perfect 4th (stable under the melody); Lydian has a raised 4th (floats, feels like a tension, not a rest).

- **Locrian mistaken for Phrygian.** Both are dark and exotic, but Locrian has a diminished 5th (tritone) while Phrygian has a perfect 5th. The tell: the 5th above the root — Locrian's tritone is actively dissonant and destabilizing; Phrygian's perfect 5th is consonant and stable, with darkness coming from the half-step 2nd instead.

- **Using parallel modes instead of relative modes.** Parallel modes (C Ionian vs. C Dorian) share the same root but different scales; relative modes (C Ionian vs. A Aeolian) are the same scale but different roots. Confusion here leads to wrong chord progressions and harmonic function analysis.

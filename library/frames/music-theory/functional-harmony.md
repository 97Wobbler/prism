# Functional Harmony (T-S-D)

## Overview

Functional Harmony sorts chords and harmonic moments into three categories — Tonic, Subdominant, and Dominant — based on the **tonal function** each chord carries within a key. Its core claim is that the same chord (e.g., C major in the key of C) can have different meanings depending on context, and the **functional category dictates the kinds of harmonic movement** that follow naturally. A chord's position in the functional hierarchy determines whether it can serve as a stable resting point, a preparatory gesture, or a directive tension demanding resolution. Sorting a harmonic moment correctly prevents confusion about why some progressions feel inevitable and others feel broken.

## Categories

1. **Tonic (I)**
   - The **stable, resting point** of the key; the harmonic home.
   - Presence of tonic creates a sense of arrival, completion, or repose.
   - Discriminating criterion: a chord that can serve as a final cadential goal without preparation; the listener hears it as "home."
   - In C major: C major (I), A minor (vi), F major (IV) when functioning as substitute tonic, or rarely E minor (iii) in deceptive motion.
   - Example: the final I chord in "I–IV–I–V–I" (the authentic cadence resolution); the opening of a piece.

2. **Subdominant (IV)**
   - The **preparatory, motion-away-from-home** function; moves harmonic gravity away from tonic.
   - Presence of subdominant creates forward motion and instability; it asks "what comes next?"
   - Discriminating criterion: a chord that destabilizes tonic and typically moves toward Dominant; its function is to prepare tension, not resolve it.
   - In C major: F major (IV), D minor (ii), B-flat major (bVI) in borrowed harmony.
   - Example: the IV in "I–IV–V–I" (the plagal approach); the ii6 in a ii6–V–I progression.

3. **Dominant (V)**
   - The **directive, tension-bearing** function; creates harmonic pull back to tonic.
   - Presence of dominant generates urgency and incompleteness; it demands resolution.
   - Discriminating criterion: a chord that is unstable and functionally obligates return to Tonic; the listener expects resolution.
   - In C major: G major (V), G dominant 7 (V7), B diminished (vii°, the leading-tone seventh).
   - Example: the V in "I–IV–V–I" (authentic cadence); a V7–I resolution.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the **key** of the passage (or the tonic chord the progression is operating within).
2. Identify the **chord in question** by its root and quality (major, minor, diminished, dominant 7, etc.).
3. Ask: **"Does this chord feel like home — a place where the music can rest?"**
   - If **yes** → **Tonic**. Proceed to step 5.
   - If **no**, go to step 4.
4. Ask: **"Does this chord create pull back to the home chord, or does it prepare motion away from home?"**
   - If **creates pull back to home** (the ear expects resolution to Tonic next) → **Dominant**.
   - If **prepares motion away** (the ear expects motion toward Dominant) → **Subdominant**.
5. Verify by checking the **actual progression that follows** in the passage:
   - If the chord is followed by rest or by a deceptive motion → confirms Tonic.
   - If followed by Dominant or V → confirms Subdominant.
   - If followed by Tonic → confirms Dominant.
6. State the functional role in writing and note what harmonic motion is **allowed or implied** by that role (see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Category | Harmonic behavior | What the composer/arranger should do |
|---|---|---|
| **Tonic** | Can stand alone; can move to any other function; natural resting point. | Use as opening, closing, or structural arrival point. No preparation needed. Multiple consecutive tonics (vi–I, IV–I, I–vi) are stable. |
| **Subdominant** | Moves toward Dominant; destabilizes the harmonic center; asks for onward motion. | Place between Tonic and Dominant in a phrase; use to build momentum. Lingering on Subdominant without moving to Dominant feels unresolved. |
| **Dominant** | Pulls back to Tonic; unstable without resolution; commands return home. | Use to create tension and expectation; always resolve to Tonic (or deceptively to vi, which implies an interrupted Tonic). Dominant without resolution (especially V7 without I) feels incomplete. |

For a lenslab agent analyzing a harmonic passage, the practical implication is which **analytical lens** to apply:
- Tonic function → structural stability lens (cadences, phrase endings, formal anchors).
- Subdominant function → transitional/preparatory lens (harmonic momentum, key modulation setup).
- Dominant function → tension/resolution lens (harmonic syntax, listener expectation, phrasing clarity).

## Common Misclassifications

- **Tonic mistaken for Subdominant.** The vi chord (e.g., A minor in C major) can feel like home in a minor-key context or when used as a substitute tonic, but it is technically a Tonic function only when it resolves the phrase or carries structural weight. The tell is whether the progression can end on it convincingly. Consequence: over-analyzing a vi–IV–I progression as Subdominant–Tonic when vi is already Tonic.

- **Dominant mistaken for Tonic.** A V chord or V7 that resolves deceptively to vi can feel like arrival, but it is not Tonic function — the vi is the true Tonic. The tell is listening for the sense of "home"; if the ear is still pulling back toward I, the V is still Dominant. Consequence: mislabeling a deceptive cadence structure.

- **Subdominant mistaken for Dominant.** The ii chord (e.g., D minor in C major) prepares motion forward but does not carry the same "pull back to home" as V. Both move toward Dominant, but only V has the harmonic obligation to resolve to I. The tell is whether the chord can be replaced by Dominant without changing the harmonic sense; if it cannot, it is Subdominant. Consequence: treating ii–V–I as V–V–I (redundant analysis).

- **Ignoring functional drift in reharmonization.** A chord can shift function depending on voicing, context, and what follows. The same C major chord is Tonic at the end of a phrase, Subdominant in the middle of "I–I–V–I" (prolonging Tonic function), or a Dominant of V in "C major to F major" modulation. The tell is the harmonic pathway, not the chord label alone. Consequence: static misclassification that misses the actual harmonic intent.

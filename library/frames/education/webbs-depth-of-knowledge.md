# Webb's Depth of Knowledge

## Overview

Webb's Depth of Knowledge is a frame that sorts learning activities,
assessment items, and instructional tasks into one of four levels based on
the **cognitive complexity and mental processes required** to complete them.
Its core claim is that different levels demand structurally different
teaching and assessment strategies — and misalignment between the rigor of
instruction and the rigor of assessment is a systematic source of unmet
learning goals. DOK is not a measure of difficulty in the sense of "harder
vs. easier"; it is a measure of the *kind* of thinking required. A simple
task can demand deep knowledge, and a complex task can demand shallow
recall.

## Categories

1. **Level 1: Recall and Reproduction**
   - Requires **retrieval of isolated facts or simple procedures**.
   - The task specifies exactly what the student must do; no interpretation
     or decision-making is necessary.
   - Discriminating criterion: the student answers from memory or applies a
     single, well-practiced procedure with no need to make choices about
     how to proceed.
   - Example: defining a term; listing the capitals of U.S. states; solving
     a single-step arithmetic problem using a memorized algorithm.

2. **Level 2: Skill and Concept**
   - Requires **understanding and application of concepts or procedures to
     new but familiar contexts**.
   - The task asks the student to use acquired knowledge, but the pathway
     is still relatively straightforward and the context is recognizable.
   - Discriminating criterion: the student must make a choice or decision
     about which procedure applies, but the categories of response are
     limited and the procedure is one the student has practiced.
   - Example: classifying organisms by kingdom; explaining a causal
     relationship using evidence; solving a multi-step math problem that
     follows a familiar structure; summarizing a text.

3. **Level 3: Strategic Thinking**
   - Requires **reasoning, planning, and the use of evidence** to support
     conclusions in a non-routine situation.
   - The task demands that the student determine **what to do** as well as
     how to do it; it cannot be solved by following a memorized procedure.
   - Discriminating criterion: the student must make judgments,
     distinctions, or inferences; there are multiple valid pathways or
     multiple defensible answers.
   - Example: analyzing the effectiveness of a historical argument;
     designing an experiment to test a hypothesis; revising an essay based
     on feedback; comparing two different mathematical approaches to the
     same problem and defending which is more efficient.

4. **Level 4: Extended Thinking**
   - Requires **synthesis, investigation, and sustained cognitive effort**
     over time.
   - The task is non-routine, open-ended, and typically requires the
     student to work with complex information and make multiple
     connections.
   - Discriminating criterion: the task takes extended time; involves
     multiple steps; may require gathering or generating new information;
     and results in a novel product or conclusion that the student must
     justify.
   - Example: conducting a multi-stage research project with original data
     collection; writing an original argument essay that synthesizes
     multiple sources; designing a solution to an open-ended problem;
     creating a model to explain a complex phenomenon.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Read or perform the task yourself.** Do not rely on the task title or
   context alone; understand what cognitive work is actually being asked.

2. **Ask: "Does the task require the student to retrieve a single fact or
   apply a memorized procedure with no decision-making?"**
   - If **yes** → **Level 1: Recall and Reproduction**.
   - If **no**, go to step 3.

3. **Ask: "Can a student who has practiced this type of task in class
   complete it by applying a familiar concept or procedure, even if the
   specific context is new?"**
   - If **yes** → **Level 2: Skill and Concept**.
   - If **no**, go to step 4.

4. **Ask: "Does the task require the student to determine what approach to
   use, make inferences or judgments, and explain or defend reasoning in a
   non-routine situation?"**
   - If **yes** → **Level 3: Strategic Thinking**.
   - If **no**, go to step 5.

5. **The task requires sustained investigation, synthesis across multiple
   sources, or generation of a novel product over extended time.**
   → **Level 4: Extended Thinking**.

6. **Record the level and the specific cognitive demand** that determined
   the classification.

## Implications per Category

| Level | Cognitive demand | Instructional implication | Assessment implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1: Recall** | Memory retrieval; reproduction of practiced procedure | Explicit instruction in facts and procedures; distributed practice for fluency. | Items should be direct, unambiguous; multiple-choice or short-answer recall; *do not overweight in the assessment*. |
| **2: Skill & Concept** | Application in familiar contexts; selection among known procedures | Guided practice with feedback; worked examples followed by independent practice; explicit connection between examples and new contexts. | Items should show a new context but reference taught categories; require procedural choice; allow short-answer or show-your-work formats. |
| **3: Strategic Thinking** | Reasoning, inference, judgment; determining the approach; defending conclusions | Problem-based learning; discussion and debate; non-routine tasks with scaffolding that fades; emphasis on metacognition ("How do you know?" "Why did you choose that?"). | Items should be open-ended or multi-part; allow multiple valid approaches; require written explanation or justification; *assessment should reflect the cognitive demand of instruction*. |
| **4: Extended Thinking** | Investigation, synthesis, sustained effort; novel product or conclusion | Project-based learning; real-world problems; research skills; revision and iteration; student ownership of question. | Holistic rubrics or performance assessments; evidence of the inquiry process, not just the final answer; extended time; clear criteria known in advance. |

**Core prioritization rule:** Align assessment to instruction. If instruction
is all Level 1 and 2, assessment items should be proportionally Level 1 and 2.
If instruction includes Level 3 and 4 work, assessment must provide
opportunity to demonstrate that thinking.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Mistaking complexity for depth.** A multi-step problem can still be
  Level 2 (skill application) if the student follows a practiced procedure
  to completion. The tell is whether the student must decide which
  procedure applies or is told/guided to the right approach.

- **Mistaking coverage for strategic thinking.** A task that touches on
  many topics is not automatically Level 3. Level 3 requires reasoning and
  judgment about relationships or conclusions. A "list the causes of the
  Civil War" task is Level 2 (concept application), not Level 3, unless
  the student must evaluate which cause was *most significant* and defend
  the choice.

- **Confusing time spent with cognitive demand.** A long task can be all
  Level 2 work stretched out; a short task can be Level 4 if it requires
  genuine investigation. The tell is the *kind* of thinking, not the
  duration.

- **Treating all open-ended tasks as Level 4.** An open-ended question that
  asks students to apply a familiar concept in a new setting is Level 3.
  Level 4 requires extended time, synthesis across multiple sources, and
  typically results in a product (research paper, model, solution) the
  student had to generate or construct.

- **Over-classifying at Level 4 without evidence of investigation.** A
  single essay assignment, even if long, is Level 3 if it applies reasoning
  to given information. Level 4 requires genuine research, data collection,
  or iterative refinement — evidence that the student had to *discover* or
  *construct* something, not just explain something.

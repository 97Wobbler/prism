# Red Flags Screening

## Overview

Red Flags Screening sorts a presenting symptom or clinical finding into one of four categories based on the **imminence and severity of potential harm** if the underlying cause is not addressed. Its core claim is that different flag categories demand **structurally different response urgencies and diagnostic depth** — and misclassifying a true red flag as benign (or over-escalating a benign variant) is a systematic source of either delayed intervention or resource waste. The frame does not diagnose; it sorts presentations by risk trajectory to determine what speed of evaluation and what depth of workup is appropriate.

## Categories

1. **Emergency Red Flag** (also "life-threat," "door-to-needle")
   - The symptom or sign cluster suggests an **immediately life-threatening condition** that will deteriorate rapidly without intervention.
   - Discriminating criterion: delay of minutes to hours materially worsens prognosis or mortality; intervention requires immediate action (airway, bleeding control, defibrillation, etc.) or emergency imaging/labs.
   - Example: acute chest pain with diaphoresis and dyspnea (acute MI), loss of consciousness with abnormal breathing (intracranial event), uncontrolled hemorrhage.

2. **Urgent Red Flag** (also "acute threat," "same-day workup")
   - The symptom or sign cluster suggests a **serious condition that requires same-day or next-few-hours evaluation** to rule out or confirm a significant diagnosis.
   - Discriminating criterion: delay of hours to days worsens prognosis; intervention is not immediately bedside but requires prompt testing, imaging, or specialist input.
   - Example: sudden-onset severe headache (subarachnoid hemorrhage, meningitis), focal neurologic deficit (stroke, TIA), acute abdomen with fever and peritoneal signs (appendicitis, perforation).

3. **Concerning Sign** (also "amber flag," "expedited workup")
   - The symptom or sign cluster suggests a **potentially significant diagnosis** that warrants prompt but not emergent evaluation — typically within 24–48 hours.
   - Discriminating criterion: delay measured in days rather than hours affects diagnostic certainty or treatment window; requires investigation but not emergency department level of speed.
   - Example: hemoptysis without respiratory distress (malignancy, TB, bronchiectasis), painless hematuria (renal pathology, malignancy), progressive cognitive decline over weeks (dementia workup, CNS infection).

4. **Routine Finding** (also "baseline," "standard workup")
   - The symptom or sign is **consistent with common, benign conditions** or normal variation; it does not suggest urgent or emergent pathology.
   - Discriminating criterion: the symptom or sign can be evaluated via standard office-based or outpatient investigation without time-critical pressure.
   - Example: mild cough without fever or dyspnea, occasional palpitations in sinus rhythm, minor knee pain after overuse.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify and list the **constellation of signs and symptoms** as the patient presents them. Include onset (sudden vs. gradual), severity (mild/moderate/severe), and any associated features (fever, hemodynamic instability, altered mental status, etc.).

2. Ask: **"Could this presentation reflect an immediately life-threatening condition that will worsen in minutes to hours without intervention?"**
   - If **yes** → **Emergency Red Flag**. Proceed to emergency management; go no further with this frame.
   - If **no**, go to step 3.

3. Ask: **"Could this reflect a serious condition (malignancy, infection, vascular event, CNS pathology, organ failure) that requires same-day evaluation and will significantly worsen if missed for 24+ hours?"**
   - If **yes** → **Urgent Red Flag**. Proceed to urgent/expedited workup.
   - If **no**, go to step 4.

4. Ask: **"Is there a concerning feature — blood in body fluids, progressive neurologic change, unexplained weight loss, night sweats, severe pain unresponsive to usual measures — that suggests pathology requiring investigation within 24–48 hours, even if not emergent?"**
   - If **yes** → **Concerning Sign**. Proceed to prompt outpatient workup.
   - If **no** → **Routine Finding**. Proceed via standard office management.

5. If the presentation includes elements from multiple categories (e.g., mild symptoms but patient is elderly, diabetic, or immunocompromised), classify by the **highest-risk element** present; mitigating factors lower threshold for escalation.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Response pattern | What the clinician should do |
|---|---|---|
| **Emergency Red Flag** | Stabilize → Diagnose → Treat | **Activate emergency response immediately.** Initiate life support, emergency imaging (CT, ECG, ultrasound), labs as parallel process, notify senior physician and relevant specialists. Do not wait for test results before starting empiric intervention if indicated. |
| **Urgent Red Flag** | Rapid assessment → Urgent testing → Diagnosis | **Proceed to ED or urgent same-day evaluation.** Obtain confirmatory testing (imaging, labs, ECG) on expedited timescale. Do not defer to office-based workup; differential includes serious pathology. |
| **Concerning Sign** | Careful history → Targeted testing → Follow-up | **Arrange prompt outpatient investigation** (24–48 hours). Perform targeted history and physical, order appropriate labs or imaging (ultrasound, X-ray, basic labs). If testing is abnormal or inconclusive, escalate to urgent or specialist evaluation. |
| **Routine Finding** | Standard history → Office assessment → Conservative management | **Evaluate via standard office protocol.** Detailed history, exam, and basic testing (urinalysis, CBC if indicated). Reassure if benign; manage expectantly or with common remedies. Schedule return if symptoms persist or worsen. |

For a lenslab agent, the practical implication is which **diagnostic lens** and **response template** to activate:
- Emergency → emergency medicine protocol; life-support and simultaneous diagnostic parallelism.
- Urgent → acute-care protocol; rapid confirmation of serious diagnoses.
- Concerning → prompt investigation protocol; rule out significant pathology within 48 hours.
- Routine → standard office protocol; reassurance and expectant management.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Urgent mistaken for Emergency.** Chest pain that is pleuritic or musculoskeletal (Urgent) vs. crushing substernal pain with diaphoresis (Emergency). The tell is that the patient is hemodynamically stable, pain is localized to a reproducible spot, and EKG is normal — but workup is still same-day, not bedside.
- **Routine mistaken for Concerning.** A patient with mild cough and no fever or risk factors (Routine) vs. mild cough in a smoker with risk factors for malignancy or TB (Concerning). The tell is the presence of a load-bearing risk factor that increases pretest probability of serious pathology.
- **Concerning mistaken for Routine.** Deferring evaluation of hemoptysis, unexplained weight loss, or progressive neurologic change to "wait and see" because the patient looks well. The tell is that the symptom suggests pathology (bleeding, malignancy, CNS disease) even if the patient's appearance is reassuring.
- **Over-escalating based on patient anxiety or severity of reported pain alone.** Severe pain or high anxiety does not automatically raise the flag category if the underlying pathology is benign; conversely, a calm patient with a serious presentation (e.g., quiet, pale, diaphoretic) may have Emergency pathology.
- **Missingcontextual red flags.** A symptom that is Routine in an otherwise healthy 30-year-old (e.g., new-onset palpitations) becomes Concerning in an elderly patient with heart disease, or Emergency in a patient on an arrhythmogenic medication. Always re-classify when significant comorbidity, age, or medication exposure is revealed.

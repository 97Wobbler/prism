# ICF (International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health)

## Overview

The ICF sorts a person's health state and disability profile across four core dimensions: body function/structure, activity limitation, participation restriction, and environmental factors. Its core claim is that disability is not a unidimensional property of the person (as older models suggested) but an **interaction between impairment and context** — the same impairment produces different functional outcomes depending on environmental supports and personal capacity. Classifying a patient or client along all four dimensions rather than stopping at diagnosis reveals which interventions (medical, activity-based, or environmental) are most likely to improve real-world functioning.

## Categories

1. **Body Functions & Structures**
   - The physiological functions and anatomical parts of the body.
   - Impairment is a loss or abnormality of body function or structure.
   - Discriminating criterion: the problem is located in the organ or system itself (e.g., muscle weakness, reduced vision, cognitive impairment from brain injury).
   - Example: loss of grip strength in the hand, reduced lung capacity, visual field defect.

2. **Activity**
   - The execution of a task or action by an individual in real or standardized conditions.
   - Activity limitation is a difficulty the individual may have in executing activities.
   - Discriminating criterion: the problem is in what the person *can do* when tested or observed in a controlled setting, independent of whether they actually do it in daily life.
   - Example: inability to walk 50 meters without pain, difficulty lifting objects above shoulder height, impaired ability to remember a list of words.

3. **Participation**
   - Involvement in a life situation — the person's **actual engagement** in real-world roles and contexts.
   - Participation restriction is a problem the individual experiences in involvement in life situations.
   - Discriminating criterion: the problem emerges when the person attempts to perform their actual social, occupational, or community roles; capacity testing alone may not reveal it.
   - Example: not returning to work despite passing rehabilitation benchmarks, social isolation despite improved mobility, inability to manage household responsibilities despite recovering strength.

4. **Environmental Factors**
   - Features of the physical and social world that interact with the person's body function, activity, and participation.
   - Environmental factors can be **facilitators** (increasing function) or **barriers** (decreasing function); they are independent of the person's impairment.
   - Discriminating criterion: the factor is external — modifiable through assistive devices, accessibility changes, or social/policy intervention, not through medical treatment of the body.
   - Example: wheelchair ramp access (facilitator), absence of workplace accommodation (barrier), family support (facilitator), stigma (barrier), hearing aid availability (facilitator).

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the person and their health condition or disability narrative. State the primary diagnosis or impairment in one sentence.
2. **Body Functions & Structures:** Ask, "What is the physiological or anatomical problem?" (e.g., Which organ or system is impaired? What is the loss or abnormality measurable in isolation?) Document the impairment.
3. **Activity:** Ask, "What can the person *not do* when assessed in a clinical or controlled setting?" (e.g., standardized mobility test, cognitive assessment, strength measurement?) Document the activities limited.
4. **Participation:** Ask, "Which of their actual life roles or situations are they unable or unwilling to engage in?" (e.g., employment, household management, social connection, recreation?) Cross-check against Activity findings — a person may pass an activity test but still not participate in that role due to pain, fear, or other contextual barriers.
5. **Environmental Factors:** Ask, "What in the physical, social, or policy environment currently supports or blocks their participation?" Identify at least one barrier and one potential facilitator for each participation restriction.
6. Synthesize: A complete ICF profile includes findings in all four dimensions. Note where the mismatch is greatest (e.g., good activity capacity but poor participation, or vice versa) — this guides intervention priority.

## Implications per Category

| Dimension | Primary intervention target | Key question |
|---|---|---|
| **Body Functions/Structures** | Medical or surgical treatment; pharmacotherapy; tissue-level rehabilitation | Can we restore or compensate for the physiological loss? |
| **Activity** | Skill training; task-specific practice; adaptive techniques | Can we train the person to do the task, or teach a workaround? |
| **Participation** | Role re-engagement; motivational work; addressing fear or self-efficacy; contextual problem-solving | What is blocking actual return to life roles, and is it capacity, confidence, or circumstance? |
| **Environmental Factors** | Assistive technology; accessibility modification; workplace/social accommodation; policy advocacy | Can we remove barriers or add facilitators faster than we can restore capacity? |

The ICF's practical implication is that **the rehabilitation or treatment plan must address all four dimensions**. A person with excellent activity capacity but severe participation restriction due to environmental barriers (e.g., no accessible transport) needs environmental intervention, not more strength training. Conversely, environmental modifications alone may not help if the underlying activity limitation is too severe.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Confusing Activity with Participation.** A patient passes a timed walking test (Activity: ✓) but does not return to their job (Participation: ✗) because the worksite is inaccessible or social re-entry feels insurmountable. The clinician may incorrectly conclude "the patient is not motivated" rather than recognizing that the barrier is environmental, not physiological.
- **Treating Body Functions as if they determine participation.** The same spinal-cord injury produces very different participation outcomes in countries with strong wheelchair accessibility versus those without. Confusing the diagnosis with the outcome leads to pessimistic prognosis and missed intervention opportunities.
- **Ignoring Environmental Factors.** A patient with moderate activity limitation in dressing may achieve full participation with a simple assistive device (environmental facilitator), but if the clinician focuses only on body-level impairment, the device is never prescribed.
- **Over-weighting diagnosis.** Two people with the same diagnosis (e.g., stroke) can have vastly different activity profiles and participation outcomes. ICF forces the clinician away from "diagnosis = prognosis" thinking.
- **Classifying environmental factors as impairments.** Poverty, lack of childcare, or absence of social support are not body-level impairments — they are environmental factors. Misclassifying them as personal deficits can lead to victim-blaming and ineffective interventions.

# Utilitarianism (Bentham/Mill)

## Overview

Utilitarianism sorts a moral action, policy, or decision by the **causal mechanism through which it produces utility** — understood as happiness, well-being, or the reduction of suffering. Its core claim is that the rightness of an action depends *solely* on whether it maximizes overall well-being for the greatest number, and that different actions or proposals may achieve this goal through fundamentally different pathways. The frame does not tell you *how much* utility each action produces (that is a lens problem), but rather *what kind of utility mechanism* is in play — and that mechanism determines which analytical tools and verification steps are legitimate.

## Categories

1. **Direct Hedonic Utility** (Bentham's core view)
   - The action produces well-being through **immediate sensory pleasure or pain reduction**.
   - Utility is quantifiable, interpersonally comparable, and aggregatable.
   - Discriminating criterion: the good is the absence of suffering and the presence of pleasure; these are intrinsically valuable and require no further justification.
   - Example: providing painkillers to a patient in agony; distributing food to the hungry.

2. **Indirect / Consequentialist Utility** (Mill's "higher pleasures")
   - The action produces well-being through a **chain of intermediate goods** — knowledge, virtue, aesthetic experience, autonomy — that are themselves intrinsically valuable or constitutive of a good life.
   - Utility is still measured by well-being, but well-being is plural and qualitatively differentiated, not a single sensory axis.
   - Discriminating criterion: the agent must justify why the intermediate good (e.g., education) is itself a component of happiness, not merely a means to it.
   - Example: funding universal education; protecting freedom of conscience; creating public art.

3. **Rule Utilitarian Utility**
   - The action is right if it conforms to a **rule that, if universally followed, would maximize overall utility** — even if the action itself produces less utility than breaking the rule would.
   - The locus of moral evaluation shifts from the individual act to the rule governing acts of that type.
   - Discriminating criterion: the agent accepts a constraint on utility-maximization in the present case in order to secure greater utility through rule-following in expectation.
   - Example: keeping a promise even though breaking it would produce more good in this instance; respecting property law even when theft would benefit the poor more.

4. **Preference Utilitarian Utility**
   - The action produces well-being by **satisfying the informed preferences or desires of the agent or affected parties**, rather than by imposing an external standard of pleasure or eudaimonia.
   - Utility is what the agent/patient themselves value, not what an observer deems valuable.
   - Discriminating criterion: the moral agent defers to the expressed or revealed preferences of stakeholders rather than imposing a conception of the good life.
   - Example: respecting an individual's choice of career or lifestyle even if an outsider believes a different path would make them happier; allowing patients to refuse treatment.

5. **Negative Utilitarian Utility** (Popper)
   - The primary moral imperative is to **minimize suffering and harm**, not to maximize pleasure or positive well-being.
   - Utility is asymmetrically weighted toward the avoidance of bad states.
   - Discriminating criterion: the agent prioritizes the elimination of acute suffering over the creation of happiness; harm prevention trumps benefit creation.
   - Example: prioritizing pandemic response over economic growth; investing in pain management before entertainment.

## Classification Procedure

1. State the action or policy in question and identify who is affected by it.
2. Ask: **"Is the mechanism of utility production through immediate sensory pleasure/pain, or through something else?"**
   - If **immediate sensation** → Direct Hedonic Utility (category 1).
   - If **something else**, proceed to step 3.
3. Ask: **"Does the justification appeal to a *rule* that should be universally followed, or to the consequences of this particular action?"**
   - If **rule-governed** → Rule Utilitarian Utility (category 3).
   - If **act-specific consequences**, proceed to step 4.
4. Ask: **"Is the mechanism of well-being production through intermediate goods (knowledge, virtue, autonomy), or through satisfaction of actual preferences?"**
   - If **intermediate goods** (and the agent must justify them as intrinsically valuable) → Indirect/Consequentialist Utility (category 2).
   - If **preference satisfaction** → Preference Utilitarian Utility (category 4).
5. Ask: **"Is the primary moral weight on minimizing suffering, or on maximizing well-being?"**
   - If **minimizing harm is the priority** → Negative Utilitarian Utility (category 5).
   - If **maximizing well-being** → return to step 2 and classify the mechanism of that maximization.
6. State the category and name the **verification procedure** implied by it (see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Category | What the agent should prioritize | Verification method |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct Hedonic** | Quantifiable reduction of pain and increase of pleasure; interpersonal comparison of sensations. | Measure reported/observable hedonic states; aggregate across all affected parties; compare to counterfactual. |
| **Indirect / Consequentialist** | Justification of why the intermediate good is intrinsically valuable; that it is plural and qualitatively ranked. | Defend the list of higher pleasures or goods; examine whether the good is truly intrinsic or merely instrumental; test whether all segments of society have equal access. |
| **Rule Utilitarian** | Identification of the rule; proof that universal compliance would maximize utility; constraint on act-level deviation. | Articulate the rule explicitly; show that rule-following is optimal in expectation; verify that the agent's action coheres with the rule. |
| **Preference Utilitarian** | Identification and respect for the informed preferences of stakeholders; avoidance of paternalism. | Elicit preferences from affected parties; verify they are informed and not coerced; give them moral weight even if an observer disagrees. |
| **Negative Utilitarian** | Elimination of acute suffering as the primary driver; asymmetric weighting of harm over benefit. | Identify the worst-case outcomes and prioritize their prevention; measure suffering reduction directly; accept opportunity costs. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Direct Hedonic mistaken for Indirect/Consequentialist.** Claiming that education is a higher pleasure when the actual justification is that education produces income (a hedonic intermediate good). The tell is that the agent cannot articulate why education itself, independent of its consequences, is intrinsically valuable.

- **Rule Utilitarian mistaken for Indirect/Consequentialist.** Justifying an action by appeal to a rule (e.g., "we must respect property") when the underlying motive is really just the hedonic consequences of respect-based social order. The tell is that the agent abandons the rule if a specific case would produce much more utility by breaking it.

- **Preference Utilitarian mistaken for Direct Hedonic.** Asking "What do people prefer?" while assuming all preferences reduce to pleasure; ignoring that some preferences are non-hedonic (autonomy, dignity, meaningful work). The tell is that the agent cannot explain why they defer to a preference that seems not to maximize the person's pleasure.

- **Negative Utilitarian mistaken for rule-based harm-minimization.** Conflating the principle "minimize harm" (which prioritizes suffering reduction) with a precautionary rule (which limits risky actions). The tell is that Negative Utilitarianism accepts actions with high upside if the downside is eliminated, whereas precaution restricts them outright.

- **Applying utilitarianism to an incommensurable good.** Treating a non-utility value (e.g., justice, rights, dignity) as if it were an input to the utility function. The tell is that the agent must either defend why the good *is* part of well-being (shifting to Indirect/Consequentialist) or acknowledge that utilitarianism is not the right frame for the decision.

# Virtue Ethics (Aristotle)

## Overview

Virtue Ethics sorts a character disposition, habit, or action into one of five categories based on whether it represents a stable, cultivated excellence of character or a deviation from it. Its core claim is that moral life is fundamentally about **developing stable traits** (virtues) through repeated practice and habituation, not about following rules or calculating outcomes. Different dispositions demand structurally different responses: a person entrenched in a vice needs a different intervention than someone in the mean, and someone in a state of moral development requires different support than someone who has achieved stable virtue. The frame asserts that the **quality of the agent's character** — not the act alone — determines moral worth.

## Categories

1. **Virtue (Arete)**
   - A stable, cultivated disposition to act, feel, and desire well in a given domain.
   - Virtue is a mean between two extremes (deficiency and excess), identified by *phronesis* (practical wisdom).
   - Discriminating criterion: the person reliably chooses the right action, at the right time, in the right measure, with the right motive, and feels the right emotions about it — and does so from a settled state of character, not from external compulsion or calculation.
   - Example: courage as the mean between cowardice and recklessness; generosity as the mean between stinginess and profligacy.

2. **Vice of Deficiency (Elleipsis)**
   - A stable disposition to fall short of the mean; to act, feel, or desire too little.
   - The vice is rooted in habit and character, not accident.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person consistently avoids the virtuous action because their character is habituated away from it, even when the situation calls for it.
   - Example: cowardice (too little courage), stinginess (too little generosity), self-deprecation (too little proper self-regard).

3. **Vice of Excess (Hyperbolē)**
   - A stable disposition to overshoot the mean; to act, feel, or desire too much.
   - Like deficiency, this is a settled character trait, not a momentary lapse.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person habitually goes beyond what the situation warrants, driven by a character that has been shaped to excess.
   - Example: recklessness (too much courage), profligacy (too much generosity), vanity (too much self-regard).

4. **Continence (Enkrateia) / Incontinence (Akrasia)**
   - A **temporary state** in which the person acts rightly (continence) or wrongly (incontinence) against their settled disposition.
   - In continence, the person knows the virtuous action but must struggle against their current desire or habit to do it.
   - In incontinence, the person knows the virtuous action but fails to do it because their appetite or passion overcomes their judgment in the moment.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is not yet habituated to virtue; they must exert effort, and their behavior is inconsistent. They are *becoming*, not yet *being*.
   - Example: someone who gives generously but only after a struggle against stinginess; someone who refrains from anger through willpower but feels the anger acutely.

5. **Moral Immaturity / Brutishness (Thēriōtēs)**
   - A state in which the person is not yet capable of virtue or has fallen below the threshold of human moral capacity.
   - This includes both those still in early childhood (not yet rational agents) and those who have become enslaved to appetite or passion to a degree that reasoning no longer guides them.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person either lacks the rational capacity to deliberate about the mean, or has so far corrupted that capacity that restoration is nearly impossible.
   - Example: an infant (pre-moral); someone consumed entirely by addiction or rage.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Identify the domain of character.** Name the virtue in question (courage, generosity, temperance, etc.) and the specific situation or pattern of behavior being assessed.

2. **Ask: Is the person's action or disposition stable and habitual?**
   - If no (the action is a struggle, or occurs inconsistently), go to step 4.
   - If yes, go to step 3.

3. **Ask: Does the person reliably choose and feel the mean, identified by practical wisdom?**
   - If yes → Virtue.
   - If no, ask: Does the person systematically fall short (fear when boldness is needed, stinge when generosity is needed)? → Vice of Deficiency.
   - If the person systematically overshoots (rash when courage is needed, profligate when generosity is needed)? → Vice of Excess.

4. **Is the person capable of rational deliberation and moral growth?**
   - If no (child, or severely corrupted) → Moral Immaturity / Brutishness.
   - If yes, ask: Does the person struggle *toward* the right action (knows it, fights appetite to do it)? → Continence.
   - Does the person struggle *against* the right action (knows it, but appetite wins)? → Incontinence.

5. **State the classification** and name what kind of moral work is required: stabilization of a new habit (for Incontinence/Continence), reversal of an ingrained vice, or further refinement of virtue.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Moral Status | What Kind of Response Is Needed |
|---|---|---|
| **Virtue** | The goal state. | Maintenance and deepening; opportunities for practice in harder contexts; mentoring of others. |
| **Vice of Deficiency** | Rooted character flaw. | Habituation in the opposite direction; repeated exposure to the virtuous action in low-stakes settings; reframing of fear or reluctance. |
| **Vice of Excess** | Rooted character flaw. | Habituation to restraint; practice in recognizing when to stop; development of a sense of proportion. |
| **Continence** | Morally praiseworthy but unstable. | Continued practice of the virtuous action until it becomes automatic; removal of obstacles to habit formation; support for the struggle. |
| **Incontinence** | Morally blameworthy and unstable. | Intervention on appetite and passion (self-care, physical environment); possibility of moral education; accountability; recognition that willpower alone is insufficient. |
| **Moral Immaturity / Brutishness** | Pre-moral or sub-moral. | Education and habituation (if child or early stage); or, if severely corrupted, isolation, restraint, or acceptance that redemption is unlikely. |

The key insight: an agent in Virtue can be trusted with autonomy. An agent in Continence or Incontinence needs support and structure. An agent in Vice needs intervention on the roots of habit, not just the surface action.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Virtue mistaken for Continence.** A person performs a virtuous act without visible struggle and is called virtuous, but they have not yet made it a stable trait — they are still in early continence. The tell is that they require willpower for every instance, or they fail under stress. The consequence is overestimating their moral reliability.

- **Vice of Deficiency mistaken for Incontinence.** A cowardly person avoids danger; a continent person who lacks courage struggles but faces it anyway. The tell is whether the person *knows* they should act otherwise and fights themselves, or whether they see no compelling reason to act. The consequence is choosing the wrong intervention (therapy for appetite vs. habituation toward courage).

- **Vice of Excess mistaken for Virtue in a different domain.** Recklessness can masquerade as courage; profligacy as generosity. The tell is whether the action is proportioned to the situation and guided by *phronesis*, or whether it overshoots. The consequence is celebrating a vice as a virtue.

- **Incontinence mistaken for Moral Immaturity.** A young adult struggles with temper and acts badly despite knowing better (incontinence); a child has not yet developed the reasoning capacity (immaturity). The tell is whether the person demonstrates rational deliberation *about* the problem, even if they fail to execute. The consequence is over-pathologizing a normal stage of moral development, or under-recognizing when someone has fallen into genuine brutishness.

- **Treating all five as equally remediable.** Virtue is stable by definition and requires maintenance, not reversal. Continence and Incontinence are temporary states that can shift with practice. Vice is entrenched and may require active de-habituation. Immaturity is pre-rational or sub-rational. Applying the same response to all five is a category error.

# Trolley Problem

## Overview

The Trolley Problem is a classification frame that sorts a moral dilemma by the **structure of agency and causation** embedded in the choice. Its core claim is that identical outcomes can generate radically different moral intuitions depending on whether the agent achieves those outcomes through **direct action** or **omission**, and that this distinction reveals whether an agent's moral reasoning is **deontological** (duty-bound, action-averse) or **consequentialist** (outcome-focused, action-neutral). The frame is not prescriptive — it does not tell you which answer is correct — but it does tell you which category of moral reasoning is in play, and therefore which kinds of objections and follow-up arguments are coherent.

## Categories

1. **Action / Positive Duty** (trolley diversion)
   - The agent must **actively intervene** to achieve the better outcome.
   - The agent's own deliberate action is the **direct causal mechanism** that produces the change.
   - Discriminating criterion: inaction leaves the status quo; action changes it. The agent's hands are not clean if they do nothing.
   - Example: pulling a lever to divert a runaway trolley from five people onto one person.

2. **Omission / Negative Duty** (trolley inaction)
   - The agent must **allow an outcome to occur** without intervening.
   - The agent's role is **passive acceptance** of a causal chain already in motion; they do not start it.
   - Discriminating criterion: intervention is required to change the outcome; inaction preserves the status quo that already exists.
   - Example: standing by while a runaway trolley kills five people, refusing to pull a lever that would divert it.

3. **Personal Force / Bodily Integrity** (pushing the bystander)
   - The agent must **use another person's body as a means** to achieve the outcome.
   - The intervention requires **violating the bodily autonomy or integrity** of an innocent third party.
   - Discriminating criterion: the harm to the one is not a side effect but a **necessary instrumentality** of the action; you cannot achieve the outcome without their participation or physical sacrifice.
   - Example: pushing a large bystander off a bridge to stop the trolley, using their body as a physical obstruction.

4. **Intended vs. Foreseen Harm** (doctrine of double effect)
   - The agent's action produces both a **desired good outcome and an undesired harmful outcome**.
   - The harm is **not the means to the good**, but a foreseen side effect.
   - Discriminating criterion: remove the harm from the causal chain; the good outcome still follows. If the harm is essential to achieving the good, this category does not apply.
   - Example: diverting a trolley to save five people, foreseeing that one person on the alternate track will die, but not *because* they die — their death is collateral, not the mechanism.

5. **Trolley-Neutral / Harm Equivalence** (no moral distinction)
   - The agent **rejects the framework** that distinguishes action from omission, intention from foresight, or personal force from impersonal harm.
   - All that matters is the **net consequence**: five deaths vs. one death.
   - Discriminating criterion: the agent explicitly states that the structure of agency is morally irrelevant; only outcomes are.
   - Example: an agent who says "pull the lever or don't; five lives are worth more than one, and that's the only fact that matters."

## Classification Procedure

1. Describe the dilemma: **What must the agent do, and what is the cost?**
2. Ask: **"Does the better outcome require the agent to actively intervene, or does it require the agent to do nothing?"**
   - If **active intervention is required to achieve the better outcome** → Action / Positive Duty.
   - If **inaction allows the worse outcome to occur** → Omission / Negative Duty.
3. Ask: **"Does achieving the better outcome require using another person's body or agency as a means?"**
   - If **yes, their physical or bodily sacrifice is necessary to the mechanism** → Personal Force / Bodily Integrity.
   - If **no; the person's harm is a foreseen side effect, not the means** → Intended vs. Foreseen Harm.
4. Ask: **"Is the agent reasoning about the structure of the choice, or only about the numbers?"**
   - If **structure matters to their reasoning** (duty, omission, intention) → one of the above.
   - If **only net outcomes matter** → Trolley-Neutral / Harm Equivalence.
5. State the category and identify which **moral framework** (deontological, consequentialist, or hybrid) licenses it.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Moral framework | What the reasoning licenses |
|---|---|---|
| **Action / Positive Duty** | Deontological | Agent bears **causal and intentional responsibility** for pulling the lever; this is psychologically and morally costly even if outcomes favor it. |
| **Omission / Negative Duty** | Deontological | Agent bears **no responsibility for acts they do not perform**; inaction is morally distinct from action and can be preferred even if outcomes are worse. |
| **Personal Force / Bodily Integrity** | Deontological / Rights-based | Even if consequences are favorable, **using an innocent person as a means violates their rights**; this violation cannot be overridden by utilitarian calculus alone. |
| **Intended vs. Foreseen Harm** | Doctrine of Double Effect (hybrid) | **Intended harms require justification**; foreseen harms can be permitted if the good outweighs them and the harm is not the means. Structure of intention matters, not only outcomes. |
| **Trolley-Neutral / Harm Equivalence** | Consequentialist | **Only outcomes matter**; all distinctions of action, omission, and intention are morally inert. A framework of pure consequence-ranking renders the dilemma structureless. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Confusing Omission with Trolley-Neutral.** An agent who refuses to pull the lever may be reasoning "I should not actively harm the one" (Omission / Negative Duty, deontological) rather than "outcomes don't matter, only principles do" (Trolley-Neutral, consequentialist). The tell is whether the agent would *also* refuse if the outcome could be reversed (pulling the lever to save the one at cost to five). True Trolley-Neutral accepts either choice; true Omission does not.

- **Mistaking Personal Force for Intended vs. Foreseen Harm.** Pushing the bystander is Personal Force (their body is the mechanism); diverting the trolley such that it happens to hit them is Foreseen Harm (they are not the means, only collateral). The tell is whether removing the person from the track removes the good outcome as well.

- **Treating all deontological objections as equivalent.** An agent may reject pulling the lever because "inaction is not my responsibility" (Omission) or because "using the one as a tool is wrong" (Personal Force / Rights). These are different deontological positions and support different follow-up questions.

- **Assuming Trolley-Neutral agents lack moral reasoning.** Pure consequentialists are not amoral; they are reasoning clearly — just from a framework that brackets structure and attends only to net good. Misidentifying this as confusion or callousness obscures the actual disagreement.

- **Applying Trolley categories to real dilemmas with uncertainty.** The Trolley frame assumes perfect knowledge of outcomes and a binary choice. Real medical, military, or policy decisions have uncertainty, distributed agency, and long causal chains; the frame becomes less discriminating in those contexts.

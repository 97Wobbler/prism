# Categorical Imperative (Kant)

## Overview

The Categorical Imperative sorts proposed actions or maxims (personal rules of conduct) by whether they can be universalized as a law of nature without logical or practical contradiction. Its core claim is that moral permissibility is not grounded in consequences, emotions, or utility, but in the **internal consistency of the principle when universalized**. An action is morally obligatory only if its maxim could become a universal law without contradiction. The frame separates duties (unconditional imperatives) from hypothetical bargains (conditional imperatives), and separates genuinely universalizable principles from those that fail the universality test.

## Categories

1. **Universalizable Duty (perfect duty)**
   - The maxim of the action can be willed as a universal law without logical or practical contradiction.
   - Universalizing the maxim produces a coherent, stable world.
   - Discriminating criterion: you can rationally will that *everyone* act this way in similar circumstances, and the practice does not undermine its own conditions.
   - Example: "Keep promises when convenient" fails (universalizing it eliminates trust, undermining promises themselves). "Tell the truth" passes.

2. **Imperfect Duty (duty with latitude)**
   - The maxim can be universalized, but admits multiple ways of fulfilling it; obligation is binding but not exhaustively prescribed.
   - You are obligated to act on the principle, but the specific form and timing are within your rational discretion.
   - Discriminating criterion: the universalized maxim remains coherent, but the agent retains latitude in *how* and *when* to instantiate it.
   - Example: "Develop your talents" or "Help others in distress" — universalizable, binding, but leave room for individual judgment on proportion and context.

3. **Non-universalizable Maxim (violates the categorical imperative)**
   - The maxim cannot be willed as a universal law without logical or practical self-contradiction.
   - Universalizing the maxim either creates a logical impossibility or undermines the very practice or institution it presupposes.
   - Discriminating criterion: if everyone acted on this maxim, the world would lack the conditions necessary for that very action to be possible or coherent.
   - Example: "Lie when it serves your interest" (universalizing it destroys the trust that makes lying work); "Default on loans while asking for credit" (universalizing it collapses the credit system).

4. **Hypothetical Imperative (not a duty; conditional)**
   - The action is rational *only if* you have a prior desire or goal, and the action is a means to that goal.
   - The obligation is conditional: "If you want X, then do Y" — not binding if you abandon the goal.
   - Discriminating criterion: the action has no moral force independent of whether the agent desires the end.
   - Example: "If you want to be healthy, exercise regularly" or "If you want to write well, study grammar" — both prudential, not moral imperatives.

5. **Treating a Person Merely as a Means (violates dignity formulation)**
   - The action uses another rational agent as an instrument for your ends, without regard for their own dignity or rational agency.
   - The agent is not respected as an end in themselves.
   - Discriminating criterion: the action denies the other person the freedom to form or pursue their own rational ends; they are manipulated or coerced.
   - Example: deceiving someone to exploit them; coercing obedience; treating a human as a tool rather than a rational agent with their own ends.
   - Note: this is the second formulation of the categorical imperative and overlaps with category 3, but is distinct in principle: it focuses on **dignity and agency** rather than logical universalizability.

## Classification Procedure

1. Write the action or decision in the form of a maxim: "I will do X in circumstance Y in order to achieve Z." Be explicit about the end.

2. Apply the **universality test**: Ask, "Could I rationally will that *everyone* in similar circumstances act on this maxim?"

3. Check for **logical contradiction**: If everyone universalized the maxim, would the world still be logically coherent? For example, if the maxim presupposes a practice (promising, lying, borrowing), would universalization destroy that practice?

4. If the maxim passes universality, check: **Is the obligation binding regardless of my desires?** If yes → Category 1 or 2. If the action is only rational given a prior desire or goal → Category 4 (not a duty).

5. Within binding duties (1 and 2), ask: **Is the way and timing of fulfillment specified, or do I retain discretion?** Narrowly specified → Category 1. Discretionary → Category 2.

6. If the maxim fails universality OR the action treats another person as a mere instrument → Categories 3 or 5. Record which.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Moral Status | What the Agent Should Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Universalizable Duty (perfect)** | Obligatory, binding, precise | Perform the action; it is a strict duty with no exceptions or latitude. |
| **Imperfect Duty** | Obligatory, binding, discretionary | Adopt the principle; fulfill it through means and timing you judge appropriate. |
| **Non-universalizable** | Forbidden | Reject the action; it is morally impermissible. No circumstance makes it obligatory. |
| **Hypothetical Imperative** | Rational but not moral | Perform it only if you endorse the underlying goal; it is not a duty. |
| **Treating as Mere Means** | Forbidden | Reject the action; respect the rational agency of others. Seek consent or reform the action to respect their ends. |

**Practical implication**: An agent in a real decision must classify the proposed action into one of these categories before deciding whether it is morally permissible. Only categories 1 and 2 yield duties. Categories 3, 4, and 5 require rejection or reframing.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Universalizable Duty mistaken for Hypothetical Imperative.** Confusing a duty that seems inconvenient ("I don't want to keep this promise") with one that is only prudential. The tell: ask whether the obligation persists even if you no longer desire the end. If yes, it is a duty.

- **Imperfect Duty mistaken for Hypothetical Imperative.** Treating "Help others" as merely conditional on benevolence ("only if I feel like it"), when it is in fact a binding duty with discretion in execution. The tell: universalizing "Refuse to help anyone" fails the categorical test; the duty is binding, not contingent.

- **Non-universalizable mistaken for Universalizable.** Believing a self-serving maxim is permissible because you have not yet universalized it. The tell: formally test universalization by asking whether rational agents, knowing all others act the same way, would create a coherent practice around it.

- **Mere Means mistaken for Legitimate Influence.** Assuming that any action that achieves your goal is permissible if the other person technically consents. The tell: was consent obtained under duress, deception, or by withholding critical information? Did you treat their rational agency as a constraint, or as an obstacle to work around?

- **Hypothetical Imperative mistaken for Duty.** Treating instrumental advice ("If you want success, work hard") as a moral obligation when it is merely prudential. The tell: the obligation vanishes if the goal changes. A true duty persists independent of outcomes or desires.

# Representational Systems (VAK)

## Overview

The Representational Systems frame sorts individuals by their **primary sensory modality** for encoding, storing, and retrieving information: Visual (sight), Auditory (sound/language), or Kinesthetic (touch/movement/emotion). Its core claim is that people who prefer different modalities will naturally describe the same experience using different language, attend to different features of the environment, and respond more readily to interventions matched to their modality. Matching communication or teaching to a person's preferred system yields faster learning and stronger rapport; mismatching is a systematic source of incomplete transfer and frustration ("you're not listening" / "you're not seeing my point").

## Categories

1. **Visual**
   - Preferentially encodes information as **mental images, spatial relationships, and visual patterns**.
   - Thinks in pictures; remembers faces and places; notices appearance and arrangement.
   - Discriminating criterion: uses visual language (see, look, picture, bright, clear, focus), recalls events as snapshots, and is distracted by visual clutter.
   - Example: an architect who mentally rotates building plans; a person who says "I see what you mean" and describes a conversation as "getting the big picture."

2. **Auditory**
   - Preferentially encodes information as **sound, rhythm, tone, and language sequences**.
   - Thinks in words and dialogue; remembers names and what people said; is attuned to how things sound.
   - Discriminating criterion: uses auditory language (hear, listen, sounds like, resonates, in tune), recalls events as dialogue or narrative, and is distracted by noise.
   - Example: a musician or podcaster who thinks through talking aloud; a person who says "that doesn't sound right" and recalls a meeting by what was discussed.

3. **Kinesthetic**
   - Preferentially encodes information as **movement, texture, emotion, and bodily sensation**.
   - Thinks through action and feeling; remembers experiences and gut reactions; is attuned to physical comfort and emotional tone.
   - Discriminating criterion: uses kinesthetic language (feel, touch, grasp, solid, tight, grounded), recalls events by how they felt emotionally or physically, and needs movement or hands-on practice to learn.
   - Example: a dancer or athlete who learns by doing; a person who says "I feel like this won't work" and needs to "get a handle on" the problem before proceeding.

## Classification Procedure

1. Observe or ask the person to **describe a recent experience** — a meeting, a decision, a memory — without prompting for a particular modality.
2. **Listen to the language used.** Mark each sensory word or phrase:
   - Visual: see, look, picture, view, focus, bright, clear, visualize, perspective, image, color, shape.
   - Auditory: hear, listen, sound, tone, voice, rings a bell, talks, discusses, rhythm, resonates.
   - Kinesthetic: feel, touch, grasp, move, solid, grounded, flow, pressure, tight, warm, handle.
3. **Tally the modalities** represented in the person's natural language. The modality with the most references is the primary system.
4. If the language is **evenly distributed**, ask a **direct probe**: "When you think about that situation, do you see it (visual), hear it (auditory), or feel it (kinesthetic) more strongly?"
5. Record the primary system *and note any secondary systems* — most people are not pure monolinguals; the frame identifies the **preferred channel**, not the only one.

## Implications per Category

| Representational System | How to communicate or teach | What the person needs |
|---|---|---|
| **Visual** | Use diagrams, charts, spatial analogies, color, and written bullet points. Describe outcomes visually ("picture the result"). | To *see* the information; written summaries and visual demonstrations. Minimizes distraction by clutter. |
| **Auditory** | Use dialogue, narrative, metaphor, and spoken explanation. Describe reasoning step-by-step ("here's how this flows"). | To *hear* the reasoning; discussion, verbal walkthrough, and the chance to talk it through. Benefits from repetition and rhythm. |
| **Kinesthetic** | Use hands-on practice, physical analogies, emotional context, and real-world experience. Describe by effect ("you'll feel the difference"). | To *do* and *feel*; trial-and-error, practice, and emotional safety. Benefits from movement and physical engagement. |

**Coaching implication:** A person stuck on a problem often needs a translation, not a repeat. If a visual learner is confused by your auditory explanation, switch modalities — draw it instead of talking about it. The issue is rarely their intelligence; it is usually a mismatch.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Confusing preferred modality with ability or intelligence.** A kinesthetic thinker who needs to move around while learning is not less intellectual than a visual one; they are using a different encoding path. Forcing one system onto someone with a different preference slows transfer, not enables it.
- **Treating modality as binary or immutable.** Most people use all three systems; the frame identifies *preference*, not exclusive use. A person can be primarily visual and still benefit from auditory input — the frame only says that visual will be their natural entry point.
- **Over-relying on surface language alone.** Someone might say "I see your point" (visual language) while actually thinking aurally. Listen for the **pattern across multiple descriptions**, not a single phrase. A one-word visual reference does not make someone visual.
- **Assuming a modality preference based on profession.** Not all engineers are visual thinkers (many are auditory or kinesthetic); not all artists are visual. Classify the individual, not the role.
- **Ignoring secondary modalities.** A person whose primary system is visual but who uses significant auditory language may benefit from both visual *and* dialogue — the frame does not rule out the secondary mode, only highlights the primary path.

# Landscape Ecology

## Overview

Landscape Ecology sorts a territorial ecosystem into three fundamental
spatial components — **patch, corridor, and matrix** — based on the
function each plays in the movement of organisms, energy, and information
across the landscape. Its core claim is that the same ecological process
(species migration, nutrient flow, disturbance spread) behaves
**structurally differently** depending on which component it travels
through, and that identifying the right component is necessary to predict
landscape-scale outcomes. A patch-level intervention may fail because the
real constraint is connectivity; a corridor-level intervention may fail
because the matrix permeability has not changed. The frame is not
prescriptive — it is sense-making that disambiguates which scale and
component the problem actually occupies.

## Categories

1. **Patch**
   - A relatively homogeneous area of habitat or land use that differs from
     its surroundings; internally uniform in structure and disturbance
     regime.
   - Discriminating criterion: organisms or processes within the patch
     respond to internal conditions more strongly than to external
     connectivity; the boundary is a meaningful filter.
   - Example: a forest stand, a prairie wetland, an urban park, a
     monoculture agricultural field.

2. **Corridor**
   - A linear or network-like landscape feature that connects otherwise
     fragmented patches; **facilitates movement and gene flow** between
     patches while itself being distinct from the matrix.
   - Discriminating criterion: the feature is traveled by organisms or
     information; its presence or absence noticeably changes connectivity
     among patches. Internal homogeneity is secondary to its connective
     function.
   - Example: a riparian buffer along a stream, a hedgerow between fields,
     a wildlife underpass beneath a highway, a greenway in an urban grid.

3. **Matrix**
   - The dominant landscape component — the background habitat or land use
     that surrounds patches and through which corridors run; usually the
     most extensive and best-connected.
   - Discriminating criterion: the matrix is identified by prevalence and
     connectivity, not by uniform internal character. Its permeability to
     movement varies but is always non-zero; organisms can traverse it,
     though at cost.
   - Example: in a forest region, grassland or agriculture is the matrix;
     in an urban region, the built environment is the matrix.
   - Note: matrix identity is relative to the organism and question being
     asked. What is a patch for one species may be matrix for another.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Define the landscape boundary and the focal organism or process.** A
   landscape is scale-relative: choose a region appropriate to dispersal
   distance, home range, or the scale of the phenomenon being studied
   (species recolonization, nutrient cycling, fire spread, etc.).

2. **Map the dominant land use or habitat types** across the region. Assign
   each area to one of the three categories: is it homogeneous and
   internally self-contained (Patch)? Linear or connective (Corridor)?
   Extensive and pervasive (Matrix)?

3. **Apply the majority rule for Matrix identification.** The land-use type
   that occupies the most area and is most connected is the matrix; all
   others are potential patches or corridors. If ambiguous, ask: *which
   component is the organism moving through most easily?*

4. **Assess corridor function.** Is the linear feature a **connector** (it
   facilitates movement between patches that would otherwise be isolated)
   or merely a **patch** (it is habitat in itself, but does not
   substantially increase patch connectivity)? The tell is whether removing
   the feature increases patch isolation.

5. **Record the classification as a map or description:** for each
   identified patch, state its size, shape, internal homogeneity, and
   nearest corridor or patch. For each corridor, state its width, length,
   continuity, and which patches it connects. For the matrix, state its
   permeability to the focal organism.

## Implications per Category

| Component | Key dynamic | Management implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Patch** | Internal habitat quality, isolation by distance, edge effects | Enlarge where possible; reduce edge-to-area ratio; ensure internal homogeneity matches organism needs. Isolation is the primary constraint. |
| **Corridor** | Connectivity, linearity, internal permeability | Maintain width and continuity; remove barriers within the corridor; use as vector for assisted migration or habitat expansion. Corridor loss is often the binding constraint on regional persistence. |
| **Matrix** | Permeability, disturbance regime, flow resistance | Modify matrix permeability where possible (e.g., reduce fragmentation, remove barriers, increase tolerance habitat); recognize that matrix changes often have larger landscape-scale effects than patch-level work. |

For a landscape agent, the practical implication is which **scale and type
of intervention** is proportionate to the problem:
- If the constraint is patch size or quality → patch-level restoration.
- If the constraint is isolation (patches exist but don't exchange) → corridor
  development.
- If the constraint is universal fragmentation or permeability → matrix-level
  land-use change.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Confusing patch with corridor.** A linear riparian habitat may *look*
  like a corridor but function as a patch if the organism cannot move along
  it efficiently. The tell is whether organisms are observed dispersing
  through the feature or aggregating within it; true corridors enable
  between-patch movement.

- **Misidentifying the matrix.** Treating the rarest habitat as the matrix
  because of its ecological importance (e.g., calling a rare wetland the
  matrix in an agricultural region). The tell is area and connectivity: the
  matrix is identified by prevalence and ease of traversal, not by value.
  The wetland is a patch.

- **Scale confusion.** A feature that is a patch at the regional scale is
  the matrix at the local scale. Failing to specify the focal organism and
  landscape boundary leads to contradictory classifications. Revisit the
  scope before retrying.

- **Treating corridors as sufficient for connectivity.** Assuming that
  restoring a corridor solves fragmentation when the matrix permeability is
  still zero. The tell is that patches remain isolated even after corridor
  installation; the matrix is the real barrier.

- **Assuming matrix homogeneity.** The matrix is not uniform — it can have
  patches and corridors within it — but it is the dominant background. A
  landscape with a heterogeneous matrix still has a single matrix identity;
  the subordinate features within it are patches or sub-corridors relative
  to it.

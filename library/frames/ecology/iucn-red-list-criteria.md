# IUCN Red List Criteria

## Overview

The IUCN Red List Criteria sorts a species into one of five risk categories based on **quantitative thresholds** applied to population size, range, decline rate, and other measurable parameters. Its core claim is that a **standardized, evidence-based system** prevents both over-investment in species that are robust and under-investment in those at genuine risk. Unlike subjective assessments, the IUCN framework makes the decision rule explicit and reproducible — different assessors applying the same data to the same species should reach the same category (absent genuine data disputes).

## Categories

1. **Extinct (EX)**
   - No individual of the species is known to exist in the wild, and no captive population exists.
   - Discriminating criterion: exhaustive surveys of known or likely habitat have been conducted over an appropriate time period (typically several decades for long-lived species) and yielded no sightings.
   - Example: Pinta Island tortoise (*Chelonoidis abingdonii*); thylacine (*Thylacinus cynocephalus*).

2. **Extinct in the Wild (EW)**
   - The species survives only in cultivation, captivity, or as a naturalized population outside its native range; no wild population remains.
   - Discriminating criterion: the wild population has been searched for and not found, but the species persists in human-managed settings.
   - Example: Arabian oryx (*Oryx leucoryx*) before reintroduction; Alagoas curassow (*Mitu mitu*).

3. **Critically Endangered (CR)**
   - The species faces an **extremely high risk of extinction** in the wild, meeting at least one of: (a) population size < 250 mature individuals or declining > 25% in 3 years; (b) range < 100 km²; (c) > 80% of population in a single subpopulation; (d) quantitative analysis showing > 50% extinction probability within 10 years.
   - Discriminating criterion: multiple data points suggest the population is on a knife's edge; without immediate intervention, extinction is likely within years to a few decades.
   - Example: Sumatran rhinoceros (*Dicerorhinus sumatrensis*); Philippine eagle (*Pithecophaga jefferyi*).

4. **Endangered (EN)**
   - The species faces a **high risk of extinction**, meeting at least one of: (a) population < 2,500 mature individuals or declining > 20% in 5 years; (b) range < 5,000 km²; (c) quantitative analysis showing > 20% extinction probability within 20 years.
   - Discriminating criterion: the population is substantially reduced or fragmented, but not yet in immediate terminal decline; conservation action can still be effective.
   - Example: Mountain gorilla (*Gorilla beringei beringei*); leatherback sea turtle (*Dermochelys coriacea*).

5. **Vulnerable (VU)**
   - The species faces a **moderate risk of extinction**, meeting at least one of: (a) population < 10,000 mature individuals or declining > 10% in 10 years; (b) range < 20,000 km²; (c) quantitative analysis showing > 10% extinction probability within 100 years.
   - Discriminating criterion: the species is affected by threats (habitat loss, overexploitation, climate change) but still has a substantial population or range across which recovery is possible with intervention.
   - Example: Giant panda (*Ailuropoda melanoleuca*); snow leopard (*Panthera uncia*).

6. **Near Threatened (NT)**
   - The species does **not currently qualify** for CR, EN, or VU, but is **close to qualifying** or likely to qualify in the near future based on current trends and threats.
   - Discriminating criterion: the species shows declining trends or faces emerging threats, but the population or range is still sufficiently large that it does not yet meet any of the quantitative thresholds above.
   - Example: Polar bear (*Ursus maritimus*); king penguin (*Aptenodytes patagonicus*).

7. **Least Concern (LC)**
   - The species meets **none of the criteria** for CR, EN, VU, or NT and does not qualify as EW or EX.
   - Discriminating criterion: the population is stable or increasing, the range is large, and threats are either absent or not driving decline.
   - Example: domestic dog (*Canis familiaris*); American robin (*Turdus migratorius*); most widespread wild species.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Confirm species identity and data availability.** Gather the best available evidence on: (a) current population size (census or estimate) and number of mature individuals; (b) geographic range; (c) population trend over the past 10–100 years; (d) known threats; (e) subpopulation structure. If data are genuinely unavailable, note this and use the Precautionary Principle (assume higher risk).

2. **Check for extinction status.** Ask: *Is there any wild population known to exist anywhere in the species' native range in the past 50 years?*
   - If no → proceed to step 3 (EX vs. EW).
   - If yes → proceed to step 4 (CR through LC).

3. **Distinguish EX from EW.** Ask: *Does the species survive anywhere outside the wild (captive breeding, zoos, cultivation)?*
   - If no → **Extinct (EX)**.
   - If yes → **Extinct in the Wild (EW)**.

4. **Apply quantitative thresholds in order of severity.** For each of the five criteria below, check whether the species meets it:
   - **(A) Population size**: mature individuals < 250 (CR), < 2,500 (EN), or < 10,000 (VU)?
   - **(B) Geographic range**: extent of occurrence < 100 km² (CR), < 5,000 km² (EN), or < 20,000 km² (VU)?
   - **(C) Population decline**: > 25% decline in 3 years (CR), > 20% in 5 years (EN), or > 10% in 10 years (VU)?
   - **(D) Population fragmentation**: > 95% in one subpopulation (CR), > 90% (EN), or > 95% with declining overall trend (VU)?
   - **(E) Quantitative analysis**: modeling shows > 50% extinction risk in 10 years (CR), > 20% in 20 years (EN), or > 10% in 100 years (VU)?

5. **Determine the highest-severity category for which the species qualifies.** If the species meets any threshold for CR, assign CR. If not, but meets an EN threshold, assign EN. If not, but meets a VU threshold, assign VU.

6. **If the species does not meet CR, EN, or VU, ask**: *Is the species showing declining trends or does it face emerging threats that suggest it will qualify in the next 5–10 years?*
   - If yes → **Near Threatened (NT)**.
   - If no → **Least Concern (LC)**.

7. **Document the assessment:** state which criterion (or criteria) the species meets and the evidence supporting the classification. Record the assessment date and the assessor.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Conservation action | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| **EX** | Extinct; no action on the wild population. Preserve knowledge and genetic material if available. | — |
| **EW** | Prioritize reintroduction if habitat is secure and causes of extinction are addressed. Maintain captive population. | Very High |
| **CR** | Immediate, intensive intervention required: emergency captive breeding if necessary, habitat protection, anti-poaching, species recovery plan. Failure to act within years likely means extinction. | Very High |
| **EN** | High-priority conservation: habitat restoration, population management, legal protection, threat mitigation. Population recovery is still achievable within 10–20 years if action is sustained. | High |
| **VU** | Moderate-priority action: habitat protection, monitoring, threat reduction, and long-term management. Population trajectory is stabilizing but not yet self-sustaining without support. | Moderate |
| **NT** | Monitor for decline; implement preventive measures. Likely to shift to VU or EN without intervention; early action is cheaper than later crisis response. | Low to Moderate |
| **LC** | Routine monitoring; protected by general conservation policy. Focus on other species. | Low |

For a conservation agency, the practical implication is **budget allocation**: CR and EW species consume disproportionate resources relative to their numbers; EN and VU species represent the bulk of conservation work; NT species are early-warning indicators; LC species are a baseline for ecosystem health.

## Common Misclassifications

- **CR mistaken for EN.** Assessors sometimes downgrade declining species to EN because the population is still in the low thousands, overlooking that the *rate* of decline or the fragmentation pattern places it in CR. The tell: the species underwent >25% decline in the past 3 years. Consequence: under-resourcing a species that may disappear within a decade.

- **VU mistaken for NT.** An assessor observes that a species is "not yet endangered" and assigns NT, but fails to check whether the range is < 20,000 km² or the population is < 10,000 mature individuals. The tell: the species meets a quantitative VU threshold on range or population size alone, regardless of trend. Consequence: delaying conservation action until decline is obvious.

- **NT mistaken for LC.** Treating a species with stable current numbers as LC when data show it has lost >50% of its range in the past century and trends are negative. The tell: historical range maps and decade-scale trend data reveal decline is ongoing. Consequence: complacency about a species that is materially at risk.

- **Confusing population size with number of *mature* individuals.** Juveniles and sub-adult animals do not count toward the CR/EN/VU thresholds. A species with 5,000 total individuals but only 500 mature individuals qualifies as CR on population size, not VU. The tell: explicit age-structure data show most individuals are not yet reproductive.

- **Assigning EW when EX is warranted.** An assessor knows of a captive breeding population and assigns EW without exhaustively searching for remnant wild populations in remote habitat. The tell: expert field surveys by multiple teams over 50+ years have found nothing; anecdotal reports are not acceptable. Consequence: misrepresenting the species' true status and misallocating reintroduction resources.

- **Ignoring subpopulation structure in the CR/EN threshold.** A species has a total population of 4,000 but 3,800 are in a single protected reserve; the remaining 200 are scattered across declining populations. This qualifies as CR or EN on fragmentation grounds, not VU. The tell: > 90–95% of the population is concentrated in one location. Consequence: false confidence in the species' security.

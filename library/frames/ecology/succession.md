# Succession

## Overview

Succession is a frame that sorts an ecosystem or plant community into one of
three developmental pathways after disturbance — **primary, secondary, or
arrested** — based on whether functional soil and propagule sources are
present, and which mechanism (competition, facilitation, or inhibition) is
governing recovery. Its core claim is that the same visual appearance of
ecological change (bare ground becoming vegetated) can arise from
structurally different processes that demand different management responses.
Misclassifying the succession type leads to interventions that either
accelerate wrong trajectories or fail to interrupt stalled ones.

## Categories

1. **Primary Succession**
   - The site has **no pre-existing soil** and no seed bank. Recovery must
     begin from substrate alone (rock, sand, volcanic ash, or glacial till).
   - Pioneer species establish from external propagule sources and gradually
     build soil, enabling successive waves of more demanding species.
   - Discriminating criterion: bare mineral substrate with zero organic
     matter, zero native seed bank, no legacy flora. Recovery is slow
     (decades to centuries) and follows a recognizable sequence because
     environmental constraints narrow the options.
   - Example: a recently exposed glacial moraine, a lava field, dune systems
     far from seed sources.

2. **Secondary Succession**
   - The site retains **functional soil and an intact seed bank** despite
     disturbance (fire, logging, windstorm, or temporary burial).
   - Recovery is rapid (years to decades) because propagules and soil
     nutrients are already present. Dominant mechanism is **competition**
     among colonists; early dominants suppress latecomers or facilitate
     them depending on local conditions.
   - Discriminating criterion: soil is present and viable; seed bank is
     intact or nearby seed sources are abundant. Recovery visibly accelerates
     and may be reversible to earlier states if re-disturbed at the right
     interval.
   - Example: a temperate forest one year after clear-cutting; an old field
     in the eastern US; a grassland after a single fire in a fire-adapted
     ecosystem.

3. **Arrested (or Alternate Stable) Succession**
   - The site is **locked in an early or off-sequence state** by chronic
     disturbance, herbivory, soil loss, or competitive dominance of a
     non-native species.
   - Recovery is blocked not by lack of resources but by a self-reinforcing
     mechanism (grazing prevents trees from establishing; a dominant
     grass-fire cycle prevents woody encroachment; soil compaction prevents
     perennial roots from establishing).
   - Discriminating criterion: the site is visibly resilient to change —
     disturbances or attempts to restore it are regularly reversed. Early
     successional states persist indefinitely despite propagule sources and
     (sometimes) adequate soil.
   - Example: a heavily grazed grassland kept treeless by continuous
     herbivory; a degraded savanna locked in grass by fire; a compacted
     industrial site that reverts to pioneer weeds regardless of planting
     attempts.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Identify the disturbance and baseline state.** What was the pre-disturbance
   ecosystem, and what is the current state (bare ground, partial damage,
   simplified flora)?

2. **Assess soil and seed bank viability.** Is functional soil present
   (organic matter, microbes, water-holding capacity)? Is a viable seed bank
   present, or are seed sources abundant nearby?
   - If **no soil and no propagules** → Primary.
   - If **soil and seed bank intact** → Secondary (provisionally).
   - If **soil present but recovery is not occurring** → move to step 3.

3. **Ask whether recovery is self-reinforcing or self-arresting.** If the site
   is re-disturbed at typical intervals (grazing, fire, mechanical damage), or
   if early dominants suppress later arrivals indefinitely, the trajectory is
   blocked. If recovery accelerates over time without repeated disturbance,
   it is unblocked.
   - If **blocked by a repeating or self-reinforcing mechanism** → Arrested.
   - If **unblocked and accelerating** → Secondary.

4. **Verify by timeline.** Secondary succession typically shows visible
   change within years. Primary succession does not. Arrested succession
   shows oscillation or stasis over decadal timescales.

5. **State the classification and the governing mechanism** (competition,
   facilitation, inhibition, or grazing/disturbance cycle). This determines
   which intervention (if any) is defensible.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Mechanism | What this means | Management implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Facilitation (species prepare soil for successors) | Slow, directional, largely predictable sequence | Patience is usually optimal; interference often delays. Intervention justified only to accelerate specific outcomes (e.g., establish nurse plants). |
| **Secondary** | Competition (early dominants outcompete, or facilitate, later arrivals) | Rapid, reversible if re-disturbed; trajectory depends on disturbance interval | Intervention (planting, weeding, fire) is effective; outcomes are predictable. Manage via disturbance regime if you want a specific endpoint. |
| **Arrested** | Inhibition or chronic disturbance (dominants suppress, grazing prevents tree establishment, fire cycle locks in grass) | Ecosystem is stuck. Change is resisted; interventions are often reversed. | High-intensity intervention required: remove the blocking agent (fencing, grazing exclusion, fire exclusion), then actively establish alternatives. Passive restoration fails. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Secondary mistaken for Primary.** Assuming a bare or simplified site with
  no visible vegetation is Primary when soil and seed bank are actually
  present but buried or dormant. The tell is that recovery accelerates once
  the disturbance stops (e.g., after logging, grass returns quickly from root
  fragments). Recovery timescale (years vs. decades) is the clearest
  discriminator.

- **Arrested mistaken for early-stage Secondary.** A site locked in early
  succession by continuous grazing or fire looks like Secondary recovery is
  just slow. The tell is that removal of the disturbing agent does not
  trigger transitions — the site stays in the same state or regresses when
  protected (e.g., excluded from grazing, it still does not transition to
  forest if soil has eroded or the grass-fire cycle is self-reinforcing).

- **Secondary mistaken for Arrested.** Assuming a site locked in a particular
  state must be Arrested when it is actually Secondary but on the "wrong"
  disturbance interval. A grassland that stays grassland because it burns
  every 5 years is Secondary to a fire-adapted endpoint, not Arrested. The
  tell is that removing the disturbance (fire exclusion, reduced grazing)
  allows transition to the next state within a reasonable timescale.

- **Confusing Arrested with "stable climax."** Some ecosystems have multiple
  stable states with different disturbance regimes (e.g., grassland or forest
  depending on fire frequency). This is not Arrested — it is Secondary with
  management-dependent outcomes. Arrested implies the endpoint is *locked*
  against all reasonable options.

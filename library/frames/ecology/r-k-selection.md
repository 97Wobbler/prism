# r/K Selection

## Overview

r/K Selection sorts organisms and populations along a reproductive strategy spectrum based on how they allocate energy between offspring quantity and offspring investment. The frame's core claim is that different environmental contexts select for structurally different life-history strategies — and that an organism's position on this spectrum predicts not just breeding behavior, but survival curve shape, generation time, body size, parental care, and vulnerability to disturbance. An organism classified as r-selected operates under fundamentally different constraints than a K-selected one, making the classification itself predictive of which subsequent life-history traits will follow.

## Categories

1. **r-Selected (Opportunistic)**
   - Reproductive strategy emphasizing **high fecundity and rapid population growth** with minimal parental investment per offspring.
   - The organism produces many offspring with low survival probability, betting on rapid reproduction and early age at first breeding.
   - Discriminating criterion: generation time is short, age at maturity is early, and a large fraction of energy budget goes to offspring production rather than growth or maintenance of individual offspring.
   - Example: insects (flies, locusts), small rodents in boom-bust habitats, weedy plants, fish that broadcast spawn millions of eggs.

2. **K-Selected (Equilibrial)**
   - Reproductive strategy emphasizing **low fecundity and high parental investment** per offspring, with offspring tuned for survival in a crowded or stable environment.
   - The organism produces few offspring but allocates substantial resources to each one's survival, growth, and competitive ability.
   - Discriminating criterion: generation time is long, age at maturity is late, offspring are few, and parental care or resource allocation per offspring is high.
   - Example: elephants, primates, large predators, slow-growing trees, long-lived birds.

3. **Intermediate / Generalist**
   - Reproductive strategy occupying the **middle of the r/K spectrum**, with moderate fecundity and moderate per-offspring investment.
   - The organism neither maximizes offspring number nor minimizes it; instead it adopts a mixed allocation strategy suited to variable or moderately stable environments.
   - Discriminating criterion: generation time is moderate, offspring survival is contingent on local conditions, and the organism can shift reproductive allocation seasonally or generationally.
   - Example: many songbirds, medium-sized mammals (dogs, ungulates), annual plants with seed dormancy, fish with parental care of moderate intensity.

## Classification Procedure

1. Obtain or estimate the organism's **generation time** (age from birth to first reproduction), **fecundity** (number of offspring per breeding event or per lifetime), and **parental investment** (time, energy, or resources allocated to each offspring's survival and development).

2. Ask: **"Does this organism produce many offspring quickly with minimal investment per offspring?"**
   - If **strongly yes** → r-Selected.
   - If **strongly no** (few offspring, long development, high parental investment) → go to step 3.
   - If **mixed or variable** → go to step 4.

3. Confirm K-Selection: **Does this organism mature slowly, live long, and produce few offspring over its lifetime?**
   - If **yes** → K-Selected.
   - If **no** → re-examine; this is rare and usually indicates Intermediate.

4. For borderline cases: **Does the organism shift reproductive allocation based on environmental conditions or predictability?**
   - If **yes, flexibly** → Intermediate.
   - If **no, consistently extreme** → re-assess steps 1–2.

5. State the classification and identify the **expected life-history syndrome** (survival curve, body size, habitat preference, sensitivity to disturbance — see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Category | Life-History Syndrome | Expected Traits |
|---|---|---|
| **r-Selected** | Type III survival curve (high early mortality) | Small body size, early maturity, high fecundity, short lifespan, generalist habitat use, high dispersal, rapid population recovery from disturbance. |
| **K-Selected** | Type I survival curve (low early mortality, actuarial senescence) | Large body size, late maturity, low fecundity, long lifespan, specialized habitat, strong parental care or competition for resources, slow population recovery. |
| **Intermediate** | Type II survival curve (constant mortality across age) | Moderate body size, variable parental investment, flexible reproductive timing, occupancy of intermediate stability habitats, can respond to sudden environmental shifts. |

For an ecological or conservation agent, the implications are:
- **r-Selected species** will recolonize disturbed habitats quickly but require large meta-population structure to buffer local extinctions.
- **K-Selected species** are vulnerable to overharvesting and habitat fragmentation; they require stable reserves and long-term protection.
- **Intermediate species** are often suitable for sustainable-use strategies; their flexibility allows both harvesting and recovery if managed appropriately.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Classifying by body size alone.** A large organism is not necessarily K-selected (some large insects are r-selected) and a small organism is not necessarily r-selected (some small mammals are K-selected). The tell is examining fecundity and parental investment, not just morphology.

- **Confusing r/K with taxonomic group.** "All birds are K-selected" is false; shorebirds and songbirds vary widely. The tell is measuring reproductive output and investment in the specific population or species.

- **Mistaking environmental stress response for r-selection.** A K-selected organism in a harsh environment may produce fewer offspring than usual, but this is phenotypic plasticity, not a shift in life-history strategy. The tell is whether the shift is consistent across generations and genetically heritable.

- **Intermediate classified as genuinely r or K.** Generalist species often show conditional breeding strategies (e.g., multiple broods in good years, one in bad years). The tell is whether the organism can shift allocation; true intermediates can; extremes cannot.

- **Ignoring the role of population density and competition.** K-selection describes strategy in stable, competitive environments; in uncrowded habitats, even K-selected species may increase fecundity. The tell is that the classification must account for the organism's actual environment, not just its capacity.

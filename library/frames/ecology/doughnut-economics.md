# Doughnut Economics

## Overview

Doughnut Economics sorts economic activities, policies, and system states by their position relative to two concentric boundaries: a **social floor** (minimum thresholds for human wellbeing) and an **ecological ceiling** (planetary boundaries). The core claim is that the goal of economic organization is not growth per se, but achievement of human thriving **within** ecological limits — a "safe and just space." An activity can overshoot the ceiling (ecological harm), undershoot the floor (human deprivation), achieve both simultaneously (the "safe zone"), or fail on one or both axes. Sorting by these boundaries reveals which kind of intervention — redistribution, efficiency, sufficiency, or regeneration — is actually needed.

## Categories

1. **In the Doughnut (Safe and Just)**
   - The activity or system **meets all social foundation thresholds** (food security, health, education, income, networks, energy, gender equality, social equity, political voice, peace and justice) **and operates within all planetary boundaries** (climate change, biodiversity loss, land conversion, freshwater use, nitrogen and phosphorus flows, ocean acidification, air pollution, ozone depletion, chemical pollution).
   - Discriminating criterion: no binding social deficit and no ecological overshoot on any axis.
   - Example: a regenerative agricultural system that provides living income, builds soil health, and maintains biodiversity; a circular manufacturing loop operating at steady-state throughput.

2. **Below the Floor (Social Shortfall)**
   - The activity or system **fails to meet one or more social foundation thresholds** — people lack food, health, education, income, or voice — **while remaining within ecological limits**.
   - Discriminating criterion: ecological footprint is acceptable, but human outcomes are inadequate.
   - Example: subsistence farming with food security but no access to clean water; a low-carbon economy with high unemployment.

3. **Above the Ceiling (Ecological Overshoot)**
   - The activity or system **respects all social thresholds** — people have income, health, education — **but transgresses one or more planetary boundaries**.
   - Discriminating criterion: human wellbeing is achieved, but at the cost of ecosystem degradation or resource depletion.
   - Example: high-income country with strong social services but carbon footprint 3× the biologically sustainable level; a manufacturing economy with full employment but toxic water discharge.

4. **Outside the Doughnut (Overshoot and Shortfall)**
   - The activity or system **fails on both axes** — it produces neither adequate human outcomes nor ecological sustainability.
   - Discriminating criterion: people are deprived *and* the system is degrading planetary systems.
   - Example: resource-extraction economies that impoverish local communities, destroy habitats, and concentrate wealth; a collapsing fishery with unemployment and ecosystem collapse.

## Classification Procedure

1. Define the **scope** of the activity or system: a region, industry, organization, or policy intervention.

2. **Assess social dimensions.** For each of the seven social foundation themes (food, health, education, income, networks, equality, voice), ask: "Are minimum thresholds met for the population affected?" If any theme falls short, mark it as a deficit.

3. **Assess ecological dimensions.** For each of the nine planetary boundaries (climate, biodiversity, land, water, nitrogen/phosphorus, ocean pH, air quality, ozone, synthetics), ask: "Does the system's throughput or impact exceed the regenerative or absorptive capacity?" If any boundary is transgressed, mark it as an overshoot.

4. **Classify:**
   - No deficits + no overshoots → **In the Doughnut**.
   - Deficits present + no overshoots → **Below the Floor**.
   - No deficits + overshoots present → **Above the Ceiling**.
   - Deficits present + overshoots present → **Outside the Doughnut**.

5. **Name the specific axes.** Rather than a generic label, specify which social themes are unmet and which boundaries are exceeded. This reveals where intervention is most urgent.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Intervention needed | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **In the Doughnut** | Maintenance and regeneration | System is regenerative and distributive; focus on resilience and deepening equity. |
| **Below the Floor** | Redistribution and sufficiency | Poverty and deprivation are the binding constraint; increase throughput or redistribute, but stay within ecological limits. |
| **Above the Ceiling** | Efficiency and sufficiency | Ecological overshoot is the binding constraint; dematerialization, circular design, and absolute reduction are needed even if it means lower material consumption. |
| **Outside the Doughnut** | System redesign | Both constraints are binding; incremental change fails; restructure production, distribution, and consumption fundamentally. |

For policy design, the implications differ sharply by category. Below-the-Floor interventions may require growth *in the right dimensions* (nutrition, healthcare, education). Above-the-Ceiling interventions require absolute *reduction* in material throughput, not green growth. Outside interventions demand both simultaneous redistribution and dematerialization.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Mistaking efficiency for sufficiency.** A system that cuts its emissions per unit of output (Above → Ceiling-bound) is often relabeled as "solved" when the category is actually unchanged — the boundary is still exceeded. True sufficiency requires *absolute reduction*, not relative improvement.
- **Conflating high income with the social floor.** A high-income country may meet income thresholds but fail on networks, voice, or equality for marginalized subpopulations. The social floor is not scalar; it is a multidimensional threshold.
- **Overlooking cumulative overshoot.** A policy that respects one boundary (e.g., climate) while transgressing another (e.g., biodiversity, nitrogen) is still Above the Ceiling. The test is all boundaries, not a single axis.
- **Assuming the Doughnut is static.** Planetary boundaries shift with climate and system state; a region In the Doughnut today may be Above the Ceiling within a generation if baselines degrade. Classification requires periodic reassessment.
- **Treating social and ecological constraints as independent trade-offs.** The doughnut is not a two-axis grid where you can be "a little over" on one boundary to help the poor. Both must be respected; trade-offs occur *within* each constraint, not between them.

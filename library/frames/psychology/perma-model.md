# PERMA Model

## Overview

The PERMA Model sorts dimensions of human wellbeing into five distinct
categories based on what each measures and enables. Seligman's core claim
is that **authentic wellbeing is not unidimensional** — happiness alone is
insufficient — and that interventions targeting one element do not
automatically improve others. Different wellbeing dimensions respond to
different levers, and a comprehensive wellbeing strategy must address all
five. The frame is useful because it prevents both the error of pursuing a
single metric (e.g., pleasure-maximization) and the error of treating
wellbeing as unmeasurable or ineffable.

## Categories

1. **Positive Emotion (P)**
   - The dimension of **feeling good**: pleasure, joy, contentment, and
     the subjective experience of life satisfaction.
   - Definition: the hedonic quality of experience — moments that feel
     good in the moment.
   - Discriminating criterion: the intervention directly increases
     immediate sensory or emotional pleasure, not through achievement or
     mastery.
   - Example: time with loved ones, enjoying food, entertainment, rest.

2. **Engagement (E)**
   - The dimension of **flow and absorption**: being fully immersed in an
     activity, losing track of time, operating at the edge of one's
     skill.
   - Definition: the state in which attention is fully deployed and
     challenge matches capability.
   - Discriminating criterion: the intervention creates conditions for
     concentration and challenge; satisfaction comes from the activity
     itself, not from external reward.
   - Example: deep work on a meaningful project, a challenging sport,
     learning a skill.

3. **Relationships (R)**
   - The dimension of **connection and belonging**: the quality and depth
     of social bonds, feeling understood and valued by others.
   - Definition: the fulfillment that comes from mattering to others and
     others mattering to you.
   - Discriminating criterion: the intervention strengthens social ties,
     enables reciprocal care, or deepens mutual understanding.
   - Example: time with close friends, mentoring, collaboration, family
     rituals, community participation.

4. **Meaning (M)**
   - The dimension of **purpose and contribution**: the sense that one's
     life matters, that effort is directed toward something larger than
     oneself.
   - Definition: the eudaimonic quality of knowing that one's actions
     serve a purpose beyond pleasure or success.
   - Discriminating criterion: the intervention connects effort to a cause
     or value larger than the individual, or clarifies the person's
     contribution to it.
   - Example: volunteering, raising a child, activism, spiritual practice,
     creating art, mentoring.

5. **Accomplishment (A)**
   - The dimension of **mastery, growth, and achievement**: making
     progress toward self-defined goals, developing competence, and
     building resilience.
   - Definition: the satisfaction of increasing capability and attaining
     things that matter to you.
   - Discriminating criterion: the intervention enables skill-building,
     progress toward a goal, or overcoming a concrete challenge.
   - Example: career advancement, finishing a project, earning a
     qualification, fitness improvement, learning.

## Classification Procedure

1. Name the wellbeing outcome, intervention, or life domain you are
   evaluating.
2. Ask: **"What is the primary source of wellbeing here?"** Does the
   person primarily feel satisfied because:
   - They feel good **right now** (Positive Emotion) → P
   - They are fully absorbed and challenged (Engagement) → E
   - They feel connected to others (Relationships) → R
   - They see purpose or meaning in it (Meaning) → M
   - They have achieved something or are building a skill (Accomplishment) → A
3. If the outcome touches multiple categories, identify the **primary**
   one (the most load-bearing source of the satisfaction or wellbeing). It
   is common to touch more than one, but the frame requires naming the
   dominant one first.
4. Note any secondary or absent categories. A complete wellbeing strategy
   should eventually address all five, but individual interventions
   typically anchor to one.
5. Explicitly state the category and the reason it applies.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Primary lever | What wellbeing strategy should do |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive Emotion** | Sensory and emotional satisfaction | Ensure access to pleasurable experiences, relaxation, comfort; prevent suffering and chronic stress. |
| **Engagement** | Challenge, skill-building, flow | Design roles and projects that demand focus and growth; eliminate boredom and learned helplessness. |
| **Relationships** | Social connection and reciprocity | Build and protect time for meaningful relationships; create belonging and mutual support. |
| **Meaning** | Purpose and contribution | Help people connect their work or life to values larger than themselves; enable service and legacy. |
| **Accomplishment** | Mastery and progress | Set clear, achievable goals; create pathways for skill development and visible progress. |

A mature wellbeing program typically addresses **all five**, but with
different weightings for different populations or life stages. A high-stress
job might prioritize Relationships and Meaning to offset limited Positive
Emotion; a retiree might need deliberate design for Engagement and
Accomplishment to offset loss of work structure.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Conflating Positive Emotion with wellbeing.** Pleasure is necessary
  but not sufficient. A life optimized for immediate pleasure alone lacks
  Meaning and Accomplishment; research shows it does not correlate with
  long-term flourishing. The tell: the person feels empty or directionless
  despite comfort.
- **Mistaking Accomplishment for Meaning.** Career success and mastery
  can exist without purpose beyond the self. The tell: the person achieves
  the goal and feels hollow, not fulfilled.
- **Confusing Engagement with Meaning.** Flow state can occur in morally
  neutral or harmful activities (e.g., addiction, competitive gaming). The
  tell: the person is absorbed but reports lack of purpose; Engagement
  alone does not produce lasting wellbeing.
- **Ignoring Relationships in an individualistic framework.** Some
  wellbeing models treat Relationships as secondary; PERMA places it as a
  foundational element. A person can be high in Accomplishment,
  Engagement, and Meaning but isolated and at risk of burnout or suicide.
- **Treating PERMA as equally weighted.** Research suggests Relationships
  and Meaning are more predictive of flourishing than Positive Emotion
  alone, and deficits in one category cannot be fully offset by excess in
  another. Context and population matter; do not assume uniform weighting.

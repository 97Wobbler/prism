# Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

## Overview

Maslow's Hierarchy sorts human needs into five levels arranged in a pyramid, with the core claim that **lower-level needs must be substantially satisfied before higher-level needs become motivators**. The hierarchy predicts that addressing the wrong need level — e.g., offering self-actualization opportunities to someone without physical safety — will fail to motivate behavior or resolve dissatisfaction. Different categories require structurally different responses: physiological and safety needs demand resource allocation and environment control, while esteem and self-actualization needs respond to recognition, autonomy, and purpose.

## Categories

1. **Physiological Needs**
   - Basic survival requirements: food, water, shelter, sleep, air, physical health.
   - These are **prerequisites for any other motivation** — a starving person cannot be motivated by purpose.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person cannot function without satisfying this need; it overrides all other considerations.
   - Example: providing meals to a homeless population, ensuring adequate rest for a sleep-deprived employee.

2. **Safety Needs**
   - Protection from physical and psychological harm; predictability, stability, structure, law.
   - Activated once physiological needs are met; the person seeks **security and freedom from threat**.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person's behavior is driven by avoiding danger or establishing routine; they prefer the known over the novel.
   - Example: job security, a safe neighborhood, consistent rules and boundaries, financial reserves.

3. **Love and Belonging Needs**
   - Connection, affiliation, acceptance by peers; the desire to be part of a group.
   - Emerges when safety is established; the person seeks **intimate relationships and community**.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is motivated by inclusion, fears rejection, seeks collaboration and social bonds.
   - Example: team belonging, friendship, family ties, membership in a community or organization.

4. **Esteem Needs**
   - Recognition, respect, status, competence, autonomy, achievement.
   - Split into two sub-levels: **esteem from others** (respect, status) and **self-esteem** (competence, independence).
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is motivated by recognition, comparison to peers, or proving capability; they need to feel valued and in control.
   - Example: public acknowledgment of work, advancement, mastery of a skill, leadership roles.

5. **Self-Actualization**
   - Fulfilling one's potential, pursuing purpose, creative expression, personal growth.
   - The **highest and least common motivator**; emerges only after the lower four levels are reasonably satisfied.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is willing to sacrifice material comfort or status for meaning; they pursue intrinsic goals over extrinsic rewards.
   - Example: an artist creating work despite low income, a researcher pursuing fundamental questions, mentoring others, pursuing a calling.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the **current state of the person or group** — what is their immediate context (employed, homeless, isolated, successful, etc.)?
2. Ask **"Are physiological needs clearly met?"** (food, shelter, sleep, health)
   - If **no** → Physiological. Stop here; address this first.
   - If **yes**, proceed to step 3.
3. Ask **"Do they feel safe and secure?"** (physical safety, job security, predictable environment, financial stability)
   - If **no** → Safety. Stop here; establish security before pursuing higher needs.
   - If **yes**, proceed to step 4.
4. Ask **"Do they have strong social bonds and feel accepted?"** (family, friends, team, community)
   - If **no** → Love and Belonging.
   - If **yes**, proceed to step 5.
5. Ask **"Are they motivated by recognition, competence, or autonomy?"** (Do they care about status, skill mastery, being heard, or control?)
   - If **yes** → Esteem. Note whether the driver is external (recognition, status) or internal (competence, independence).
   - If **no**, proceed to step 6.
6. Ask **"Are they pursuing meaning, purpose, or personal growth despite material tradeoffs?"**
   - If **yes** → Self-Actualization.
   - If **no**, return to step 4; the primary unmet need is likely Love and Belonging.

## Implications per Category

| Category | What it means | Appropriate interventions |
|---|---|---|
| **Physiological** | The person cannot function; basic survival is at risk. | Provide food, shelter, medical care, rest. Motivation appeals will not work until these are met. |
| **Safety** | The person has basics but lacks stability or security. | Establish predictable rules, remove threats, offer job security, build reserves. They prefer the known to the novel. |
| **Love and Belonging** | The person is secure but isolated or excluded. | Foster team belonging, create social opportunities, build community, ensure inclusion. They need to feel part of something. |
| **Esteem** | The person belongs but lacks recognition or competence. | Provide acknowledgment, create advancement paths, enable mastery, grant autonomy. Distinguish external (status) from internal (competence) drivers. |
| **Self-Actualization** | The person has met lower needs and seeks purpose. | Offer meaningful work, creative freedom, growth opportunities, purpose alignment. They will trade money for meaning. |

**Critical note:** Maslow's hierarchy is **dynamic**, not static. A person can move between levels based on circumstances (a sudden job loss drops safety needs back into focus), and individuals vary in the order and intensity with which they pursue levels. The frame predicts which category of intervention will be **salient** and **motivating** at a given moment.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Assuming all employees are at Esteem or Self-Actualization level.** Many are still focused on Safety (job security, predictability) or Love and Belonging (team inclusion). Offering a "growth opportunity" to someone whose job is at risk misses the actual need.
- **Treating Esteem as monolithic.** External esteem (status, rank, recognition) and self-esteem (competence, autonomy) are driven by different levers. Giving someone a title without competence, or competence without recognition, satisfies only half the need.
- **Assuming Self-Actualization is universal.** Some people are satisfied at Safety or Belonging; not everyone seeks to maximize their potential. Forcing a "find your purpose" conversation on someone content with security and community is a common coaching error.
- **Ignoring regression.** A person at Self-Actualization can drop to Safety needs if their job becomes precarious. Failing to recognize the shift leads to ineffective motivation.
- **Conflating Love and Belonging with Esteem.** Feeling accepted in a group (Belonging) is different from feeling respected or competent within it (Esteem). A team can be tight-knit but low-performing; addressing one does not address the other.

# Kohlberg's Stages of Moral Development

## Overview

Kohlberg's frame sorts moral reasoning into six stages organized into three levels, based on the **primary motivation and reference point** that drives the reasoning. The core claim is that moral development is a progression from external enforcement to internalized principles, and that individuals reason differently about ethical dilemmas depending on their stage. Understanding which stage a person or group is reasoning from constrains which arguments will persuade them and which appeals will fall flat — a person reasoning from Stage 3 (social approval) will not be moved by Stage 6 arguments (universal ethical principles), and attempting the latter can produce resistance rather than alignment.

## Categories

1. **Stage 1: Punishment and Obedience Orientation**
   - Moral reasoning is driven by **fear of punishment and respect for authority**.
   - The actor obeys rules to avoid consequences, not because the rules are just.
   - Discriminating criterion: the primary concern is "What will happen to me if I disobey?" Physical consequences determine morality.
   - Example: a child refrains from stealing because they fear being caught and punished, not because theft is wrong.

2. **Stage 2: Instrumental Relativist Orientation**
   - Moral reasoning centers on **satisfying one's own needs and occasionally those of others** in exchange.
   - Right action is what serves the individual's interests; fairness is a reciprocal bargain ("you scratch my back, I scratch yours").
   - Discriminating criterion: morality is transactional; the actor can justify cooperation if there is personal benefit.
   - Example: "I'll share my toy if you share yours," or a business agreement where both parties gain.

3. **Stage 3: Good Interpersonal Relationships Orientation**
   - Moral reasoning is driven by **desire for social approval and conformity** to group norms.
   - Right action is what wins approval from peers or authority figures; the actor seeks to be seen as "good."
   - Discriminating criterion: the reference point shifts from personal consequence to what others think; the actor internalizes group expectations.
   - Example: a teenager refrains from vandalism because "good people don't do that," or a professional adheres to workplace norms to be liked.

4. **Stage 4: Law and Order Orientation**
   - Moral reasoning is based on **respect for authority, established rules, and social duty**.
   - Right action is defined by laws, codes, and institutional structures; the actor obeys rules because they maintain social order.
   - Discriminating criterion: rules and institutions are viewed as legitimate, not arbitrary; compliance is a duty to the system, not just fear or social pressure.
   - Example: paying taxes because "it's the law," or following company procedure even when the individual might achieve faster results by breaking it.

5. **Stage 5: Social Contract Legalistic Orientation**
   - Moral reasoning recognizes that **rules and laws are human constructs** that can be evaluated and changed, and that they derive legitimacy from consent.
   - Right action respects individual rights and seeks to maximize the greatest good for the greatest number, or respects prior commitments.
   - Discriminating criterion: the actor can distinguish between the rule and the underlying principle; laws can be unjust and may warrant civil disobedience.
   - Example: supporting a law you disagree with because the democratic process is more important, or breaking an unjust law because individual rights supersede it.

6. **Stage 6: Universal Ethical Principles Orientation**
   - Moral reasoning is guided by **self-chosen universal ethical principles** (justice, human dignity, equality) that transcend law and convention.
   - Right action follows conscience and abstract principles, even when it conflicts with law, social opinion, or personal cost.
   - Discriminating criterion: the actor reasons from first principles about human dignity and justice, not from rules or consequences; moral stands are taken regardless of approval or punishment.
   - Example: a person who refuses to participate in an unjust system even at great personal cost, or who acts to protect human rights as a fundamental principle.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify a real moral decision or dilemma the actor is facing, or a statement they have made about why an action is right or wrong.
2. Ask: **"What is the primary reason given for this choice?"** Listen for the reference point.
   - If the answer is fear of punishment or consequences to self → **Stage 1**.
   - If the answer is personal benefit or reciprocal exchange → **Stage 2**.
   - Go to step 3.
3. Does the actor mention **other people's approval, expectations, or group norms**?
   - If yes, and they show concern for being seen as good → **Stage 3**.
   - If no, go to step 4.
4. Does the actor cite **law, rule, authority, or institutional duty**?
   - If yes, and they treat the rule as legitimate and stable → **Stage 4**.
   - If yes, but they question whether the rule is just → go to step 5.
   - If no, go to step 5.
5. Does the actor distinguish between **law and underlying principle**, or invoke a right that supersedes rules?
   - If yes, and they defer to democratic process or social contract → **Stage 5**.
   - If yes, and they invoke universal principle (justice, dignity) regardless of law or approval → **Stage 6**.
   - If the reasoning is unclear, ask: "Would you follow this principle even if the law, your peer group, and your personal interest all opposed it?" A yes suggests Stage 5 or 6; a no suggests Stage 3 or 4.
6. Record the stage and note the **key phrase or reasoning** that placed it there.

## Implications per Category

| Stage | Core Motivation | What persuades the actor | What fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stage 1** | Avoid punishment | Enforcement, concrete consequences, authority | Appeals to conscience, social good, abstract principle |
| **Stage 2** | Self-interest & exchange | Personal benefit, clear reciprocal gain | Altruism, sacrifice, collective good without personal return |
| **Stage 3** | Social approval & group norms | Peer acceptance, reputation, "good people do X" | Being the only one, breaking group expectation |
| **Stage 4** | Institutional order & duty | Legitimate rules, authority, "it's the law" | Questioning the rule's justice, civil disobedience framing |
| **Stage 5** | Social contract & rights | Democratic process, harm to rights, greater good, civil disobedience justified | Dogmatic authority, appeals to pure law without justice |
| **Stage 6** | Universal ethical principle | Justice, human dignity, conscience, standing alone | Consequences, group opinion, institutional order |

For a communicator or agent: **match the argument to the stage**. A Stage 3 actor will be moved by "good people do this" and peer leadership; a Stage 6 actor will see that as evasion. A Stage 4 actor needs the rule and the legitimacy of the system; a Stage 5 actor needs to see the rule's limits and the principle beneath it.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Stage 1 mistaken for Stage 2.** Both involve personal consequence, but Stage 1 is purely fear-based, while Stage 2 involves negotiation and exchange. The tell: Stage 2 actors can articulate mutual benefit or fairness; Stage 1 actors speak only of what happens to them.
- **Stage 3 mistaken for Stage 4.** Both involve internalization of external standards, but Stage 3 is peer-driven ("good people," social approval) while Stage 4 is system-driven ("the law," institutional order). The tell: Stage 3 reasoning shifts with peer group; Stage 4 reasoning is stable across contexts because it references the rule itself.
- **Stage 4 mistaken for Stage 5.** Both respect rules, but Stage 4 treats them as fixed and legitimate, while Stage 5 sees them as provisional and subject to principle. The tell: ask "Is this law always just?" A Stage 4 actor says yes or defers to authority; a Stage 5 actor admits it can be unjust but follows it anyway for system integrity, or breaks it for a higher principle.
- **Stage 5 mistaken for Stage 6.** Both invoke principle over rule, but Stage 5 still defers to social contract and democratic process, while Stage 6 acts on conscience alone. The tell: Stage 5 actors will sacrifice their position to preserve the system's legitimacy; Stage 6 actors will sacrifice the system for the principle.
- **Assuming adult = Stage 4 or higher.** Many adults reason from Stage 3 or even Stage 2 in domains outside their expertise. Stage is **not age-correlated**; it is context- and domain-specific.

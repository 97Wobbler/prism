# Erikson's Psychosocial Stages

## Overview

Erikson's frame sorts a person's life into eight stages, each characterized by a **central psychosocial conflict** that must be navigated to achieve healthy development. Its core claim is that different life stages present structurally different identity work — and attempting to resolve a stage-5 conflict (identity vs. role confusion) using stage-2 strategies (autonomy vs. shame) is a systematic source of developmental stalling. The frame is not retrospective labeling; naming the stage is the intervention, because it reveals which relational and intrapsychic work is developmentally *appropriate* at this moment.

## Categories

1. **Infancy: Trust vs. Mistrust** (0–18 months)
   - The central conflict is whether the world and caregivers are reliable and safe.
   - The developing capacity is **basic trust** — the expectation that needs will be met.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is preverbal or early-verbal; object permanence is still forming; the primary relationship is dyadic (caregiver–infant).
   - Example: a 6-month-old whose caregiver is consistently responsive develops a secure attachment; inconsistent care produces anxiety and withdrawal.

2. **Early Childhood: Autonomy vs. Shame and Doubt** (18 months–3 years)
   - The central conflict is whether the child can act independently or must defer to external control.
   - The developing capacity is **will** — the ability to choose, decide, and take initiative within safe bounds.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is mobile, language-using, able to say "no," and testing limits; the primary work is separation from the caregiver.
   - Example: a toddler who is allowed to dress themselves (with support) develops autonomy; one who is shamed for toileting accidents or constrained unnecessarily develops shame and doubt about their own competence.

3. **Play Age: Initiative vs. Guilt** (3–5 years)
   - The central conflict is whether the child can plan, undertake, and pursue goals or becomes paralyzed by guilt over perceived transgressions.
   - The developing capacity is **purpose** — the ability to imagine a goal and pursue it.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person engages in symbolic and imaginative play; asks "why" constantly; is beginning to understand rules and consequences but still tests them.
   - Example: a child who is encouraged to make plans (a treasure hunt, a drawing project) develops initiative; one who is frequently told "don't" or made to feel guilty for curiosity develops inhibition.

4. **School Age: Industry vs. Inferiority** (6–12 years)
   - The central conflict is whether the child can master skills, compete fairly, and gain recognition or becomes convinced of their own inadequacy.
   - The developing capacity is **competence** — the ability to produce work, follow rules, and feel effective in a social group.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is in formal schooling or structured group settings; peer relationships and comparative achievement become salient; the locus of evaluation shifts from family to teachers and peers.
   - Example: a child who succeeds at schoolwork and is recognized for effort develops a sense of industry; one who consistently fails or is told they are "stupid" develops inferiority and learned helplessness.

5. **Adolescence: Identity vs. Role Confusion** (12–18 years)
   - The central conflict is whether the person can synthesize childhood identifications into a coherent sense of self or remains fragmented across conflicting roles.
   - The developing capacity is **fidelity** — the ability to commit to values, relationships, and ideologies despite their contradictions.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is sexually maturing; beginning to separate from family and align with peer groups; asking "who am I?" and trying on identities; future work and relationships are becoming real questions.
   - Example: a teenager who explores different peer groups, values, and careers and arrives at a provisional but stable sense of direction develops identity; one who is forced into a parental mold or receives no support for self-exploration remains diffuse or prematurely forecloses on an identity.

6. **Young Adulthood: Intimacy vs. Isolation** (18–25 years, extending into early 30s)
   - The central conflict is whether the person can achieve close, mutual relationships or withdraws into protective isolation.
   - The developing capacity is **love** — the ability to commit to another person, group, or cause with openness and vulnerability.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person has formed a stable identity and is now choosing partners (romantic, professional, ideological); the risk of losing self in fusion or losing other through distance is real; long-term commitment decisions are made.
   - Example: a young adult who forms a romantic partnership with genuine reciprocity and commitment develops intimacy; one who avoids commitment or merges completely without maintaining autonomy develops isolation or pseudo-intimacy.

7. **Middle Adulthood: Generativity vs. Stagnation** (25–65 years)
   - The central conflict is whether the person can create, mentor, contribute beyond themselves, or becomes self-absorbed and stalled.
   - The developing capacity is **care** — the commitment to contribute to the next generation and the wider world.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person has children, mentees, creative work, or community involvement; the question "what am I leaving behind?" becomes existential; there is awareness of finitude.
   - Example: a parent who actively invests in children's development, or a professional who mentors junior colleagues, or an artist who creates public work develops generativity; one preoccupied with their own comfort or status, or who feels their contributions do not matter, stagnates.

8. **Old Age: Ego Integrity vs. Despair** (65+ years)
   - The central conflict is whether the person can accept their life as meaningful and complete or rejects it as wasted and futile.
   - The developing capacity is **wisdom** — the integration of all previous stages into a coherent life narrative.
   - Discriminating criterion: the person is at the end of life; death is imminent; there is time for reflection but not for major revision; the question is whether the life lived is acceptable as-is.
   - Example: an elder who reviews their life with a sense of accomplishment, forgiveness toward themselves and others, and peace with mortality develops ego integrity; one filled with regret, bitterness, and denial of death falls into despair.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the person's chronological age and current life circumstances (school status, family formation, work, health).
2. Ask: **What is the primary relational or identity question they are grappling with right now?** (Not what adults think they *should* be grappling with — what the person actually evidences.)
3. Match the question to the list below:
   - "Can I trust this person/world?" → Infancy.
   - "Can I do this myself, or will I be shamed?" → Early Childhood.
   - "Can I plan and pursue goals without guilt?" → Play Age.
   - "Can I master skills and compete fairly?" → School Age.
   - "Who am I, and how do I fit in?" → Adolescence.
   - "Can I be close to someone without losing myself?" → Young Adulthood.
   - "What am I building that will outlast me?" → Middle Adulthood.
   - "Was my life worth living?" → Old Age.
4. Note that **stage does not strictly follow chronological age**. A 40-year-old still negotiating identity confusion is developmentally in stage 5; a 20-year-old caring for aging parents may be in stage 7. Use the *psychological* question, not the birthday.
5. State the stage and name the **central conflict** explicitly. This names the kind of relational or intrapsychic work that is age-appropriate and reparable at this stage.

## Implications per Category

| Stage | Central Conflict | What the agent should understand |
|---|---|---|
| **Infancy** | Trust vs. Mistrust | The person needs consistency, safety, and reliable response. Repair work centers on building or rebuilding secure attachment. |
| **Early Childhood** | Autonomy vs. Shame and Doubt | The person needs permission to act, make mistakes, and learn from consequences without being shamed. Repair involves restoring agency within safe limits. |
| **Play Age** | Initiative vs. Guilt | The person needs encouragement to imagine, plan, and pursue goals. Repair involves separating initiative from guilt and re-permitting creative action. |
| **School Age** | Industry vs. Inferiority | The person needs opportunities to develop and demonstrate competence, to fail safely and recover, and to be recognized for effort. Repair involves skill-building and earned success. |
| **Adolescence** | Identity vs. Role Confusion | The person needs space to explore, experiment with identities, and receive affirmation of their emerging self. Repair involves supporting coherence without premature closure. |
| **Young Adulthood** | Intimacy vs. Isolation | The person needs safe-to-fail opportunities for mutual vulnerability and commitment. Repair involves building capacity for interdependence without self-loss. |
| **Middle Adulthood** | Generativity vs. Stagnation | The person needs meaningful contribution and mentorship roles; recognition that their work matters. Repair involves connecting present work to legacy. |
| **Old Age** | Ego Integrity vs. Despair | The person needs to review their life with perspective and acceptance. Repair is primarily narrative — integrating regret, loss, and meaning. |

For a clinical or coaching agent, the implication is which **relational stance** is appropriate: secure attachment for infancy, permission for early childhood, encouragement for play age, recognition for school age, affirmation for adolescence, reciprocity for young adulthood, mentorship for middle age, and witnessing for old age.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Treating a developmental issue as if it belongs to a later stage.** A 30-year-old with unresolved autonomy issues (chronic deference to authority, inability to set boundaries) is developmentally in stage 2, not stage 6. Addressing them as if their conflict is intimacy misses the real work.
- **Chronological age overriding psychological age.** Assuming a 15-year-old is in stage 5 (identity) when they are still in stage 4 (industry) and struggling with social comparison and failure. The tell is that identity work appears premature and the person is not ready.
- **Skipping a stage as "too late."** Believing a 50-year-old cannot repair an infancy-stage trust wound ("they're too old to change"). Erikson's frame allows **lifelong renegotiation** — the stage does not expire.
- **Confusing the conflict with its resolution.** Stage 5 is identity *vs.* role confusion — not "the person successfully found their identity." If a teenager seems to have a stable identity but is actually foreclosing (accepting a parent's or peer group's label without exploration), they are still in the conflict, not resolved.
- **Using the frame to pathologize normal development.** A 7-year-old's shyness or a 20-year-old's relationship instability is not automatically stage failure; it may be healthy variation within stage-appropriate work. The frame names the conflict, not the outcome.

# Just War Theory

## Overview

Just War Theory sorts armed conflicts and military actions into three chronological phases — before, during, and after — and applies distinct ethical and legal criteria to each. The core claim is that legitimacy is not binary (war or not war) but **phase-specific**: a war may be justly initiated but unjustly conducted, or justly fought but followed by unjust terms. Each phase has a separate set of discriminating criteria, and failure to meet the criteria in any phase undermines the legitimacy of the entire conflict. The frame is used in military law, international humanitarian law, and policy debates to classify whether a use of force is permissible.

## Categories

**Phase 1: Jus ad Bellum (Justice of Going to War)**

The criteria that must be met *before* military action begins.

1. **Just Cause**
   - The reason for initiating force must be grave and substantial — typically self-defense, defense of others, or restoration of a serious wrong.
   - Discriminating criterion: the harm prevented or corrected is greater than the harm caused by war itself; vengeance, economic gain, or ideological conversion alone do not qualify.
   - Example: responding to an armed invasion; intervening to stop genocide.

2. **Right Intention**
   - The actual motivation of the decision-maker must align with the stated just cause; not a pretext for other ends.
   - Discriminating criterion: the leadership would pursue the same action even if some collateral benefits (territorial gain, resource access) were not present.
   - Example: a nation declares war to stop aggression, and its private strategic aim is the same; a nation declares humanitarian intervention while privately seeking mineral rights — fails this criterion.

3. **Legitimate Authority**
   - Only a sovereign state or (in modern interpretation) a recognized international body can declare war; not private actors, warlords, or rogue units.
   - Discriminating criterion: the authority has a monopoly on force within its jurisdiction and can be held accountable by law or constituents.
   - Example: a nation-state's government; (debatably) UN-authorized intervention; not a corporation, militia, or terrorist organization.

4. **Last Resort**
   - All reasonable non-military alternatives (negotiation, sanctions, arbitration) must have been attempted or exhausted before force is used.
   - Discriminating criterion: evidence exists that diplomatic or economic measures were genuinely pursued and failed, not merely considered and rejected for convenience.
   - Example: years of failed negotiations followed by military action; declaring war without attempting talks — fails.

5. **Probability of Success**
   - The military action must have a reasonable chance of achieving the just cause; using force when it will certainly fail is reckless.
   - Discriminating criterion: military analysts can articulate a plausible path to the stated objective; quixotic or symbolic gestures do not meet this criterion.
   - Example: defending a territory you can realistically hold; launching a war you know will be lost — fails.

6. **Right Proportion**
   - The good expected from the war (cessation of aggression, restoration of justice) must outweigh the anticipated harm (deaths, destruction, economic cost).
   - Discriminating criterion: reasonable prior estimate suggests the benefit is greater than the cost; the war is not fought for a trivial gain at massive expense.
   - Example: defending your nation from invasion passes; starting a war to recover a small border dispute — fails.

**Phase 2: Jus in Bello (Justice in Conducting War)**

The criteria that govern how force is used *during* armed conflict.

7. **Discrimination (Distinction)**
   - Combatants must distinguish between military targets and civilians; attacks must not intentionally target non-combatants.
   - Discriminating criterion: the attacker takes deliberate precautions to minimize civilian harm and does not treat civilian populations as legitimate targets.
   - Example: bombing a military base; intentionally attacking a market to terrorize civilians — fails.

8. **Proportionality in Means**
   - The force used in any single attack or campaign must be proportional to the military advantage gained; not using a thermonuclear weapon to destroy a single building.
   - Discriminating criterion: the military advantage is not vastly outweighed by the incidental harm and collateral damage.
   - Example: using precision munitions on a command center; using indiscriminate carpet bombing on a small position — fails.

9. **Prohibition of Prohibited Weapons**
   - Certain weapons (chemical, biological, anti-personnel mines, cluster munitions) are prohibited by international law because they cannot be used discriminately or proportionately.
   - Discriminating criterion: the weapon is on a binding international prohibitions list (Chemical Weapons Convention, Biological Weapons Convention) or is inherently indiscriminate.
   - Example: chemical agents, biological pathogens, anti-personnel mines; conventional bombs, precision munitions — permitted within other constraints.

10. **No Prohibited Acts**
    - Certain conduct is forbidden regardless of military advantage: torture, rape, enslavement, summary execution, perfidy (false surrender or misuse of flags).
    - Discriminating criterion: the act is explicitly prohibited in the Geneva Conventions or customary international law, or violates the dignity of combatants and prisoners.
    - Example: executing prisoners of war; torturing detainees; these are war crimes regardless of tactical benefit.

**Phase 3: Jus post Bellum (Justice After War)**

The criteria that apply *after* military hostilities end.

11. **Right Intention in Victory**
    - The victorious party must not use the cessation of conflict as an opportunity to pursue punitive or vengeful settlement beyond what justice requires.
    - Discriminating criterion: peace terms are aimed at restoring the pre-war order or a just new order, not at humiliation or permanent subjugation of the defeated.
    - Example: demanding surrender and occupation with the goal of eventual sovereignty restoration; demanding permanent subjugation or enslavement — fails.

12. **Discrimination in Punishment**
    - Responsibility and punishment must be assigned on an individual basis (to those who made decisions and combatants) and not collectively to the entire defeated population.
    - Discriminating criterion: trials or accountability mechanisms focus on decision-makers, commanders, and those who committed war crimes; civilian populations are not punished for their nation's actions.
    - Example: prosecuting generals for war crimes; imposing collective penalties on a civilian population — fails.

13. **Proportionality in Settlement**
    - Reparations, territorial changes, and penalties imposed on the defeated party must be proportional to the harm caused and the wrong that justified the war.
    - Discriminating criterion: the settlement is calibrated to correct the original injustice, not to extract maximum value or territory as punishment.
    - Example: restoring annexed territory and requiring compensation for damages; requiring the defeated nation to cede additional territory as "punishment" — fails.

14. **Right Restoration**
    - The post-war settlement should, where possible, restore the legitimate order that was violated or establish a just new order; it should not entrench new injustices.
    - Discriminating criterion: the settlement creates conditions for sustainable peace and respect for law, not conditions for future grievance and conflict.
    - Example: internationally-supervised elections to restore civilian governance; imposing a puppet government indefinitely — fails.

15. **Rehabilitation and Reintegration**
    - The victor should, where feasible, assist the defeated party in rebuilding so that it can eventually rejoin the international community as a peer.
    - Discriminating criterion: resources and assistance are extended to reduce suffering and restore capacity for self-governance, not merely to extract advantage.
    - Example: Marshall Plan-style reconstruction; extractive occupation with no intent to build institutions — fails.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Identify the conflict or military action in question.** Write a one-paragraph description: who initiated force, against whom, and what is the stated justification?

2. **Classify the phase.** Is the question about whether to *initiate* force (jus ad bellum), how it is being *conducted* (jus in bello), or what *after* hostilities should look like (jus post bellum)? These are sequential; a conflict must pass jus ad bellum to be legitimate, then must satisfy jus in bello during, then jus post bellum after.

3. **For jus ad bellum (initiation):** Check all six criteria.
   - Does the stated cause qualify as just (self-defense, defense of others, correction of grave wrong)?
   - Does the leadership's actual motivation align with the stated cause?
   - Is the authority initiating force sovereign and accountable?
   - Have non-military alternatives been genuinely exhausted?
   - Is there a reasonable military path to the objective?
   - Does the anticipated benefit outweigh the anticipated cost?
   - If *all six* are met, the initiation is just. If *any one* fails, the war is unjustly initiated.

4. **For jus in bello (conduct):** Check all five criteria as the conflict proceeds.
   - Are attacks discriminating between combatants and civilians?
   - Is the force used in each action proportional to the military advantage?
   - Are prohibited weapons being used?
   - Are prohibited acts (torture, summary execution, etc.) occurring?
   - If *all five* are met, the conduct is just. If *any one* fails, specific acts or campaigns are unjust, even if initiation was just.

5. **For jus post bellum (settlement):** Check all five criteria after hostilities end.
   - Is the peace settlement proportional to the original wrong, not punitive?
   - Are accountability and punishment individualized, not collective?
   - Are reparations and territorial changes calibrated to correct, not punish?
   - Does the settlement establish just conditions for future peace?
   - Are efforts made to rehabilitate and reintegrate the defeated party?
   - If *all five* are met, the peace is just. If *any one* fails, the settlement is unjust, potentially sowing new conflict.

6. **Record the verdict per phase.** State whether the conflict is just or unjust in initiation, conduct, and settlement separately. A conflict can be justly initiated but unjustly conducted; the failure in any phase taints the legitimacy of the whole.

## Implications per Category

| Phase | All Criteria Met | At Least One Criterion Fails |
|---|---|---|
| **Jus ad Bellum** | Force is **justified in law and ethics**; the war is legitimate to initiate. Proceed to jus in bello. | Force is **unjustified**; the war should not be initiated. Continued pursuit of non-military alternatives is required. If force is used anyway, it is an unjust war and all parties bear moral liability. |
| **Jus in Bello** | Conduct is **lawful and ethical** within the legitimate scope of warfare. Continue conduct as planned; violations are prosecutable as war crimes. | Specific acts or campaigns are **unlawful**; they are war crimes or violations of humanitarian law. Perpetrators bear individual criminal liability; commanders who knew or should have known bear responsibility. Cease the violating conduct immediately. |
| **Jus post Bellum** | Settlement is **just and sustainable**; it restores or establishes legitimate order and creates conditions for durable peace. Implement as planned; it honors the original justification for the war. | Settlement is **unjust and destabilizing**; it sows grievance, entrenches new injustices, or imposes collective punishment. Renegotiate terms to align with justice criteria; an unjust peace may legitimize future resistance or conflict. |

For legal and policy analysis, the practical implication is which **investigative and adjudicative process** is appropriate:
- jus ad bellum violations → questions for political leaders, legislatures, and diplomatic bodies; retrospective accountability through international courts (ICJ, ICC).
- jus in bello violations → questions for military commanders and combatants; prosecutions for war crimes (ICC, ad hoc tribunals, domestic courts).
- jus post bellum violations → questions for negotiators and settlement implementers; remedial obligations under international law and humanitarian principles.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Jus ad bellum mistaken for jus in bello.** A decision-maker argues that the war is justified because the *conduct* has been lawful — treating "we obeyed the rules of engagement" as evidence that "we were right to go to war." These are independent; just conduct does not retroactively justify an unjust cause. The tell is that the original just-cause criteria (exhaustion of alternatives, probability of success, right intention) are not examined.

- **Jus in bello violations dismissed as "collateral damage."** Treating every civilian casualty as permissible if "proportional" to military advantage, without distinguishing between lawful proportionality and unlawful indiscrimination. The tell is that attacks are not preceded by precautions to minimize civilian harm, or civilians are knowingly targeted.

- **Jus post bellum collapsed into jus ad bellum.** Arguing that because the settlement will be harsh, the war was unjustly initiated — treating post-war plans as the cause of the war rather than separate criteria. While harsh settlement may be evidence that intention was punitive from the start, the two phases must be evaluated separately. A justly initiated war can become unjust in settlement, or vice versa.

- **Applying jus ad bellum criteria after the war has begun.** Once hostilities start, jus ad bellum is no longer actionable; the relevant criteria are jus in bello (how to conduct the ongoing war) and, later, jus post bellum (how to end it justly). Retroactively re-litigating initiation criteria mid-conflict is a category error.

- **Ignoring the "all six" or "all five" requirement.** Treating Just War Theory as a checklist where meeting three criteria is enough. The theory requires *all* criteria in a phase to be met for that phase to be just. Failure in any single criterion (e.g., lack of legitimate authority, use of prohibited weapons, collective punishment) renders that phase unjust.

- **Applying jus post bellum criteria to an ongoing conflict.** Arguing that because the victor has imposed harsh terms, the war was unjust — when the war is still being fought. Jus post bellum applies only *after* hostilities cease and settlement begins. During active conflict, the relevant criteria are jus in bello.

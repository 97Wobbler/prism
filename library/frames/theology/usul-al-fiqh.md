# Usul al-Fiqh

## Overview

Usul al-Fiqh (the roots or foundations of Islamic jurisprudence) is a hierarchical frame that sorts a legal or ethical question into one or more of four primary sources of Islamic law. Its core claim is that different questions have different **source authorities and evidentiary weights** — and attempting to derive a ruling from a weaker source when a stronger one applies is a systematic error. The frame also establishes a **priority order**: when multiple sources speak to the same question, the hierarchy determines which takes precedence. For an Islamic jurist (faqih) or agent consulting Islamic law, correctly placing a question within Usul al-Fiqh is the prerequisite for choosing the right interpretive method and the right school (madhhab) of jurisprudence.

## Categories

1. **Quran (القرآن)**
   - The revealed word of God; the **primary and highest authority** in Islamic law.
   - Statements in the Quran are binding, explicit, and require no further evidence.
   - Discriminating criterion: the question is **directly and unambiguously addressed** in a Quranic verse whose meaning is clear (muhkam) to trained scholars.
   - Example: prohibitions on usury (riba), rules of inheritance for spouses and children, dietary laws (halal/haram).

2. **Sunnah (السنة)**
   - The **authenticated practices and sayings** of the Prophet Muhammad, transmitted through reliable chains (hadith).
   - Sunnah clarifies, narrows, or applies Quranic principles; it can establish a ruling independent of the Quran if no Quranic text addresses the matter.
   - Discriminating criterion: the question is **addressed by a well-authenticated hadith** (sahih or hasan grade) whose chain of transmission is sound, OR the practice is established as widespread (mutawatir) among the Prophet's companions.
   - Example: the manner of ritual prayer (salah), the prohibition on selling an animal without naming a price, the requirement for two witnesses in marriage.
   - Caveat: strength of a Sunnah ruling depends on the grade of the hadith; weaker transmissions (da'if) carry less weight than those graded sahih.

3. **Ijma (الإجماع)**
   - **Consensus** of qualified Islamic scholars (mujtahids) of a particular generation on a legal question.
   - Ijma is binding and considered infallible because it is believed to be guided by the Prophet's statement: "My community will not agree upon an error."
   - Discriminating criterion: the question has **no direct Quranic or Sunnah text**, AND **all (or an overwhelming supermajority of) recognized mujtahids of a given era agree** on the ruling.
   - Example: the illegality of deliberately killing a Muslim without just cause (beyond the Quranic ayat); consensus that a caliph must be Muslim.
   - Caveat: Ijma is difficult to prove conclusively; silence from a scholar is not always agreement, and historical Ijma may not bind later generations if contexts change.

4. **Qiyas (القياس)**
   - **Analogical reasoning**: extending a ruling from a case explicitly addressed in Quran or Sunnah (the "original case") to a new case (the "branch") that shares the **same effective cause (illah)**.
   - Qiyas is the **weakest of the four primary sources**; it is used only when no direct Quranic, Sunnah, or Ijma text applies.
   - Discriminating criterion: the question involves a **new or unprecedented situation** NOT directly addressed by Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma, AND a trained jurist can identify a **clear parallel case and its underlying reason (illah)** that justifies extending the original ruling.
   - Example: analogizing the prohibition on wine (khamr) to modern intoxicating drugs; reasoning that a transaction haraam for one reason should be haraam if that reason is present in a new context.
   - Caveat: Qiyas is controversial; some schools reject certain types (especially those that contradict apparent Sunnah). The validity and strength of Qiyas depends on the clarity and universality of the illah.

## Classification Procedure

1. **State the question clearly.** Write the legal or ethical question in plain language, including context (who is asking, what are the circumstances).

2. **Does the Quran directly address this question?**
   - If **yes, and the meaning is clear (muhkam)** to trained interpreters → the ruling is from **Quran**. Stop.
   - If **yes, but the meaning is ambiguous or metaphorical (mutashabih)** → consult Sunnah to clarify. Go to step 3.
   - If **no** → go to step 3.

3. **Does an authenticated Sunnah (hadith of strong grade, or widespread practice) directly address this question?**
   - If **yes** → the ruling is from **Sunnah**. Stop.
   - If **no** → go to step 4.

4. **Is there explicit Ijma (documented consensus of qualified scholars) on this question?**
   - If **yes** → the ruling is from **Ijma**. Stop.
   - If **no** or **unclear whether Ijma exists** → go to step 5.

5. **Can the question be resolved by analogy (Qiyas) to a case in Quran or Sunnah?**
   - Identify the **original case** (the Quranic or Sunnah precedent).
   - Identify the **effective cause (illah)** — the reason or principle that made the original ruling necessary.
   - Ask: **"Is that same illah present in the new case?"**
   - If **yes, and the analogy is sound** → the ruling is by **Qiyas**. Stop.
   - If **no valid analogy can be drawn** → the question is **indeterminate within Usul al-Fiqh**; resort to secondary sources (maslaha, urf, or istihsan, depending on school).

6. **Record the primary source (or sources, if multiple apply) and state which takes precedence** according to the hierarchy: Quran > Sunnah > Ijma > Qiyas.

## Implications per Category

| Source | What it means | How the agent should proceed |
|---|---|---|
| **Quran** | Binding, highest authority, no further justification needed. | Apply the ruling as stated; if the meaning is ambiguous, consult tafsir (Quranic interpretation) to clarify. |
| **Sunnah** | Authoritative, but strength depends on hadith grade. Sahih > Hasan > Da'if. | Verify the grade of the hadith; if sahih or hasan, apply with confidence. If da'if, note the weakness and check for corroboration from other sources. |
| **Ijma** | Binding and infallible; used when Quran and Sunnah are silent. | Apply without qualification; Ijma is considered a closed door in most schools. |
| **Qiyas** | Permissible but weakest; used only when no higher source applies. Valid Qiyas requires sound illah and clear parallel. | Identify the illah explicitly; if the illah is disputed across schools, note the disagreement. Qiyas can be overridden by stronger Sunnah evidence or Ijma. |

**Practical implication:** When consulting a jurist or referencing Islamic law, always state which source grounds the ruling. A ruling based on Quran carries different weight than one based on Qiyas. Different schools (Maliki, Hanafi, Shafi'i, Hanbali) may weight Qiyas or secondary sources (maslaha) differently, so identifying the source constrains which school's reasoning applies.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Confusing weak Sunnah (da'if hadith) with no Sunnah at all.** A da'if hadith is not absent evidence; it exists but carries lower weight. The tell is that a jurist may still rely on da'if hadith for non-core rulings or supplementary points. The error is dismissing it outright or treating it as binding.

- **Claiming Ijma when only silence exists.** A scholar's silence on a question is not Ijma; Ijma requires explicit agreement. The tell is that later scholars may dispute what earlier ones only remained quiet on. The consequence is overstating the binding force of a ruling.

- **Applying Qiyas when Sunnah or Ijma already exists on the question.** Using analogy to overrule a direct source. The tell is that the jurist has overlooked a hadith or consensus. The consequence is deriving a ruling weaker than the evidence warrants.

- **Choosing an invalid illah for Qiyas.** Analogizing to the wrong reason. Example: prohibiting all intoxicants by analogy to wine based on "mind-altering" as the illah, when the authentic illah is "intoxication to the point of prayer-incompetence" or even narrower reasons depending on school. The consequence is overextending or misapplying the original ruling.

- **Treating Qiyas as equivalent to Quran or Sunnah.** Qiyas is a last resort, not a primary source. The tell is reasoning that "by analogy, one can derive X," when a direct text already forbids or permits X. The consequence is undermining confidence in the ruling.

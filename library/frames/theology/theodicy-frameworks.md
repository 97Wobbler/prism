# Theodicy Frameworks

## Overview

A theodicy is a proposed resolution to the problem of evil — the apparent incompatibility between an omnipotent, omniscient, omnibenevolent God and the existence of suffering. This frame sorts theodicies by their **core metaphysical commitment about the source and purpose of evil**, not by rhetorical force or pastoral comfort. Different theodicies rest on fundamentally different claims about God's nature, human agency, and the nature of creation itself. Sorting a theodicy into its category reveals which other theodicies it is compatible with, which it contradicts, and what kind of follow-up challenge each one faces.

## Categories

1. **Augustinian / Soul-Making Through Privation**
   - Evil is the **absence of good**, not a substance in itself; it arises because finite creation is necessarily imperfect.
   - God permits evil (especially moral evil through human free will) as the price of genuine human agency.
   - Discriminating criterion: the theodicy asserts that evil is *not created by God* but rather results from the created order's lack of perfection or human misuse of freedom; free will is the non-negotiable value that justifies permitting moral evil.
   - Example: Augustine's account in *Confessions* and *City of God*; Leibniz's "best of all possible worlds"; modern reformulations that emphasize libertarian free will as inviolable.

2. **Irenaean / Soul-Making Through Adversity**
   - Evil (especially suffering) is a **necessary instrument for spiritual and moral development**; a soul formed in a consequence-free paradise would not be virtuous.
   - God permits evil not because creation is imperfect, but because growth requires resistance, struggle, and real stakes.
   - Discriminating criterion: the theodicy treats suffering as *pedagogically purposeful*; natural evil (disease, hardship) is justified on developmental grounds, not as mere privation; the emphasis is on what the person becomes, not on what God could have prevented.
   - Example: Irenaeus of Lyons; John Hick's *Evil and the God of Love*; theodicies that emphasize character formation through trial.

3. **Process Theology / Persuasive Divine Power**
   - God is **not omnipotent** in the classical sense; God influences the world through persuasion, not coercion.
   - Evil arises because creation has genuine autonomy and God cannot unilaterally control outcomes without violating the metaphysical structure of reality itself.
   - Discriminating criterion: the theodicy **redefines omnipotence** to exclude logical impossibilities (including the coercion of free agents) and asserts that God's power is real but limited by the nature of a world with genuine agents; God suffers alongside creation rather than standing outside it.
   - Example: Whitehead, Charles Hartshorne; open theism variants; theologies that sacrifice classical omnipotence to preserve both God's goodness and creaturely freedom.

4. **Skeptical Theism / Divine Mystery**
   - Our cognitive and moral framework may be **radically inadequate** to God's reasons; we cannot expect to have a satisfying theodicy because we may not be capable of understanding why God permits evil.
   - Evil is real and unjustified *from a human perspective*, but God may have reasons that are opaque to us — not because they are incoherent, but because we lack the requisite concepts.
   - Discriminating criterion: the theodicy *declines to offer a positive explanation* for evil and instead argues that the *absence* of a human-comprehensible justification is not evidence against God's existence; it invokes epistemic humility rather than a theodicean theory.
   - Example: Alvin Plantinga's later work; Trent Dougherty's formulations; responses that prioritize the logical coherence of theism over the intelligibility of evil.

5. **Free Will Defense (Modern Libertarian)**
   - Moral evil is **logically necessary** because genuine freedom includes the metaphysical possibility of wrong choice; even God cannot guarantee that free agents will always choose rightly.
   - God could not create a world with free agents who always freely choose good — it is a logical contradiction, like a "round square."
   - Discriminating criterion: the theodicy limits God's power on *logical* grounds (not metaphysical or epistemic limitation); moral evil is justified entirely by the value of libertarian freedom; this does not address natural evil directly.
   - Example: Plantinga's *God, Freedom, and Evil*; contemporary analytic theodicies; libertarian responses that do not claim God's persuasion or educational purposes justify suffering.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Identify the core claim about the origin of evil.** Ask: "Is evil treated as a substance/force that God created, a privation/absence, a limitation on God's power, or a logical necessity?"
   - Substance or privation → proceed to step 2.
   - Limitation on God's power → likely **Process Theology**.
   - Logical necessity only (moral evil) → likely **Free Will Defense**.

2. **If evil is treated as privation or absence, ask: "Is the justification grounded in human freedom, spiritual development through suffering, or God's epistemic limits?"**
   - Freedom (and God must respect it) → **Augustinian** or **Free Will Defense**.
   - Spiritual development / growth through adversity → **Irenaean**.
   - Epistemic limits / God's reasons unknowable → **Skeptical Theism**.

3. **Distinguish Augustinian from Free Will Defense:** Does the theodicy claim God's inability to guarantee good choices (logical) or God's commitment to respect human autonomy (volitional)? Does it address natural evil?
   - Logical impossibility, focuses on moral evil → **Free Will Defense**.
   - Volitional respect, addresses imperfection of creation → **Augustinian**.

4. **If the theodicy emphasizes that God is not omnipotent in the classical sense**, or that God's power is inherently limited by the structure of reality, or that God works through persuasion not coercion → **Process Theology**.

5. **If the theodicy refuses to provide a positive justification for evil and instead invokes epistemic humility or God's transcendence**, it is **Skeptical Theism** regardless of other commitments.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Strengths | Vulnerabilities | What follows |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Augustinian** | Preserves omnipotence; explains moral evil via freedom; deeply traditional. | Struggles with natural evil (disease, children's suffering) and the apparent waste of suffering that produces no growth. | Needs supplementary account of natural evil; vulnerable to charges of circular reasoning (God permits evil so we can be free, but why freedom at this cost?). |
| **Irenaean** | Explains natural evil as pedagogically purposeful; aligns with observed human development. | Requires that massive suffering (childhood cancer, genocide) be justified on developmental grounds — a heavy burden; struggles with suffering that appears *only* destructive, not formative. | Vulnerable to the charge that infinitely many lesser-evil-producing worlds exist that God could have created instead. |
| **Process Theology** | Radically honest about the problem; abandons the tension by redefining God; explains why God cannot prevent evil. | Violates classical theism; requires reinterpretation of traditional doctrines; God becomes less powerful and less comforting. | Invites questions about whether a limited God is still worthy of worship and still "God" in the classical sense. |
| **Skeptical Theism** | Logically rigorous; avoids false theodicies; preserves classical omnipotence and benevolence. | Provides no *positive* understanding of evil; can seem evasive or defeatist; vulnerable to the charge that it makes God unknowable or indifferent. | Requires pairing with a *defensive* strategy: show that the problem of evil is not a *logical* contradiction (even if it remains a *logical mystery*). |
| **Free Will Defense** | Powerful explanation of moral evil; logically tight; does not require redefining God or sacrificing human agency. | Does not address natural evil; can seem to trivialize suffering by reducing it to a logical consequence; vulnerable to the charge that God could have created fewer free agents or structured freedom differently. | Often paired with Skeptical Theism (for natural evil) or Irenaean supplementation (for suffering's constructive role). |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Augustinian mistaken for Irenaean.** Both invoke human freedom, but Augustinian treats freedom as the intrinsic good that justifies permitting evil, while Irenaean treats suffering as a *means* to growth. The tell: does the theodicy say "freedom is worth it" (Augustinian) or "suffering makes you better" (Irenaean)? Irenaean can justify suffering that diminishes freedom (a child's illness); Augustinian cannot.

- **Free Will Defense mistaken for Augustinian.** Free Will Defense claims God *cannot* guarantee a free agent's choices (logical necessity); Augustinian claims God *chooses not to* override freedom (volitional). The tell: does the theodicy treat libertarian freedom as metaphysically inviolable (Free Will) or as a value God respects (Augustinian)? Free Will Defense does not address why God could not create a world with fewer opportunities for sin.

- **Process Theology mistaken for Skeptical Theism.** Both limit what we can know about God's reasons, but Process Theology provides a *positive* metaphysical reason (God is not omnipotent), while Skeptical Theism declines to (our concepts are inadequate). The tell: does the theodicy claim God is *actually* limited in power (Process) or merely that we *cannot know* God's reasons (Skeptical)?

- **Skeptical Theism mistaken for evasion or atheism.** Skeptical Theism is logically compatible with classical theism; it simply refuses to solve the problem at the human level. The tell: a genuine Skeptical Theist asserts that God *exists and is good*, and that the apparent contradiction dissolves when human cognitive limits are acknowledged — not that the problem is unsolvable because God does not exist.

- **Treating multiple theodicies as compatible when they contradict.** Irenaean (suffering justified as growth) and Augustinian (suffering justified as price of freedom) can coexist, but Free Will Defense (moral evil is a logical necessity) and Process Theology (God is not omnipotent) are in tension — they make incompatible claims about what God can and cannot do. The tell: look at whether the theodicy asserts God's omnipotence or denies it; if one asserts and one denies, they cannot both be true.

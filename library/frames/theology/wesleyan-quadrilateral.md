# Wesleyan Quadrilateral

## Overview

The Wesleyan Quadrilateral sorts theological claims and ethical decisions by identifying which of four sources of authority — Scripture, Tradition, Reason, and Experience — grounds them. Its core claim is that these four sources are **not hierarchical in the abstract**, but rather their relative weight **depends on the kind of question being asked** and the community asking it. A claim that appears well-supported by Reason and Experience may still require validation against Scripture; a claim rooted in Tradition may need to survive Reason's scrutiny. Sorting a theological problem by which source is doing the load-bearing work prevents false consensus and clarifies where actual disagreement lies.

## Categories

1. **Scripture-Primary**
   - The claim rests fundamentally on direct reading or interpretation of biblical text.
   - Other sources (Tradition, Reason, Experience) may *support* the scriptural reading, but Scripture is the decisive court of appeal.
   - Discriminating criterion: removal or reinterpretation of the scriptural foundation collapses the claim.
   - Example: the doctrine of the Trinity, grounded in New Testament passages; the affirmation of resurrection as literal physical event.

2. **Tradition-Primary**
   - The claim is rooted in the accumulated teaching, liturgy, or practice of the church across generations.
   - Scripture may be cited as *support*, but the central authority is that "the church has always taught this."
   - Discriminating criterion: the claim has deep historical continuity, and its loss would rupture continuity with the communion of saints.
   - Example: the creeds (Nicene, Apostles'); the liturgical calendar; the doctrine of apostolic succession in episcopal traditions.

3. **Reason-Primary**
   - The claim follows from philosophical argument, logical inference, or intellectual coherence requirements.
   - Scripture and Tradition may be *consistent* with the reasoning, but Reason provides the compulsion.
   - Discriminating criterion: the claim would follow even if Tradition were silent, and appeal is made to universal rational intelligibility, not community-specific authority.
   - Example: natural law ethics; the philosophical argument that God is omnipotent; coherence requirements in trinitarian theology.

4. **Experience-Primary**
   - The claim is grounded in lived encounter, testimony, or the observed fruits of faith in practice.
   - Scripture, Tradition, and Reason may all affirm it, but the warrant is "*I have encountered this*" or "*this practice yields these goods*."
   - Discriminating criterion: the claim would stand even if no scriptural proof-text existed, because the testimonial evidence is direct.
   - Example: the reality of the Holy Spirit's work (known through prayer, conversion, healing); the goodness of forgiveness (known through lived reconciliation).

## Classification Procedure

1. State the theological claim or ethical decision in a single sentence.
2. Ask: **"Is there a biblical text that directly grounds this claim?"**
   - If **yes**, ask whether Scripture is *decisive* (removing it would collapse the claim) or merely *supporting* (the claim would survive reinterpretation of the text). If decisive → Scripture-Primary. If supporting, go to step 3.
   - If **no**, go to step 3.
3. Ask: **"Does this claim rely on the historical continuity or authority of the church's teaching?"**
   - If **yes and it is decisive** → Tradition-Primary.
   - If **yes but only as support**, go to step 4.
4. Ask: **"Does this claim follow from philosophical or logical argument that would hold independent of any specific tradition?"**
   - If **yes and it is decisive** → Reason-Primary.
   - If **yes but only as support**, go to step 5.
5. Ask: **"Is the primary warrant the lived testimony or observed fruits of faith?"**
   - If **yes** → Experience-Primary.
   - If none of the above are decisive, the claim is **under-grounded** and requires further work to specify which source(s) should carry the weight.
6. State the classification and note which secondary sources provide supporting evidence.

## Implications per Category

| Category | What it means | How to respond |
|---|---|---|
| **Scripture-Primary** | The claim's validity depends on fidelity to biblical interpretation. | Theological work must engage exegesis directly; alternative readings of the text are live options and must be addressed. |
| **Tradition-Primary** | The claim's validity depends on maintaining continuity with the church's received wisdom. | Theological work must account for why the tradition formed as it did and what would be lost by departing from it. Innovation requires showing why the tradition itself calls for development. |
| **Reason-Primary** | The claim's validity depends on logical coherence and philosophical intelligibility. | Theological work must clarify the argument and invite rational scrutiny; appeal is to universal reason, not community authority. |
| **Experience-Primary** | The claim's validity depends on its fruits in lived faith and testimony. | Theological work must attend to how the claim functions in actual discipleship, prayer, and community; abstract argument alone does not settle it. |

For a theological community, the practical implication is which **voices and methods** carry authority in deliberation:
- Scripture-Primary → exegetes and biblical scholars lead; other traditions may inform but cannot override.
- Tradition-Primary → historians and liturgists lead; continuity with the communion of saints is a non-negotiable constraint.
- Reason-Primary → philosophers and systematic theologians lead; arguments are subjected to rigorous logical scrutiny.
- Experience-Primary → pastors, practitioners, and witnesses lead; empirical evidence of faith's fruits is decisive.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Treating all sources as equally weighted.** The frame is not a "vote" among four equals; one source typically carries decisive weight. Conflating a supporting source with a primary source obscures where actual disagreement lies.
- **Scripture-Primary mistaken for Reason-Primary.** Confusing "the claim is supported by a logical argument" with "Scripture grounds the claim." The tell is that the argument would work equally well outside the Christian tradition.
- **Tradition-Primary mistaken for Scripture-Primary.** Assuming that because a teaching is ancient, it must be biblical. The tell is that careful exegesis of the relevant passages produces ambiguity or silence on the point, yet the tradition is clear.
- **Experience-Primary mistaken for Reason-Primary.** Treating personal testimony as if it were a universal argument. The tell is that the argument depends on the speaker's identity ("this happened to me") rather than standing on logical grounds anyone could grasp.
- **Classifying a claim as grounded in a source when it actually lacks grounding.** A claim may be *consistent* with Scripture, Tradition, Reason, and Experience without being *grounded* in any of them. The discriminating move: ask what would have to change for the claim to be false. If the answer is "nothing," it is not grounded, only asserted.

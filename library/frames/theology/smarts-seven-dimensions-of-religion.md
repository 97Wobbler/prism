# Smart's Seven Dimensions of Religion

## Overview

Smart's Seven Dimensions is a taxonomic frame that sorts the observable and experiential aspects of a religious tradition into seven analytically distinct categories. Its core claim is that religions are **multidimensional systems**, and that reducing a tradition to a single dimension (doctrine alone, or ritual alone) produces systematic misunderstanding. A complete view of Islam, Christianity, Buddhism, or any other tradition requires examining how it manifests across all seven dimensions — and understanding that a tradition may be strong in some dimensions and weak in others. The frame is particularly useful for comparative religion, because it allows traditions to be sorted and compared on a level playing field rather than privileging one dimension (e.g., doctrine) as primary.

## Categories

1. **Doctrinal (교리)**
   - The systematic set of beliefs, creeds, and theological propositions a tradition affirms.
   - One-sentence definition: the cognitive and propositional content that adherents are expected to assent to or at least understand.
   - Discriminating criterion: explicit, teachable, and often written down; it is what a scholar can extract from scripture, theology texts, or formal creeds.
   - Example: the Trinity in Christianity, the Five Pillars in Islam, the Four Noble Truths in Buddhism.

2. **Mythic (신화)**
   - The narrative traditions, stories, and symbolic accounts of sacred history and cosmology.
   - One-sentence definition: the sacred stories and archetypal narratives that anchor a tradition's sense of time, origin, and cosmic order.
   - Discriminating criterion: whether true or false by scientific standards, these narratives are **normative and meaning-bearing** for adherents; they explain *why* the world is as it is.
   - Example: the creation account in Genesis, the life story of the Buddha, the Qur'anic narrative of the prophets.

3. **Ethical (윤리)**
   - The moral norms, laws, codes of conduct, and prescriptions for how adherents ought to live.
   - One-sentence definition: the framework of right and wrong action that a tradition transmits and expects its members to embody.
   - Discriminating criterion: it is prescriptive and performance-oriented; it tells you what to do, not just what to believe.
   - Example: the Ten Commandments, the Eightfold Path, the Sharia, monastic vows.

4. **Ritual (의식)**
   - The formal practices, ceremonies, prayer forms, and prescribed actions through which adherents engage with the sacred.
   - One-sentence definition: the structured, often repetitive actions and ceremonies that mark transitions, commemorate events, or commune with the sacred.
   - Discriminating criterion: it is enacted and embodied rather than merely believed; it is **performed and participatory**.
   - Example: the Christian Eucharist, the Islamic salat, Hindu puja, Jewish Shabbat observance.

5. **Experiential (경험)**
   - The subjective, affective, and mystical experiences that adherents report — ecstasy, transcendence, encounter with the divine, inner peace.
   - One-sentence definition: the felt, phenomenological dimension of religious life — what it is like to undergo a religious experience.
   - Discriminating criterion: it is first-person, interior, and often described as ineffable or beyond language; it is what practitioners report *feeling* or *encountering*.
   - Example: the Sufi's mystical union with God, the Christian's "born again" conversion, the yogin's samadhi, the Hasid's devekut.

6. **Institutional (제도)**
   - The organized structures, hierarchies, leadership roles, communities, and organizational forms through which the tradition is preserved and transmitted.
   - One-sentence definition: the human social apparatus — churches, temples, monastic orders, clerical structures, and communities — that sustains the tradition across generations.
   - Discriminating criterion: it is visible, hierarchical, and often formally organized; it is the **infrastructure of authority and belonging**.
   - Example: the Catholic priesthood and episcopal structure, the ummah and Qadi system in Islam, the Sangha in Buddhism, the Rabbinate in Judaism.

7. **Material (물질)**
   - The physical artifacts, sacred spaces, art, architecture, vestments, and objects that embody and express religious meaning.
   - One-sentence definition: the tangible, sensory, and visual dimension — sacred buildings, icons, reliquaries, texts, and objects of devotion.
   - Discriminating criterion: it is **physically present** and can be perceived through the senses; it creates an environment and aesthetic through which the tradition is experienced.
   - Example: the Christian cathedral's architecture and stained glass, the Islamic mosque's calligraphy and geometric design, Hindu temple sculpture, the Jewish Torah scroll.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Name the religious tradition or expression** you wish to examine. It can be a major world religion, a sect, a local practice, or a historical period (e.g., "Catholicism in medieval Europe" or "contemporary American Evangelical Christianity").

2. **For each of the seven dimensions, write a short paragraph describing how that dimension manifests** in this tradition. Ask yourself:
   - Doctrinal: What core beliefs or creeds define this tradition?
   - Mythic: What sacred narratives or cosmological stories frame its worldview?
   - Ethical: What moral norms and codes of conduct govern adherent behavior?
   - Ritual: What ceremonies, prayers, or prescribed actions are central?
   - Experiential: What kinds of subjective or mystical experiences are valued or reported?
   - Institutional: What organizations, roles, and community structures sustain it?
   - Material: What physical artifacts, sacred spaces, or objects embody its meaning?

3. **Identify which dimensions are most salient** (strongest, most visible, most emphasized) and which are weakest or absent in this tradition.

4. **Note any tension or inconsistency across dimensions.** For example, a tradition might have rich ritual and material dimensions but weak experiential emphasis; or strong doctrine but fragmented institutional structure.

5. **Use the resulting map to frame your analysis or comparison.** Different traditions will show different dimensional profiles: Quakerism is strong in the Experiential but minimal in the Ritual; Roman Catholicism is rich across all seven; some contemporary Western "spiritual but not religious" expressions may be strong in Experiential and Ethical but weak in Doctrinal and Institutional.

## Implications per Category

Once you have located a tradition (or an aspect of it) in each dimension, the frame tells you:

| Dimension | What to look for | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Doctrinal** | Written statements, creeds, theology texts, scriptural interpretation. | Reveals the intellectual and propositional foundation; shows what adherents are taught to assent to. |
| **Mythic** | Sacred stories, creation accounts, hagiography, apocalyptic narratives. | Reveals the narrative framework that gives time and history meaning; shows how adherents understand their place in a cosmic story. |
| **Ethical** | Laws, moral codes, prescriptions, monastic rules, behavioral standards. | Reveals the practical framework for living; shows what a tradition considers virtuous and what it prohibits. |
| **Ritual** | Ceremonies, liturgies, prayer forms, seasonal observances, rites of passage. | Reveals the performative and embodied engagement with the sacred; shows how adherents *participate* rather than just believe. |
| **Experiential** | Mystical accounts, conversion narratives, testimonies, ecstatic phenomena. | Reveals the interior, subjective dimension; shows what practitioners report *feeling* and *encountering*. |
| **Institutional** | Church structures, priesthoods, monastic orders, councils, seminaries. | Reveals the social and organizational machinery; shows how authority is distributed and how the tradition is preserved. |
| **Material** | Sacred buildings, icons, reliquaries, vestments, liturgical objects, art. | Reveals the aesthetic and sensory dimension; shows how the tradition is **embedded in space and made visible**. |

**Comparative utility:** Two traditions may both affirm similar doctrines (e.g., God's transcendence) but manifest very differently in ritual, material culture, or institutional structure. Smart's frame allows you to compare them systematically across all seven dimensions rather than concluding they are "the same" based on doctrine alone.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Reducing a tradition to doctrine alone.** The tell is that you can describe the tradition's beliefs but cannot explain its lived practice, art, or community experience. This is a common scholarly error and produces an impoverished understanding.

- **Mistaking one dimension for another.** Example: confusing a myth (narrative, Mythic) with a doctrine (propositional claim, Doctrinal). A creation story is not a creed; it may support a creed, but they are analytically distinct. The consequence is unclear category boundaries when you try to compare traditions.

- **Assuming all seven dimensions carry equal weight.** They do not; a tradition may be intellectually austere but ritually rich. The tell is that you are trying to force-fit a weak dimension where it does not actually matter in the tradition's self-understanding. Instead, describe the actual profile: strong in Ritual and Material, weak in Doctrinal and Experiential.

- **Conflating Institutional with Doctrinal.** The tell is that you are attributing a rule or practice to "what the tradition teaches" when it is actually an organizational policy or leadership structure. For instance, clerical celibacy in Catholicism is Institutional (a disciplinary rule), not Doctrinal (not a binding theological claim).

- **Treating Material as merely decorative.** The tell is that you describe sacred objects or architecture as "nice" but not essential. In Smart's frame, the Material dimension is constitutive — the built environment, the icon, the Torah scroll are not optional ornaments but are **how the tradition is experienced sensorialy**.

# Stellar Classification (OBAFGKM)

## Overview

Stellar Classification sorts stars into categories along two orthogonal axes: **spectral type** (determined by absorption line strength, which correlates with surface temperature) and **luminosity class** (determined by apparent magnitude and spectral features, which correlates with radius and evolutionary state). The core claim is that a star's position in this two-dimensional space **uniquely constrains its physical properties** — temperature, radius, mass, lifetime, and evolutionary stage — and predicts which observational strategies and follow-up analyses are appropriate. A star classified as O5V (a hot, main-sequence dwarf) demands entirely different interpretation than a K2III (a cool, giant): the first burns its hydrogen violently in a few million years; the second has already exhausted its core hydrogen and is expanding. Classification is not merely labeling; it is the gateway to the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram and stellar evolution theory.

## Categories

### Spectral Types (Temperature sequence: hottest to coolest)

1. **O (Ultraviolet-Dominated)**
   - Surface temperature: **30,000 K and above**.
   - Absorption lines: ionized helium, weak hydrogen Balmer lines.
   - Discriminating criterion: ionized helium absorption lines are the strongest feature; neutral helium is barely visible.
   - Example: Zeta Orionis (O9.7Ib), a blue supergiant in Orion; 10 Lacertae (O9V), a hot main-sequence star.

2. **B (Blue)**
   - Surface temperature: **10,000–30,000 K**.
   - Absorption lines: neutral helium dominates; hydrogen Balmer lines strengthen.
   - Discriminating criterion: neutral helium lines are prominent; ionized helium is absent or very weak.
   - Example: Rigel (Beta Orionis, B8Iae), a blue supergiant; Vega (Alpha Lyrae, A0V), technically A but often cited as B boundary.

3. **A (Blue-White)**
   - Surface temperature: **7,500–10,000 K**.
   - Absorption lines: hydrogen Balmer lines reach maximum strength; metals (Ca, Fe) begin to appear.
   - Discriminating criterion: hydrogen Balmer lines dominate the spectrum; ionized metals are weak or absent.
   - Example: Vega (Alpha Lyrae, A0V), the standard for A0; Sirius (Alpha Canis Majoris, A1V).

4. **F (White)**
   - Surface temperature: **6,000–7,500 K**.
   - Absorption lines: hydrogen Balmer lines weaken; metallic lines strengthen; ionized calcium (Ca II) begins.
   - Discriminating criterion: metal lines are now as prominent as hydrogen; ionized calcium (H and K lines) appears.
   - Example: Procyon (Alpha Canis Minoris, F5IV–V); Canopus (Alpha Carinae, F0Ib), a yellow supergiant.

5. **G (Yellow)**
   - Surface temperature: **5,200–6,000 K**.
   - Absorption lines: ionized calcium (H and K) strong; neutral metals pronounced; hydrogen lines weak.
   - Discriminating criterion: ionized calcium lines are the dominant feature; the spectrum shows a "forest" of weak metal lines.
   - Example: the Sun (G2V, the standard); Alpha Centauri A (G2V); Capella A (G1III).

6. **K (Orange)**
   - Surface temperature: **3,700–5,200 K**.
   - Absorption lines: neutral metals dominate; ionized calcium still present but weaker; molecular bands (CN, CH) begin to appear.
   - Discriminating criterion: neutral metal lines (Fe, Ca, Ti) are much stronger than ionized lines; molecular absorption starts.
   - Example: Arcturus (Alpha Boötis, K2III), a giant; Aldebaran (Alpha Tauri, K5III).

7. **M (Red)**
   - Surface temperature: **below 3,700 K**.
   - Absorption lines: molecular bands (titanium oxide TiO, vanadium oxide VO) dominate; neutral metals very strong.
   - Discriminating criterion: TiO molecular bands are clearly visible and strong; the spectrum is crowded with molecular and neutral metal features.
   - Example: Antares (Alpha Scorpii, M1Ib), a red supergiant; Betelgeuse (Alpha Orionis, M2Iab), a red supergiant; Proxima Centauri (M6Ve).

### Luminosity Classes (Radius and evolutionary state)

1. **0–Ia, Ia (Hypergiant and Bright Supergiant)**
   - Absolute magnitude: **≤ –7** (extremely luminous).
   - Physical state: **enormous radius**, relatively low surface gravity, extended atmosphere.
   - Discriminating criterion: spectral lines are very broad due to strong gravity effects and rapid rotation; show P Cygni profiles (emission + absorption in Balmer lines) and extended wind signatures.
   - Example: Eta Carinae (P60 Ia+), a hypergiant binary; Rigel (Beta Orionis, B8Iae).

2. **Ib–II (Supergiant)**
   - Absolute magnitude: **–5 to –7** (highly luminous).
   - Physical state: **very large radius**, low surface gravity, strong stellar winds.
   - Discriminating criterion: spectral lines moderately broad; supergiant characteristics visible in line width and ionization balance.
   - Example: Betelgeuse (Alpha Orionis, M2Iab), a red supergiant; Deneb (Alpha Cygni, A2Iae).

3. **III (Giant)**
   - Absolute magnitude: **+0.5 to –5** (intrinsically bright).
   - Physical state: **expanded radius** (10–100 solar radii), moderate surface gravity, exhausted core hydrogen.
   - Discriminating criterion: spectral lines narrower than supergiants but broader than main-sequence; ionization balance shows intermediate gravity; CN/CH molecular bands enhanced (due to CNO processing in the envelope).
   - Example: Arcturus (Alpha Boötis, K2III); Aldebaran (Alpha Tauri, K5III).

4. **IV (Subgiant)**
   - Absolute magnitude: **+2 to +5** (intermediate brightness).
   - Physical state: **slightly expanded** from main-sequence size; just beginning to evolve away from the main sequence after exhausting core hydrogen.
   - Discriminating criterion: gravity intermediate between giant and main-sequence; spectral lines narrower than giants; gravity-sensitive lines (Ca II H and K) show hybrid morphology.
   - Example: Procyon B (companion to Sirius, F5IV); Altair (Alpha Aquilae, A7IV–V) is borderline.

5. **V (Main-Sequence Dwarf)**
   - Absolute magnitude: **+5 to +15+** (intrinsically faint to very faint).
   - Physical state: **compact radius** (roughly solar to sub-solar), **high surface gravity**, actively fusing hydrogen in core.
   - Discriminating criterion: sharp, narrow spectral lines; strong pressure broadening of Balmer lines; Ca II H and K lines are narrow and symmetric.
   - Example: the Sun (G2V); Sirius A (A1V); Proxima Centauri (M6Ve).

6. **VI (Subdwarf)**
   - Absolute magnitude: **≥ +7** (very faint).
   - Physical state: **extremely compact**, **very high surface gravity**, old metal-poor population II stars.
   - Discriminating criterion: spectral lines are very sharp and narrow; metallicity is visibly low ([Fe/H] typically –0.5 to –3); the spectrum shows enhanced ionization for a given temperature.
   - Example: LHS 2924 (M6.5VI), a metal-poor subdwarf; stars in the halo.

7. **VII (White Dwarf)**
   - Absolute magnitude: **+8 to +16+** (extremely faint despite high temperature).
   - Physical state: **Earth-sized radius**, **stellar-mass material compressed to white-dwarf density**, no ongoing fusion.
   - Discriminating criterion: spectrum shows no visible hydrogen and helium lines (if DA white dwarf) or very broad, pressure-broadened lines; extremely high surface gravity produces line broadening unmatched in any other category.
   - Example: Sirius B (A1V companion, the standard white dwarf); Procyon B (F5IV companion).

## Classification Procedure

1. **Obtain a spectrum** (or extract one from a survey) and measure the **equivalent width of hydrogen Balmer lines** (Hα, Hβ, Hγ) and **detect absorption features** (ionized helium, neutral helium, metals, molecules).
2. **Assign spectral type (O through M):**
   - If ionized helium (He II) lines are the strongest → O.
   - If neutral helium (He I) is prominent → B.
   - If hydrogen Balmer lines are maximum-strength and metals are weak → A.
   - If hydrogen is weakening and ionized calcium (Ca II H and K) is rising → F.
   - If ionized calcium dominates and metal forest is visible → G.
   - If neutral metals dominate and ionized calcium weakens → K.
   - If molecular bands (TiO, VO, CN) are visible → M.
3. **Refine the subtype (0–9)** by relative line strengths within the assigned class. (O5 is hotter/bluer than O9; M0 is hotter than M6.)
4. **Measure luminosity** via one of:
   - **Parallax and apparent magnitude** → compute absolute magnitude → map to luminosity class.
   - **Spectroscopic parallax** (use the spectral type as a proxy for the star's intrinsic brightness, then infer distance and thus absolute magnitude).
   - **Spectral line broadening** (narrow lines suggest low gravity/giant; sharp lines suggest high gravity/dwarf).
5. **Assign luminosity class (0–VII)** by matching absolute magnitude and spectral line character:
   - Very broad lines + very high luminosity → Ia or Ib.
   - Moderately broad lines + high luminosity → II or III.
   - Intermediate gravity signature → IV.
   - Sharp lines + faint absolute magnitude → V.
   - Very sharp, narrow lines + subdwarf characteristics → VI.
   - Extreme pressure broadening + no obvious fusion → VII.
6. **Write the classification** as spectral type + luminosity class (e.g., G2V, M2Iab, A1V) and cross-check on the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.

## Implications per Category

| Spectral Type | Temperature | Lifetime | Next Interpretive Step |
|---|---|---|---|
| **O** | >30,000 K | **millions of years** | Search for binary companions, massive wind signatures, X-ray emission; expect short remaining lifetime. |
| **B** | 10,000–30,000 K | **millions to tens of millions** | Assess rotation (often very fast, producing line broadening); model circumstellar disks if present. |
| **A** | 7,500–10,000 K | **hundreds of millions** | Check for Vega-like debris disks; these are young enough to retain warm dust. |
| **F** | 6,000–7,500 K | **1–3 billion years** | Benchmark against solar-analog samples; assess metallicity for age and chemical-evolution clues. |
| **G** | 5,200–6,000 K | **10+ billion years** | Search for habitable-zone planets; assess age via lithium depletion and rotation. |
| **K** | 3,700–5,200 K | **tens of billions of years** | Prime target for exoplanet searches; assess activity and magnetic cycle. |
| **M** | <3,700 K | **hundreds of billions of years** | Flare activity, rotation period, and metallicity dominate; most abundant stellar population. |

| Luminosity Class | Evolutionary State | Physical Scale | Implications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ia–II** | Young, massive, expanding | Radius: 100–1000 R☉ | Short-lived; expect strong winds; high chance of supernova or hypernova. |
| **III** | Post-core-hydrogen-burning | Radius: 10–100 R☉ | Ascending or descending the red-giant branch; assess metallicity and age. |
| **IV** | Transitional | Radius: 1–10 R☉ | Leaving main sequence; useful age indicator. |
| **V** | Core-hydrogen burning | Radius: 0.1–2 R☉ | Age is the key unknown; use isochrones and activity/rotation to constrain. |
| **VI** | Old, metal-poor, main-sequence | Radius: 0.1–0.5 R☉ | Population II or extreme halo star; expect high space velocity and low metallicity. |
| **VII** | Post-asymptotic-giant-branch | Radius: ≈ 0.01 R☉ | Cooling white dwarf; lifetime is age of Galaxy; use cooling age to date the stellar population. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **A-type mistaken for F-type.** The boundary is hydrogen Balmer line strength at maximum (A) versus weakening (F). The tell: measure Ca II H and K equivalent width. If they are comparable to or stronger than Balmer, it is F. Consequence: incorrect temperature estimate by ~1000 K and wrong evolutionary inference.

- **K-type mistaken for M-type.** Both have strong metals and weaken hydrogen, but M shows clear TiO or VO molecular bands. The tell: look for molecular absorption around 4200–4300 Å (TiO). If absent, it is K; if present, it is M. Consequence: temperature overestimate by ~1000–2000 K; affects age and mass inference.

- **Giant (III) mistaken for Subgiant (IV).** Both show intermediate gravity, but giants have higher luminosity and broader spectral lines. The tell: measure absolute magnitude. If Mv < +1, it is a giant; if Mv > +2, it is a subgiant. Alternatively, measure line width and ionization balance — giants show more dramatic line broadening. Consequence: age estimate off by 1–2 Gyr; population classification error.

- **Main-sequence (V) mistaken for Subdwarf (VI).** Both are compact, but subdwarfs are metal-poor ([Fe/H] < –0.5) and show anomalous ionization (ionized lines too strong for the temperature). The tell: measure metallicity via iron-line strength or spectral subtraction. Also, subdwarfs often show high proper motion. Consequence: age and chemical composition incorrectly inferred.

- **Supergiant (Ib) mistaken for Giant (III) in unresolved photometry.** If distance and apparent magnitude are misjudged, a supergiant's absolute magnitude can be underestimated, placing it in the giant domain. The tell: obtain a high-resolution spectrum and measure pressure-broadening and wind signatures (P Cygni profiles in Balmer lines). Also, check for extended emission nebulae around the star. Consequence: mass and lifetime catastrophically underestimated.

- **White Dwarf (VII) overlooked in a binary.** A white dwarf is so faint that in a close binary it is outshone by the companion and not detected in optical surveys. The tell: measure proper motion and radial velocity of the visible star; if it shows orbital motion, search infrared and ultraviolet for a faint companion. Consequence: binary composition and evolutionary history missed.

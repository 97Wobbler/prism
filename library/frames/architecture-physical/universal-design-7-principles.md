# Universal Design 7 Principles

## Overview

Universal Design (UD) is a frame that sorts design decisions and artifacts by their adherence to seven foundational principles of accessibility and usability. Its core claim is that designing for the full range of human ability — including people with disabilities, elderly users, children, and non-native speakers — produces better artifacts for *everyone*, not a separate "accessible version." Each principle identifies a distinct dimension of usability; a design can excel in some dimensions while failing in others. UD sorts a design by which principles it honors and which it neglects, so that remediation can be targeted rather than afterthought.

## Categories

1. **Equitable Use**
   - The design is usable and appealing to people with diverse abilities; it does not stigmatize or disadvantage any user.
   - Discriminating criterion: all users can accomplish the core task using the same method or, if not, equivalent methods that preserve dignity and do not mark anyone as "special."
   - Example: a museum exhibit with ramps alongside stairs (not hidden in back); a website navigable by keyboard alone or by mouse (not forced into one mode).

2. **Flexible in Use**
   - The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities; users can choose the method or pace that works for them.
   - Discriminating criterion: the artifact allows multiple modes of use — left-handed and right-handed, sitting and standing, fast and slow, novice and expert — without re-engineering.
   - Example: a desk height adjustable from sitting to standing; a door with both push bar and manual handle; text with adjustable font size.

3. **Simple and Intuitive Use**
   - The design is easy to understand and navigate, eliminating unnecessary complexity; the logic is obvious to users regardless of experience, language, or cognitive ability.
   - Discriminating criterion: a first-time user with no instructions can attempt the task successfully; instructions, if needed, are minimal and presented in plain language.
   - Example: consistent button placement in an interface; clear wayfinding with symbols in a building; a phone with labeled buttons.

4. **Perceptible Information**
   - The design communicates necessary information clearly to users with different sensory abilities; redundant cues are used so no single sense is required.
   - Discriminating criterion: information is available in at least two modalities — visual + auditory, text + icon, or spoken + captioned — so users with hearing loss, vision loss, or language barriers can access it.
   - Example: traffic signals with sound for blind users; form errors shown both in red text and with icons; videos with captions and transcripts.

5. **Tolerance for Error**
   - The design minimizes hazards and adverse consequences of accidental or unintended actions; critical operations are reversible or require confirmation.
   - Discriminating criterion: users with tremors, poor vision, or cognitive differences can use the artifact safely; mistakes do not result in data loss or danger.
   - Example: a delete button that asks "are you sure?"; a thermostat with a visible current setting before adjustment; a medication dispenser that only releases one dose per interval.

6. **Low Physical Effort**
   - The design can be used efficiently and comfortably with minimal fatigue or repetitive strain; force, grip, or dexterity demands are low.
   - Discriminating criterion: users with limited strength, arthritis, or tremors can operate it; sustained use does not cause pain or fatigue in excess of the task itself.
   - Example: a door with an automatic opener; a keyboard that does not require hard pressure; controls within arm's reach when seated.

7. **Size and Space for Approach and Use**
   - The design provides adequate space and reach zones for users of all body sizes and mobilities, including wheelchair users, people of short stature, and those with limited dexterity.
   - Discriminating criterion: users of diverse heights, those using mobility aids, and those with limited reach can approach, use, and exit the artifact without assistance or contortion.
   - Example: a counter at multiple heights; doorways wide enough for a wheelchair; thermostat controls at mid-chest height rather than overhead.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Identify the artifact or space** — a physical object (appliance, tool, clothing), a built space (room, building, playground), or a digital interface.
2. **List the primary users and their abilities** — consider age range, disabilities, language backgrounds, and physical variation. Be specific; "people" is too broad.
3. **For each of the seven principles, ask in order:**
   - **Equitable Use**: Can all primary users accomplish the core task, or only some? Are any users marked as "special"?
   - **Flexible in Use**: How many distinct methods exist for the same task? Can the user choose, or is one method mandatory?
   - **Simple and Intuitive**: Can a first-time user figure out how to use it without instructions? Is the logic obvious?
   - **Perceptible Information**: Is critical information available in more than one sense? Can a blind user, deaf user, or non-native speaker access it?
   - **Tolerance for Error**: What happens if the user makes a mistake? Can they undo it? Is there confirmation on critical actions?
   - **Low Physical Effort**: How much strength, grip, or fine motor control is required? Can a user with arthritis, tremor, or limited strength use it?
   - **Size and Space**: Can users of all heights and mobilities approach and use it? Is there adequate clearance?
4. **Rate each principle as Met, Partially Met, or Not Met** based on evidence from the design.
5. **Identify the two or three principles in worst shape** — these are your remediation priorities.

## Implications per Category

| Principle | Met | Partially Met | Not Met |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Equitable Use** | All users can use the same method equally. | Some users must use an adapted or secondary method. | Some users cannot accomplish the task at all. |
| **Flexible in Use** | Multiple methods exist; user chooses. | Multiple methods exist but one is clearly preferred and others are laborious. | Only one method is available; users must adapt themselves. |
| **Simple and Intuitive** | First-time user succeeds without instruction. | First-time user needs minimal guidance or inference. | First-time user is confused; instructions are long or non-obvious. |
| **Perceptible Information** | Information is redundantly encoded (visual + auditory, text + icon). | Information is available in one strong modality; others are weak. | Information is only available in one sense (e.g., color alone, sound alone). |
| **Tolerance for Error** | Critical actions are reversible or gated with confirmation. | Errors are possible but have minor consequences. | Errors are possible and have severe consequences (data loss, danger, frustration). |
| **Low Physical Effort** | Use is easy for users with low strength or dexterity; no fatigue on sustained use. | Use is possible but requires moderate strength or is tiring on sustained use. | Use requires significant strength, complex dexterity, or causes pain on sustained use. |
| **Size and Space** | All body sizes and mobilities can approach, use, and exit independently. | Most users can, but some (very tall, very short, wheelchair users) must adapt or request help. | One or more user populations cannot use without significant accommodation or assistance. |

**Remediation strategy**: Start with principles rated "Not Met" — these are exclusionary. Then address "Partially Met" to broaden the user base. "Met" principles should not regress.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Treating accessibility as a single dimension.** Universal Design is seven dimensions, not one. A space can have excellent Equitable Use (ramps) but poor Simple and Intuitive (confusing wayfinding). Fixing one does not fix the other.
- **Confusing Equitable Use with Flexible in Use.** Equitable Use means all users can do the task the same way. Flexible in Use means there are multiple ways to choose from. A design can be equitable (everyone uses the same stairs) but inflexible (stairs only).
- **Assuming "special accommodations" are UD compliance.** A wheelchair ramp hidden behind a building while the main entrance is stairs is not Equitable Use; it marks users as different. UD uses the same entrance for all.
- **Over-relying on Perceptible Information and neglecting Physical Effort.** Adding captions (Perceptible) to a video player with buttons too small to press (Physical Effort) helps deaf users but not users with arthritis.
- **Classifying based on intent rather than outcome.** A designer who "intended" the artifact to be simple (Simple and Intuitive) but made it confusing anyway has failed the principle. Classification is by actual usability, not stated intent.
- **Treating UD as binary (compliant / not).** UD is a spectrum across seven dimensions. An artifact can be strong in some principles and weak in others; the frame helps identify which.

# BCG Growth-Share Matrix

## Overview

The BCG Growth-Share Matrix sorts a portfolio of business units or product
lines into one of four quadrants based on two dimensions: **relative market
share** (high or low within the competitive set) and **market growth rate**
(high or low for the industry segment). Its core claim is that cash
generation and reinvestment needs differ sharply across the quadrants, and
applying the wrong financial strategy to a quadrant (e.g., harvesting a
Star, or investing heavily in a Dog) leads to systematic misallocation of
capital. Each quadrant implies a **fundamentally different cash management
and investment posture** — the classification itself dictates which playbook
is appropriate.

## Categories

1. **Star** (high growth, high share)
   - The business is growing fast in a growing market and holds a strong
     competitive position.
   - Cash requirements are high (reinvestment needed to maintain share in
     a growing market), but cash generation is also strong.
   - Discriminating criterion: relative market share is higher than the
     largest competitor or equals the leader; absolute market size is
     growing at or above GDP + inflation.
   - Example: cloud infrastructure services in the early 2010s; smartphone
     apps during mobile adoption surge.

2. **Cash Cow** (low growth, high share)
   - The business dominates a mature or slow-growing market and generates
     strong cash returns with minimal reinvestment needs.
   - Cash extraction is the primary objective; these fund the rest of the
     portfolio.
   - Discriminating criterion: relative market share is high; market
     growth rate is at or below inflation, or the market is explicitly
     mature/saturating.
   - Example: carbonated beverages in developed markets; print
     advertising in legacy media.

3. **Question Mark** (high growth, low share)
   - The business operates in a growing market but holds a weak position
     relative to competitors.
   - Cash requirements are high (to build share and compete in a growth
     market), but cash generation is low; outcome is uncertain.
   - Discriminating criterion: relative market share is lower than rivals;
     market growth rate is high, making share defensible but not yet
     established.
   - Example: early electric vehicle startups; a social media platform
     entering a region where a competitor dominates.

4. **Dog** (low growth, low share)
   - The business holds a weak position in a mature or declining market.
   - Cash generation is modest and reinvestment returns are poor; the unit
     is a cash drain relative to alternatives.
   - Discriminating criterion: relative market share is low; market growth
     rate is flat or negative.
   - Example: fax machine business post-2000; niche legacy software in
     consolidated categories.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the business unit or product line to classify and define its
   relevant competitive set (the market it competes in).
2. Determine **relative market share**: calculate this business unit's
   market share divided by the largest competitor's market share in that
   same market. If this ratio is ≥1.0, mark share as **high**; if <1.0,
   mark as **low**.
3. Determine **market growth rate**: calculate the year-over-year growth
   rate of the *market* (not the business unit). Compare it to a baseline
   (typically 2–3%, or your company's cost of capital, or GDP growth in the
   relevant geography). If growth is above baseline, mark as **high**; if
   at or below baseline, mark as **low**.
4. Cross-reference the two binary answers on a 2×2 grid:
   - High share + High growth → **Star**
   - High share + Low growth → **Cash Cow**
   - Low share + High growth → **Question Mark**
   - Low share + Low growth → **Dog**
5. Record the classification and the **implied financial strategy** (see
   Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Category | Cash strategy | Investment posture | Typical action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Star** | Neutral to balanced | Reinvest to defend/grow share | Maintain market position; build brand and scale; fund from corporate or external capital. |
| **Cash Cow** | Maximum extraction | Harvest/maintain | Minimize reinvestment; extract cash to fund Stars and Question Marks; defend against erosion. |
| **Question Mark** | Negative (cash burn) | Selective, high-conviction | Decide to invest (move to Star) or harvest (divest/exit); do not hold indefinitely. |
| **Dog** | Low extraction | Divest or milk | Exit, sell, wind down, or hold only if strategically linked to a Star. Avoid reinvestment. |

The matrix implies a portfolio balance: **Cash Cows fund Stars; Question
Marks are temporary and must be resolved (invest or exit); Dogs are
eliminated or justified by strategic linkage, not cash logic.**

## Common Misclassifications

- **Question Mark mistaken for Star.** A fast-growing market and a new
  entrant can feel like a winner, but if relative share is weak, cash burn
  is structural and the outcome is genuinely uncertain. The tell is whether
  the unit is gaining or losing share *relative to the leader* in the same
  market, not just growing in absolute terms.

- **Dog mistaken for Question Mark.** Confusing low share with a growth
  opportunity. If the *market* is not growing, low share in a Dog cannot
  become a Star by investment alone. The tell is asking: "Is the market
  itself expanding?" If no, it's a Dog.

- **Cash Cow mistaken for a Star.** A business with high absolute profit
  or cash generation can feel like a high-performer, but if the market is
  mature and not growing, reinvestment returns are poor. The tell is
  comparing the business's growth rate to the market's growth rate, not to
  the company's overall profitability.

- **Misdefining the competitive set.** A business can be a Star in a
  narrow segment (high share of a niche growing market) but a Dog in the
  broader market. The tell is explicitly naming the market and confirming
  that both share and growth are measured in the same market definition.

- **Using absolute size instead of relative share.** A large revenue
  number does not mean high share; share must be relative to competitors.
  A $100M business in a $200M market is high share; a $100M business in a
  $1B market is low share.

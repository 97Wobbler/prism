# Ansoff Matrix

## Overview

The Ansoff Matrix sorts growth initiatives into one of four archetypes based on two binary questions: **Is the market new or existing?** and **Is the product new or existing?** Its core claim is that different quadrants demand structurally different capabilities, levels of risk tolerance, and execution playbooks — and confusing a Market Penetration play with a Diversification play is a systematic source of resource misallocation and strategic failure. The act of placing an initiative into its quadrant reveals which organizational competencies are load-bearing and which new capabilities must be built or acquired.

## Categories

1. **Market Penetration**
   - Existing product sold into existing market.
   - Growth comes from increasing share of current customers, stealing customers from competitors, or increasing usage frequency/depth within your current customer base.
   - Discriminating criterion: the customer, the product, and the market are all known quantities; only the transaction volume is growing.
   - Example: McDonald's opening more locations in a city where it already operates; a SaaS vendor adding new seats to existing customers.

2. **Product Development**
   - New product sold into existing market.
   - Growth comes from innovation that serves your current customers' evolving needs or expands their use cases with you.
   - Discriminating criterion: you understand the market and customer behavior, but must invent or acquire a new offering to serve them.
   - Example: adding a premium tier to an existing product; a bank offering wealth management to its retail customer base.

3. **Market Development**
   - Existing product sold into new market.
   - Growth comes from geographic expansion, new customer segments, or new use cases for an existing product.
   - Discriminating criterion: the product is proven and battle-tested, but the buyer, the distribution channel, or the geography is unfamiliar.
   - Example: a U.S. software company expanding to Asia; a B2B SaaS vendor selling the same platform to a new vertical (e.g., healthcare instead of finance).

4. **Diversification**
   - New product sold into new market.
   - Growth comes from entering a space where the company has neither a proven product nor an established customer relationship.
   - Discriminating criterion: both the market and the product are unproven; this is the highest-risk, highest-learning quadrant.
   - Example: Apple entering wearables (watches, AirPods); a financial services firm acquiring or building into e-commerce.

## Classification Procedure

1. Name the growth initiative or business line in one sentence: what offering are you taking to which customer segment or market?
2. Ask **"Do we have a proven, battle-tested product in this space today?"**
   - If **yes**, go to step 3.
   - If **no**, the product axis is **New**.
3. Ask **"Are the target customers or the target market geography/segment ones we currently serve?"**
   - If **yes**, the market axis is **Existing**.
   - If **no**, the market axis is **New**.
4. Map the two axes onto the matrix:
   - Existing product + Existing market → **Market Penetration**.
   - New product + Existing market → **Product Development**.
   - Existing product + New market → **Market Development**.
   - New product + New market → **Diversification**.
5. State the classification and name the **risk posture and capability requirement** implied by the quadrant (see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Quadrant | Risk level | Core capability required | Playbook focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Market Penetration** | Low | Sales, distribution, market share tactics | Capture customers, reduce churn, increase wallet share. Focus on margin and market efficiency. |
| **Product Development** | Medium | R&D, product management, customer intimacy | Innovate to serve existing segments better. Customer research is cheap; market entry is proven. |
| **Market Development** | Medium–High | Distribution, localization, new sales motion | Adapt the product to new geographies or segments; build new go-to-market. Unproven customer behavior. |
| **Diversification** | High | M&A, venture, new org capability building | Highest learning curve; consider acquisition or partnership to acquire one axis faster. Requires portfolio thinking. |

For portfolio construction, typical guidance: **Penetration funds operations; Product Development funds near-term growth; Market Development funds medium-term expansion; Diversification funds optionality and long-term hedging.** Most mature companies run a blend across all four, weighted by risk appetite.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Market Penetration mistaken for Product Development.** Adding a feature the market didn't ask for but you think will increase adoption, then classifying it as "serving existing customers better." The tell is that the product capability already existed in the market (via competitors or adjacent tools); you are just surfacing it. This inflates the risk of the initiative.

- **Market Development mistaken for Penetration.** Entering a new geography with the same product and sales motion, assuming "it worked in Market A, it will work in Market B." The tell is discovering that buyer behavior, regulatory requirements, or distribution channels differ fundamentally. True Market Development requires localization.

- **Diversification mistaken for Product Development or Market Development.** Underestimating the compounding risk when both the product *and* the market are new. The tell is a severely underestimated timeline and budget, because you are implicitly assuming either the product will work as-is or the market will adopt it via your existing motion.

- **Classifying based on novelty alone rather than market/product axes.** "New initiative" is not enough; you must specify whether the newness is on the product side, market side, or both. An initiative with a new feature sold to existing customers is **not** Diversification.

- **Using "new to the company" instead of "proven/unproven."** A product that is new to your company but proven and mature in the market is not the same risk as one that is genuinely new. The tell is historical adoption curves and competitive maturity; if the category already has mature players, the product axis is less risky than a greenfield category.

# Three Horizons Framework

## Overview

Three Horizons sorts an initiative, project, or business unit by its **time horizon to value delivery** and its **relationship to the current core business**. Its core claim is that different horizons require **different success metrics, funding models, team structures, and tolerance for failure** — and applying the wrong playbook to a horizon (e.g., holding a moonshot to quarterly ROI targets, or treating a core-efficiency project as if it were a venture) is a systematic drag on both. The frame forces explicit portfolio-level thinking: most organizations underweight H2 and H3 while over-optimizing H1, starving future growth.

## Categories

1. **Horizon 1 (H1): Core Business / Harvest**
   - Delivers value **within 12–24 months** from current operations and mature markets.
   - The business model and customer base are **known and stable**; the focus is optimization, margin, and operational excellence.
   - Discriminating criterion: an initiative in H1 is measured on quarterly or annual ROI, cash flow, and market share in existing segments. Failure to execute is a serious financial risk.
   - Example: process automation in manufacturing, cost reduction in supply chain, deepening market penetration in a known geography.

2. **Horizon 2 (H2): Emerging / Transition**
   - Delivers value in **3–5 years** from new or adjacent markets, or from new business models applied to existing markets.
   - The market or capability is **partially proven**; there is evidence of demand but material uncertainty remains.
   - Discriminating criterion: an initiative in H2 is measured on progress toward product-market fit, customer acquisition cost, and leading indicators of scale. Some failures are acceptable; the goal is learning velocity and option value.
   - Example: a new product line in a related category, entry into a neighboring geographic market, a pilot of a subscription model in a business that was traditionally transactional.

3. **Horizon 3 (H3): Options / Moonshot**
   - Delivers value in **5+ years** (or not at all) from markets or capabilities that may not yet exist.
   - The opportunity is **largely unproven**; success requires fundamental discovery or disruption.
   - Discriminating criterion: an initiative in H3 is measured on learning, capability building, and portfolio optionality, not on near-term revenue. High failure rates are expected and accepted.
   - Example: a venture-scale bet on an emerging technology (AI, quantum, biotech), entry into a category that doesn't yet exist for the company, a foundational R&D program.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the initiative, project, or business unit and its **expected time to material financial contribution** (revenue, cost savings, or cash impact that moves the overall company needle).

2. Ask **"Is the market or customer segment where value will be delivered **known and mature** (we already operate there or have high confidence we can)?"**
   - If **yes** → go to step 3.
   - If **no, but evidence of demand exists** → Horizon 2.
   - If **no, and the market/capability is largely unproven** → Horizon 3.

3. (For H1 candidates only) Ask **"Is the time to material financial impact **within 12–24 months**?"**
   - If **yes** → Horizon 1.
   - If **no** → re-examine step 2; the initiative may belong to H2.

4. (For H2 candidates) Confirm the **3–5 year window** and that there is at least one **leading indicator of progress** (traction with early customers, proof of concept, achieved unit economics in a test market).

5. (For H3 candidates) Confirm that **failure is acceptable** in the portfolio sense — i.e., no single initiative is existential, and the goal is building options and capabilities, not short-term revenue.

6. Assign the initiative to the horizon and note the **success metric and governance model** implied by the category (see Implications below).

## Implications per Category

| Horizon | Time to Value | Success Metric | Funding Model | Team Structure | Risk Tolerance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **H1** | 12–24 mo | ROI, cash flow, market share | Annual budget, P&L ownership | Functional, experienced, managed by KPI | Low; failures are costly |
| **H2** | 3–5 yr | Progress toward PMF, CAC, cohort economics | Milestone-based, ring-fenced budget, option thinking | Cross-functional, mixed seniority, learning-velocity focus | Medium; some failures expected |
| **H3** | 5+ yr | Learning, capability, optionality | Portfolio allocation, venture-style, high autonomy | Small, entrepreneurial, experimental | High; most initiatives will not scale |

**Portfolio implication:** A healthy company typically allocates roughly **70–80% to H1** (cash generation and efficiency), **15–20% to H2** (adjacent growth), and **5–10% to H3** (breakthrough options). Severe imbalance in either direction is a signal of strategic risk.

## Common Misclassifications

- **H1 mistaken for H2.** Claiming an initiative is "adjacent" or "strategic" when it is actually an incremental extension of a core business with 12–24 month payoff. The tell is that the success metric is ROI or market share, not learning. Consequence: H2-style autonomy and patience are given to something that should be held to H1 discipline.

- **H2 mistaken for H1.** Pushing a genuinely new market entry or business model into the H1 portfolio because pressure to show short-term results is high. The tell is that the customer base, distribution, or unit economics are not yet proven. Consequence: premature scale investment, burnout from unrealistic targets, and underestimation of true time-to-value.

- **H3 mistaken for H2.** Treating a foundational R&D effort or moonshot as if it should deliver material revenue in 3–5 years, when the market or capability is still fundamentally unproven. The tell is conflating "we've spent 2 years on this" with "we have proof of concept." Consequence: continued investment in a fundamentally mis-scoped bet, missed signals to kill it early.

- **H2 mistaken for H3.** Abandoning or under-resourcing an emerging market entry because "it's too risky," when early customer traction and a 3–5 year horizon suggest H2 classification and appropriate risk tolerance. The tell is overweighting downside scenarios and conflating H2 uncertainty with H3 optionality. Consequence: loss of strategic option that was winnable.

- **Classifying by budget size instead of time horizon.** A large, expensive H1 project is still H1; a small, cheap H3 bet is still H3. Budget and horizon are independent dimensions. The tell is arguing "it's H3 because we can only spend $500k on it."

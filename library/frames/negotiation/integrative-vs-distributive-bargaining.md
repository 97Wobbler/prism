# Integrative vs Distributive Bargaining

## Overview

This frame sorts a negotiation into one of two structural postures based on
whether the parties are expanding the total pie (integrative) or dividing a
fixed pie (distributive). The core claim is that the two postures demand
**fundamentally different strategies, information flows, and relationship
trajectories** — and confusing them (e.g., offering integrative moves to a
party committed to distributive tactics) is a systematic source of
exploitation and failed deals. The frame has no third category: every
negotiation *is* one or the other, though the parties may have conflicting
perceptions about which one they are in.

## Categories

1. **Integrative Bargaining** (also: collaborative, interest-based, win-win)
   - The negotiation **expands the total value available** by uncovering
     trades that benefit both sides asymmetrically — you gain on X, I gain
     on Y, both gain overall.
   - Parties share information about underlying interests, constraints,
     and creative options.
   - Discriminating criterion: the agreement produces an outcome that
     neither side could have reached alone, and both sides move away from
     their opening positions *in the same direction*.
   - Example: a contract negotiation where Party A values speed-to-market
     and Party B values long-term volume; swapping a faster delivery
     schedule for lower unit pricing creates value for both that a
     50-50 split of a fixed pool never could.

2. **Distributive Bargaining** (also: positional, competitive, zero-sum)
   - The negotiation **divides a fixed or shrinking pie** — my gain is
     your loss, and both sides are optimizing their slice.
   - Parties conceal information about their true bottom line and leverage
     asymmetries to claim as much value as possible.
   - Discriminating criterion: the agreement moves toward a midpoint or
     power imbalance between opening positions; no new value appears on
     the table, only redistribution of what was already there.
   - Example: haggling over the price of a used car; the total value of
     the transaction is fixed at the car's actual condition and market
     rate, and negotiation moves the split between buyer and seller.

## Classification Procedure

1. Describe the negotiation: what is being negotiated, who are the parties,
   and what have they opened with (their stated demands)?
2. Ask: **"Are there underlying interests or constraints that differ
   between the parties?"** Examples: different time horizons, different
   cost structures, different risk tolerances, different things they value.
   - If **no** — the parties want the same thing and only disagree on how
     much each gets → Distributive.
   - If **yes**, go to step 3.
3. Ask: **"Have the parties shared information about their underlying
   interests, or are they concealing it?"**
   - If **concealing** → Distributive (even if interests differ, the
     parties are not exploiting the difference).
   - If **sharing**, go to step 4.
4. Ask: **"Is the proposed agreement creating trades where one side gains
   on an issue it cares about and loses on an issue it cares less about,
   and vice versa?"**
   - If **yes** → Integrative (value is being created via asymmetric
     trades).
   - If **no** → Distributive (the agreement is a compromise or split).
5. State the classification and note whether both parties perceive it the
   same way. Mismatches (one party playing integrative, the other
   distributive) are the highest-risk negotiation scenarios.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Information flow | Appropriate tactics | Relationship trajectory |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Integrative** | Transparent about interests and constraints; questions about "why" this matters to you | Asking, reframing, option generation, contingent agreements, expanding scope | Trust-building; relationship can support future negotiations |
| **Distributive** | Opaque about true bottom line; strategic signaling of strength and alternatives | Anchoring, concession pacing, deadline pressure, walk-away threats, selective disclosure | Transactional; relationship is secondary to claiming value this round |

For a negotiator, the practical implications are profound:
- **In Integrative mode:** share information first (to unlock reciprocal
  sharing), ask about underlying interests, propose trades that give each
  side what it values most, and expect that repeated positive-sum deals
  build trust and future opportunity.
- **In Distributive mode:** reveal as little as possible, assume the other
  side is concealing, anchor high (if claiming value) or low (if defending
  against a claim), and expect that advantage goes to the side with better
  alternatives and patience.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Integrative misidentified as Distributive.** Parties have genuinely
  divergent interests (different time horizons, cost structures) and are
  willing to share information, but neither side recognizes the integrative
  opportunity. The tell is that the initial "split the difference" outcome
  leaves both sides worse off than a creative trade would. Consequence: a
  deal happens but value is left on the table, and the relationship gets
  no trust dividend.

- **Distributive misidentified as Integrative.** One or both parties claim
  to be exploring creative options but are actually using questions about
  "interests" as a way to extract information and anchor harder. The tell
  is that the party asking "why does this matter to you?" immediately uses
  the answer against you, or that "options" proposed are one-sided. Consequence: premature transparency, exploitation, and relationship damage.

- **Confusing integrative negotiation with "both sides win equally."**
  Integrative deals almost never split value 50-50; asymmetric gains are
  the point. The confusion causes negotiators to reject integrative
  proposals that are genuinely better for them, because they look unequal.
  Consequence: rejection of Pareto improvements.

- **Failing to notice a shift mid-negotiation.** A negotiation can start
  integrative (information sharing, option generation) and flip to
  distributive (clock running out, one side plays hardball) or vice versa.
  The tell is an abrupt change in candor or tactics. Consequence: being
  caught off-guard; having shared information that is now used against you.

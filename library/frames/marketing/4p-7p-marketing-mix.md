# 4P / 7P Marketing Mix

## Overview

The Marketing Mix frame sorts a go-to-market initiative, product feature, or customer-facing decision into one of seven dimensions based on which **type of customer value** it creates and which **operational systems** it must align with. The core claim is that different dimensions have fundamentally different constraints — a pricing decision cannot be solved the same way as a distribution decision, and conflating them leads to weak execution. The 4P model (Product, Price, Place, Promotion) covers goods; the 7P extension (adding People, Process, Physical Evidence) is necessary for services, where the delivery system and human interaction are inseparable from the offering itself.

## Categories

1. **Product**
   - The tangible or intangible offering itself — what the customer receives and uses.
   - Discriminating criterion: decisions about the core functionality, features, quality, branding, packaging, or range of variants.
   - Example: adding a new coffee blend to a café menu; designing the user interface of a SaaS tool; the warranty included with a car.
   - Operational anchor: R&D, product management, design, supply chain.

2. **Price**
   - The monetary exchange and the mechanisms by which value is captured.
   - Discriminating criterion: decisions about list price, discounting, bundling, payment terms, or pricing model (subscription vs. one-time, tiered, value-based).
   - Example: charging per user vs. per seat; offering a premium tier; running a limited-time sale.
   - Operational anchor: finance, business strategy, revenue operations.

3. **Place** (Distribution)
   - The channels, locations, and logistics through which the customer accesses the offering.
   - Discriminating criterion: decisions about retail vs. online, wholesale vs. direct, geographic availability, inventory stocking, or fulfillment speed.
   - Example: opening a flagship store in a new city; shifting from direct sales to a partner channel; launching same-day delivery.
   - Operational anchor: supply chain, logistics, channel partnerships, retail operations.

4. **Promotion** (Communication)
   - The messaging, awareness, and persuasion activities that inform and convince the customer to buy.
   - Discriminating criterion: decisions about advertising channels, creative strategy, sales tactics, content marketing, or influencer partnerships.
   - Example: running a TV campaign vs. a TikTok campaign; hiring a sales team vs. self-serve signup; a PR blitz before launch.
   - Operational anchor: marketing communications, sales, brand management, media buying.

5. **People** (Services only; often called "Participants")
   - The human actors — employees, agents, or intermediaries — whose behavior and judgment directly shape the customer experience.
   - Discriminating criterion: decisions about hiring, training, empowerment, incentive alignment, or the role of human interaction in the service delivery.
   - Example: deciding whether a concierge should be available 24/7; training a support team to handle edge cases; empowering frontline staff to issue refunds without approval.
   - Operational anchor: HR, training, customer service, organizational design.

6. **Process** (Services only)
   - The step-by-step system or workflow by which the service is delivered; the choreography of interactions.
   - Discriminating criterion: decisions about how long delivery takes, how many steps the customer navigates, how much customization is possible, or how exceptions are handled.
   - Example: redesigning a bank's account-opening process from 45 minutes to 15 minutes; allowing customers to book an appointment online vs. by phone; automating a manual approval step.
   - Operational anchor: operations, process engineering, systems design, customer journey mapping.

7. **Physical Evidence** (Services only)
   - The tangible environmental and digital cues that signal quality and professionalism — the "stage" on which the service is delivered.
   - Discriminating criterion: decisions about the look and feel of a physical space (décor, cleanliness, layout), digital interfaces, documentation, or branded materials.
   - Example: renovating a salon's waiting room; redesigning an app's dashboard; choosing the material quality of a branded gift.
   - Operational anchor: facilities, design, brand experience, UX.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Identify the core customer interaction or value creation in the initiative.** State it in one sentence: what does the customer directly perceive or do?

2. **Ask: Is this about the offering itself (what the customer gets) or how it is delivered/sold?**
   - If **offering itself** (tangible product, feature, capability) → **Product**.
   - If **how it is sold or conveyed** → go to step 3.

3. **Ask: Is this primarily a financial/commercial decision, or an operational/delivery decision?**
   - If **financial** (price, revenue model, margin) → **Price**.
   - If **operational** → go to step 4.

4. **Ask: Is this about getting the offering to the customer (channels, logistics, availability), or about persuading them to buy it?**
   - If **channels / availability / logistics** → **Place**.
   - If **persuasion / awareness / messaging** → **Promotion**.

5. **For services only: Does the decision primarily affect the human interaction, the workflow, or the environment?**
   - If **human actors and their judgment** (hiring, training, empowerment) → **People**.
   - If **the sequence and rules of the service delivery** (speed, steps, customization) → **Process**.
   - If **sensory and tangible cues** (look, feel, materials, digital interface aesthetics) → **Physical Evidence**.

6. **Record the classification and the operational system responsible.** This determines which team leads the decision.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Operational Owner | Primary Constraint | What to prioritize |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Product** | R&D / Product Mgmt | Customer need, feasibility, differentiation | Does it solve a real problem? Is it defensible? |
| **Price** | Finance / Business | Willingness to pay, competitive position, unit economics | What can customers afford? What drives volume vs. margin? |
| **Place** | Supply Chain / Ops | Logistics, partner availability, customer convenience | Can we reach the customer? At what cost per unit distributed? |
| **Promotion** | Marketing / Sales | Audience reach, message resonance, conversion efficiency | Who needs to hear this? What will move them? |
| **People** | HR / Service Delivery | Staff capability, motivation, consistency | Do they have the skill and incentive to deliver? |
| **Process** | Operations / Eng | Speed, reliability, scalability, exception handling | Can we deliver consistently and quickly? |
| **Physical Evidence** | Design / Brand | Brand perception, trust signaling, usability | Does the environment communicate quality and professionalism? |

A complete go-to-market program balances all four (or all seven for services). Gaps in any dimension undermine the others: a great product (P1) at a wrong price (P2) in unavailable channels (P3) with no awareness (P4) will fail.

## Common Misclassifications

- **Product confused with Promotion.** A new tagline or marketing angle is not a product change. The tell is whether the core offering itself has changed — if only the messaging changed, it is Promotion.
- **Place confused with Promotion.** Opening a new sales channel (e.g., direct online) is Place; advertising that channel is Promotion. The tell is whether the customer's access method changed.
- **Price confused with Promotion.** A discount *offer* is Promotion if it is temporary and tactical; a change to the underlying pricing model is Price. The tell is permanence and strategic intent.
- **People confused with Process.** A decision to hire more staff is People; a decision to redesign the service workflow so fewer staff are needed is Process. The tell is whether the issue is staffing/training vs. workflow redesign.
- **Physical Evidence confused with Product.** A new website design is Physical Evidence (the stage); adding a new feature to the product is Product. The tell is whether the offering's capability changed or only its presentation.
- **Treating all 7P as equally urgent.** In early stages, Product and Price are typically load-bearing; Places and Promotion amplify once the core is proven. For services, People and Process are often the hardest to scale. Sequence matters.

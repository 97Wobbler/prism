# Eisenstein's 5 Types of Montage

## Overview

Eisenstein's framework sorts montage sequences by the **principle that links
one shot to the next** — and claims that different linking principles
produce fundamentally different cognitive and emotional effects on the
viewer. The core argument is that montage is not merely temporal
juxtaposition; the *relationship* between shots generates meaning. A
sequence edited by metric logic creates tension through rhythm; one edited
by intellectual collision creates meaning through symbolic or conceptual
clash. Choosing the wrong montage type for your intent wastes the
compositional work and muddles the effect.

## Categories

1. **Metric Montage** (purely rhythmic editing)
   - Shots are joined by **equal or proportional duration**, regardless of
     their content.
   - The viewer experiences tension through the *regularity or acceleration
     of cutting itself*, not through shot content.
   - Discriminating criterion: the cuts are felt as a pulse or drumbeat;
     removing a shot breaks the rhythm; the same effect works with any
     image sequence of the same lengths.
   - Example: Eisenstein's Battleship Potemkin, the Odessa Steps montage —
     quick cuts of soldiers, steps, victims, wheels in strict rhythmic
     intervals create mounting dread.

2. **Rhythmic Montage** (content-aware pacing)
   - Shots are joined by **duration that echoes internal movement within
     each shot** — a pan, a gesture, an object in motion.
   - The cutting is *synchronized to the motion content*, not imposed from
     outside.
   - Discriminating criterion: if you lengthen or shorten a shot, the
     rhythm breaks because the cut no longer aligns with the action inside
     it.
   - Example: a dancer's leap cut to land in sync with the next shot's
     motion; a swinging door that matches the cut to the next falling
     object.

3. **Tonal Montage** (emotional or atmospheric continuity)
   - Shots are joined by a **shared emotional tone, color palette, or
     sensory mood** — not by literal action or strict rhythm.
   - The viewer experiences continuity of *feeling* rather than logic or
     motion.
   - Discriminating criterion: the shots may depict unrelated actions or
     locations, but they resonate together through atmosphere, lighting,
     or implied emotional state.
   - Example: a sequence of images of urban decay, loneliness, and
     industrial harshness — each shot distinct, but all drenched in grey
     light and silence, building a single emotional register.

4. **Overtonal Montage** (synthesis of tone and rhythm)
   - Shots are joined by **both emotional resonance and rhythmic/motion
     alignment** — the combination of multiple linking principles working
     together.
   - The editing is richer and more controlled than pure tonal work; it
     demands that tone and rhythm reinforce each other.
   - Discriminating criterion: removing either the emotional coherence or
     the rhythmic coherence would weaken the sequence; both are essential.
   - Example: a montage of a character's grief, where each shot cuts to
     music, the light and color reinforce desolation, and the timing of
     each cut matches an internal gesture — all four elements in concert.

5. **Intellectual Montage** (conceptual collision)
   - Shots are joined by a **symbolic or logical relationship** — not by
     motion, rhythm, or tone, but by *what they mean when placed
     together*.
   - The collision of two images creates an idea that neither image holds
     alone.
   - Discriminating criterion: the shot order is crucial; reversing two
     shots changes the meaning fundamentally; the effect is mental, not
     sensory.
   - Example: Eisenstein's October, the Kerensky/Mechanical Peacock
     montage — a shot of a general strutting, cut to a mechanical peacock
     opening its tail — the juxtaposition alone *creates* the statement
     that the general is vain and artificial.

## Classification Procedure

1. Write a one-sentence description of what the montage sequence is *doing*
   — what effect or meaning it aims to create.
2. Ask **"Is the primary linking principle the *duration* of shots
   themselves, regardless of content?"**
   - If **yes** → Metric. Stop.
   - If **no**, go to step 3.
3. Ask **"Is the primary linking principle the *motion or gesture inside
   each shot*?"**
   - If **yes** → Rhythmic. Stop.
   - If **no**, go to step 4.
4. Ask **"Is the primary linking principle a *symbolic or logical idea that
   only emerges from the collision itself*?"**
   - If **yes** → Intellectual. Stop.
   - If **no**, go to step 5.
5. Ask **"Is the primary linking principle an *emotional tone, mood, or
   sensory atmosphere*?"**
   - If **yes**, ask whether the sequence *also* has strong rhythmic
     alignment with motion and music.
     - If **yes** → Overtonal. Stop.
     - If **no** → Tonal. Stop.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Effect on viewer | Editorial strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Metric** | Tension, momentum, primal excitement via rhythm alone | Cut to a strict beat or tempo; let the rhythm dominate; any images will work if the timing is right. |
| **Rhythmic** | Flow, organic momentum, integration of action and time | Sync cuts to internal motion; lengthen shots with slow motion, shorten those with quick action; edit *with* the footage, not against it. |
| **Tonal** | Immersion in mood or emotional state; atmosphere over narrative | Compose and light each shot to a single emotional key; prioritize color, sound design, and air over action; let the viewer *feel* continuity. |
| **Overtonal** | Rich, controlled emotional momentum; feeling + propulsion together | Layer tone and rhythm intentionally; use music, color, and motion timing in concert; this requires the most craft. |
| **Intellectual** | Cognitive surprise, ideological argument, meaning-making | Compose shots for their individual symbolic weight; treat shot order as language; each juxtaposition is a claim. |

Implicit strategy: **Metric and Rhythmic for action and propulsion; Tonal
for immersion and mood; Overtonal when you have both music and emotional
content to layer; Intellectual when you want the edit to *argue* something.**

## Common Misclassifications

- **Rhythmic mistaken for Metric.** A sequence with good motion-matching
  feels rhythmic, but if the cuts would work equally well at different
  timings, it is Metric. The tell: change the tempo; Metric survives it,
  Rhythmic breaks.
- **Tonal mistaken for Intellectual.** A moody, atmospheric sequence and an
  ideological montage can *look* similar, but the difference is intent and
  order. Tonal says "feel this mood"; Intellectual says "notice this idea
  by the collision." The tell: reverse the shot order; if it still works
  emotionally, it is Tonal; if the meaning inverts, it is Intellectual.
- **Overtonal mistaken for Tonal or Rhythmic alone.** When a sequence has
  both strong rhythm and strong tone, it is easy to call it one or the
  other. The tell is that removing *either* the rhythmic or the tonal
  element noticeably weakens the sequence.
- **Intellectual mistaken for Tonal.** A stark, austere sequence with
  symbolic images may feel Tonal, but Intellectual montage depends on *why*
  the two images collide, not how they feel. The tell: can you articulate
  the *idea* that emerges from the juxtaposition? If yes, it is
  Intellectual.
- **Classifying the whole when sections serve different purposes.** Most
  complex film sequences are hybrids; the useful move is to identify the
  *dominant* principle in each sub-section and name it.

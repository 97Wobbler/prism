# Genre Theory

## Overview

Genre Theory sorts films into categories based on the **constellation of conventions** they employ—visual codes, narrative structures, character types, themes, and audience expectations—and observes that genres operate as a contract between filmmaker and viewer. The core claim is that a film's effectiveness and meaning depend partly on how it negotiates that contract: fulfilling it (reassurance), subverting it (critique or surprise), or hybridizing it (fusion). A genre is not a fixed label but a **recurring pattern of repetition and difference** — each film in a genre repeats recognizable elements while introducing variations that define the era, authorial voice, and cultural moment.

## Categories

1. **Classical Genre** (also "stable genre" or "high-consensus genre")
   - A set of conventions that is widely recognized, heavily codified, and
     relatively stable across decades of production.
   - The conventions are **explicit and teachable**: a film student can name
     them; audiences recognize them instantly.
   - Discriminating criterion: the film deploys all or most core conventions
     without significant parody or deconstruction, and audiences do not
     question whether it belongs in the genre.
   - Example: a Western with a lone gunslinger in a frontier town, a clear
     moral code, and a climactic shootout; a heist film with a team assembly,
     a detailed plan, a complication, and a final reveal.

2. **Emerging Genre** (also "proto-genre")
   - A set of conventions that is **not yet fully codified** but shows
     recognizable repetition across recent films; the pattern is becoming
     visible.
   - Audiences and critics perceive a shared DNA, but there is little
     institutional consensus on the name or precise criteria.
   - Discriminating criterion: the films share narrative or thematic DNA and
     are often grouped together in retrospect, but the genre has no stable
     term yet (or has one very recently); the conventions are still being
     negotiated film to film.
   - Example: the "elevated horror" or "folk horror" cluster of the 2010s–2020s
     (e.g., *The Witch*, *Midsommar*), or the "mumblegore" movement of the
     mid-2000s.

3. **Hybrid / Cross-Genre**
   - A film that **deliberately combines core conventions from two or more
     established genres** in a way that creates a new identity, not merely
     borrowing elements.
   - The combination is **systematic and visible**, not incidental.
   - Discriminating criterion: removing either parent genre's conventions
     would significantly change the film's structure and meaning; the film
     is readable as a successful negotiation between two genre contracts
     simultaneously.
   - Example: *Shaun of the Dead* (zombie horror + sitcom buddy comedy);
     *Brokeback Mountain* (Western + intimate romance); *Get Out* (horror
     + social thriller + satire).

4. **Revisionist Genre** (also "deconstruction" or "subversive genre")
   - A film that **invokes genre conventions explicitly but undermines,
     inverses, or critiques them** to expose their assumptions or ideological
     content.
   - The conventions are recognizable precisely so they can be **questioned or
     rejected**.
   - Discriminating criterion: the film could not make its argument without
     the audience knowing the genre contract it is violating; its meaning
     depends on genre literacy in the viewer.
   - Example: *Johnny Guitar* (Western that inverts the masculine hero and
     frontier mythology); *Mulholland Drive* (deconstructs the Hollywood
     success narrative and the femme fatale archetype); *The Ballad of Little
     Jo* (revisionist Western centering a woman passing as a man).

5. **Post-Generic / Anti-Generic**
   - A film that **deliberately avoids or refuses genre convention** — either
     by mixing conventions so densely that no single genre applies, or by
     using a deliberate aesthetics of refusal.
   - There is no clear contract with the audience; expectations are
     frustrated or made ambiguous.
   - Discriminating criterion: the film resists being placed in any
     established category; critics may struggle to name it or may resort to
     describing its surface rather than its genre.
   - Example: *Stalker* (Tarkovsky), *Mulholland Drive* (when read as
     anti-narrative), *Holy Motors* (Leos Carax); experimental or avant-garde
     work that operates outside genre logic altogether.

## Classification Procedure

1. **Identify the core plot and visual / sonic codes.** Write a short
   paragraph describing the film's narrative engine, setting, visual palette,
   and tone. Note recurring imagery, music style, and recurring character
   types.

2. **Ask: "Do I recognize these conventions? Do they match a known genre?"**
   - If **yes and strongly**, go to step 3.
   - If **yes but weakly, or divided between genres**, go to step 4.
   - If **no, or I cannot name the pattern**, go to step 5.

3. **Is the film deploying the conventions straightforwardly, or is it
   questioning them?**
   - If **straightforwardly** (the conventions are affirmed, not critiqued)
     → **Classical Genre**.
   - If **questioning or inverting** (the conventions are the point of
     critique) → **Revisionist Genre**.

4. **Does the film combine conventions from multiple genres in a way that is
   essential to its meaning, or is it just borrowing?**
   - If **essential combination** (you need both genre contracts to read the
     film) → **Hybrid / Cross-Genre**.
   - If **borrowing incidentally** (the film is primarily one genre with
     elements of another), re-classify as Classical or Revisionist of the
     primary genre.

5. **Can the conventions be identified at all, or does the film resist naming?**
   - If **conventions are identifiable but very new** (not yet stable or
     widely codified) → **Emerging Genre**.
   - If **conventions are deliberately mixed or refused** (the film's
     aesthetics include refusal of generic coherence) → **Post-Generic /
     Anti-Generic**.

6. **Record the classification and name the convention cluster that applies.**
   If Hybrid or Revisionist, name both the parent genre(s) and the specific
   conventions being deployed or questioned.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Interpretive approach | What the analyst should do |
|---|---|---|
| **Classical** | Analyze the film within its genre contract; measure success against how well it executes familiar conventions and where it introduces variation. | Use established genre lenses (e.g., Western mythology, detective procedural logic); ask what this version contributes to the genre's evolution. |
| **Emerging** | Track the nascent conventions and their filmmakers' negotiation of them; expect instability and variation across films. | Name the shared DNA explicitly; do not force a premature label; observe what becomes stable and what gets dropped. |
| **Hybrid / Cross-Genre** | Analyze the tension and synthesis between parent genres; ask how their contracts conflict or reinforce each other. | Use two genre lenses in parallel; examine where the film chooses to privilege one genre over the other. |
| **Revisionist** | The genre conventions are the **subject**, not the framework; analyze what assumptions the film is critiquing. | Ask what ideological or narrative work the original genre contract did, and what the revision exposes or argues. |
| **Post-Generic / Anti-Generic** | Analyze the film's refusal of categorization as a deliberate formal choice; look for its own internal logic instead. | Use medium-specific, formal lenses (color, rhythm, spatial composition) rather than narrative-genre lenses; tolerate ambiguity. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Classical mistaken for Revisionist.** Assuming any deviation from the
  most common variant of a genre means the film is critiquing the genre. The
  tell: the film is not arguing *against* the genre; it is simply producing
  a new variant within it. *The Searchers* revised the Western in some ways,
  but it is Classical, not Revisionist, because it ultimately affirms the
  genre's values (however uncomfortably).

- **Emerging mistaken for Post-Generic.** Treating a nascent but coherent
  pattern as if it has no conventions yet. The tell: you can name shared DNA
  across films (e.g., folk-horror or elevated horror), even if the term is
  new. If you cannot name the pattern at all, it is post-generic.

- **Hybrid mistaken for Classical (one parent only).** Treating a genuinely
  cross-genre work as if it belongs to its primary genre only. The tell: the
  film's meaning and structure depend *equally* on two genre contracts; drop
  either one and it falls apart. *Shaun of the Dead* is not a zombie film
  with comedy elements; it is a 50/50 negotiation of horror and sitcom.

- **Revisionist mistaken for Classical.** Assuming a film that follows genre
  conventions is not critiquing them. The tell: look at the *argument* — what
  is the film saying about the genre's assumptions? *Mulholland Drive* invokes
  Hollywood narratives explicitly in order to dismantle them; it is
  Revisionist, not Classical, because its meaning depends on you knowing what
  it is rejecting.

- **Anti-Generic mistaken for Emerging.** Treating a film that actively
  refuses genre coherence as if it is simply a new genre being born. The
  tell: an emerging genre has *recognizable repetition across films*; a truly
  post-generic work resists that repetition as a formal principle. *Stalker*
  is not a new genre; it is a refusal of genre logic altogether.

# Metz's Grande Syntagmatique

## Overview

Metz's Grande Syntagmatique is a taxonomic frame that sorts narrative film sequences into eight structural categories based on how they organize **diegetic time** (the story's time) and **screen time** (the film's time). Its core claim is that different sequence types construct narrative meaning through fundamentally different temporal and logical relationships — and that identifying the type of a sequence reveals what kind of narrative work it is performing. The frame is essential for understanding how cinema as a language communicates story across the basic unit of the sequence.

## Categories

1. **Autonomous Shot (Segment Autonome)**
   - A single shot that stands alone as a complete narrative unit, **not embedded in a broader syntagm**.
   - The shot has internal temporal coherence but no causal or temporal link to surrounding sequences.
   - Discriminating criterion: removal of the shot would not break narrative continuity; it is episodic.
   - Example: an establishing shot of a cityscape; a symbolic or poetic insert that ornaments but does not advance plot.

2. **Parallel Syntagm (Syntagme en Parallèle)**
   - **Two or more sequences that unfold simultaneously** in diegetic time but are presented sequentially on screen.
   - Each sequence is narratively autonomous (could stand alone) but gains meaning from the **montage relationship** to the other.
   - Discriminating criterion: the sequences are cut back and forth between each other; they do not show the same action.
   - Example: cross-cutting between a heist in progress and police closing in; two lovers' phone conversations intercut.

3. **Descriptive Syntagm (Syntagme Descriptif)**
   - A sequence that **catalogs objects, spaces, or attributes** within a single diegetic moment or location.
   - No causal or temporal progression; the sequence unfolds as a spatial or analytical tour.
   - Discriminating criterion: the sequence could be rearranged (shot reordered) without breaking logic; it is about *showing*, not *narrating*.
   - Example: a camera pan through a room's décor; a montage of details that establish a character's wealth or taste.

4. **Alterning Syntagm (Syntagme Alternant)**
   - **Two or more actions that alternate** in a single syntagm with a **causal or functional relationship** between them.
   - Unlike Parallel, the alternation creates meaning through rhythm and convergence, not simultaneity.
   - Discriminating criterion: the alternating shots build toward a single outcome or emotional peak.
   - Example: cutting between a character's face and an approaching threat, building suspense; alternating between pursuer and pursued in a chase.

5. **Scene (Scène)**
   - A sequence in which **diegetic time equals screen time** — what is shown on screen takes as long to unfold as it would in the story world.
   - Typically a dialogue exchange, an action set-piece, or an interaction with minimal ellipsis.
   - Discriminating criterion: the passage of time on screen matches the passage of time in the story; no temporal compression.
   - Example: a conversation between two characters in real time; a dance or physical performance shown in its entirety.

6. **Episode (Épisode)**
   - A sequence in which **diegetic time far exceeds screen time** — significant story time is compressed into brief screen time through ellipsis or montage.
   - The sequence is still coherent as a narrative unit; its events follow causally or chronologically.
   - Discriminating criterion: the passage of time in the story is much longer than the time it takes to show on screen.
   - Example: a montage showing a character's training over months; a sequence abbreviating a journey from morning to evening.

7. **Summary (Résumé)**
   - A brief sequence that **condenses an extended period or complex chain of events** into minimal screen time, often through voice-over, montage, or rapid cutting.
   - The sequence is **summative** rather than detailed; it conveys that something happened rather than showing the happening.
   - Discriminating criterion: the viewer understands the gist and outcome but not the particulars; the sequence is a narrative report.
   - Example: a voice-over recapping backstory; a title card stating "Ten years passed"; a rapid montage of consequences.

8. **Bracket Syntagm / Parenthetical Syntagm (Syntagme Parenthétique)**
   - A sequence that **interrupts or brackets** the main narrative line, often to provide context, flashback, or editorial comment.
   - The sequence is **embedded within or interruptive of** the primary narrative; its inclusion is optional to plot but adds meaning.
   - Discriminating criterion: the sequence begins and ends with a clear signal of interruption (dissolve, music cue, fade); removing it does not break plot logic.
   - Example: a flashback within a scene; a dream sequence; a brief cutaway to a reaction or consequence; an authorial intrusion.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the **boundaries of the sequence** — where does the narrative unit begin and end?

2. Ask: **"Does this sequence show two or more locations/actions that unfold at the same diegetic moment, cut together?"**
   - If **yes** → Parallel Syntagm.
   - If **no**, go to step 3.

3. Ask: **"Does the sequence primarily *show* or *describe* a space, object, or set of details without advancing plot or action?"**
   - If **yes** → Descriptive Syntagm.
   - If **no**, go to step 4.

4. Ask: **"Does the sequence present two or more alternating actions that build toward a single outcome or emotional peak?"**
   - If **yes** → Alterning Syntagm.
   - If **no**, go to step 5.

5. Ask: **"Is this sequence embedded within or a bracket around the main narrative — a flashback, dream, or interruption?"**
   - If **yes** → Bracket Syntagm.
   - If **no**, go to step 6.

6. Ask: **"Does the diegetic time equal the screen time? Is the action shown in real time?"**
   - If **yes** → Scene.
   - If **no**, go to step 7.

7. Ask: **"Is the sequence a coherent chain of events, but with significant time compressed via ellipsis or montage?"**
   - If **yes** → Episode.
   - If **no** (the sequence is fragmentary, very brief, or purely summative) → Summary.

8. If the sequence is **a single shot with no relation to surrounding material**, classify as Autonomous Shot.

## Implications per Category

| Category | Narrative function | Temporal logic | Agent approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Autonomous Shot** | Ornament, symbol, or punctuation | Suspended; no internal development | Analyze for thematic contribution or aesthetic function; question whether removal changes meaning. |
| **Parallel Syntagm** | Establish simultaneity, irony, contrast, or convergence | Multiple simultaneous timelines; rearrangement breaks rhythm | Examine the meaning of the juxtaposition; what does cutting between them communicate? |
| **Descriptive Syntagm** | Establish place, mood, or character detail | Spatial rather than temporal; rearrangeable | Analyze as *exposition* or *world-building*; ask what aspect of the diegesis is being shown. |
| **Alterning Syntagm** | Build suspense, intensity, or irony; drive toward climax | Rhythmic; timing and pacing are crucial | Analyze the emotional arc; the cutting pattern *is* the meaning. |
| **Scene** | Dramatize character interaction, dialogue, or action in full | Real time; no ellipsis | Treat as a stage scene transposed to cinema; dialogue and gesture carry meaning. |
| **Episode** | Narrate a chain of events or progression with selective detail | Compressed but coherent; ellipsis is selective | Analyze what was omitted and why; the montage pattern signals importance. |
| **Summary** | Convey backstory, consequences, or passage of time efficiently | Highly compressed; editorial | Treat as *voice-over narration* or *report*; analyze what is being communicated implicitly. |
| **Bracket Syntagm** | Provide context, memory, or reflexive commentary | Interruptive; clearly marked as aside | Analyze the relationship between bracket and main narrative; is it causally necessary or thematic? |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Parallel mistaken for Alterning.** Both involve cutting between two actions, but Parallel emphasizes simultaneity while Alterning emphasizes functional convergence. The tell: in Parallel, the two actions are independent; in Alterning, one is triggered by or responds to the other.

- **Episode mistaken for Summary.** Both compress time, but Episode maintains coherent causal or chronological flow whereas Summary reports outcomes without showing the chain. The tell: Episode can be expanded into multiple scenes; Summary is irreducibly brief.

- **Descriptive mistaken for Episode.** Both may be shot as montage, but Descriptive is spatial (a tour of a place) while Episode is temporal (events over time). The tell: Descriptive can be rearranged; Episode has a logical order.

- **Scene mistaken for Episode.** Both show action, but Scene runs in real time while Episode compresses. The tell: Scene contains dialogue or sustained interaction; Episode omits or abbreviates intervening moments.

- **Bracket Syntagm mistaken for part of the main narrative.** A flashback can be confusing if it is not clearly marked with a visual or musical cue. The tell: a bracket is formally punctuated (dissolve, fade, music change) and has a discrete start/end.

- **Autonomous Shot miscounted as a syntagm.** A single shot is not a syntagm because it has no internal structure or relationship to narrate. The tell: a syntagm contains at least two elements in relationship; an autonomous shot stands alone.

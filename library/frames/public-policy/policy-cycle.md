# Policy Cycle

## Overview

The Policy Cycle sorts a policy initiative or problem into one of five sequential stages based on where it stands in its lifecycle — from initial recognition of a problem through final assessment of outcomes. Its core claim is that **each stage has structurally different participants, decision logics, and failure modes**. A lever that works in Formulation (expert deliberation, evidence synthesis) will not work in Implementation (bureaucratic coordination, resource allocation); applying the wrong playbook to a stage is a systematic source of policy failure. The cycle is not truly linear — policies loop back, stall, or reverse — but classifying the current stage clarifies which constraints are active and which actors hold the deciding power.

## Categories

1. **Agenda-Setting**
   - A public problem has been **recognized and placed on the political agenda** for consideration by decision-makers.
   - The defining moment is when a problem moves from private concern to collective action priority — visibility and legitimacy are established, but not yet a solution.
   - Discriminating criterion: decision-makers acknowledge the problem exists and agree it warrants public intervention, though they may not agree on a cause or remedy.
   - Example: a public health crisis (e.g., opioid epidemic) is declared a national emergency; climate change is formally recognized as a policy priority.

2. **Formulation**
   - **Policy options and potential solutions are designed, researched, and debated** among experts, advocates, and analysts.
   - Multiple plausible instruments (regulation, incentive, subsidy, information campaign) are proposed and compared.
   - Discriminating criterion: the locus of work is analysis and deliberation; decision-makers are not yet required to choose, only to receive evidence and expert guidance.
   - Example: a health ministry convenes working groups to draft treatment guidelines; economists model tax policy alternatives.

3. **Decision**
   - **A binding choice is made** — a law is passed, a regulation is issued, or an executive order is signed.
   - The decision selects one or more policy instruments from the range formulated and allocates authority and resources.
   - Discriminating criterion: the moment when political power (legislature, executive, judiciary) formally adopts a specific course of action; from this point, reversing the choice requires explicit action.
   - Example: Congress votes on a healthcare bill; a regulatory agency issues a final rule; a government announces a target for carbon emissions reduction.

4. **Implementation**
   - **The adopted policy is operationalized** — bureaucracies, vendors, and target populations execute the policy rules.
   - Money is spent, rules are enforced, services are delivered, or behavior is expected to change in response to the policy.
   - Discriminating criterion: the locus of work moves from political/deliberative to administrative/operational; success or failure is now constrained by organizational capacity, coordination, and compliance.
   - Example: a tax agency enforces a new tax code; schools implement new curriculum standards; a regulatory agency inspects compliance with environmental standards.

5. **Evaluation**
   - **The outcomes and effects of the implemented policy are measured and assessed** against the original problem or stated goals.
   - Evidence is gathered on whether the policy reduced the problem, created unintended consequences, or produced other observable effects.
   - Discriminating criterion: the primary question shifts from "Is this being done?" to "Did it work? For whom? At what cost?"
   - Example: a government audits the effectiveness of a subsidy program; researchers measure health outcomes after a medication approval policy; an agency conducts a retrospective cost-benefit analysis.

## Classification Procedure

1. Identify the policy initiative or intervention by name and the primary problem it addresses.
2. Ask **"Has a decision-maker formally adopted a binding policy choice?"**
   - If **no**, proceed to step 3.
   - If **yes**, proceed to step 4.
3. Ask **"Have expert and stakeholder groups deliberated multiple options and produced evidence-based analysis?"**
   - If **no** → **Agenda-Setting** (problem recognized, solutions not yet formulated).
   - If **yes** → **Formulation** (options analyzed, decision not yet made).
4. Ask **"Is a formal policy choice now being executed by administrative agencies or target populations?"**
   - If **no** → **Decision** (choice made, implementation not yet underway).
   - If **yes**, ask: **"Are outcomes being systematically measured and assessed against the original goal?"**
     - If **no** → **Implementation** (policy is live and operating).
     - If **yes** → **Evaluation** (outcomes are being studied).
5. Record the stage and note whether the initiative is **moving forward** (e.g., Formulation → Decision) or **cycling back** (e.g., Evaluation → Formulation, if new evidence prompts redesign).

## Implications per Category

| Stage | Primary actors | Dominant constraint | Next step if stuck |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Agenda-Setting** | Activists, media, issue entrepreneurs, elected officials | **Visibility and political will** — the problem must be salient enough to displace competing priorities. | Amplify evidence of harms; build a coalition; link the issue to a broader narrative. |
| **Formulation** | Experts, analysts, civil servants, advocacy groups, think tanks | **Knowledge and credibility** — decision-makers need enough evidence and consensus to justify a choice. | Commission more research; convene broader expert panels; run pilots to reduce uncertainty. |
| **Decision** | Legislatures, executives, courts, regulatory bodies | **Political consensus and constitutional authority** — the chosen instrument must be legally defensible and politically coalable. | Negotiate; broker compromise; resolve constitutional ambiguity; secure budget allocation. |
| **Implementation** | Bureaucracies, service providers, employers, target populations | **Organizational capacity and compliance** — the policy cannot work faster or better than the agencies executing it. | Train staff; redesign workflows; adjust timelines; address incentive misalignments; remove bottlenecks. |
| **Evaluation** | Auditors, researchers, oversight bodies, media | **Measurement and interpretation** — attribution is hard; multiple stories can be told about the same data. | Invest in longitudinal data; use comparison groups; publish findings transparently; distinguish intended from unintended effects. |

## Common Misclassifications

- **Agenda-Setting mistaken for Formulation.** A problem has been named and is in the news, but no serious policy research has begun. The tell is that when asked "What are the candidate solutions?", there is only one vague proposal or competing ideological assertions, not evidence-based options. Consequence: a premature decision that reflects ideology over analysis.

- **Formulation mistaken for Decision.** Expert working groups have produced detailed policy briefs, but no binding decision has been formally made by an authorized body. The tell is that the policy can still be rejected or redesigned without amendment or repeal. Consequence: treating a draft proposal as law and beginning implementation prematurely.

- **Decision mistaken for Implementation.** A law has been signed, but the agencies charged with execution have not yet operationalized it — rules are not written, budgets not allocated, staff not trained. The tell is that a target population or regulated entity cannot yet identify what they are required to do. Consequence: assuming the policy will execute on schedule when the hard work is just beginning.

- **Implementation mistaken for Evaluation.** A policy is being carried out, but systematic outcome measurement has not begun or is not yet completed. The tell is that there is no independent assessment of whether the policy is achieving its stated goal. Consequence: continuing to invest in a policy that may not be working, or stopping it prematurely because no one has looked.

- **Cycling without recognizing it.** Evaluation reveals that a policy failed or had perverse effects, and the cycle loops back to Formulation (redesign) or Agenda-Setting (the original problem is still unsolved, a new approach is needed). The tell is that the stage labels change but the actors and constraints look familiar. Consequence: policy fatigue or whipsaw if each cycle is treated as a fresh start instead of learning from the previous iteration.
